Nvidia's earnings miss triggers a Nasdaq rout that spreads to European equities overnight

The Nasdaq MarketSite display in Times Square — the index retreated in heavy selling after Nvidia's guidance disappointed elevated market expectations. — Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 3.0
In one sentence
Nvidia's $91B revenue guidance beat consensus but fell short of whisper numbers, sending NVDA down 1.8% and triggering a cross-continental tech selloff from Nasdaq to European chipmakers.

Nvidia reported quarterly results on Wednesday evening that, by any traditional yardstick, were extraordinary. Revenue of $81.6 billion — up 85 percent year on year — beat consensus by more than two billion dollars. Adjusted earnings per share came in at $1.87 against a $1.77 estimate. Gross margins held at 75 percent. Data centre revenues of $75.2 billion outpaced projections by a further $1.7 billion. None of it was enough.

By Thursday's close in New York, Nvidia shares had shed 1.77 percent to $219.51, pulling semiconductors broadly lower. The Nasdaq retreated on heavy volume, reflecting wide participation in the liquidation rather than a narrow reaction to a single stock. By the time European markets opened Friday morning, the selling had crossed the Atlantic.

The bar that moved

The explanation lies not in what Nvidia delivered but in what the market had demanded it deliver. The company's guidance for the current quarter — approximately $91 billion in revenue — cleared the median Street estimate of roughly $87 billion by a comfortable margin. What it failed to clear was the ceiling set by the most bullish forecasts, which ran as high as $96 billion. Bloomberg characterised the forward outlook as "lackluster," and that framing, however ungenerous to a business growing at near-doubling rates, captured the market's mood with some precision.

"Wall Street has pulled itself above the company's own guide for the first time in this cycle, which raises the bar for what counts as a beat."

The dynamic is a familiar feature of crowded consensus trades: when positioning is sufficiently concentrated, the required positive surprise outpaces what the underlying business can plausibly deliver. Nvidia's guidance for the May–July quarter would, in any prior cycle, have been received as bullish. In this one, it registered as a shortfall.

Chief Executive Jensen Huang used the call to emphasise Nvidia's progress diversifying beyond the handful of hyperscalers that have funded its expansion — citing robotics, autonomous vehicles, and sovereign AI projects as emerging revenue pillars. The business logic is sound. The market was focused elsewhere.

Competitive pressure provided a secondary source of anxiety. AMD has grown its AI chip business sharply, and domestic Chinese chipmakers have begun substituting for Nvidia's export-restricted H200 products in specific customer segments. The question of whether Nvidia retains pricing power and share as the AI infrastructure cycle matures became harder to dismiss after Wednesday's results. With $80 billion in buybacks announced and the quarterly dividend lifted to 25 cents, the capital return message was unambiguous — but the market read it as a signal that revenue hypergrowth may be plateauing rather than as confirmation of confidence.

Contagion at dawn

Nvidia's post-earnings slide did not stay contained to California. European semiconductor and equipment stocks, which track AI infrastructure sentiment closely because of their supply-chain exposure, fell when trading opened on the continent.

ASML, the Dutch lithography company without whose extreme-ultraviolet machines Nvidia's most advanced chips cannot be manufactured, fell in sympathy — a reliable proxy for the direction of AI capital expenditure. STMicroelectronics, whose broad semiconductor exposure makes it a leading gauge of sector confidence, moved lower as well. The logic is direct: if markets revise down their expectations for AI infrastructure spending growth, demand for the upstream equipment and components that make the chips follows accordingly.

The FTSE 100 retreated in London as the overnight technology weakness weighed on indices. The UK benchmark has limited direct semiconductor exposure, but its internationalised large-caps reflect global risk appetite, and a broad reappraisal of AI-adjacent positioning hit sentiment across sectors. Technology and growth-oriented holdings bore the heaviest losses.

The cross-regional character of the selling underlines a structural feature of the current market: Nvidia has become so central to the AI investment thesis — so thoroughly embedded in the positioning of growth-oriented funds across time zones — that its earnings reports function as a referendum on the broader trade. A disappointed reading travels at the speed of the overnight session.

What the numbers reveal

The episode is worth examining for what it says about pricing, rather than fundamentals. Nvidia's business is genuinely extraordinary: an 85 percent revenue increase is not a modest result, and $75 billion in quarterly data centre sales reflects real and continuing demand for AI infrastructure. The selloff is not a verdict on Nvidia's business model.

It is, instead, a verdict on how the stock — and by extension the wider tech sector — had been positioned going into Wednesday evening. When a single stock's failure to match whisper numbers, rather than analyst consensus, triggers cross-continental deleveraging at this scale, the trade is crowded. The mechanism requires no fundamental deterioration to produce sharp drawdowns; it requires only a gap between expectation and delivery, however narrow that gap proves to be.

For any positioning strategy built on the assumption that AI profitability is both inevitable and imminent, this week's action provides a useful corrective: execution matters, the bar keeps moving, and the consensus is exposed each time Nvidia steps to the podium.

The next test arrives in approximately twelve weeks, when Nvidia is scheduled to report second-quarter results against its own guidance figure of $91 billion — a target that now functions as the floor rather than the ceiling. Whether the market builds another whisper premium above it in the interim, and how large that premium grows, will determine whether this week's reset was a healthy recalibration or the opening chapter of a more extended unwind.
