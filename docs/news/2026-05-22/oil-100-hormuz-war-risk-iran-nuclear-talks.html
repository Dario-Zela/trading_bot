Oil holds above $100 as Hormuz war-risk premiums surge despite nuclear diplomacy

The Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly one-quarter of global seaborne oil trade passes — currently the central lever in US-Iran peace negotiations — Perry-Castañeda Library / University of Texas at Austin, Public Domain
In one sentence
Brent holds above $104 as Iran confirms enrichment and Hormuz stay unresolved, while Lloyd's $10m–$14m per-voyage premiums signal markets see no near-term deal.

Crude oil held above $100 a barrel on Friday as traders weighed a senior Iranian official's blunt assessment against diplomatic signals from Washington that a deal to end the US-Iran conflict might be imminent — and concluded the gap was still too wide to sell.

Brent crude was trading near $104 a barrel, recovering from a brief dip below $100 on Wednesday after President Donald Trump declared the talks were in their "final stages." The bounce tracked a concurrent announcement from Iran's Supreme Leader that the country's enriched uranium reserves would not be sent abroad — hardening Tehran's position on one of Washington's central demands and snapping back the geopolitical premium that had briefly deflated.

The whipsaw captures the tension now embedded in energy markets: every optimistic signal from the negotiating table is quickly counterpunched by evidence that the hardest issues remain untouched.

Narrowing gaps, unmoved sticking points

A senior Iranian source told Reuters on Thursday that the two sides had made incremental progress but were still gridlocked on the fundamentals. "No agreement has been reached so far, but the gaps have narrowed," the source said. "Uranium enrichment and our control of the Strait of Hormuz remain among the main points of contention."

That two-sentence summary contains the architecture of the entire impasse. Tehran wants any deal sequenced on its terms: end the war first, settle Hormuz second, and only then discuss nuclear concessions. Washington has been pushing the opposite order, under pressure from Trump to secure a visible diplomatic win quickly. The proposed 14-point memorandum of understanding — which would open a 30-day detailed negotiation window — requires Iran to accept an enrichment moratorium of at least 12 years, capping activity above 3.67% uranium concentration. That is a compromise between Tehran's five-year counter-offer and Washington's original 20-year demand, but the sequencing question still sits in front of the substance.

Iran is reviewing the latest US proposal. Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei confirmed Tehran had received Washington's views and was studying them. Trump said he was "willing to wait a few days to get the right answers" — though aides briefed reporters separately that military options were being reconsidered with more seriousness than in recent weeks. US Secretary of State Marco Rubio described "some encouraging signs." Markets have learned not to price those in.

The insurance market's verdict

Where diplomatic statements remain ambiguous, the insurance market is not. Lloyd's of London underwriters are quoting war-risk premiums of $10 million to $14 million for a single voyage through the Strait of Hormuz on a standard very large crude carrier — a vessel insured at around $138 million. That represents a rate of roughly 7%–10% of hull value for a single transit, against a pre-war baseline of 0.15%–0.25%.

The number matters because it is a market-clearing price for uncertainty. Insurers are not paid to be optimistic. At $10m–$14m per trip, a round voyage from the Gulf to East Asia and back can add $20 million or more in additional freight costs alone — enough to reshape trade routing decisions and, ultimately, the price of the cargo itself. Marine insurers have been explicit that a ceasefire declaration will not be sufficient to bring premiums back to pre-war levels; they are waiting for verified, sustained reopening of the strait under conditions that do not expose vessels to seizure or strike.

The strait remains the single most consequential chokepoint in global energy logistics. Roughly one-fifth of global oil consumption — and around one-quarter of all seaborne oil trade — transits the 33-kilometre-wide passage between Iran and Oman. Around one-fifth of global LNG shipments, primarily from Qatar, also pass through it. When Iran's negotiating team signals that control of the strait is a bargaining chip rather than a settled matter of international law, those percentages become the market's exposure.

Some Asian operators — notably two Chinese-owned VLCCs — resumed transits in the third week of May, a sign that at least some charterers were prepared to pay the premium. That is not normality returning; it is the price-discovery phase of a disruption that has not resolved.

What $100 oil is actually pricing

A Bloomberg survey of market participants, published Thursday, found a growing consensus that crude will remain capped near $100 a barrel for the next year, as demand gradually adjusts to the loss of millions of barrels of daily Gulf supply. The $100 level has become less a price target than a clearing price — where constrained supply meets demand destruction from elevated costs.

The scenario for a sharp correction — a quick deal opening Hormuz by June — would push Brent toward $80 by year-end, according to modelling cited by Bloomberg. But the insurance premium data, the Supreme Leader's uranium stockpile order, and the continued deadlock over sequencing collectively suggest that scenario is not the one the market is underwriting.

The next concrete indicator will be Tehran's formal response to the current US proposal — likely within days, given Trump's stated patience window. If Iran insists on settling Hormuz before any nuclear discussion, and Washington refuses again, energy markets will have their answer: $100-plus is not a disruption premium. It is the new floor.
