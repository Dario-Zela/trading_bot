Senate recesses without passing Trump's reconciliation package

Senate Majority Leader John Thune, who told reporters the White House "would have gotten plenty of advice" had it consulted Congress before announcing the fund. — U.S. Senate Photographic Studio / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Senate recesses for Memorial Day without passing a $70bn reconciliation bill after up to 25 GOP senators revolted over a $1.8bn DOJ anti-weaponization fund the White House created without consulting Congress.

Senate Majority Leader John Thune told Republican senators Wednesday that the chamber would leave for its Memorial Day recess without voting on the administration's sweeping immigration enforcement package — a $70 billion reconciliation bill that had been the White House's clearest near-term legislative priority. The vote is now expected no earlier than the week of June 1.

The proximate cause was not Democratic obstruction. It was a rebellion inside the Republican conference.

A fund nobody asked for

The sticking point arrived last week when the Justice Department announced a $1.776 billion "Anti-Weaponization Fund" as part of a settlement under which President Trump voluntarily dropped his $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS over leaked tax records. Money for the fund comes from the federal Judgment Fund — a permanent Treasury appropriation — and is to be distributed by a five-member commission appointed by the Attorney General, with a mandate to compensate people who believe they were targeted by the government.

The White House had not consulted Senate leaders before the announcement. Thune, who is usually careful not to publicly criticise the administration, did not disguise his irritation. "It would have been nice if they had consulted," he told reporters. He added, with notably dry understatement: "It's water under the bridge now."

Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche was dispatched to the Hill on Thursday morning to contain the damage. The meeting, which ran close to two hours, was by multiple accounts unusually hostile. As many as 25 Republican senators spoke — a rarity in such gatherings — and virtually none defended the fund. Former Majority Leader Mitch McConnell framed the problem with characteristic bluntness: "The nation's top law enforcement official is asking for a slush fund to pay people who assault cops?"

Trump, for his part, posted a social-media warning that Republicans who failed to deliver should expect to find themselves "looking for a job much sooner than you thought possible." The threat did not noticeably move opinion in the conference room.

Fiscal calendar slips

The scale of what was being held up matters for budget watchers. The reconciliation vehicle contained roughly $70 billion in funding for Immigration and Customs Enforcement and Customs and Border Protection — agencies that have been running without regular appropriations since mid-February. ICE and CBP funding is the core of the administration's domestic enforcement agenda, and the reconciliation route was chosen precisely to avoid the 60-vote Senate threshold.

Delaying that package into June tightens an already compressed fiscal calendar. The administration had signalled it wanted the bill cleared before the Memorial Day break to establish a signing deadline; that goal is now gone. Broader reconciliation negotiations — including tax provisions, Medicaid changes, and clean-energy credit phase-outs — remain open, and each week of delay adds complexity to conference negotiations with the House.

The anti-weaponization fund may itself be stripped before a final vote, or subjected to restrictions limiting eligibility. Either outcome would require further negotiation with an administration that has shown little appetite for compromise on the provision. The first test of whether that gap can close will come when senators return to Washington on June 1.
