Air France and Airbus convicted over AF447 as the civil liability question now opens

F-GZCP, the Air France Airbus A330-203 that operated Flight 447, photographed before the June 2009 crash that killed all 228 aboard — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
A Paris appeals court convicted Air France and Airbus of involuntary manslaughter over the 2009 AF447 crash, reversing two acquittals and reopening civil liability questions.

The Paris Court of Appeal ruled on Wednesday that Airbus and Air France bear criminal responsibility for the 2009 crash of Flight AF447, which killed all 228 people aboard when an Airbus A330 plunged into the Atlantic en route from Rio de Janeiro. The verdict — the first criminal conviction in a legal saga that began seventeen years ago — overturns two prior acquittals and attaches a criminal record to two of Europe's most prominent publicly traded companies.

The fine is almost irrelevant as a financial matter. French law caps corporate penalties for involuntary manslaughter at €225,000 per company; for Airbus, which reported revenues above €65 billion last year, and for Air France-KLM, that figure is rounding error. The weight of Wednesday's ruling lies elsewhere: in the precedent it sets, in the civil litigation it could reanimate, and in what it signals about European courts' willingness to hold large corporations criminally responsible for disasters long treated as unclassifiable accidents.

Seventeen years to a verdict

Flight AF447 departed Galeão Airport in Rio de Janeiro on the night of May 31, 2009, and disappeared from radar in the early hours of June 1. Recovery vessels found debris within days, but the aircraft's black boxes were not retrieved from 3,980 metres below the surface until May 2011 — nearly two years after the crash.

The BEA, France's accident investigation bureau, concluded that ice crystals had clogged the aircraft's Thales-made pitot tubes — the sensors that measure airspeed — triggering a cascade of failures. The autopilot disconnected and the aircraft transitioned into "alternate law," losing its automated stall protections. The crew, confronted with contradictory instrument readings at 35,000 feet over the equatorial Atlantic in the early hours of the morning, failed to identify that the plane was in an aerodynamic stall. For more than three minutes, despite continuous stall warnings, the first officer maintained a nose-up pitch that the aircraft could not recover from.

What the appeals court found decisive was not that the crew erred, but that the error was foreseeable and preventable at the corporate level. Regulators and both companies had known for years that Thales pitot tubes were vulnerable to icing at cruise altitude. The fix was in progress — Air France had begun systematically replacing the suspect sensors on its A330 fleet the morning of June 1, 2009. The aircraft operating AF447 had not yet been updated.

Presiding judge Sylvie Madge described the crash as "a disaster waiting to happen" and one "that could have been avoided if each of the companies involved had fully grasped the seriousness of the failure." The court found Airbus had underestimated the sensor vulnerability and failed to adequately brief airlines about the risks. Air France had failed to provide its crews with training for high-altitude unreliable airspeed emergencies — the exact scenario that unfolded. Judge Madge noted that "the pilots of AF447 truly tried everything to get out of this absolutely dreadful situation," a line that shifted the moral burden entirely to the institutions above them.

The legal trail that ran cold — twice

The path to Wednesday's ruling was not linear. In 2019, the investigating judges dismissed the case, concluding that the causal chain between corporate failures and the crash was too attenuated for criminal liability. Prosecutors appealed that dismissal. A first-instance trial followed, and in 2023 a Paris court acquitted both companies again — acknowledging mistakes, but declining to cross the threshold into criminal wrongdoing.

The appeals court has now rejected that reasoning twice over. Both companies, in the court's words, were "solely and entirely responsible" for the crash. Airbus and Air France confirmed they will take the case to the Cour de Cassation, France's highest court, which examines questions of law rather than fact and is unlikely to re-litigate the evidentiary record. Airbus noted in a statement that the verdict contradicts the position taken by the Public Prosecutor at both stages of proceedings — an accurate observation that also reveals how contested the causal logic has been across successive chambers.

For the families of the 228 victims — 72 French nationals, 58 Brazilian, drawn from 33 countries — the symbolic currency of the word "guilty" is not nothing after seventeen years. Daniele Lamy, who heads the French victims' association, said the ruling had finally given weight to "the pain of the families." Nelson Faria Marinho, representing Brazilian families, tempered the response: he found the verdict incomplete because no individual executive had been charged.

The civil liability question now opens

The criminal fine settles nothing financially. Civil settlements between Air France and victim families had largely been concluded years earlier, and the criminal verdict does not automatically reopen those agreements. But a confirmed criminal conviction — should the Cour de Cassation uphold it — would shift the evidentiary baseline for any outstanding claims and could shape how future proceedings in European jurisdictions evaluate corporate culpability in complex system failures.

For equity investors, the direct penalty is inconsequential. The harder question is structural: if the conviction stands, it establishes a precedent for European courts to pursue corporate criminal liability in aviation disasters where institutional failures, rather than individual acts, produced the outcome. Insurance underwriters are already examining their actuarial assumptions on corporate manslaughter exposure; hull and liability premiums for major carriers are expected to reflect the repricing of long-tail litigation risk.

Airbus shares moved lower in Paris trading on Wednesday, though the market reaction was complicated by unrelated news — the company was also absorbing reports of A350 delivery delays linked to supply-chain disruption at a US components facility. Isolating the verdict's price contribution from broader noise is difficult.

What is not in doubt is that the Cour de Cassation now carries the weight of a seventeen-year reckoning. Its ruling — expected to take at least a year — will determine whether Wednesday's verdict stands as a durable shift in European corporate accountability for catastrophic failures, or becomes one more turn in a case that has already outlasted most public attention. The next datapoint to watch is the formal lodging of Airbus's cassation appeal, which will set out the legal grounds the company believes can unravel a conviction that two lower courts declined to deliver.
