Reeves deepens North Sea levy as April borrowing overshoot squeezes her fiscal room

The Deepsea Delta drilling rig in the North Sea — UK output has fallen 77% from its 1999 peak and may drop below 1 million boe/d for the first time in 2026. — Erik Christensen / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
April borrowing hit £24.3bn — the second-highest on record — leaving Reeves with little choice but to deepen the North Sea levy and accelerate an investment retreat the basin can ill afford.

The numbers released by the ONS this morning removed whatever remained of the Chancellor's room for manoeuvre. Britain's public sector borrowed £24.3 billion in April, the second-highest April deficit on record, against a Reuters economist consensus of around £20.9 billion. The Office for Budget Responsibility's March 2026 forecast had projected the full-year deficit shrinking to 3.6 per cent of GDP — the tightest outturn since before the pandemic. One month in, the starting gun has misfired.

It is against that backdrop that Rachel Reeves's recent energy tax moves take on their full weight. Last month she told Parliament that the government was extending and thickening the windfall framework applied to energy companies, announcing a new Oil and Gas Price Mechanism to replace the Energy Profits Levy permanently, raising the Electricity Generator Levy from 45 to 55 per cent from 1 July, and closing a loophole that had allowed overseas oil and gas groups to channel UK trading profits through lightly-taxed foreign branches. Together, Reeves said the changes would raise "hundreds of millions of pounds a year."

A deficit wider than forecast

April's £24.3 billion shortfall — some £3.4 billion above consensus — is the first data point of the 2026/27 financial year and not an auspicious one. Monthly figures revise heavily and the rest of the year can compensate, but the overshoot narrows headroom the IFS had already described as wafer-thin at the time of the March Spring Statement.

The Iran conflict has complicated the picture in both directions. Rising oil prices produce higher EPL receipts — Treasury coffers benefit by roughly £20 million a day from elevated prices, according to one estimate — but the same conflict raises the probability of an economic slowdown that drags on income tax and VAT. The OBR's baseline, set six weeks before the Hormuz premium widened, already looks like a forecast drawn in calmer conditions.

It was that same geopolitical shock that extinguished the option Reeves had been quietly considering through February and March: ending the EPL early. Talks between the Chancellor and North Sea operators in early March had signalled a serious government discussion about bringing the levy's sunset forward, given pre-conflict forecasts that oil prices would drop below the EPL's threshold of $78.65 per barrel before long. When Middle East hostilities pushed prices sharply higher, the fiscal calculus reversed overnight. The concession became an extension.

The levy ratchets higher

The Energy Profits Levy has been recalibrated so many times since its 2022 introduction that the baseline rate is almost beside the point. Reeves's November 2024 Budget raised it from 25 to 38 per cent and pushed the sunset to March 2030. Combined with the 30 per cent Ring Fence Corporation Tax and a 10 per cent Supplementary Charge, the headline rate on North Sea oil and gas profits reached 78 per cent — among the steepest upstream burdens in the OECD.

The April statement added further layers. The Oil and Gas Price Mechanism is designed as a permanent architecture: a dynamic rate tied to commodity prices that would replace the blunter EPL, though detailed implementation remains to be settled. The Electricity Generator Levy increase to 55 per cent widens the tax net further into renewables and low-carbon power generation. And the crackdown on foreign oil and gas groups using overseas branch structures to minimise UK corporation tax on domestic trading profits closes a route that had been visible for years.

The government's argument is that elevated energy prices mean the sector can absorb the additional burden without undue strain. The industry's argument is that future investment decisions are made on long-run economics, not quarterly windfalls.

Investment in structural retreat

Offshore Energies UK has calculated that roughly nine in ten North Sea operators are now cutting capital expenditure. The industry body estimates that £50 billion of potential investment is being withheld, contingent on the levy regime being reformed or replaced. BP disclosed that the November 2024 rate increase alone would cost it an additional £539 million; Shell's comparable figure was approximately £382 million. Harbour Energy announced around 100 redundancies in its wake.

The underlying geology means the basin was shrinking regardless of tax policy. Production peaked at around 4.5 million barrels of oil equivalent per day in 1999 and has since fallen to approximately 1 million boe/d. The NSTA has said 2026 may be the last year output holds above that threshold; its central projection has production declining to 620,000 boe/d by 2030. No exploration wells were drilled on the UK Continental Shelf during 2024-25 — the first blank year in the basin's history — and none are scheduled for 2026. North Sea investment is expected to fall below $3.5 billion this year, its lowest level in real terms since the 1970s.

BP is now reviewing North Sea assets that could raise up to £2 billion in disposal proceeds, as part of a broader $20 billion global divestment programme. Whether its 45 per cent stake in the Clair field — the largest producing field on the UKCS — ends up on the block will be the clearest read on whether the exit represents portfolio trimming or something larger. Equinor and Shell have already merged their UK upstream operations; Eni absorbed Ithaca Energy; Repsol combined with Neo Energy. Structural consolidation has quietly become the dominant mode of a shrinking industry.

The contradiction at the heart of the Chancellor's position is unresolved. The EPL and its surrounding framework deliver revenues she needs now, and at current prices the numbers are not trivial. But each incremental tightening reduces the probability that the private capital needed for new development — and for the energy security the government has separately pledged — will materialise in the basin that once supplied a third of Britain's gas. Whether the Oil and Gas Price Mechanism, when it is fully designed, can offer enough stability to reverse that trend is the question the industry will study closely. The autumn Budget will be the next moment at which Reeves must show her hand. The intervening monthly borrowing figures will tell her how much room she has.
