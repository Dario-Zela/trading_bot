Brussels publishes AI Act compliance rules as London launches a voluntary frontier code

The European Commission's offices in Brussels, where draft high-risk AI classification guidelines were published on 19 May 2026 — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
EU draft classification rules arrive days after a deal already pushed enforcement to 2027; the UK issues voluntary frontier AI expectations with statutory teeth still pending.

Two weeks apart this month, Europe's twin regulatory capitals each took a step on artificial intelligence — one toward harder law, the other firmly away from it.

On 19 May, the European Commission published long-awaited draft guidelines explaining how companies should determine whether their AI systems qualify as "high-risk" under the EU AI Act. Two weeks earlier, on 7 May, the EU Council and Parliament had struck a political deal to push the compliance deadline for most of those same systems back by up to 16 months. And on 15 May, the Bank of England, Financial Conduct Authority and HM Treasury jointly issued voluntary guidance on frontier AI risk in the financial sector — nudging rather than mandating.

The juxtaposition is instructive. Brussels is clarifying rules it is simultaneously extending. London is issuing expectations it cannot currently enforce.

Brussels finally draws the line

The May 19 guidelines address a question companies have been asking since the AI Act entered force in 2024: which systems actually have to comply? The Act identifies two routes to "high-risk" classification under Article 6. The first applies when an AI system forms part of a product — medical devices, toys, industrial machinery — that already requires third-party safety certification under existing EU product law. The second catches stand-alone systems deployed in eight sensitive domains listed in Annex III: biometric identification, critical infrastructure management, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration processing, and the administration of justice.

What the draft guidelines add is interpretive substance. Classification, they explain, turns on "intended purpose" — the use a provider designs and documents the system for — not on whether a system could theoretically be deployed in a listed domain. An AI tool built to schedule HR meetings does not become high-risk by sitting inside an HR department; a system explicitly designed to rank job candidates does. The guidelines work through concrete examples across Annex III categories, which is precisely what regulators and companies have been requesting for two years.

The consultation period runs until 23 June. The guidelines will not bind anyone until formally adopted — but companies operating in the EU market should treat this window as their last practical opportunity to shape the final text.

The deadline that moved first

The classification guidance arrived just as many affected businesses had been calculating, with mounting anxiety, how much time remained before the original August 2, 2026 enforcement date. The answer had already changed.

Under the so-called Digital Omnibus deal struck on 7 May, the compliance date for standalone high-risk AI systems under Annex III shifts to December 2, 2027 — a 16-month extension. Systems embedded in regulated products under Annex I have until August 2, 2028. The Omnibus package still requires formal legislative adoption, but EU sources expect an accelerated process given the proximity of the original deadline.

The extension does not dissolve the compliance burden. Legal and compliance professionals consistently estimate that conformity assessments — the documented process by which providers and deployers demonstrate they meet the Act's requirements — take between three and six months to complete properly. Anyone who treats a December 2027 deadline as an invitation to start fresh in mid-2027 will miss it. The draft classification guidelines, even in their current form, are the practical starting point: companies cannot begin a conformity assessment until they know whether they need one.

The financial stakes are unchanged. Fines for non-compliance run to €35 million or 7 percent of global annual turnover — higher than GDPR's maximums on both counts.

London's softer gradient

The Starmer government's approach continues to favour soft law over binding rules. On 15 May, the Bank of England, FCA and HM Treasury published a joint statement on frontier AI and cyber resilience, naming five action areas for regulated financial firms: board-level governance, vulnerability management, third-party and supply-chain risk, technical controls, and response and recovery. The document carries no statutory weight but signals clear regulatory expectations from three institutions that do have enforcement powers over financial sector firms.

The broader policy posture is consistent with the UK's stated line since the AI Opportunities Action Plan: voluntary frameworks first, statutory powers held in reserve. DSIT has published a voluntary code of practice covering AI cybersecurity, and legislation to give the AI Security Institute formal powers over frontier model developers remains pending. The government has signalled a Frontier AI Bill could follow the next King's Speech — but it has made similar noises before without legislation appearing.

What the UK approach gains in flexibility and speed — the joint statement was issued weeks after the risks emerged in public view and can be updated without a legislative timetable — it sacrifices in predictability. Global firms running EU and UK compliance programmes in parallel face structurally different obligations: documented conformity assessments in Brussels, board-level awareness exercises in London.

What to watch

Three dates matter most. The EU's public consultation on its draft classification guidelines closes on 23 June; industry responses will shape what the final text requires. Formal adoption of the Digital Omnibus amendments is expected in the coming weeks, locking in the 2027 and 2028 deadlines. And the UK's summer legislative window will reveal whether a Frontier AI Bill materialises in the King's Speech or defers once again to voluntary guidance.

The regulatory distance between Brussels and London is not narrowing. For any company with material AI deployments on both sides of the Channel, the divergence is no longer a background risk — it is a compliance design problem.
