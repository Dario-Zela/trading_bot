SpaceX quarterly filing reveals $4.3bn loss as Starship development costs mount

SpaceX's Starship SN8 prototype lifts off from Boca Chica, Texas — the development programme cost $3bn in 2025 alone — Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 4.0
In one sentence
SpaceX's IPO prospectus reveals a $4.3bn Q1 loss — driven more by its xAI merger than Starship — as the company eyes a $1.75tn Nasdaq debut on June 12.

Elon Musk's rocket company spent two decades as the most closely held secret in American aerospace. On May 20 that changed, when SpaceX filed its S-1 prospectus with the SEC — the first time the company has opened its books to public scrutiny — ahead of a planned Nasdaq debut on June 12 under the ticker SPCX. What the filing revealed was not, on the face of it, pretty: a net loss of $4.27 billion in the first quarter of 2026, on revenue of $4.7 billion. The previous year's first-quarter loss was $528 million. Headlines duly appeared.

A small correction to how the story was packaged: this is not a "quarterly filing" in any standard sense. SpaceX has no obligation to publish quarterly accounts and has never done so. What the SEC received was an IPO prospectus — the formal document required of any company seeking a public listing. The distinction matters for how seriously the sourcing should be taken. When Benzinga attributed the loss figure to a "quarterly filing," it was conflating the mechanism. The number is real; the framing was loose.

The rocket is not, actually, the problem

The headline assigned most of the blame to Starship development. That is partly fair: the rocket programme spent $930 million on research and development in the quarter alone and consumed $3 billion in 2025. The space division posted a $662 million operating loss. But the Starship narrative is smaller than the one buried deeper in the prospectus.

In February 2026, SpaceX absorbed xAI — Musk's artificial intelligence start-up — into its corporate structure. The consolidated AI unit lost nearly $2.5 billion in the quarter. Capital expenditure nearly doubled to $20.7 billion, with more than $12.7 billion allocated to AI infrastructure. Before the xAI merger, SpaceX was profitable: the company is estimated to have returned $791 million in net income in 2024. The accumulated deficit now stands at $41.3 billion.

The only clean profit centre is Starlink, the satellite internet service, which generated $1.19 billion in operating profit in Q1. That one segment is effectively subsidising everything else — the rockets, the AI ambitions, the Mars aspirations.

Patrick Boyle, a former hedge fund manager and finance professor, described SpaceX as a "money furnace" within hours of the filing landing. "This is the most out-there valuation of any company you can think of," he said. "It's way beyond the valuation of Nvidia, and Nvidia is hugely profitable with massive margins. SpaceX just isn't." Nvidia, which has its own rough week documented elsewhere in this edition, still makes money. SpaceX, at present, makes Starlink money and burns the rest.

The valuation the company is seeking: $1.75 trillion. The targeted raise: roughly $75 billion, about two and a half times the size of Saudi Aramco's 2019 record IPO. NYU's Aswath Damodaran, a reliable sceptic in these situations, puts fair value closer to $1.22 trillion. Polymarket currently assigns 71% odds that the post-listing market cap exceeds $2 trillion. Somewhere in that gap is the trade, if there is one. June 12 is when the argument becomes real.
