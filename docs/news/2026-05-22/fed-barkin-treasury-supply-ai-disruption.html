Fed's Barkin flags structural Treasury supply shift and AI job disruption still shallow

The Federal Reserve Bank of Richmond building on East Byrd Street, designed by Minoru Yamasaki and completed in 1978. — Wikimedia Commons / formulanone (CC BY-SA 2.0)
In one sentence
Richmond Fed's Barkin declines to offer forward guidance and flags a possible structural shift in long-term Treasury supply-demand, while noting AI job disruption remains confined to software.

Richmond Federal Reserve President Tom Barkin used remarks in Raleigh on Thursday to raise a question carrying real weight for long-duration bond investors: whether the structural balance between supply and demand in the long-term Treasury market has permanently shifted under the weight of US borrowing.

"I wonder if the balance between supply and demand in the long-term Treasury market has shifted given the amount of US supply," Barkin said, speaking at ULI Triangle Capital Markets. The comment arrived without fanfare — Barkin's register rarely runs that way — but its implication is pointed. With the government running persistent deficits and the Treasury leaning on long-dated issuance, the pool of price-insensitive buyers that absorbed supply through the quantitative-easing era — foreign central banks, the Fed itself — has thinned. What replaces them, and at what yield, remains an open question.

Barkin hedged the concern: "Bond yields are still in a reasonable zone," he said, signaling no acute alarm. But raising a possible structural imbalance — even cautiously — is notable from an official who simultaneously declined to offer anything resembling forward guidance. "I do not feel like today is a time for strong forward guidance," he said, citing uncertainty across inflation, employment, and artificial intelligence's economic trajectory. He described himself as "nervous about the tails on both sides of the mandate" — a formulation that deliberately avoids signalling which way the Fed's next move falls. The 10-year Treasury yield was near 4.59% as of last week, the 2-year at 4.09%.

The backdrop is a supply picture that has not improved. The Senate has recessed without passing the White House's reconciliation package — meaning the fiscal arithmetic behind Treasury issuance is, if anything, more uncertain than it was a month ago.

Where AI Fits — and Where It Doesn't

On artificial intelligence, Barkin's framing was measured to the point of bluntness. Employers outside of software are not yet reducing headcount because of AI, he observed — a distinction that matters for anyone reading the labour market's recent resilience as justification for sustained tightening. Call centres and coding roles are seeing real displacement; the broader workforce has not yet registered it. "It is hard to reach conclusions on the short- vs long-term effects of AI," he said, effectively removing AI-driven macro disruption from the near-term policy calculus.

That caution sits alongside an inflation picture that remains well above target and has for years. Headline PCE ran at 3.5% year-over-year in March 2026, core PCE at 3.2%, against the Fed's 2% objective — now missed for more than five consecutive years. Barkin flagged the risk that successive supply shocks — COVID supply chains, the Ukraine invasion, tariff rounds, Middle East energy — could de-anchor inflation expectations through sheer accumulation. Businesses, he noted, are "much less confident about their ability to raise consumer prices to recoup costs," suggesting pricing power has eroded even as headline numbers stay elevated.

The next signal of whether Barkin's Treasury concern is gaining traction inside the Fed will come at the June FOMC meeting and in the composition of Treasury auctions through summer. If 30-year yields continue to drift higher without a demand-side response, the structural worry he outlined Thursday will start to look less like a question and more like a diagnosis.
