CENTRAL BANKS · By M. Halloway · 2026-05-22 · 5 min read

ECB, BoJ and BoE all push back on rate-cut bets as services inflation stays stubborn

The European Central Bank's twin-tower headquarters rises above Frankfurt's Main riverbank — the institution's hawkish pivot is at the centre of a coordinated central bank pushback. — Gerda Arendt / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
Three major central banks simultaneously push back on rate-cut bets, with sticky services inflation keeping the ECB, BoJ, and BoE firmly on hold—or moving higher.

The message from three of the world's most closely watched central banks landed within days of each other this week, and it pointed in one direction. The European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, and the Bank of England have each — in their own register — signaled that rate cuts are either further away than markets assumed or no longer the operative question at all. For traders who had built positions around an aggressive global easing cycle, the coordinated pushback carries meaningful repositioning risk.

Frankfurt Holds the Line

The ECB's hawkish shift has been building since March, when governing council minutes confirmed the bank was in no rush to ease. The April 30 decision to hold the deposit rate at 2.0% was widely expected; the tone surrounding it was not. ECB Executive Board member Isabel Schnabel, long the governing council's most influential hawk, warned in recent remarks that eurozone inflation risks are "tilted slightly upward" — a formulation that functions less as an economic assessment than as a signal that the rate-cut door has narrowed considerably.

The technical backdrop supports her. Eurozone headline CPI hit 3.0% in April, up from 2.6% in March, propelled partly by energy costs inflated by the Middle East conflict. Services inflation — the measure ECB policymakers track most closely, as it reflects domestic wage dynamics rather than external shocks — printed at 3.0% in April, down marginally from March's 3.3% but well above any level the governing council can plausibly call target-consistent. Wage growth in France and Italy continues to run above 4% annually.

ECB President Christine Lagarde has declined to pre-commit to any rate path, reiterating the bank's "data-dependent and meeting-by-meeting approach." The formula sounds anodyne, but it is doing real work: it keeps the June 11-12 meeting nominally open in both directions, which is precisely how governing councils signal that dovish market pricing has drifted from reality. As of Friday, contracts on Polymarket implied a 76.5% probability of a 25-basis-point hike at the June meeting — a striking reversal from the cut expectations that had characterized the consensus as recently as late 2025. Geopolitical risk adds weight to the hawkish case: Lagarde has warned that the longer the Middle East conflict runs, the stronger the inflation spillover into the broader economy becomes through energy pass-through and second-round effects.

Tokyo's Carry Trade Reckoning

The Bank of Japan is in a structurally related but distinct situation. The BoJ held its policy rate at 0.75% at the April 27-28 meeting, but the vote was 6-3 — the widest dissent since Governor Kazuo Ueda took office. The three dissenters, board members Hajime Takata, Naoki Tamura, and Junko Nakagawa, voted to raise the rate to 1.0% immediately. Ueda has since signaled that the bank expects at least one additional 25-basis-point hike before year-end, with September the most likely window.

The yen has responded. Dollar-yen slid toward 159 this week, the firmest level for the yen in three months, as the interest rate differential between the US and Japan — which peaked at around 525 basis points in 2024 — has compressed to roughly 300. That compression carries a specific mechanism: it erodes the economics of the yen carry trade.

For years, investors borrowed at near-zero cost in yen to fund positions in higher-yielding assets — US Treasuries, global equities, emerging-market debt. As Japanese borrowing costs rise, those positions deteriorate and unwind. The August 2024 episode showed how abrupt the process can be: a surprise BoJ hike knocked global markets sharply before calm returned. The current unwind is more gradual — Japan sold nearly $30 billion of US Treasuries in the first quarter of 2026, the fastest pace in four years — but the direction is set.

Britain's Wage-Inflation Problem

The Bank of England entered this cycle from a different starting point — Bank Rate at 3.75%, already in restrictive territory — and markets had priced in near-term cuts. That assumption is increasingly strained.

An MPC member, speaking at a City event this week, warned that services inflation is proving more persistent than the bank's May forecast assumed. The concern is specific: wage-driven price pressure in the services sector is not unwinding at the pace central bank models had anticipated. Monthly annualized underlying services inflation ran between 4% and 5% in the first quarter of 2026, against the roughly 2.75% to 3% range the BoE considers target-consistent. The March services CPI reading was 4.5%; the bank's own forecasts put it falling to 3.8% by September, but internal and external observers are growing skeptical of that trajectory.

The MPC voted 8-1 to hold at its April 29 meeting, with one member pushing for an immediate increase to 4.0%. That lone dissenter is no longer a curiosity — it reflects a committee that is more divided than the headline vote implies. The Bank's Agents' survey puts the median wage settlement for 2026 at 3.5%, above the 3.25% the BoE considers target-consistent. The gap is small but persistent, and in a services-dominated economy it feeds directly into core inflation with a lag that keeps officials cautious well past the point when the initial wage shock has faded.

The Implication: A Repricing Risk

What makes this week notable is the simultaneity. Three institutions operating in distinct economies with distinct inflation dynamics have arrived at the same message: the rate-cutting cycle is not proceeding on the schedule markets assumed.

The BoJ's September meeting is the most precise near-term catalyst to watch. If the board executes the hike Ueda has flagged, the yen carry unwind could accelerate and ripple through global risk assets in ways that echo 2024. For the ECB, the June 11-12 decision is the next inflection point, and the June 2 eurozone flash CPI print will set the tone going in. The BoE follows on June 19, by which point a further round of UK wages data will have landed.

Services inflation is the binding constraint across all three. Until wage growth in the eurozone, Japan, and the United Kingdom convincingly drops to levels consistent with 2% headline targets, none of these banks can ease without risking the credibility they spent three years rebuilding. That is a constraint that does not resolve on a trader's timeline.
