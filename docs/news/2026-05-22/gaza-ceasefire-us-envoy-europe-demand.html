US envoy in Tel Aviv as France, Germany and UK jointly demand a Gaza ceasefire

Gaza City, seen from the east — the territory's ceasefire has been in formal effect since October 2025 but is increasingly fragile — Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 3.0
In one sentence
Witkoff presses Israeli officials in Tel Aviv while Britain, France and Germany jointly demand a ceasefire, but seven months of stalled talks offer little reason for confidence.

The seven-month-old ceasefire that halted the Gaza war is showing signs of fraying again. Steve Witkoff, the Trump administration's special envoy to the Middle East, is in Tel Aviv pressing Israeli officials for movement toward a more durable agreement, while France, Germany and the United Kingdom issued a coordinated joint statement demanding an immediate ceasefire and an expansion of humanitarian aid access into the territory.

The convergence of US and European pressure in a single news cycle is unusual. Washington and the three major European powers have rarely moved in step on Gaza diplomacy — their public positions have typically diverged on questions of Israeli military conduct, aid conditions and what to demand of Hamas. The joint E3 statement, in the name of their foreign ministers, signals a hardening of the collective Western line as the second phase of Trump's twenty-point peace plan remains dead in the water.

A deal that keeps slipping away

The October 2025 ceasefire was sold as a turning point. Israel and Hamas signed the first phase of Trump's peace plan on October 9 in Sharm el-Sheikh; fighting paused the following day. All living hostages held in Gaza were released within weeks, and the remains of 25 others were subsequently returned. In January, Witkoff announced the beginning of phase two — which was supposed to produce Hamas disarmament, a phased Israeli military withdrawal, and the deployment of an international stabilisation force.

None of that has materialised. The Board of Peace, the US-backed oversight body led by high representative Nickolay Mladenov, told the UN Security Council this week that Hamas's "refusal to accept verified decommissioning" remains the principal obstacle. Hamas has called the disarmament demand "unacceptable" and linked any surrender of weapons to guarantees of Palestinian statehood — a threshold Israel has made clear it will not cross.

Meanwhile, the terms already agreed are being violated daily. The ceasefire called for 600 aid trucks to enter Gaza per day; Israel has averaged around 145. Israeli forces have expanded their operational footprint to roughly 59 percent of Gaza's territory, pushing the ceasefire boundary westward. Hamas and other armed factions cite those breaches as justification for refusing further concessions. Mladenov has been blunt about where this leads: "Every act of violence risks unravelling what remains of the agreement."

The European dimension

The E3 statement arrives at a moment when European governments are running short of patience with both sides. Earlier this month, Israeli commandos intercepted a flotilla of more than 50 boats attempting to reach Gaza. European capitals condemned the interception as "unacceptable." On the Nakba anniversary last week, a large demonstration in Brussels called for European sanctions on Israel and suspension of the EU-Israel Association Agreement — pressure that has not yet translated into formal policy but which makes it harder for governments to maintain quiet diplomacy.

The joint statement from the British, French and German foreign ministers calls for an immediate ceasefire and expanded humanitarian access — language that mirrors the October agreement's own provisions, provisions Israel has not honoured in full. The three governments also reiterated support for the Board of Peace framework and the permanent disarmament of Hamas, keeping them technically aligned with the Trump administration's position even while applying pressure on Israel over aid flows.

That balancing act has been the E3's default posture throughout the conflict. The signatories have never imposed material costs — sanctions, arms embargoes, diplomatic downgrade — commensurate with the urgency of their language. Statements demanding humanitarian access while maintaining normal bilateral relations carry limited leverage, a point European critics in Brussels and at the UN have made repeatedly.

Witkoff's presence in Tel Aviv, where he is meeting Prime Minister Netanyahu and senior military officials, suggests Washington is also trying to accelerate the process. US officials say he will press for progress on aid flows and renewed phase-two talks. Whether that pressure extends to conditioning US support — Israel receives the bulk of its missile-defence funding from the Pentagon — remains publicly unspecified. What to watch

The next concrete test comes when Mladenov presents the Board of Peace report to the full UN Security Council. The debate will reveal how far European permanent members — the UK and France — are prepared to push for resolution language that goes beyond reaffirming existing commitments.

On the ground, the more immediate risk is a return to open conflict. Senior Israeli military officials said earlier this month that renewed fighting was "almost inevitable" unless Hamas accepted the disarmament roadmap. The killing of Hamas's Gaza leader, Izz al-Din al-Haddad, in an Israeli strike last week removed one interlocutor from the negotiation track at a sensitive moment. Whether the combined diplomatic push from Washington and the E3 can produce a concrete framework before another outbreak — with oil already trading above $100 on Hormuz war-risk premiums — is the question that will determine how much of this week's coordination was signal and how much was noise.
