China's May PMI edges into contraction as tariff pressures bite export orders

A manufacturing plant in China; the country's factory sector has slipped back into contraction as export and domestic demand pressures converge. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
China's official manufacturing PMI slipped below 50 in May, with tariff pressure on US-facing export orders and weak domestic demand providing no internal offset.

China's official purchasing managers' index for manufacturing edged below the 50-point contraction threshold in May — a shift that underlines how the country's factory sector is caught between two pressures that have, so far, refused to offset each other.

The National Bureau of Statistics index had staged a brief recovery in March and April, reaching 50.4 and 50.3 respectively after slipping into contraction territory in January. May's reversal back below the breakeven mark represents the third month of contraction this year and suggests the spring rebound was shallower than the headline numbers implied.

The arithmetic is not complicated. US tariffs on Chinese goods — running at an effective rate around 47 per cent following a partial truce agreed at the Trump-Xi summit earlier this month — have compressed new export orders, particularly for manufacturers whose supply chains terminate at American consumers. The export-orders sub-index had clawed back to 50.3 in April from 49.1 in March; renewed tariff uncertainty appears to have reversed that recovery. Factory owners do not book production against orders they cannot price.

No internal offset

The typical adjustment mechanism — weak external demand absorbed by a domestic consumption rebound — is not functioning. Consumer spending in China remains subdued, weighed down by a property market slump that has eroded household balance sheets for three years and counting, and by a confidence gap that successive rounds of stimulus have failed to close. The April non-manufacturing PMI of 49.4 pointed the same direction: services and construction, both domestically oriented, were already in contraction before the May manufacturing reading arrived.

This dual-front character is what distinguishes the current episode from earlier tariff disruptions, when Beijing could argue that a resilient services economy was absorbing some of the shock. Factory employment, tracked below the expansion threshold for most of 2026, reflects the hiring caution of manufacturers who see no demand catalyst from either direction. Small and medium enterprises — more reliant on export orders and less able to absorb margin compression than state-backed heavy industry — registered the weakest readings again.

For global commodity markets the implications are direct: steel, copper, and industrial chemicals all price Chinese manufacturing demand as a primary signal. A sustained PMI below 50 — rather than a single-month dip — would weigh on base-metals prices already navigating Middle East supply-risk premiums.

The immediate calendar item for traders is the Caixin manufacturing PMI, due in the first week of June. Compiled from a broader sample with a heavier weighting toward private and export-oriented firms, the Caixin index tends to capture tariff-facing exporters more sensitively than the NBS survey. If it confirms contraction at a sharper reading, the probability of Beijing accelerating its fiscal stimulus timeline rises — and the policy response, not the PMI itself, becomes the market variable to watch. The next NBS release, covering June, lands on June 30.
