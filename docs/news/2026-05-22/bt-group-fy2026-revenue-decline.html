BT Group posts another year of declining revenue as fibre growth trails legacy erosion

BT Centre, the company's former headquarters at 81 Newgate Street in the City of London. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
BT Group's FY2026 adjusted revenue fell 4% to £19.6bn as legacy voice and copper erosion outweighed fibre gains, though flat EBITDA and a £1.5bn FCF floor offered partial reassurance.

Britain's dominant telecoms operator has filed another year of falling revenue, and the numbers illustrate the blunt arithmetic facing every incumbent telco attempting to build its way out of structural decline. BT Group's full-year adjusted revenue came in at £19.6 billion for FY2026 — down 4% year-on-year — even as Openreach laid fibre to record numbers of homes.

The calculus that won't balance

The core problem is one of scale and timing. Openreach passed 4.8 million new premises with full-fibre (FTTP) during the year, a company record, bringing its total footprint to 23 million premises — roughly two-thirds of the UK. Consumer adoption has been creditable: take-up reached 38.3%, with BT's own retail FTTP base growing 31% to 4.5 million customers. Yet adjusted UK service revenue still slid 1% to £15.4 billion, dragged down by falling voice volumes in both the business and consumer divisions, handset trading weakness, and continued attrition in legacy managed contracts.

The maths are unsparing. Legacy copper and voice revenue is eroding in absolute pound terms faster than fibre subscriptions generate new revenue to replace it. International revenues fell by roughly 15% to approximately £2.1 billion as BT continued to divest non-core assets and legacy managed contracts wound down. Total broadband lines at Openreach fell by 825,000 across the year — a function of competitive pressure and migration — with roughly 800,000 further losses pencilled in for FY27.

CEO Allison Kirkby put a measured gloss on proceedings. "FY26 was another year of strong delivery against BT's strategy," she said, citing fibre momentum and the company's cost transformation as evidence that the business is executing in the right direction even if top-line recovery remains elusive.

The one number that carries weight

For investors, the credible counter-argument to the revenue story lives in the free cash flow line. Normalised FCF came in at £1.51 billion — down modestly year-on-year due to higher capital expenditure of £5.1 billion and increased interest costs — but management reiterated a target of approximately £2.0 billion for FY27 and roughly £3.0 billion by the end of the decade. Adjusted EBITDA held flat at £8.23 billion, a signal that cost discipline — annualised savings of £580 million, achieved through a 7% workforce reduction — is at least preventing margin erosion from compounding the revenue headwinds.

The full-year dividend was raised 2% to 8.32 pence per share, a statement of intent toward income investors even as the balance sheet carries £20 billion of net debt.

What comes next

BT's own FY27 guidance makes clear that top-line pressure is not easing. Management projects adjusted revenue of £19.0–19.5 billion for the year ahead — down from £19.65 billion — with an estimated £150–200 million additional drag from PSTN closure as copper voice lines migrate. Openreach's 25-million premises target is due for completion by December 2026; whether the eventual fibre penetration rate can generate enough incremental revenue to turn the tide remains the central open question for the stock.

Morgan Stanley called the underlying KPIs "solid" and reiterated a modest outperform view, pointing to the reiterated long-term FCF targets as the load-bearing pillar. Hargreaves Lansdown analysts flagged the balance sheet as "stretched" and near-term earnings growth as limited — a lukewarm endorsement that accurately captures the tension at the heart of BT's investment case.

The next significant test will come when BT reports its first-quarter FY27 numbers, due around August. Investors will watch whether the FCF trajectory remains intact as peak fibre capex begins to ease.
