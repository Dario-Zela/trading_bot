Nestlé disputes cereulide contamination claims as its defensive valuation premium frays

Nestlé's headquarters in Vevey, Switzerland — the company faces fresh cereulide allegations while its defensive stock premium shows signs of strain — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nestlé denies fresh cereulide contamination claims for cereal products, but offers no specifics, as a year of food-safety crises erodes the trust premium that justifies its stock's defensive valuation.

The Swiss food giant is fighting a rearguard action on two fronts simultaneously. Fresh allegations — whose precise origin and evidentiary basis have not been disclosed — claim that some Nestlé cereal product lines carry unsafe levels of cereulide, the heat-stable emetic toxin produced by certain strains of Bacillus cereus. Nestlé has pushed back, asserting that any affected products meet current regulatory safety thresholds, but the company has not detailed which specific lines are under scrutiny, who levelled the charges, or what testing methodology underpins them.

The absence of particulars cuts both ways. Without named accusers or documented test results, the allegations are difficult to assess on their merits. But Nestlé's rebuttal — anchored to regulatory compliance rather than independent verification — does little to close the credibility gap. The company has learned this year, at considerable cost, that "meets current standards" offers limited reassurance to a public already primed for scepticism.

A year of accumulating scrutiny

Nestlé's cereulide troubles did not begin in May. Since late 2025 the company has been managing the fallout from a recall of infant formula products — NAN Stage 1 among them — spanning more than 60 countries after cereulide was detected at its Dutch manufacturing facility. Austria's health ministry called it the largest recall in the company's history; Nestlé took a $210m financial hit and warned of further drag on 2026 results. The contamination was traced to an arachidonic acid oil supplied by a third party based in Wuhan, which Nestlé initially declined to name publicly.

The European Food Safety Authority, filling what critics had called a glaring regulatory gap, set an action limit for cereulide in infant formula at 0.43 ng/g in February. France moved independently to set a lower threshold. This week, three European public broadcasters — Radio France, RTBF, and RTS — published a joint investigation alleging that Nestlé had detected the toxin at the Dutch factory ten days before notifying Dutch authorities, and that quiet recalls had begun in Austria and Germany on December 24 without public disclosure. Nestlé called these characterisations "inaccurate and misleading" and maintained it had "acted with full transparency." A criminal probe into the reporting delay is understood to be ongoing in at least one European jurisdiction.

What the new cereal-specific allegations add to this established history is a widening of scope rather than a deepening of evidence. Repeated accusations — whether fully substantiated or not — signal that regulators, journalists, and third-party testing labs are unlikely to accept Nestlé's food safety assurances at face value for some time.

A premium that no longer pays for itself

Nestlé was trading at around CHF 78 on the SIX exchange in mid-May — down roughly 9.5% over the preceding year and nearly 27% over three years. A P/E multiple of around 21x sits above the broad food-industry average of 15.9x but below the immediate peer-group average of 22.8x: a valuation that once commanded a straightforward defensive premium has become a contested proposition.

Analysis by Simply Wall St has questioned whether that premium multiple can hold given the structural headwinds the company faces. Consumers across Germany, the UK, and North America have been trading down to private-label alternatives, eroding volume in categories where Nestlé's pricing power was assumed to be durable. Q1 2026 organic growth came in at 3.5%, decomposing into 2.3% pricing and 1.2% volume — a ratio suggesting Nestlé continues to lean on price to support the top line even as basket sizes shrink. Competition from private-label has been particularly acute in discount-heavy German retail and in the petcare segment, where budget-conscious owners are switching away from Purina.

Food safety controversies have historically weighed on branded-food valuations not only through direct recall costs but through the subtler erosion of the trust premium that justifies charging more than a store-brand alternative. If consumers already inclined to trade down are handed a fresh reason to question the Nestlé name on a box, arresting the volume decline becomes materially harder. The immediate question is whether the new cereulide allegations attract substantive regulatory follow-up — or dissolve, as vague allegations sometimes do, into ambient noise. EFSA's new testing framework means regulators now have cleaner tools to answer that question.
