Pentagon and White House clash over $80m rare-earths loan to ReElement Technologies

Refined rare-earth oxides — the processed powders at the centre of a US-China supply-chain rivalry, and the output ReElement's Indiana facility is designed to produce — Peggy Greb / USDA / Wikimedia Commons (public domain)
In one sentence
Pentagon due-diligence doubts may kill an $80m conditional loan to ReElement Technologies, exposing inter-agency friction over US strategy to break China's grip on rare-earth refining.

The Pentagon is weighing whether to cancel an $80 million conditional loan to ReElement Technologies Corp., a rare-earths refiner based in Marion, Indiana — six months after the deal was announced as a cornerstone of the US drive to reduce dependence on Chinese critical minerals processing. The money has not moved. The delay has produced a visible inter-agency row.

The dispute pits the Pentagon's Office of Strategic Capital, which has been conducting ongoing due diligence on ReElement, against White House senior counsellor for trade and manufacturing Peter Navarro, who has publicly accused the Defence Department of "over-burdensome due diligence" that ends up penalising small, innovative companies. Pentagon spokesman Sean Parnell countered that the office "expertly balances lightning speed with rigorous diligence." That formulation does not resolve the immediate question of whether the loan gets written.

The Scaling Problem

At the centre of the Pentagon's hesitation is ReElement's proprietary chromatographic separation technology. Rather than conventional solvent extraction — the industrial method that China spent decades refining and that now underpins roughly 90% of global rare-earth refining capacity — ReElement processes electronic waste and end-of-life magnets to recover high-purity rare earth oxides exceeding 99.5% purity for neodymium, dysprosium, and terbium. The latter two are heavy rare earths for which China controls upwards of 98% of global processing. ReElement's Marion facility, once operational, would be among the largest rare-earth oxide producers in the country, with a stated production target above 9,000 metric tons annually.

The problem is that as of October 2025, ReElement remained a pre-revenue company. Pentagon officials have raised pointed questions about whether its chromatography approach can scale to meet defence-grade demand, and whether the long-range revenue projections in the loan application reflect a realistic path to commercial viability. Those questions have not been answered to the vetting team's satisfaction.

The $80 million to ReElement is part of a broader package: a further $620 million goes to Vulcan Elements for magnet manufacturing, with the full initiative — spanning Defence and Commerce Department contributions, including incentives under the CHIPS and Science Act — totalling some $1.4 billion. State Department officials praised the deal at a critical minerals summit in February. ReElement has separately secured a $200 million equity facility from Transition Equity Partners, providing some runway on private financing. But private capital cannot substitute for the federal government's intent signal if the conditional loan is formally withdrawn.

The strategic rationale for the original deal is not in dispute on either side. The US can mine rare-earth ore domestically — Mountain Pass in California being the principal example — but refining the resulting concentrates requires either shipping them to China or building processing infrastructure that does not yet exist at meaningful scale. Every tonne of domestically mined ore that travels to a Chinese refinery for separation undercuts the supply-chain independence the Pentagon is nominally trying to establish. ReElement's Indiana plant was supposed to close that gap at the one step where Chinese dominance is most concentrated.

Deputy Defence Secretary Stephen Feinberg, who oversees the critical minerals programme, will be the decision point. Whether he sides with the vetting team's caution or the White House's impatience will determine whether the conditional loan survives. ReElement is pressing ahead with the Indiana facility on private financing for now; how far that can take the project before Washington reaches a verdict will be the next concrete indicator of which side of this argument the administration ultimately lands on.
