US 30-year yield hits highest since 2007 as bond selloff deepens

In one sentence
The 30-year Treasury yield hit 5.20%, its highest since 2007, as CPI at 3.8% and PPI at 6% force markets to reprice Fed cuts as hikes.

The US 30-year Treasury yield climbed to 5.20% on Tuesday — its highest level since July 2007, the summer before the financial crisis reshaped global finance — as investors continued dumping long-dated government debt on fears that a second wave of inflation is now entrenched enough to force the Federal Reserve into rate hikes rather than the cuts markets had been counting on just three months ago.

The move of seven basis points on the day was notable less for its size than for what it confirms: the long bull market in bonds that began in the early 1980s is functionally over, and a new, more painful regime for duration holders is taking shape in real time.

The break from a two-decade narrative

For most of the post-2008 era, long Treasuries served a specific portfolio function: they were the ballast that rose when equities fell, the asset you ran to in a crisis. That relationship — negative equity-bond correlation — was the backbone of the 60/40 portfolio, of pension fund liability matching, of trillions of dollars in carry trades that paired cheap leverage with duration premium.

Tuesday's move signals the sustained unwinding of that logic. When bonds sell off in tandem with equities — the Russell 2000 has shed roughly 4% over the past three sessions — the hedging calculus breaks down. Duration is no longer a safe haven. It is a source of risk.

For pension funds, the repricing is double-edged: higher discount rates reduce the present value of liabilities, which improves funding ratios on paper, but the price losses on long-bond portfolios used to hedge those same liabilities erode asset values. The net effect depends on the hedging ratio. Funds that left duration gaps are now underwater.

What is driving the selloff

Three forces are feeding into each other.

First, the inflation data is unambiguous. April's Consumer Price Index rose 3.8% year-over-year, its fastest pace since 2023, while the Producer Price Index surged 6.0% annually — more than double Wall Street's forecast and the steepest reading since 2022. Oil above $100 per barrel, a direct consequence of the escalating US-Iran conflict, is the main accelerant. Shelter inflation, which doubled in April, is adding a second layer of stickiness that energy price retreats alone cannot fix.

Second, fiscal supply is relentless. Primary dealers are projecting a $1.95 trillion federal budget deficit for the fiscal year ending in September — and the Treasury is absorbing that deficit at scale. The government sold $691 billion in securities in a single week in mid-May, including $31 billion of 30-year bonds that cleared at 5.046%, the first 30-year auction to settle above 5% since 2007. More supply is coming. With no credible path to deficit reduction, the market is demanding compensation to absorb it.

Third, the Fed's policy trajectory has inverted. At the start of the Iran conflict in late February, futures markets priced three rate cuts by year-end. As of Tuesday, that number has collapsed. Futures now imply a 42% probability of a rate hike before December, according to CME FedWatch data — a swing of historic proportions in less than three months.

New chair, no easy options

Kevin Warsh, confirmed by the Senate this week as Federal Reserve chair, arrived at the Marriner Eccles Building promising a "regime change" at the central bank. He may get more than he bargained for.

Ed Yardeni, the veteran analyst who coined the phrase "bond vigilantes" in the 1980s, wrote this week that the Fed has effectively ceded control of the long end of the curve. "The Fed must catch up to the bond market to avoid losing control of borrowing costs and to appease the Bond Vigilantes," Yardeni wrote, arguing that a 25-basis-point hike at the July meeting is "likely." At present, futures assign only a 4.2% probability to that outcome — suggesting the market is not yet there, but the gap is narrowing.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has said the administration remains committed to reducing borrowing costs, putting the administration broadly at odds with where the bond market is pricing reality.

What the strategists say

Analysts are not offering much comfort to those sitting long duration.

Ajay Rajadhyaksha, chairman of global research at Barclays, framed the structural case plainly: with "debt growing faster than growth, inflation expectations deteriorating, and no political will for fiscal reform, there is little reason to reach for long-dated bonds." Barclays and Citigroup strategists have warned clients that yields could breach 5.5% — a level last seen in 2004.

Benjamin Schroeder at ING described the market as having shifted to a "clear hiking bias," driven by energy-price pressures that are "morphing into something more sustained" rather than the transitory spike of previous oil shocks.

At Morningstar, Liz Templeton attributed the move to "ongoing uncertainty around Fed policy, energy-driven cost pressures, and heavier Treasury issuance" combining to push the market toward a higher-for-longer rate assumption that has no obvious near-term catalyst for reversal.

The global spread

The selloff is not contained to the US. The UK 30-year gilt hit its highest yield since 1998 on Tuesday. Japan's 30-year bond rose to a record. The common thread is inflation that central banks in each jurisdiction are being forced to take more seriously than their forward guidance suggested only months ago. Bond markets globally are repricing the cost of delay.

What to watch

A Bank of America survey published Tuesday — covering 200 fund managers overseeing $517 billion — found that 62% of respondents now expect the 30-year Treasury yield to reach 6%, a level not seen since late 1999. Forty percent cited a "second wave" of inflation as their biggest tail risk, ranking it ahead of any single geopolitical scenario.

The next concrete test comes in June, when the Warsh-led Fed holds its first policy meeting under the new chair. Whether the statement drops forward guidance language favoring easing — which Yardeni identified as the first signal of a hawkish tilt — will tell markets whether the Fed is moving toward the bond market or waiting for it to blink first. In the meantime, Thursday brings another reading on jobless claims, and the following week delivers PCE inflation data, the Fed's preferred gauge. Neither is expected to be reassuring.
