US seizes Iran-linked oil tanker in the Indian Ocean

A Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) supertanker in open water — vessels of this class form the backbone of Iran's shadow fleet. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
The U.S. seized the Skywave, a 302,000-ton Iran-linked supertanker carrying over a million barrels of crude, in the Indian Ocean — the latest in a series of interdictions under Operation Economic Fury.

The U.S. Navy has seized the Skywave, an Iran-linked supertanker, in the Indian Ocean, the Wall Street Journal reported on Tuesday, citing three U.S. officials. The vessel — a very large crude carrier of 302,481 deadweight tons, formerly named the Blue Gulf — had loaded more than one million barrels of Iranian oil at Kharg Island in February and was transiting between Malaysia and Sri Lanka when American forces took custody of it.

The seizure is the latest in a series of interdictions that began in April, when U.S. forces boarded the Touska, the Tifani, and the Majestic X in the Gulf of Oman and the Indian Ocean. The Tifani and Majestic X alone carried roughly 3.8 million barrels of Iranian crude destined for Chinese refiners — the primary market for discounted Iranian oil since sanctions tightened after Washington withdrew from the 2015 nuclear agreement.

A legal architecture built in stages

The Skywave did not become a target overnight. The Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control sanctioned the vessel — then operating as the Blue Gulf — on March 31, 2025, as part of a broader action targeting United Tankers, a Marshall Islands-registered company, and Lake View Ship Management Private Limited, an Indian firm, for their roles in Iranian oil transportation. Once sanctioned, the vessel continued to sea under successive flag registrations in Palau, Comoros, and Botswana — a practice known as flag-hopping, designed to preserve a veneer of legality.

The Department of Justice has filed forfeiture documents for the seized tankers, treating the oil cargo as proceeds of sanctions violations. That framework — OFAC sanctions providing the predicate, DOJ forfeiture providing the enforcement mechanism — is now the standard template for what U.S. officials have termed Operation Economic Fury.

How the shadow fleet works

The Iranian shadow fleet is not a formal institution. It is a loose network of vessels, often aging, operating under false identities with falsified documentation and disabled or spoofed Automatic Identification System transponders — the systems that allow port authorities and coast guards to track ships in real time. Vessels routinely change names and flags, and oil cargoes are blended with legitimately sourced crude at ship-to-ship transfer points in international waters to obscure their origin before delivery.

According to energy analytics firm Vortexa, most of the fleet consists of VLCCs capable of carrying up to 2 million barrels each. The network has generated billions in revenue for Tehran, with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps estimated to control roughly half of Iran's oil exports through it — funds that underwrite Iran's military budget and its network of regional proxies.

China has been the fleet's central customer, drawn by heavily discounted crude. When the U.S. declared a naval blockade of Iranian oil exports on April 13, Beijing condemned the move as "irresponsible and dangerous" and contrary to the international community's interests, but has stopped short of direct countermeasures.

The blockade and what it costs Iran

The April 13 blockade declaration marked a significant escalation — from targeted vessel seizures to a formal interdiction posture covering Iranian oil exports broadly. By May 1, the Pentagon estimated 31 tankers carrying 53 million barrels of Iranian crude remained bottled up in the Gulf, unable to proceed. The Defense Department put Iran's revenue losses at $4.8 billion between April 13 and May 1 alone, roughly $500 million per day by the White House's own calculation.

But interdiction is an imperfect instrument. Lloyd's List documented at least 26 Iranian shadow fleet vessels that managed to bypass the blockade. At its peak, analysts at Kpler estimated Iranian exports — including refined products and feedstocks alongside crude — reached 2.8 million barrels per day, a figure that reflects how structurally embedded discounted Iranian crude had become in Chinese industrial supply chains before the blockade took effect.

Tehran's response

Iran has not absorbed the seizures quietly. Foreign Ministry Spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei described them as "the outright legalisation of piracy and armed robbery on the high seas." Iran retaliated by seizing the Liberia-flagged MSC-Francesca and the Panama-flagged Epaminodes, characterizing those actions as proportionate legal enforcement. On April 18, Tehran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed — a move that pushed oil prices sharply higher before partial transit resumed under pressure from China and regional shipping operators.

What it means for the oil market

Each tanker seizure sends a signal that extends well beyond the vessel itself. The question for oil traders is not just whether spot prices will move — U.S. crude stocks are already drawing at an accelerated pace, with API data showing a 9.1 million barrel decline in the most recent week against a 3.4 million barrel estimate — but whether secondary sanctions enforcement will become consistent enough to alter the behavior of Chinese refiners and the shipping intermediaries that serve them.

Previous rounds of U.S. secondary sanctions on Iranian oil, applied after the 2018 JCPOA withdrawal, initially curtailed purchases before China's appetite for discounted crude created structural workarounds. The core question now is whether physical interdiction, rather than financial penalties alone, proves more durable as a coercive tool.

What to watch

The Islamabad Talks between U.S. and Iranian negotiators collapsed in April, reportedly over Iran's refusal to make binding commitments on its nuclear program. Follow-on talks have shown little progress. The Trump administration has kept open the threat of resumed military strikes, and the rate of vessel seizures and sanctions designations — 19 additional tankers were targeted alongside the Skywave operation — suggests Washington intends to tighten the net rather than ease it.

For the oil market, the critical questions are whether DOJ forfeiture actions hold up to legal challenge, how Chinese refiners respond to increasingly credible enforcement risk, and whether Iran identifies new routing options outside the Indian Ocean corridor. The Strait of Malacca — where tracking data placed the Skywave just before its seizure — has become the central chokepoint in that contest.
