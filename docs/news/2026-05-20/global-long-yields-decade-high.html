Global long-bond yields climb to highest in almost two decades

The US Treasury Building in Washington, DC — the department whose bond issuance sits at the center of the global long-yield selloff. — Rchuon24 / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Long-dated sovereign bond yields hit 20-year highs globally, with the US 30-year at 5.20%, as war-driven inflation and fiscal expansion force a durable repricing of duration.

Government bonds across the developed world are selling off in ways not seen since the era of the global financial crisis, pushing long-dated yields to multi-decade highs and forcing a reckoning in markets that have long treated sovereign debt as the ballast of any portfolio.

The US 30-year Treasury yield touched 5.20% on Tuesday — its highest since July 2007 — up roughly 60 basis points since the start of the Iran conflict alone. The UK's long gilt yields haven't been here since 1998. Japan's 30-year government bond yield hit a record, the highest in data going back to 1999. Bloomberg's global gauge of sovereign debt yields for maturities of 10 years and beyond has now climbed to its highest since July 2008, at the peak of the credit crisis. Global bond returns for longer maturities are down 4.6% in 2026, reversing a 3% gain through late February.

What's pushing yields higher

The proximate cause is the Iran war and what it has done to oil prices. The disruption to shipping through the Strait of Hormuz has choked supply at a moment when demand remained firm, sending crude sharply higher and reigniting inflation expectations that many investors had quietly hoped were settled.

But energy costs are only part of the story. The bond selloff has been building on a foundation of fiscal deterioration that long predates the current conflict. In the US, the Congressional Budget Office estimates the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" — extending existing tax cuts and layering on new ones — could add $3.4 trillion to the federal debt by 2034, with only modest spending offsets. Japan has shifted toward a more expansionary fiscal stance. European governments are lifting defense budgets. The supply of government bonds is growing globally even as some of the most reliable buyers — central banks and sovereign wealth funds — have become less predictable in their appetite.

Patrick Coffey of Barclays put it plainly: "We're seeing a broader repricing of duration driven by fiscal realities, persistent inflation risks and some political uncertainty."

How far does this go

Strategists are not in agreement on where yields top out, but most are leaning higher rather than lower. Barclays and Citigroup have told clients to prepare for 30-year Treasury yields to breach 5.5%, a level last seen in 2004. Bloomberg's Alyce Andres identifies 5.25% as the next near-term target. Monica Hsiao of Triada Capital, who focuses on where yields stabilize, is watching oil: "The main issue is longer-term oil prices and the war."

A Bank of America fund manager survey published this week found that 62% of respondents expect 30-year government borrowing costs to exceed 6% within a year — a striking consensus that would have seemed implausible eighteen months ago.

Not everyone is calling the same play. Goldman Sachs sees emerging measures of value at current levels but urges clients not to rush back in. BlackRock's head of research is recommending reducing exposure to developed-market government bonds in favor of equities — a sharp reversal of the traditional safe-haven logic that has anchored asset allocation for a generation.

The regime question

The debate underneath the yield moves is whether markets are repricing a cyclical spike or something more durable. Long-dated yields are particularly sensitive to long-run growth and inflation expectations, and a sustained break above 5% on 30-year paper implies that investors are demanding more compensation not just for today's energy shock but for a future in which governments borrow heavily, central banks move cautiously, and inflation settles above the 2% targets that defined the post-crisis era.

That interpretation has direct consequences for asset prices across every class. Higher risk-free rates raise the discount rate applied to future corporate earnings, compressing valuations — particularly in long-duration growth sectors. Equity markets have already noticed: the Nasdaq 100 fell 1% as 30-year yields cleared 19-year highs. If yields push toward 6%, the pressure on portfolios built around a low-rate assumption becomes substantially harder to absorb.

What to watch

The Federal Reserve remains on the sidelines. Treasury Secretary Bessent this week said the US should wait and see before moving on rates — a posture that provides no near-term relief to bond investors. The next read on US inflation arrives in two weeks. Oil prices and developments in the Iran talks are the swing variable with the shortest fuse. If either breaks favorably, the selloff could stall. If neither does, 5.5% on the long end becomes a live target rather than a tail scenario.
