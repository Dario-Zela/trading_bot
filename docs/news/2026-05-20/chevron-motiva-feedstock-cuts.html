Chevron and Motiva cut motor oil feedstock deliveries as Iran war rattles supply chains

Chevron's Richmond, California refinery, one of five wholly owned U.S. refineries that produce base oils and fuels. — Bastique / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
Chevron and Motiva have cut Group II/III base oil deliveries to contract minimums, as Iran War supply disruptions ripple into motor oil feedstock chains.

Two of North America's largest base oil producers have quietly tightened the spigot. Chevron (CVX) and Motiva Enterprises — the Saudi Aramco-owned refiner that operates the largest base oil plant in the Western Hemisphere — have both reduced deliveries of Group II and Group III base oils to minimum contractually obligated levels, according to Bloomberg. Group II and Group III are the primary feedstocks for modern engine lubricants: the processed, hydrocracked inputs that make synthetic and semi-synthetic motor oils possible.

No volume figures or customer-specific details have been disclosed publicly. The cuts appear to be the first visible sign that the Iran War's disruption to Persian Gulf energy flows is reaching into refined-product specialty chains beyond crude and diesel.

Why base oil is exposed

The U.S. draws roughly 44% of its Group III base oil from Persian Gulf producers — a dependency that the Strait of Hormuz disruption has made structural, not incidental. Shell's Qatar facility, which produced approximately 30,000 barrels per day of Group III before the conflict, was disabled by Iranian strikes. South Korea, which accounts for around 30% of U.S. Group III imports, relies heavily on Persian Gulf crude as its feedstock input; its refineries may be physically intact, but their input supply is under pressure.

Motiva's Port Arthur, Texas refinery is the centerpiece of this story. It is the second-largest base oil producer in the world by output, supplying the aramcoPRIMA (Group II) and aramcoULTRA (Group III) product lines to domestic customers and more than 15 countries. Its feedstock runs through the same chokepoint that has frozen much of Gulf energy trade since the conflict began.

The force majeure context

Several base oil and additive suppliers have already declared force majeure — the contractual escape clause for disruptions outside a company's control. Chevron and Motiva have so far taken the narrower step of cutting to minimums rather than declaring force majeure outright, but the direction is the same. When producers who are contractually obligated to deliver start delivering as little as possible, customers begin rationing.

Industry estimates put base oil inventory at blending facilities at roughly one month. That's the lag before a sustained supply reduction becomes a visible downstream problem — higher prices for finished motor oil, potential specification shortfalls, and pressure on blenders who must meet API licensing requirements to sell under branded names.

New domestic Group III capacity being developed by Chevron and ExxonMobil is not scheduled to come online until 2027. There is no near-term domestic offset.

What it means for traders

The motor oil feedstock market is niche — it doesn't move crude benchmarks, and the immediate financial impact on Chevron or Motiva's earnings is unlikely to be material. But the pattern it represents is not niche. If a specialty refined product with 44% Gulf import exposure is already at minimum delivery levels after roughly two months of conflict, the question for energy traders is which other refined-product chains — lubricants, additives, petrochemical feedstocks — are on a similar exposure profile and haven't yet surfaced publicly.

Whether this is a one-off routing adjustment or a sign of deeper feedstock tightness should become clearer as blenders report on inventory levels in coming weeks. Watch for any force majeure declarations from Chevron or Motiva specifically, and for any signal from API licensing bodies about specification waivers — that would indicate the shortage has moved from inconvenient to structural.
