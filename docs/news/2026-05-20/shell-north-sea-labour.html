Shell urges UK Labour to reopen North Sea drilling to boost the economy

The Deepsea Delta drilling rig in the North Sea — Shell wants Labour to approve two major fields requiring fresh production sign-off after court rulings in 2025. — Erik Christensen / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Shell CEO Wael Sawan is pressing Labour to approve Jackdaw and Rosebank North Sea fields, pitting Treasury growth instincts against Ed Miliband's climate commitments.

Shell's chief executive Wael Sawan has publicly pressed the UK's Labour government to grant production approvals for two major North Sea fields — Jackdaw and Rosebank — arguing that domestic output is essential to Britain's energy security and, by extension, its economic stability.

"National security sits on the back of energy security, and energy security you can get through imports, but ideally you want to get it through homegrown resources," Sawan said, pitching Shell's position as a matter of strategic necessity rather than corporate opportunism. The timing is not accidental. With the Iran conflict throttling supply through the Strait of Hormuz and driving energy costs higher across Europe, the economic case for domestic production has sharpened considerably.

The two fields at the centre of the standoff

Jackdaw is a gas field 150 miles east of Aberdeen, wholly owned by Shell through its BG International subsidiary. Originally approved in 2022, with production targeted for 2026, it was among the projects whose approvals were ruled unlawful in early 2025. The Supreme Court had determined that Environmental Impact Assessments must account for scope 3 emissions — the carbon released when extracted fuel is ultimately burned. At peak output, Jackdaw would represent 6.5% of the UK's projected gas production and could supply roughly 1.4 million homes.

Rosebank, northwest of Shetland, is the UK's largest undeveloped oil field, with an estimated 300–500 million barrels of oil equivalent. It is held through Adura, a joint venture between Shell and Equinor. Like Jackdaw, it lost its original approval and awaits fresh sign-off. Both projects require new decisions from Energy Secretary Ed Miliband's department.

The political fault line inside Labour

Miliband, who once described additional North Sea development as "climate vandalism," has shown little appetite to approve either project. He is reported to be willing to greenlight Jackdaw — a gas field whose output could reduce import dependence — while resisting Rosebank, which is predominantly an oil play.

Chancellor Rachel Reeves has taken a markedly different tone. In April she said she would be "very happy" to back exploration, citing jobs and tax revenues — a signal that the Treasury sees the economic argument as live, even as the energy department digs in. Prime Minister Keir Starmer has acknowledged the decision legally rests with Miliband, which neatly sidesteps without resolving the tension.

Labour backbenchers are split along predictable lines. Pro-growth MPs argue that drilling is compatible with decarbonisation goals. Critics point to a figure from campaign group Uplift: Jackdaw would supply just 2% of UK demand across its nine-to-twelve-year lifetime, which they say makes the energy-security argument thin.

Shell's leverage, and its limits

Shell's public push carries an implicit comparison. The company paid £122 million in UK taxes and fees in 2025 — against £3.8 billion to the Norwegian government for North Sea operations there. Norway's regulatory framework, Sawan's framing implies, offers a more hospitable environment for the same barrels. That number is not a threat, exactly, but it is a data point that lands in any conversation about where capital flows next.

Shell has also lost roughly 300,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day in production exposure from Qatar as the Iran conflict disrupts operations — around 10% of the company's total output. The commercial logic for maximising stable, domestically approved production has rarely been clearer from the company's perspective.

What to watch

Miliband's decision on Jackdaw is expected to come first, given his reported openness to the gas case. Rosebank is the harder political call: its oil content makes it a more direct test of the government's net-zero credibility. Any Labour move to approve both fields would be read by energy markets — and ESG-oriented investors — as a meaningful dilution of the government's climate posture. A refusal, against a backdrop of high energy prices and a restless backbench, would invite its own political cost.

Watch UK-listed energy stocks on any formal announcement, and monitor the Reeves-Miliband dynamic: it is one of the cleaner proxies for whether the Treasury or the energy department is setting the pace on growth.
