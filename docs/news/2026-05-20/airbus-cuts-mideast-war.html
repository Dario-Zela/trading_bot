Airbus orders 10% cuts to non-essential spending amid Middle East war

Airbus A380 assembly line at the company's manufacturing facility in Toulouse, France — Nicolas Halftermeyer / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
Airbus ordered a 10% cut to non-essential spending across commercial and HQ operations, citing Middle East war disruption — while shielding its record 870-plane delivery target.

Airbus has told employees across its commercial aircraft division and corporate headquarters to cut non-essential spending by 10 percent compared to last year's baseline, according to an internal document confirmed by AFP. The directive, framed as a response to the economic disruption caused by the Middle East war, instructs staff to "significantly pause all activities and costs that are not strictly essential to our industrial activities and operations."

The memo names the obvious targets — team events, corporate gatherings, conference attendance, and travel — but also extends to new subcontracting agreements not tied to core operations. "We are asking for your support in adopting several cost containment measures to help our company navigate this unstable situation," the document reads. Airbus declined to comment when contacted by AFP.

What's being protected

Critically, the cuts are confined to overhead and non-industrial spending. Factory-floor production costs are explicitly excluded. That distinction matters: Airbus is still targeting a record 870 commercial aircraft deliveries in 2026, up from 793 last year, and has no apparent intention of scaling back that goal. The cost-containment drive runs in parallel to the existing LEAD performance improvement program rather than replacing it — an additional layer of financial discipline laid over an ongoing efficiency effort.

The ring-fencing of production budgets signals that management is absorbing the uncertainty in discretionary spend rather than admitting any operational slowdown. It is a textbook pre-emptive move: protect revenue-generating capacity, squeeze everything else.

The exposure problem

Airbus's Middle East backlog exposure is real but not dominant. The region accounts for roughly 9 percent of the company's order book — lower than Boeing's 14 percent — with Airbus holding a 43 percent share of total Mideast commercial backlog versus Boeing's 57. Even at that level, a sustained conflict is enough to make customers hesitate. Middle Eastern carriers have already put discussions on future deals and leasing contracts on temporary hold, and some Asian airlines — including carriers in Indonesia — are reviewing timelines for large-jet purchases. A handful are reportedly considering invoking force majeure clauses to defer scheduled deliveries without penalty.

Airbus has also been contending with a separate headwind: Pratt & Whitney GTF engine shortages and A320neo panel quality issues have slowed deliveries and eaten into adjusted earnings. Through April, the company had delivered 67 aircraft that month — its strongest monthly result of the year, but still behind the pace needed to hit 870 by December.

What to watch

The directive is a signal, not yet a number. Airbus has not revised guidance, disclosed a specific revenue impact, or quantified how much discretionary spend a 10 percent cut actually represents in euros. That ambiguity is the point: management is tightening before it has to say anything publicly.

For investors holding aerospace and defense exposure, the peer comparison is the next logical step. Boeing's earnings commentary on Middle East demand, along with updates from European defense and avionics suppliers such as Thales and Safran, will indicate whether this is Airbus-specific caution or the start of a sector-wide margin squeeze. Airbus's next scheduled financial update is its mid-year results, due in late July.
