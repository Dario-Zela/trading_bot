Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk accelerate global rollout of obesity pills

In one sentence
Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly are pressing GLP-1 obesity pills into 40+ markets in 2026, betting international demand can close the gap on U.S.-dominated revenues — if payers and patents cooperate.

The race to put GLP-1 weight-loss drugs into pill form has produced a clear early leader — for now. Novo Nordisk launched its oral Wegovy (semaglutide 25 mg, once daily) in the United States on January 5, 2026, at retail prices of $149 to $299 a month depending on dose. The drug generated $355 million in its debut quarter. Eli Lilly followed on April 1, when the FDA approved Foundayo (orforglipron), a structurally distinct small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist with one notable practical advantage: it can be taken without food or water restrictions, at any time of day. Now both companies are moving to take the fight abroad — on timelines that reveal how much confidence, or at least declared confidence, each is bringing to international markets.

The expansion push

Novo Nordisk has submitted its Wegovy pill to the European Medicines Agency and other regulatory bodies, with the company projecting its first decisions outside the U.S. during the second half of 2026. Analysts point to the UK, Germany, and Denmark as likely initial European launch markets. Emil Kongshøj Larsen, Novo's Executive Vice President for International Operations, has said the company will "go all in" when regulatory approvals arrive — calibrating launches to factors like physician training density and the availability of telehealth partners. On April 29, Novo moved into Southeast Asia with a Wegovy injectable launch in Thailand, its first foothold in the region.

Eli Lilly is running a broader geographic bet. CEO David Ricks disclosed in April that the company had submitted orforglipron for regulatory review in more than 40 countries. Corporate guidance places broad international access at roughly 2027, with country-by-country approvals expected to arrive on a rolling basis. The practical consequence: Lilly is unlikely to be a meaningful international competitor in oral obesity pills before Novo has had 12 to 18 months of runway in European markets.

What the numbers actually show

Novo Nordisk's Q1 2026 results provided a partial preview of what international obesity revenues can look like before the oral pill enters those markets. International operations obesity care sales grew 44% at constant exchange rates — Europe and Canada up 63%, Asia-Pacific up 87%, Emerging Markets up 18%. China fell 37%, a reminder that "international" is not monolithic. Markets with early biosimilar penetration or government price pressure behave differently.

The U.S. still accounts for more than 75% of global GLP-1 weight-loss revenues. The oral pill format changes one structural constraint: tablets don't require the cold-chain logistics that slowed injectable rollouts, which could meaningfully accelerate launches in markets where storage infrastructure was a bottleneck.

Pricing and access — the missing data

Novo Nordisk's U.S. launch price of $149–$299 a month was described by its CEO as a "sweet spot." International pricing has not been disclosed in detail. Late in 2025, both companies struck agreements with the Trump administration to align direct-to-consumer prices closer to European market levels — suggesting international pill prices could come in well below U.S. list prices. But without confirmed market-by-market pricing, this remains a signal, not a fact.

The ETF angle — narrowly

GLP-1 enthusiasm has generated dedicated investment vehicles. The Roundhill GLP-1 & Weight Loss ETF (OZEM) has returned 23.1% over the past year; the Tema Heart & Health ETF (HRTS) is up 18.7%; the Amplify Weight Loss Drug & Treatment ETF (THNR) has gained 9.3% over the same period. These figures reflect the sector's broad trajectory, not specifically this week's expansion announcements. Absent data on inflows or outflows tied to the international rollout news, attributing capital movement to these specific disclosures would be speculative.

Risks that warrant attention

Three structural pressures complicate the expansion story.

First, patent exposure. Semaglutide's patent protection is already lapsing in key markets including India and China — exactly the large markets where volume would be most significant. Biosimilar manufacturers are entering, and the countries Novo needs most for scale are the ones facing the sharpest generic competition earliest.

Second, payer durability. About 55% of U.S. commercial employers cover GLP-1s for obesity, but 15% of those have already dropped coverage citing unsustainable costs. International markets with public health systems face similar budget pressures, and reimbursement decisions — not approvals — will ultimately determine whether these launches translate into revenue.

Third, concentration risk. GLP-1 and obesity drugs now account for an estimated 38% of projected commercial inflows from the 2025 late-stage pharmaceutical pipeline. That concentration amplifies the downside from any sector-specific shock: a safety signal, a payer pullback, or a late-stage clinical disappointment from a competitor.

What's next

The most concrete upcoming data points are the EMA and UK regulatory decisions, both expected before the end of 2026. Those rulings will put European pricing and reimbursement strategy on record for the first time, and begin to answer whether declared international confidence matches commercial reality. Lilly's rolling approvals from its 40-country submission wave — particularly any Gulf State or Asian market decisions — will provide an additional read on competitive timing. The real test, though, is whether international payers agree that GLP-1 obesity pills belong in their formularies at prices that make the launches worth running.
