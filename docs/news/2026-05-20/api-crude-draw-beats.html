US crude stocks fall 9.1 million barrels, far exceeding the 3.4 million estimate

Large crude oil storage tanks at the ESSO Oil Storage Depot, Purfleet, UK — Geograph.org.uk / Creative Commons
In one sentence
The API reported a 9.1M-barrel crude draw for the week ending May 15 — 168% above the 3.4M consensus — signaling supply tighter than markets had priced in.

US commercial crude inventories fell 9.1 million barrels for the week ending May 15 — nearly three times the 3.4 million barrel draw analysts had forecast, according to the American Petroleum Institute. The 5.7 million barrel miss represents a 168% overshoot relative to consensus, and in a market already stretched thin by the Strait of Hormuz closure, the number carries weight beyond a single week's accounting.

The draw doesn't arrive in isolation. Since US-Iran hostilities effectively shut down the Strait of Hormuz in late February — disrupting roughly 20% of global seaborne oil trade — the market has been repricing supply assumptions on a near-weekly basis. The IEA has called it the largest supply shock in the history of global oil markets, with an estimated 14 million barrels per day of Middle Eastern output offline. Against that backdrop, weekly US inventory data has become one of the few real-time gauges traders can use to measure demand against whatever supply is being rerouted around the Gulf.

More than just crude

The headline number wasn't the only telling figure. Gasoline inventories dropped 5.8 million barrels in the same reporting period. Distillate stocks fell 1.0 million barrels. Most striking: the Strategic Petroleum Reserve drew down 9.9 million barrels — a record weekly decline — bringing it to its lowest level since July 2024. Read together, the data suggests consumption outpaced supply across the entire petroleum complex, not just at the crude level.

The acceleration is worth noting. The prior week's crude draw came in at 2.19 million barrels — elevated but not alarming. The jump to 9.1 million in a single week is a sharp break in pace, not a continuation of a gradual trend.

Market and policy implications

With Brent crude trading near $110 per barrel and WTI just below $100 — both up roughly 65–70% since early 2026 — a large portion of supply risk was already priced in. The question is whether a miss of this magnitude confirms what was priced in or signals something worse: consumption proving more durable than expected even at elevated prices.

If demand is holding firm despite year-on-year Brent gains of around 67%, that points to price inelasticity in energy consumption — an uncomfortable input for the Fed. Energy-driven inflation that stays elevated longer than expected complicates the case for near-term rate cuts. Treasury Secretary Bessent this week maintained a "wait and see" posture on lowering rates, a position that becomes harder to abandon if crude drawdowns keep beating forecasts by this margin. The 30-year Treasury yield hitting its highest level since 2007 — separately in the news this session — suggests bond markets are already pricing in that wait.

The IEA's May forecast projects global inventories drawing at approximately 8.5 million barrels per day through Q2, with May and June expected to see the steepest declines. This week's API figure fits that trajectory — though it came in well above even those elevated projections.

What to watch

The EIA's weekly petroleum status report, released the day after the API figures, will confirm or diverge from Tuesday's data. Traders will focus on Cushing, Oklahoma — the WTI pricing hub — which was sitting near 29 million barrels in prior weeks, still above critically low levels but tracking downward. A second consecutive outsized draw in the next cycle would tighten the inflation narrative considerably, and complicate any diplomatic progress on Iran that might otherwise offer the market some relief.
