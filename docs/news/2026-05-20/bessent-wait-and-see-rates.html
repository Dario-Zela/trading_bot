Bessent: US should wait and see before lowering rates

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, whose April comments on rates have gained renewed attention as long yields spike — U.S. Treasury Department / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Bessent endorsed the Fed's pause at a Semafor summit, saying the US should 'wait and see' on cuts — just as 30-year yields hit a 19-year high.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent told an audience at the Semafor World Economy Summit in April that the United States should "wait and see" before cutting interest rates — a position that has gained new market relevance as long-dated Treasury yields push to levels not seen since before the financial crisis.

Speaking at the conference on April 13, Bessent was asked directly whether rates should come down. His answer: "Eventually. I think now that we have to wait and see." The following day he clarified the framing — affirming that the Fed will "need to cut rates" and that its current posture of watchful waiting is "the right thing" — but the sequencing was clear. No near-term action.

The inflation backdrop driving the pause

The arithmetic behind the caution starts with the Iran war, which began in late February and drove a sharp spike in energy costs. April's consumer price index came in at 3.8% year-over-year — the hottest reading since May 2023 — with monthly price gains of 0.9% on the consumer side and 0.5% at the producer level. Energy drove the bulk of both figures.

Bessent, who describes himself as "Team Transitory" on inflation, said he was "quite confident" core prices would continue declining and that forward inflation expectations remained well-anchored. The war disruption, in his framing, is a temporary shock rather than a regime change. But he conceded the Fed needed to see how the energy overhang resolved before moving.

That logic — observe first, cut later — is now market consensus. CME FedWatch assigns roughly 99.9% probability to no change at the June 17–18 FOMC meeting, with the fed funds rate parked in its current 3.50%–3.75% range. September is the earliest date at which cut probability exceeds 40%. Some futures positioning has even begun pricing the possibility that the next move is a hike, not a cut.

Bond yields tighten the context

What has sharpened the relevance of Bessent's April remarks is what has happened to long yields since. The 30-year Treasury hit 5.197% on May 19 — its highest level since July 2007 — while the 10-year was trading around 4.67%. The selloff reflects a market working through the possibility that rate relief is not just delayed but structurally more distant than consensus assumed as recently as early spring.

For the Treasury secretary, commenting that the Fed should hold is not a neutral act. It effectively endorses a steeper yield curve: front-end rates anchored by a patient Fed, long end continuing to price fiscal risk and duration uncertainty. That combination pressures rate-sensitive sectors — mortgage origination, leveraged real estate, and growth equities whose valuations depend on discounted cash flows projected years out.

What's next

Two near-term events will test how durable the "wait and see" posture is. Kevin Warsh's confirmation hearing for Fed chair is expected next week; his own policy disposition will matter to how aggressively or gradually the Fed eventually moves once it does begin easing. And the June FOMC meeting is the next hard anchor for market expectations.

Bessent's comments also came with a broader growth view: he acknowledged the current quarter would require some recovery but suggested strong catch-up potential ahead, and noted that a ceasefire scenario could lead to a sharp drop in oil that would ease the inflation pressure currently justifying the pause. If that materializes, the "wait and see" framing could transition to action faster than futures markets currently imply. If it doesn't, the Treasury's measured tone will look increasingly prescient.
