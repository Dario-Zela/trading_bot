Bond yields, not Iran, are the bigger threat to AI ETFs — with Nvidia as the test

Nvidia headquarters in Santa Clara, California, where Q1 fiscal 2027 earnings are due after market close on May 20. — Wikimedia Commons / Creative Commons Attribution 2.0
In one sentence
30-year yields at 5.2% — not Iran — are squeezing AI ETF valuations, with Nvidia's Q1 earnings tonight the clearest test of the rally's durability.

On Tuesday, US stocks fell even as headlines played up hints of geopolitical de-escalation. That disconnect deserves attention. The 30-year Treasury yield closed at 5.197% — its highest since 2007 — while the 10-year reached 4.687%, a 16-month peak. For holders of AI-focused equity positions, these numbers matter more than dispatches from the Strait of Hormuz.

The mechanics are straightforward. Growth stocks carry most of their value in earnings projected years out. When the discount rate rises, those future earnings are worth less today. A semiconductor ETF is not a coupon bond, but the math rhymes: extend the duration, and rate sensitivity goes up. AI ETFs — heavily weighted toward Nvidia, ASML, Broadcom, and a handful of other names — sit at the long end of that spectrum. On Tuesday morning, VanEck's SMH and iShares' SOXX each fell more than 2%. Invesco's QQQ, which had hit an all-time close of 719.79 just five trading days earlier, dropped nearly 1% before partially recovering.

The Iran connection is real, but indirect

Iran is not irrelevant. The conflict has pushed oil prices higher, and that inflation — April CPI already came in at its highest annual rate in three years — is part of why the Federal Reserve has stayed on hold and why traders have begun pricing in the possibility of a rate hike rather than a cut. In that sense, Iran is upstream of the problem. But investors watching news flow for a geopolitical headline to trade are solving the wrong equation. The direct threat to AI valuations is the yield curve, not Iranian military capacity.

When Treasuries approach 5%, the case for holding expensive growth stocks requires a proportionally higher earnings growth assumption to compensate. That bar is exactly what Nvidia faces tonight.

Nvidia as the test case

Nvidia reports Q1 fiscal 2027 results after the close on May 20. The consensus sits at $79.08 billion in revenue — roughly 80% year-over-year growth from the $44.06 billion posted a year ago — with earnings per share of $1.76 against $0.96 last year. Data center is expected to carry $72.85 billion of that total. The company has beaten the revenue bar in 14 consecutive quarters and cleared the EPS line 13 straight times.

The issue is that a beat is already priced in. Analysts widely note that Nvidia would likely need to demonstrate something closer to 85% top-line growth — or materially raise forward guidance — for the stock to see a meaningful post-earnings move higher. NVDA has lost ground in each of the past three sessions, shedding 1.3% on Tuesday alone to close near $221.75, down from $235.74 a week ago. The H20 chip export restrictions to China have removed a revenue stream, adding a discrete headwind on top of the macro rate pressure.

Price target upgrades remain in circulation — Cantor Fitzgerald at $350, HSBC at $325, KeyBanc and DA Davidson at $300 — but targets calibrated to pre-yield-spike discount rates are worth less than they were when written.

What to watch

If the 30-year yield advances to 5.25%, several strategists have said equity valuations will face a more sustained drawdown. Nvidia's results after the close on May 20 will set the near-term tone: a genuine beat — revenue well above $79 billion plus strong guidance — could stabilize semiconductor ETFs. A narrow beat or any guidance miss in an environment of 5.2% long-bond yields would likely accelerate the rotation out of growth and into the defensive and energy names that have shown relative strength this week.

The Fed's next policy meeting is in June. Between now and then, Nvidia's numbers are the market's best read on whether AI capital spending is robust enough to justify current valuations at any discount rate.
