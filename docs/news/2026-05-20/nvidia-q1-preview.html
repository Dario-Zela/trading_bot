Nvidia Q1 earnings preview: can the AI chip giant make it 14 straight beats?

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, pictured in 2025, has guided for $78 billion in Q1 revenue and described a $1 trillion demand pipeline through 2027 — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nvidia reports Q1 results Wednesday with ~$78.8B revenue expected — a 79% YoY jump — but the real test is Q2 guidance and whether the 13-quarter beat streak still moves the stock.

Nvidia reports its fiscal first-quarter 2027 results on Wednesday after the close of trading — and for the fourteenth time in as many quarters, Wall Street expects the company to beat its own numbers. The question heading into the print isn't really whether Nvidia will top estimates. The question is whether 80 percent revenue growth still counts as a win when the market has been running on that assumption for two years.

What the numbers say

Analysts are projecting revenue of roughly $78.8 billion for the quarter, compared to $44.1 billion a year ago — growth of about 79 percent year-over-year. Non-GAAP earnings per share consensus sits around $1.77, up from $0.61 in the same quarter two years prior. On EPS alone, Nvidia has beaten analyst estimates in each of the past 13 consecutive quarters; it has cleared the revenue bar for 14 straight.

The company itself set the baseline in February, when it guided for Q1 revenue of $78 billion — plus or minus 2 percent. That guidance, notably, explicitly excludes any data center revenue from China, a market Nvidia estimates at roughly $50 billion annually but which it is effectively locked out of under current U.S. export controls.

Data center is where the action is. That segment is expected to generate $73 billion in revenue for the quarter, up from $39.1 billion a year ago. It now represents more than 90 percent of Nvidia's total revenue, a concentration that would be alarming in most industries but reflects where practically every dollar of enterprise AI infrastructure spending currently flows.

Blackwell and beyond

The current Blackwell architecture is driving roughly 70 percent of data center compute revenue, with the older Hopper generation filling out most of the rest. Nvidia's CEO Jensen Huang said at GTC 2026 in March that he can see "at least $1 trillion" in purchase orders for Blackwell and the forthcoming Vera Rubin chips through 2027 — double the figure he cited a year earlier. Supply, not demand, has been the constraint: Blackwell chips have been sold out months in advance.

What analysts will watch most closely on Wednesday isn't the headline revenue beat. It's the gross margin and, above all, the Q2 guidance.

Consensus gross margin expectation sits at 74.5 percent, just below Nvidia's own guidance of approximately 75 percent. A reading below 73 percent would signal pricing pressure as Blackwell scales. One analyst framing has it bluntly: margin direction will move the stock more than the revenue beat itself.

For Q2 FY2027, the Wall Street consensus is guidance in the range of $85 to $87 billion. The whisper number — the buy-side expectation that traders actually position around — is closer to $90 billion. Anything above $87 billion sends the stock higher; anything below $85 billion and the deceleration narrative has a data point to hang on.

The China knot

No factor introduces more uncertainty into Nvidia's numbers than its China exposure. The Biden-era export controls that barred Nvidia from selling its highest-performance chips to China — later tightened to include the H20, a chip specifically designed to comply with earlier restrictions — have effectively removed Nvidia from one of the largest AI infrastructure markets on earth.

A complicating wrinkle emerged earlier this year when Reuters reported that Chinese AI startup DeepSeek had trained its latest model on Nvidia Blackwell chips in apparent violation of U.S. export controls, according to a Trump administration official. The allegation put Nvidia at the center of U.S.-China AI policy tension at a sensitive moment — just as the company was hoping for some regulatory clarity on what it can sell and where.

Huang has described the China loss as a real hit, not a rounding error. The company's fiscal 2026 annual report acknowledged being "effectively foreclosed" from competing in China's data center market, and noted that the foreclosure had given competitors room to build larger developer ecosystems. Sovereign AI deals in the Middle East, Europe, and Southeast Asia are cited as offsets, but they are not yet large enough to fully replace what China represented.

Wins priced in, but beats still punished less than misses

Nvidia stock has underperformed its earnings results on four of the past five earnings days, dropping even after beats that would have driven euphoria two years ago. The stock entered Wednesday's report down about 3 percent over a three-day losing streak, pressured partly by the broader bond market selloff that has pushed 30-year Treasury yields to their highest level since 2007. Rising yields compress technology valuations mechanically, and Nvidia — with a market cap that makes it one of the largest components in both the S&P 500 and the Nasdaq 100 — absorbs that pressure disproportionately.

At roughly 24 times next-twelve-months earnings, Nvidia is actually cheaper than several of its closest peers on that metric: Broadcom trades at 31 times, ASML at 36 times, AMD at more than 53 times. The discount reflects a genuine risk: geopolitical exposure and China revenue loss, not a fundamental loss of competitive position.

Analyst ratings are overwhelmingly bullish. Of approximately 60 analyst ratings tracked by aggregators, 57 are buys; fewer than five are holds or sells. Price targets have been rising. Cantor Fitzgerald's CJ Muse lifted his target to $350 from $300, calling the current 14-times-2027-earnings multiple "incredibly low." Morgan Stanley raised its target to $285, KeyBanc to $300, HSBC to $325, DA Davidson to $300. Bank of America has a $320 target, anchored to a projection of a $1.7 trillion AI data center market by 2030.

Options markets are pricing an 8 to 10 percent move in either direction following the report — elevated but not extraordinary for a company this size reporting at this scale.

What to watch Wednesday

Three things will determine the market's reaction more than the headline numbers: gross margin (the signal for whether Blackwell's profitability is holding up as volumes scale), Q2 revenue guidance (the signal for whether the growth trajectory stays intact through the summer), and China commentary (the signal for whether any regulatory clarity is emerging, or whether things are getting worse).

If Nvidia guides Q2 above $87 billion with margins near 75 percent and offers any positive read on the China situation, the stock will recover its pre-earnings losses and then some. If the guidance is conservative, or if management signals margin compression as Blackwell shipments ramp at high volume, the deceleration thesis gets its first real foothold — and every AI-adjacent holding in a portfolio gets repriced alongside it.

The streak sits at 13 consecutive EPS beats, 14 on revenue. Wednesday will determine whether that number goes to 14 and 15, or stops.
