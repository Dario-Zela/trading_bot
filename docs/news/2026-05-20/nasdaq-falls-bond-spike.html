Nasdaq 100 falls 1% as 30-year yields reach a 19-year high

The Nasdaq MarketSite in Times Square, New York City. — Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 3.0
In one sentence
The 30-year Treasury yield hit a 19-year high near 5.2%, driving the Nasdaq 100 down 1% with chipmakers leading losses as rate math turned against growth stocks.

U.S. equities retreated Tuesday as the 30-year Treasury yield climbed to its highest level since 2007, ratcheting up the cost of capital for duration-sensitive growth stocks and sending the chip sector into a third consecutive losing session.

The Nasdaq 100 fell 1% to close at 28,703. The S&P 500 shed 0.6% to 7,355. The Dow Jones Industrial Average lost 261 points, or 0.5%, to 49,425. The Russell 2000 underperformed the large-cap benchmarks, declining 1.3% to 2,740.

The yield that's doing the damage

The 30-year Treasury bond yield rose 6 basis points to 5.18%, briefly touching 5.197% intraday — its highest print since July 2007, before the financial crisis broke. The 10-year yield climbed 8 basis points to 4.67%, a 16-month peak. Even the 2-year note, typically more anchored to Federal Reserve expectations, moved up 7 basis points to 4.13%.

The curve's move was broad and simultaneous, not a rotation between maturities. That pattern usually signals a market revising its view of the long-run neutral rate upward — or demanding more term premium for the risk of holding government debt at all.

The inflation backdrop explains the bid for term premium. U.S. consumer prices rose in April at the fastest annual pace in three years, and the ongoing conflict with Iran has kept energy costs elevated, clouding the disinflation path that the Fed requires before it can ease. Treasury Secretary Bessent, speaking separately Tuesday, said the U.S. should "wait and see" before cutting rates — a signal that any relief from the short end remains some distance off.

The Bank of America Global Fund Manager Survey, released Tuesday and covering 200 managers overseeing $517 billion in assets, found that 62% expect the 30-year yield to eventually reach 6%. "Second-wave inflation" ranked as the top tail risk in the survey, cited by 40% of respondents. The same survey found equity allocations had surged — a 37-percentage-point jump in net overweight to U.S. equities versus April — which suggests institutional positioning is stretched just as the cost of holding equities against bonds is rising.

Why semiconductors absorb the worst of it

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index fell for the third day running, extending a losing streak that has shaved roughly 6.8% from the sector's recent peak. Among the session's individual movers: Qualcomm fell 3.1%, Broadcom lost 1.9%, and Nvidia declined ahead of its quarterly results due Wednesday after the close.

The mechanics are straightforward. Semiconductor stocks — particularly those whose valuations rest on AI-driven revenue projections extending three to five years out — are effectively long-duration assets dressed as equities. Their prices are discounted present values of future cash flows. When the discount rate rises 6 or 8 basis points in a single session, that math turns unfavorable fast. The higher the forward P/E multiple, the more the stock is exposed.

Goldman Sachs, in notes circulating this week, characterized the shift as a transition from "AI CAPEX boom" pricing to "rate and bond supply" pricing — meaning the market is beginning to weigh the cost of building out AI infrastructure against the alternative of parking capital in Treasuries yielding north of 5%.

Not every chip name moved lower. Marvell Technology gained 6.6% after a cluster of analysts raised price targets, and Astera Labs jumped 10% on multiple upgrades. The bifurcation is telling: stocks with near-term catalysts or recently reset expectations absorbed buying; those carrying premium multiples on distant earnings got sold.

Defensive rotation and the oil background

The day's sector rotation reflected straightforward risk-off logic. Healthcare (XLV) gained 1.3% — the session's best-performing major sector — and utilities added 0.8%, lifted in part by NextEra Energy's announced $67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy. Clean energy (PBW) fell 4%, caught between the rate headwind and the broader equity retreat.

Oil held above $103 a barrel. The Iran standoff remains unresolved, with President Trump saying Tuesday that a decision was "still days away" and that the U.S. was "not leaving Iran yet, we're going to do it right." The crude market is taking him at his word — prices have not retreated meaningfully since the conflict began — but oil was not Tuesday's driver. It is holding a floor, not moving the tape.

Gold fell 1.4% to $4,503 an ounce, down roughly 6.6% month-to-date, as rising real yields diminish the appeal of non-yielding assets.

What to watch

Nvidia reports Wednesday evening. The stock is coming off three straight down sessions and carries more weight in the Nasdaq 100 than any single name. Fourteen consecutive earnings beats have built the expectation into the price; the question is whether a fifteenth is sufficient against a backdrop where the discount rate is moving structurally higher.

Beyond Wednesday, the market needs to decide how much of the yield move it has already priced. At 5.18%, the 30-year is within 82 basis points of the 6% level that a majority of professional investors now see as the eventual destination. If that repricing continues, the rotation out of high-multiple tech into defensives and short-duration assets has further to run.
