Trump signs executive order to streamline fintech regulation

Fintech industry segments spanning payments, lending, digital assets, and capital markets — all potentially affected by the new regulatory review mandate — Kevin Moseri / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
Trump's fintech EO orders a 90-day regulatory review and asks the Fed to evaluate payment rail access for non-banks — direction is clear, details are not.

President Trump on Monday signed an executive order directing federal financial regulators to review and modernize rules governing fintech companies, with a specific instruction to the Federal Reserve to evaluate whether non-bank payment processors and digital asset firms should gain direct access to the central bank's payment rails.

The order, titled "Integrating Financial Technology Innovation into Regulatory Frameworks," assigns the six principal federal financial regulators — the CFPB, SEC, NCUA, CFTC, FDIC, and OCC — 90 days to audit existing regulations, guidance, supervisory practices, and application processes that affect fintech firms. Agencies then have six months to act on whatever they find.

The White House framed the current rulebook as an anachronism. In the order's policy statement, existing regulations are described as "relics of a time when financial services were predominately provided in brick-and-mortar-centric settings." The administration specifically flagged payment services access rules, third-party risk management requirements, and the absence of clear integration pathways for digital assets as friction points the review should address.

The Fed payment rail question

The Federal Reserve's assignment carries the most direct market relevance. Within 120 days, the Fed must assess its legal authority to extend Reserve Bank payment accounts and services to uninsured depositories and non-bank financial companies — including digital asset firms. If the Fed establishes a permissive framework, the order further requires it to decide on individual access applications within 90 days of receipt.

Direct Fed payment rail access is currently limited largely to chartered banks. Non-bank payment processors, stablecoin issuers, and crypto infrastructure companies have long sought that access as a way to bypass the correspondent banking layer that mediates their transactions today. Whether the Fed concludes existing statute permits the expansion — or whether legislative changes are required — will determine whether Monday's order has practical trading consequences or stays administrative.

Scope is real; specifics are not

The order does not name any regulation for repeal, nor does it identify which companies stand to benefit. That is by design: the review process is meant to surface candidates. But it also means that actual rulemaking, where it follows, could stretch well past the six-month action deadline. Executive orders direct reviews; they do not change rules.

A companion order signed the same day moved in the opposite direction on at least one dimension, tightening customer identification and anti-money laundering requirements. The administration framed the pair as complementary — lower barriers for compliant fintechs, harder walls against illicit finance.

Industry reaction ran from procedural appreciation to watchful waiting. The Defense Credit Union Council called the order a step toward modernizing "outdated regulatory frameworks." America's Credit Unions said the directive "appears to align" with its requests for stakeholder input and time to implement changes, without committing to support.

What to watch

The review calendar gives markets two concrete inflection points: the 90-day regulatory audit deadline, which falls in mid-August, and the 180-day action deadline in mid-November. The Fed's 120-day payment access report lands in mid-September. None of those dates guarantee rulemaking — they guarantee reports — but they are the earliest moments at which specifics will surface.

For traders positioning in payments, lending platforms, or crypto infrastructure, the order's direction is clear even if the destination is not. The policy signal favors non-bank fintechs seeking regulatory parity with chartered institutions. The magnitude of any benefit depends entirely on which rules the agencies actually propose to change — and that answer is months away.
