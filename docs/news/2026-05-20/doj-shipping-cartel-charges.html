DOJ charges seven Chinese shipping executives in pandemic price-fixing case

Stacked shipping containers at the Port of Antwerp — the four indicted companies collectively manufacture roughly 95% of the world's standard dry shipping containers. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
DOJ unseals indictment of 4 Chinese container makers and 7 executives for cartel that doubled container prices and boosted profits ~100x during COVID.

The U.S. Department of Justice unsealed a federal indictment Tuesday charging four of the world's largest shipping container manufacturers and seven of their executives with conspiring to fix prices during the COVID-19 pandemic — a scheme prosecutors say affected roughly $35 billion in international commerce and amplified one of the worst supply chain crises in modern history.

The indictment, filed in the Northern District of California, names China International Marine Containers (CIMC), Singamas Container Holdings, Shanghai Universal Logistics Equipment (known as Dong Fang), and CXIC Group Containers Co. Collectively, the four companies manufacture approximately 95% of the world's standard unrefrigerated shipping containers.

How the cartel operated

Prosecutors allege the conspiracy ran from November 2019 through at least January 2024 — spanning the pandemic onset, the global supply crunch, and well into the recovery. The mechanism was straightforward: the manufacturers agreed to restrict output by capping production shifts, banning construction of new factories, and imposing financial penalties on any member that exceeded agreed output ceilings. To monitor compliance, the companies installed surveillance cameras at each other's facilities.

The timing was not coincidental. As COVID-19 disrupted global trade flows and demand for manufactured goods surged through 2020, the cartel's artificial output limits amplified what was already a historic container shortage. Standard container prices roughly doubled between 2019 and 2021. Container manufacturers' profits increased approximately one hundredfold during that window. Spot freight rates from Asia to the U.S. West Coast hit $26,000 per 40-foot box by late 2021 — more than 330% above year-earlier levels, a number that cascaded into elevated prices for nearly every consumer good that moves by sea.

The seven defendants

The executives charged span all four companies. From CIMC: CEO Boliang Mai, Vice President Tianhua Huang, and General Manager Yongbo Wan. From Singamas: CEO Siong Seng Teo and Marketing Director Vick Nam Hing Ma. From Dong Fang: General Manager Qianmin Li. From CXIC: CEO Yuqiang Zhang.

Of the seven, only Ma is in custody. He was apprehended in France in April 2026; extradition proceedings are underway. The remaining six are at large — almost certainly in China, which has no extradition treaty with the United States. The indictment was secured in 2025 and kept under seal until Ma's arrest gave prosecutors a reason to unseal it.

DOJ's framing

Associate Attorney General Stanley Woodward did not choose mild language. "We are holding these Chinese bad actors accountable for exploiting the pandemic to fill their own coffers," he said. Acting Assistant Attorney General Omeed Assefi added that the defendants "lined their own pockets by choking the world's supply" at the pandemic's height.

The rhetoric is deliberate. The case arrives amid sustained trade tension between Washington and Beijing, and it draws an explicit line between Chinese corporate conduct and the economic pain felt by U.S. businesses and households during the supply chain crisis.

The enforcement gap

The indictment is meaningful on paper. Its practical reach is more constrained. Six defendants are almost certainly beyond U.S. jurisdiction. CIMC and Singamas are listed in Hong Kong and have no U.S. operational footprint to attach. Criminal antitrust convictions require defendants to appear in court; absent extradition, charges frequently become permanent standoffs.

The lag between conduct and prosecution is also notable: the alleged conspiracy began in November 2019, ran through January 2024, and the charges became public only in May 2026. Container spot rates have largely normalized since 2022. The deterrent effect of a prosecution announced after the market has already corrected is, by definition, limited.

That said, DOJ has put on record that pandemic-era collusion by foreign manufacturers — even those with no direct U.S. presence — falls within American criminal antitrust reach. That precedent extends beyond shipping.

What to watch

Ma's extradition from France is the immediate test. France and the U.S. have a functioning extradition treaty, making the proceeding credible — but French courts allow defendants to challenge at multiple layers, and timelines stretch. Any U.S. commercial relationships or assets held by the four companies become secondary leverage points for prosecutors.

For traders with exposure to container shipping equities or logistics names, the indictment itself is unlikely to move near-term fundamentals. The cartel's conduct is finished, the profits have long since been booked, and container markets have normalized. The story to watch is whether any civil follow-on litigation from U.S. importers — who may now have cleaner standing to sue — gains traction in the months ahead.
