Siemens beats estimates, but market looks through the statutory profit

Siemens AG corporate headquarters at Wittelsbacherplatz in central Munich. — Álvaro de la Paz Franco / Wikimedia Commons (CC0)
In one sentence
Siemens beat adjusted EPS estimates in Q2 FY2026, but one-time gains and operational misses in revenue and Mobility profit left the stock 6% below its 52-week high.

Siemens Aktiengesellschaft (ETR: SIE) reported second-quarter fiscal 2026 results on May 13 that, on the surface, cleared the bar analysts had set. Earnings per share before purchase price allocation — the adjusted figure management prefers — came in at €2.81, ahead of the €2.68 consensus. The headline looked fine. The market disagreed.

Shares fell as much as 1.5% in early Frankfurt trading before recovering to a modest gain. As of May 20, SIE is trading at €257.90, roughly 6% below its 52-week high of €275.75. That gap tells a more complicated story than the EPS line does.

What the adjustments are hiding

The €2.81 adjusted EPS figure included a previously disclosed €172 million gain from the sale of Siemens' airport logistics business in the United States. Strip that out and the underlying performance looks thinner. For context, the prior-year Q2 figure also contained a non-recurring item — a €300 million gain from exiting Smart Infrastructure's wiring accessories business — which means year-on-year comparisons are muddied on both ends.

Zoom out to the trailing twelve months and the picture sharpens. Analysis from Simply Wall St estimates that roughly €674 million in unusual items have boosted statutory profit over that period, raising the reasonable question of what the baseline earnings power actually is when the one-time gains stop arriving.

On the operational metrics the market watches more closely, Siemens missed. Revenue came in at €19.76 billion — flat year-on-year and short of the €20.14 billion consensus. Currency accounted for a 6 to 7 percentage point drag on nominal growth, but that explanation only travels so far with investors who have heard it for several quarters running. Industrial Business profit landed at €2.97 billion, a 15.4% margin, against a consensus estimate of €3.05 billion. Mobility was worse: €208 million in profit at a 6.9% margin, far below the €276 million estimate, as U.S. tariffs added 170 basis points of margin pressure and several projects encountered delays.

Where the numbers held up

The order book is the strongest argument in Siemens' favor. Orders rose 18% on a comparable basis to €24.11 billion, beating the €22.29 billion estimate by a wide margin. The backlog now stands at a record €124 billion. Smart Infrastructure posted record order intake of €7.53 billion, up 35% on a comparable basis, with data center demand described as triple-digit growth. Digital Industries — the software-heavy division that investors treat as the growth engine — earned €857 million at an 18.5% margin, beating the €786 million estimate, and raised its full-year comparable revenue growth guidance to 7%–10% from 5%–10%.

Free cash flow of €1.72 billion, up 71% year-on-year, gave management the confidence to announce a €6 billion share repurchase program to be executed over up to five years. JPMorgan acknowledged the buyback but noted analysts "had hoped for either a higher number" or a shorter execution window. Bernstein was more direct, citing the "lack of a group guidance upgrade" as "likely to be a slight disappointment," particularly given stronger results from some competitors in the industrial software space.

CEO Roland Busch, addressing the geopolitical backdrop — which now includes active conflict in the Middle East, persistent U.S. tariff pressure, and a bond market repricing that is raising financing costs globally — said the company is "continuing our successful path to profitable growth, despite the still very tense geopolitical environment."

What the market is pricing in

For algorithmic and quantitative traders, the setup here is a familiar one: a company posts a headline beat, but the beat is partially manufactured by gains that won't recur, while operational metrics that actually drive forward earnings miss. The stock's failure to sustain a meaningful post-earnings rally is the market's way of expressing skepticism about earnings quality rather than reporting an error.

The group-level full-year guidance was reaffirmed at 6%–8% comparable revenue growth and EPS pre-PPA of €10.70–€11.10, but that reaffirmation cuts both ways — it implies H2 execution needs to be clean to land in range, without further help from asset sales. Mobility's full-year outlook was cut to 5%–7% comparable revenue growth from 8%–10%, and tariff risk has not stabilized.

The next hard data point will be Q3 FY2026 results, expected in August. Between now and then, investors will be watching whether Digital Industries' software revenue can sustain double-digit growth, whether Smart Infrastructure's data center tailwind continues to offset sluggishness elsewhere, and whether Mobility can stabilize margins once the initial tariff shock is absorbed. A clean quarter with no unusual items would be the most useful signal the market could get.
