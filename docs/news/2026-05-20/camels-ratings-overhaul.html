US regulators propose CAMELS overhaul to sharpen focus on financial risk

The entrance to FDIC headquarters in Washington, D.C., one of the member agencies of the FFIEC. — G. Edward Johnson / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 4.0)
In one sentence
The FFIEC proposes the first CAMELS overhaul since 1996, shifting bank ratings from process compliance to measurable financial risk, with public comment due August 17.

Federal bank regulators unveiled a proposal Tuesday to overhaul the CAMELS rating system — the primary tool used to assess the health of U.S. banks — for the first time since 1996. The Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council is seeking public comment on changes that would redirect supervisory attention away from procedural compliance and toward what it calls "material financial risks."

CAMELS is an acronym for Capital adequacy, Asset quality, Management, Earnings, Liquidity, and Sensitivity to market risk. Every federally supervised bank receives a composite CAMELS score on a 1-to-5 scale; the rating determines deposit insurance premiums, constrains dividend payments, and signals to markets how regulators view an institution's safety and soundness. A downgrade can restrict lending capacity and push up funding costs. The framework has not been substantively updated in three decades.

What the proposal would change

The most consequential shift is to the Management component — the "M" in CAMELS. Under the current system, the management rating carries special weight in determining the composite score, which critics argue creates a channel for examiners to dock banks for documentation gaps and procedural shortfalls rather than genuine financial deterioration. The proposal would remove that special weighting and require that management-related downgrades be tied directly to demonstrable financial risks.

OCC Comptroller Jonathan Gould was blunt about the rationale: "Historically, the management rating has reflected deficiencies already captured in other components."

The proposal also eliminates reputational risk as a factor in supervisory ratings, aligning CAMELS with earlier moves by the FDIC and OCC to strip reputational risk from their examination frameworks. Rating-scale definitions would be rewritten to distinguish more clearly between institutions that are "strong," "satisfactory," "deficient," and "critically deficient" — language tied to measurable financial performance rather than process adherence.

FFIEC Chair Michelle Bowman, who also serves as the Federal Reserve's Vice Chair for Supervision, described the overhaul as "a decisive shift toward transparency, quantitative factors, and predictability of supervisory oversight."

FDIC Chairman Travis Hill framed the intent similarly, saying the revision moves emphasis "away from a bank's process for managing risks and towards factors and risks that materially impact a bank's financial condition." Hill added that internal controls and risk management practices would "remain relevant" — just no longer the primary driver of ratings.

Industry reaction

The banking industry welcomed the direction, if cautiously. Bank Policy Institute CEO Greg Baer argued the management component "has had undue weight in determining bank ratings." ABA President Rob Nichols was more measured: "There are opportunities to make thoughtful improvements prioritizing safety and soundness without imposing unnecessary compliance burdens."

The proposal fits a broader deregulatory posture from the Trump administration on bank oversight, and Gould has signaled he would go further — arguing the Management component continues to double-count risks already measured elsewhere in the CAMELS framework.

Market implications

The timing matters. U.S. 30-year Treasury yields are at their highest since 2007, and bond markets have been pricing in persistent fiscal and rate uncertainty. In that environment, the direction of bank supervisory ratings carries real weight: CAMELS scores feed directly into lending capacity, deposit insurance assessments, and — when they leak, as they sometimes do — market valuations of individual banks.

A framework that anchors downgrades more firmly to quantitative financial metrics rather than examiner discretion over process quality could reduce the frequency of rating actions driven by procedural findings. For banks currently navigating duration risk and deposit repricing pressures, that may offer some relief from supervisory overhang. But the same shift means that genuine financial stress — the kind that hard numbers reveal — becomes harder to paper over.

What's next

Comments on the proposed revisions are due August 17, 2026. After the comment period closes, the FFIEC and its member agencies — the Federal Reserve, FDIC, OCC, and NCUA — will review submissions before any final rule takes effect. Given the proposal's alignment with current agency leadership priorities, the broad contours are likely to survive intact, though the precise treatment of the management component may see further debate.
