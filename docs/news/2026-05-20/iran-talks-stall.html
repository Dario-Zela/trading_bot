Iran talks show little progress despite mediation efforts

The Jinnah Convention Centre in Islamabad, where US and Iranian delegations held 21 hours of talks in April 2026 without reaching a deal — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
US-Iran post-ceasefire talks remain deadlocked over nuclear enrichment as mediators report little progress and Washington weighs resuming strikes.

Forty-three days after a ceasefire halted direct US-Israeli strikes on Iran, the diplomatic effort to convert that pause into a durable settlement is showing little momentum. The Wall Street Journal reported Tuesday that mediators see scant sign of a breakthrough, even as both sides formally remain at the table.

The sticking point is familiar: Iran's nuclear program. Washington is demanding Tehran suspend uranium enrichment and surrender its existing stockpile of highly enriched uranium — preconditions for sanctions relief and a formal end to hostilities. Iran has offered verbal commitments not to pursue a nuclear weapon but has stopped well short of operational concessions. A senior US official, cited by Axios, called Tehran's latest counterproposal a collection of "token improvements" — more words, no deeds. "We are really not making a lot of progress," the official said. "We are at a very serious place today."

The ceasefire, which took effect April 8 after US and Israeli strikes degraded much of Iran's nuclear infrastructure, was supposed to open a 30-day negotiating window. The Islamabad Talks on April 11–12 brought delegations of several hundred officials face-to-face for 21 hours across three rounds, ending without agreement. Subsequent exchanges through Pakistani intermediaries have not moved the needle.

Pakistan's mediation carries its own structural constraints. Pakistani officials have struggled to develop a reliable channel to Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps, which — following the assassination of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in late February — has become the effective center of Iranian decision-making. Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi has reportedly told multiple mediators, including those from Egypt, Turkey, and Qatar, that there is no consensus inside the Iranian leadership on how far to go on nuclear concessions.

The military backdrop has not eased. Trump halted a planned strike Tuesday after describing unspecified "positive developments," but warned that without real flexibility from Tehran, "we will have a conversation through bombs." US and Israeli forces are reported to be readying contingency operations. In a separate move, the US Navy seized the Iranian-linked tanker Skywave in the Indian Ocean, carrying more than one million barrels of crude — a signal that economic pressure is running alongside the diplomatic track.

Oil, trading near $104 a barrel, already reflects the regional disruption. The Strait of Hormuz remains a friction point, and Iran continues to contest transit through it. No specific market outcome — sanctions relief, frozen asset releases — is imminent from the current negotiating state.

The next indicator will be whether Pakistan's Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi, who traveled to Tehran last week in a bid to salvage the process, can arrange a fresh round of talks with substantive concessions on the table rather than rhetoric. Iranian leadership division and US military pressure are pulling in opposite directions, and the window before one side blinks — or strikes — is narrowing.
