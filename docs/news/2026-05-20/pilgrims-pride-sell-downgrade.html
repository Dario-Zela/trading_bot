Three analysts pile on Pilgrim's Pride with sell ratings as cash flow deteriorates

Pilgrim's Pride processing facility in Nacogdoches, Texas — Billy Hathorn / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
PPC's Q1 earnings fell 66% and Zacks slapped a Strong Sell on the stock, but Barclays upgraded to Overweight on free cash flow, leaving analysts split on whether the worst is priced in.

Pilgrim's Pride has drawn competing reads from Wall Street after a punishing first quarter, with a significant split emerging between analysts focused on near-term earnings momentum and those looking past the pain to free cash flow. The net result is an unusual configuration: a quantitative strong-sell signal alongside a fresh bullish upgrade — and the stock down roughly 20% year-to-date with no clear consensus on whether the worst is priced in.

The Q1 2026 numbers were ugly enough to move the needle on their own. Net income fell to $101.5 million from $296.3 million a year earlier — a 66% drop — on revenue that was essentially flat at $4.53 billion. Adjusted EBITDA margin compressed from 12.0% to 6.8%. Management attributed the squeeze to softer commodity pricing in its U.S. Fresh and Big Bird segment, plant downtime from winter storms, and elevated SG&A from legal settlements. Capital expenditures hit $234.8 million in the quarter against $140.8 million in operating cash flow, leaving the company cash flow negative on a free basis for the period.

Zacks and UBS on the cautious side

Zacks Investment Research has assigned Pilgrim's Pride a Rank #5 — its Strong Sell designation — following a sustained run of downward earnings revisions. The current fiscal-year EPS consensus has been marked down roughly 10% over the past 30 days to $3.87, implying a 25% year-over-year decline. The next two quarterly estimates have each moved lower by mid-to-high single digits over the same span.

Zacks's ranking system is driven by the direction and magnitude of analyst estimate revisions rather than intrinsic valuation — a distinction worth keeping in mind. A Rank #5 does not claim the stock is overpriced; it signals that the earnings revision cycle is running against the company right now, and that historically such momentum tends to persist.

UBS holds a Neutral rating, flagging near-term profit headwinds as the dominant catalyst to watch. The firm is not forecasting a collapse, but it is not willing to lean in ahead of better evidence on margins.

Barclays takes the other side

On May 1, Barclays analyst Benjamin Theurer upgraded Pilgrim's Pride to Overweight from Equal-Weight — moving the stock from hold to buy — while simultaneously lowering the price target to $42 from $45. The apparent contradiction makes sense in context: Theurer sees the stock's 18% year-to-date decline as an overreaction and expects the company to generate roughly $350 million in free cash flow during fiscal 2026 despite the heavy capital spending cycle.

The bull thesis rests on two pillars. First, Pilgrim's ongoing plant conversions and new Prepared Foods capacity should improve margins meaningfully in 2027 and beyond as the capital drag fades. Second, poultry's affordability advantage over beef — with cattle prices at elevated levels — is expected to sustain volume demand through the margin trough. The stock's P/E of around 8x provides a cushion that Theurer views as compelling.

What it adds up to

The divergence between Zacks's sell signal and Barclays's upgrade is not purely semantic. It reflects a genuine analytical disagreement about which metric governs the stock's near-term trajectory. Earnings revision momentum says the pain is still arriving. Cash flow analysis says the market is already overestimating it.

Broader analyst consensus, based on S&P Global data, runs closer to neutral: eight analysts tracked by the service carry a consensus buy, with an average price target around $40-$43 — roughly 25-35% above where the stock trades. Goldman Sachs and BMO Capital both hold Hold ratings at $39 and $40, respectively.

For the stock to move decisively in either direction, the Q2 earnings report — expected in late July — will need to show whether the margin compression is cyclical, as Barclays believes, or structural, as the downward revisions trend implies. Any signal from management on whether plant downtime has normalized and whether commodity pricing has stabilized will be closely read. So will free cash flow, which has become the contested variable in this debate.
