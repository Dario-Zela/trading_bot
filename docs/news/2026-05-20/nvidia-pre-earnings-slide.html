Nvidia extends three-day losing streak ahead of quarterly results

Nvidia's Endeavor campus on San Tomas Expressway in Santa Clara, California — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nvidia slid 0.8% to $220.61 in a three-day skid before Wednesday's earnings, where analysts expect $79B revenue — but the stock has fallen after 3 of its last 4 reports.

Nvidia shares fell 0.8% to $220.61 on Tuesday, closing out a third consecutive losing session just hours before the company is scheduled to report its fiscal first-quarter results after the bell on Wednesday.

The decline was modest in absolute terms — $1.71 per share — but its timing carried outsize significance. Nvidia enters Wednesday's report with a market capitalization of roughly $5.4 trillion and a stock trading at approximately 45 times forward earnings, a valuation that leaves limited margin for disappointment. Analysts expect revenue of $79.2 billion for the quarter ended April 26, a 79.5% jump year over year, with earnings per share of $1.78 — up approximately 120% from the same period last year.

Those are extraordinary numbers. The question is whether the market has already priced them in.

The context behind the slide

The three-day retreat follows a period of strength. Nvidia shares rose roughly 10% over the past month and are up about 19% year to date, with shares touching an all-time high of $235.74 earlier in May. The pattern heading into earnings has become familiar: despite beating guidance in each of its last 13 quarterly reports, the stock has declined after three of the four most recent releases. The clearest example came in February, when results showing revenue accelerating to 73% year-over-year growth were met with a 5.5% next-day selloff, followed by an 11% drawdown within a month.

The macro backdrop on Tuesday added pressure. The 30-year Treasury yield reached 5.197%, a level not seen in years, and the 10-year settled at 4.687% — both creating headwinds for high-multiple growth stocks across the board. Nvidia's roughly 45x earnings multiple makes it acutely sensitive to rate moves.

Three things investors are watching

Forward guidance is the most immediate tripwire. Wall Street's current fiscal Q2 revenue estimate sits around $87 billion. Any figure materially below that level — even accompanied by a strong Q1 beat — risks a negative market reaction, given how much of the upside case now depends on uninterrupted sequential growth.

Vera Rubin, Nvidia's next-generation chip platform and successor to Blackwell, is the medium-term test. The company has committed to commencing production shipments in the second half of 2026. Investors want updated data on yields and customer pull-through.

China remains the unresolved variable. U.S. export restrictions on the H20 chip resulted in roughly $5.5 billion in charges during the quarter, and CEO Jensen Huang has described Nvidia's AI chip market share in China as having fallen to zero from roughly 90%. The Trump administration eased some H200 restrictions in January, adding a 25% tariff pathway; Huang has said the market will "open over time," but no revised revenue guidance on China has been offered.

The demand foundation underneath all of this is real. Amazon, Microsoft, Alphabet, and Meta plan a combined $725 billion in capital expenditure in 2026, up sharply from roughly $410 billion the prior year, and Nvidia's data center GPUs remain the primary mechanism through which that spending flows into silicon. Data center revenue hit $62.3 billion in Q4 alone — 91% of total company sales.

Of 61 Wall Street analysts tracked by S&P Global Intelligence, 58 rate Nvidia a buy, two hold, and one sells. Consensus is rarely this lopsided. The risk is not that the company disappoints on results; it is that the results, however strong, are already in the price. Results arrive after Wednesday's close.
