Rio Tinto marks iron ore milestone with Japan while weighing copper expansion

A Rio Tinto iron ore shiploader in operation at its Pilbara facility in Western Australia — CSIRO / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 3.0)
In one sentence
Rio Tinto marks 60 years of Pilbara-to-Japan iron ore supply; the strategic bet is whether a Los Azules copper stake increase signals real commitment to the green capex cycle.

Eight billion tonnes is a number that requires context. Rio Tinto loaded the milestone cargo aboard the Juno Horizon at Cape Lambert port in Western Australia on 19 May, bound for Nippon Steel Corporation — the same Japanese steelmaker that took delivery of the first Pilbara iron ore shipment 60 years ago, in August 1966. The symmetry is deliberate and the ceremony is real: six decades of unbroken supply is not a trivial commercial relationship.

But for investors parsing Rio Tinto's capital story, the more consequential disclosure is happening several thousand miles east, in the Andes.

A different kind of tonne

Iron ore remains the engine of Rio Tinto's cash generation. Pilbara volumes run in the mid-300-million-tonnes-per-annum range — the company guides toward 345–360 mt mid-term — and the undeveloped Rhodes Ridge deposit, in which Rio holds a 50% interest, could add roughly 100 mt of annual capacity to that picture. Matthew Holcz, Rio Tinto Iron Ore chief executive, called the eight-billion-tonne mark "a testament to the generations of people who have built and sustained our operations."

That cash engine, however, is a lagging indicator. Steel demand tracks construction and industrial activity; iron ore prices tend to shadow Chinese property sentiment. Copper is the other trade — a forward proxy for electric vehicles, grid infrastructure, and the data-centre buildout that has consumed so much capital in recent years.

Los Azules: the number that matters

Rio Tinto holds a 17.2% stake in McEwen Copper's Los Azules project in Argentina's San Juan province, acquired through its Nuton copper technology venture at a cost of around $100 million. The company is now evaluating whether to increase that position, according to people familiar with the discussions — talks that Rio Tinto has declined to comment on publicly.

The project's October 2025 feasibility study provides the framework: after-tax net present value of $2.9 billion, first production targeted for 2030, and average annual output of roughly 205,000 tonnes of copper cathode in the opening five years. The capital requirement is steep — approximately $4 billion — which is why McEwen Copper is simultaneously planning a roughly $300 million IPO later this year. Stellantis holds an 18.3% stake; Nuton is field-testing its proprietary heap-leaching technology on site, the results of which will partly determine the economics of any expanded commitment.

McEwen Copper managing director Michael Meding has been direct about Rio Tinto's logic: the company "have a mandate to add copper for their production profile," he said, adding that Nuton's technology "makes so much sense" for the deposit.

Whether a stake increase materialises turns on two variables: what the Nuton leaching trials show, and how Rio Tinto's board weighs the $4 billion capital call against dividend coverage commitments — a tension analysts have already flagged. The iron ore milestone is a celebration of endurance. The McEwen Copper IPO, targeted for late 2026, will be the clearest near-term signal of whether Rio Tinto is prepared to move from a 17% position to something that begins to reshape its production mix.
