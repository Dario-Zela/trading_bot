VOO tops $59bn in inflows as investors keep rotating out of SPY

The NYSE Advanced Trading Floor — designed by Asymptote Architecture and installed in 2001 — where ETF market makers handle the bulk of S&P 500 index product flows. — Eduard Hueber / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
VOO's $59bn in 2026 inflows vs. SPY's $9bn in outflows reflects the same multi-year cost-driven shift — no signal for traders, both track the same index.

Vanguard's S&P 500 ETF has taken in $59 billion of new money so far this year, while the market's original index fund vehicle has shed close to $9 billion — a gap that grows less surprising with each quarter it repeats.

VOO now holds roughly $927 billion in assets, putting it within range of becoming the first exchange-traded fund to cross $1 trillion. State Street's SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust (SPY), which introduced the ETF category when it launched in January 1993, stands at $797 billion.

The divergence is not a performance story. Both funds track the S&P 500 — the same 500 stocks, the same weightings, near-zero basis risk between them. An investor in VOO and an investor in SPY have owned essentially identical market exposure since Vanguard listed its version in 2010. What they have not owned identically is cost.

Cost, Structure, and the Slow Grind

VOO charges 0.03% annually. SPY charges 0.09%. Three basis points: not the kind of number that commands a headline, but it compounds. On a $1 million position, the annual cost difference is roughly $600. Across the tens of millions of retail and institutional accounts that hold passive equity vehicles, it aggregates into a durable headwind for the higher-cost product.

There is a structural layer, too. SPY was established as a Unit Investment Trust — a legal form that requires dividends to be distributed as cash before reinvestment, leaving money idle between payment dates. VOO, organised as an open-end fund, reinvests dividends immediately. Over a multi-decade holding period, that difference compounds alongside the fee gap. Morningstar now rates VOO gold and SPY silver, with cost and structure cited among the primary factors.

The trend is not new. Over the past 18 months, VOO has gathered a net $196 billion. SPY has shed $20 billion in the same window. The 2026 year-to-date figures — $59 billion in versus $9 billion out — are consistent with that trajectory rather than an acceleration of it.

State Street has not ignored the pressure. Its lower-cost SPYM variant, launched at a 0.02% expense ratio — cheaper than VOO — has attracted $45 billion since early 2024 and now holds $148 billion. The same pattern is visible in Nasdaq-tracking products: Invesco's lower-cost QQQM has gathered $31 billion since January 2024 against QQQ's $24 billion over the same stretch, with QQQ carrying a 0.25% fee to QQQM's 0.15%.

For algorithmic and institutional traders, the rotation is structurally irrelevant. VOO and SPY are interchangeable in index-exposure terms, and SPY retains a meaningful liquidity premium — tighter bid-ask spreads, deeper options markets — that keeps it the preferred vehicle for short-duration and hedging flows. The outflows reflect long-term, buy-and-hold money following the lowest cost, not a shift in the underlying market.

The immediate marker to watch is VOO's path to $1 trillion in assets. At this year's pace of inflows, that threshold could arrive before December — a round number, but also a measure of how comprehensively the calculus of passive investing has shifted since Vanguard's first index fund raised a disappointing $11 million in 1976.
