easyJet shares gain 7.6% on interim earnings beat

An easyJet Airbus A320-214 on approach at Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport — Anna Zvereva / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 2.0)
In one sentence
easyJet shares gained 7.6% after H1 losses landed in line with guidance, but analysts cut full-year EPS forecasts 79% and issued no upgrades.

easyJet plc shares jumped 7.6% to close at £3.70 on 21 May after the budget carrier posted half-year results that, while showing wider losses, landed within the bounds of guidance the airline had itself issued weeks earlier. For a stock trading well below its consensus analyst target, "no worse than expected" was sufficient.

The headline numbers are not comfortable reading in isolation. easyJet recorded a pre-tax loss of £552 million for the six months to March 2026 — up from £394 million in the equivalent period a year ago, a £158 million deterioration. Headline EBIT loss widened to £533 million. Chief executive Kenton Jarvis, the former finance director who took the top job in January 2025, cited high fuel prices amplified by Middle East supply disruptions, an unexpected £25 million fuel spike in March, a £32 million increase in legal provisions, and deliberate capacity investment at new Italian bases in Milan Linate and Rome Fiumicino. The airline's load factor held at 90%, which says something for demand even if costs swallowed the yield.

The one number worth watching

easyJet Holidays — the package-travel arm the airline has been quietly building for several years — delivered a 50% jump in EBIT to £48 million, with customer volumes up 22% to 1.3 million and margin gaining a point to 9%. That figure carries more interpretive weight than the group headline: it is a diversification play that is actually working, generating cash in the seasonally weak half-year while the airline segment bleeds through winter. If the holidays unit can sustain this trajectory into summer, it materially changes the earnings mix.

The 7.6% share reaction reads as relief more than endorsement. The stock had drifted to £3.44 ahead of results — implying the market had priced in something worse. With liquidity at £4.7 billion and net cash of £434 million, easyJet is not under financial pressure; the H1 loss is seasonal by structural design, and the carrier has consistently made the bulk of its annual profit in the summer months.

Analysts hold targets, cut earnings

The analyst reaction that followed was ambiguous at best. The consensus price target was left unchanged at £4.50 — more than 20% above where the stock now trades — but the full-year 2026 earnings-per-share forecast was cut from £0.30 to £0.12, a 79% reduction. Revenue projections for the full year were held at £10.9 billion, meaning the analyst community believes aggregate demand is intact; the problem is cost pass-through to profit. No firm issued a public upgrade or broke from the existing "neutral" consensus. The price target held; the conviction behind it is harder to assess.

The next meaningful read will come from summer booking data. Jarvis flagged "increased caution" in the summer booking window on the earnings call — not a profit warning, but enough of a qualifier to contain any recovery narrative. easyJet makes nearly all its annual profit in H2; if load factors or yields soften from current levels, the full-year EPS consensus of £0.12 looks exposed. The September full-year results will be the cleaner verdict on whether this week's bounce was earned or simply a reset from oversold.
