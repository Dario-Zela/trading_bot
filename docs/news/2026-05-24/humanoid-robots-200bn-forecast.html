Humanoid robots: a $200bn market by 2035, with many caveats

In one sentence
Barclays projects a $200bn humanoid robot market by 2035, but analyst estimates range from $30bn to $200bn — a gap that reflects unsettled questions about battery life, actuator supply, and software reliability.

As of early 2026, humanoid robots have moved from promotional demo reels to factory production lines. Figure AI's Figure 02 spent ten months at BMW's Spartanburg, South Carolina plant, contributing to more than 30,000 SUVs. Boston Dynamics unveiled its first commercial Atlas at CES in January, with all 2026 deployments already committed to Hyundai's new Metaplant near Savannah, Georgia, and to Google DeepMind. Against that backdrop, Barclays Research has attached a number to where this market eventually goes: $200 billion globally by 2035.

That number is worth parsing carefully before it gets treated as a forecast rather than a ceiling.

A projection, or five?

The spread in analyst estimates for humanoid robots at the end of the decade tells you as much as any individual figure. Goldman Sachs puts the 2035 market at $38 billion. IDTechEx, in a report published this month, lands at roughly $29.5 billion by 2036 with around 1.8 million annual unit shipments. Morgan Stanley has projected a $5 trillion opportunity by 2050, a timeline long enough to cover for any number of near-term obstacles. Barclays, at $200 billion, sits at the optimistic end of its own range — the report makes clear this is a bull-case scenario, not a central estimate.

The divergence reflects genuine uncertainty about two interdependent variables: whether production costs fall fast enough to trigger mass-market adoption, and whether software matures enough to make humanoids reliably useful outside of curated settings.

On costs, the directional trend is unambiguous. IDTechEx tracks average selling prices falling from $114,700 in 2024 to roughly $37,000 by 2030 — a 68% decline in six years. Tesla is targeting an eventual $20,000-$30,000 price point for Optimus at mass-production volumes. But the current market is still small: approximately 2,000 units were deployed globally in 2024, rising to an estimated 15,000 in 2025. Projections for 2026 range to 60,000 units — enough to feel like momentum, not enough to anchor a $200 billion valuation.

The real bottleneck is not the price tag

Analysts broadly agree that hardware costs are falling. Actuators — the motorised joints that give a robot its physical range — account for roughly half of humanoid production costs, and that figure is declining as order volumes rise. Barclays frames the technology challenge through what it calls the "three Bs": brains (AI software), brawn (actuators and mechanics), and batteries.

Batteries remain the most operationally visible constraint. Agility Robotics' Digit, deployed in Amazon warehouses, operates in 30-minute intervals with a 90-minute recharge cycle. That cycle time makes shift-based industrial scheduling difficult, and no AI improvement changes the underlying electrochemistry.

The software problem is subtler but, by some assessments, larger. Humanoid systems perform well in controlled demonstrations — the choreographed product video has become its own marketing genre — but struggle with what engineers describe as long-horizon task chains: sequences of actions involving judgment calls in unpredictable environments. The feedback loop is circular: better software requires operational data, and operational data requires deployment at scale. That loop closes slowly.

IDTechEx puts the challenge plainly: "the hardware cost problem is largely solved." The decisive variable now is whether software capability can meet the hardware, and whether enterprise buyers hold on long enough for it to happen.

China is moving fastest. Domestic manufacturers — Unitree shipped more than 5,500 units in 2025 and is targeting 10,000-20,000 in 2026 — account for roughly 85% of global humanoid deployments. Chinese government policy has explicitly targeted a full-stack domestic ecosystem, from actuators to AI frameworks. Western players have superior brand visibility and, in some cases, more capable hardware; the supply-chain geography is shifting regardless.

What the next twelve months will clarify

A handful of concrete milestones will sharpen the picture. Tesla has pushed volume production of Optimus at its Fremont facility to late July or August 2026, after declining to set a new annual unit target on its Q1 2026 earnings call — the company missed its stated goal of 10,000 units for 2025. The figure that emerges from Fremont this year will either validate the optimistic adoption curves or force a reset.

For the supply chain, the key variable is actuator production capacity, particularly harmonic drives and dexterous hand components that remain expensive and fragile at current volumes. Barclays notes that European manufacturers hold a structural advantage in precision engineering relevant to actuators — a supply-chain angle that has received little attention relative to the AI narrative.

The gap between Goldman's $38 billion and Barclays' $200 billion, both projecting to the same year, will narrow as deployment data accumulates. The software question — whether robots can reliably execute long, multi-step tasks outside demonstration conditions — is where the answer will come from.
