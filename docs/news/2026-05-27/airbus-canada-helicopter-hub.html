Airbus eyes Canadian helicopter hub as NATO spending rises

An Airbus H175M at the 2023 Royal International Air Tattoo — the type Airbus is pitching for Canada's nTACS competition. — Tim Felce (Airwolfhound) / Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 2.0
In one sentence
Airbus will build helicopters in Canada for global export if it wins nTACS and two other pending government contracts — a conditional bet on Ottawa's $63bn defence surge.

Airbus is positioning itself for a significant expansion of its Canadian manufacturing footprint, contingent on winning a string of upcoming defense contracts — the latest sign of how rising NATO budgets are redirecting aerospace capital.

The European aerospace group has indicated it could open a helicopter final-assembly line in Canada if it wins one or more of three pending government procurements: a Canadian Armed Forces tactical helicopter replacement under the Next Tactical Aviation Capability Set (nTACS) programme, a Canadian Coast Guard polar helicopter contract, and a separate order for the Royal Canadian Mounted Police. The nTACS requirement alone covers roughly 80 aircraft, to replace the aging Bell CH-146 Griffon fleet.

Airbus Helicopters EVP for Global Business Olivier Michalon framed the logic plainly: "if Airbus helicopters are selected for any of the big upcoming campaigns and there is an industrial project which is tied to this contract, it's an opportunity to export what would be manufactured here to the worldwide market." The premise is straightforward — a Canadian line would serve not just Ottawa's orders but Airbus's broader export roster of roughly 170 countries.

The platform Airbus is promoting for nTACS is the H175M, a seven-tonne super-medium twin whose military variant received Transport Canada type certification in February 2026. The programme's supply chain has also been restructured: Airbus confirmed it has removed Chinese aerostructures from the H175M, replacing them with European sources — a decision that aligns with NATO allies' growing emphasis on supply-chain security. The H175's engines are already produced by Pratt & Whitney Canada, giving the platform a domestic-content argument that Ottawa's procurement rules now favour.

The $63-billion backdrop

The timing is not coincidental. Canada hit NATO's benchmark of 2% of GDP in defence spending during the 2025–26 fiscal year — for the first time since roughly 1990 — reaching $63 billion annually, a 65% increase from 2014 levels. Prime Minister Mark Carney has made military sovereignty and reduced reliance on U.S. suppliers a central plank of his government's industrial strategy.

Airbus CEO Guillaume Faury visited Ottawa in April, meeting senior lawmakers. "Canada is now at a turning point moment," Faury told reporters afterward. He said he came away believing Airbus could be "a very good partner" and spoke of writing a "long-term roadmap" with Ottawa. Airbus already employs more than 5,000 people in Canada, sources roughly C$2 billion annually from more than 850 domestic suppliers, and maintains facilities in Fort Erie, Ontario (helicopters), Mirabel, Quebec (A220 commercial jets), and Ottawa (defence and space).

The deal flow has already begun moving. A $3.6-billion contract for A330 tanker-transport aircraft was awarded in 2023; a $1.5-billion support contract followed this year. Nineteen H135 training helicopters — the FAcT programme — marked the first time Canadian forces will operate Airbus rotorcraft.

The helicopter-hub pitch, though, remains conditional. No contracts have been signed, no production volumes committed, no assembly-line investments announced. What Airbus has done is establish industrial readiness and political positioning ahead of procurement decisions Ottawa has yet to finalise. Whether that translates into an expanded Fort Erie line or simply another bidder's unexercised option will depend on outcomes expected to clarify before year-end.
