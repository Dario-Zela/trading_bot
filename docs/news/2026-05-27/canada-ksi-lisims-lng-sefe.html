Canada set to sign Ksi Lisims LNG deal with Germany's SEFE

Nass Bay viewed from near Gingolx, B.C. — the coastal waters where the Ksi Lisims LNG terminal is planned — miguelphotobooth / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 2.0)
In one sentence
Germany's ex-Gazprom firm SEFE agrees to buy 1Mt/yr of LNG from Canada's Ksi Lisims for 20 years — a third offtake deal pushing the $10bn project toward its final investment decision.

Germany's state energy company SEFE is set to sign a long-term agreement to buy liquefied natural gas from Ksi Lisims, a planned British Columbia export terminal, marking the facility's third major offtake commitment and edging it closer to the final investment decision its backers have been working toward for years.

The deal, set to be announced in Vancouver by Natural Resources Minister Tim Hodgson, would have SEFE take one million tonnes of LNG per year for up to 20 years, with shipments starting in the early 2030s. Shell and TotalEnergies have each already committed to two million tonnes annually under separate 20-year agreements, meaning the three contracts together would cover roughly five million tonnes — slightly under half of the facility's planned 12-million-tonne annual capacity.

SEFE — short for Securing Energy for Europe — carries history that makes the agreement more than a routine procurement. The company began life as Gazprom Germania GmbH, a wholly owned subsidiary of Russia's state gas giant, until Berlin seized it in April 2022, weeks after Russia's invasion of Ukraine disrupted the continent's energy supply chains. Germany nationalized the company outright that December, injecting €6.3 billion in capital to stabilize a firm that supplies roughly 200 terawatt-hours of gas annually across nine European markets. The company remains state-owned today, with privatization expected by 2028.

That lineage makes the Ksi Lisims deal legible as strategic infrastructure, not just a commodity purchase. Rather than replacing one Russian dependency with another, SEFE has been diversifying its LNG sourcing across multiple geographies — it holds agreements with Venture Global in the United States, Southern Energy in Argentina, and BOTAS in Turkey. A long-term Canadian supply line adds another spoke to that wheel.

Toward a Final Investment Decision

The facility would be built on Pearse Island in the Portland Canal, on Nisga'a treaty land about 15 kilometres west of Gingolx. At 12 million tonnes per year, Ksi Lisims would be Canada's second-largest LNG export terminal after LNG Canada in Kitimat, which produced its first cargo in June 2025. The project is backed by the Nisga'a Nation, Houston-based Western LNG, and Rockies LNG, a consortium of western Canadian natural gas producers. Unlike LNG Canada's fixed onshore plant, Ksi Lisims plans to use floating liquefaction platforms fabricated in South Korea — a design that limits land disturbance but adds supply-chain complexity.

B.C. Premier David Eby described the reported deal as "a key step toward the partners deciding to go ahead," and said the provincial government looks forward to celebrating it alongside the Nisga'a. The project received an environmental assessment certificate from B.C. in September 2025, and a positive federal decision statement followed. The expected final investment decision — still targeted for 2026 — is what converts signed offtake contracts into committed capital.

Two legal challenges remain: the Lax Kw'alaams Band and the Metlakatla First Nation, neither of which has granted consent, are contesting the project in court. SEFE's agreement reportedly includes flexibility to route cargos globally rather than exclusively toward Europe — a structuring choice that acknowledges both the fungible nature of LNG markets and the possibility that European demand patterns shift over a 20-year term. The signing is the political moment; the final investment decision is when steel is ordered.
