Starlink wins American Airlines as SpaceX's IPO case sharpens—and divides analysts

An American Airlines Airbus A321neo — the narrowbody type set to receive Starlink installation from Q1 2027 — Joseph E. Zadeh / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
American Airlines is putting Starlink on 500+ Airbus planes starting 2027, handing SpaceX a marquee enterprise win days before its expected $2 trillion IPO roadshow.

American Airlines passengers flying Airbus narrowbodies will soon be able to stream video and join video calls at 35,000 feet without cursing the buffering wheel. The airline announced Tuesday it has chosen SpaceX's Starlink to equip more than 500 of its Airbus A321neo and A321XLR aircraft with satellite-powered Wi-Fi, with installation set to begin in the first quarter of 2027. The deal is the latest and largest marker in Starlink's methodical takeover of commercial aviation connectivity — and it lands at a moment of maximum strategic importance for SpaceX, whose IPO roadshow is expected to kick off in the coming days.

The aviation play: Viasat's market evaporates

For years, in-flight Wi-Fi was a grudging afterthought — slow, expensive, and reliably maddening. The technology was dominated by operators like Viasat and Gogo, whose geostationary satellites sit roughly 22,000 miles above Earth, a distance that imposes hard physical limits on latency and throughput. Starlink's low-Earth orbit constellation — circling at around 340 miles up — collapses those limits. SpaceX says Starlink can deliver up to 1 Gbps per antenna, enough to support streaming, gaming, and live video conferencing simultaneously aboard a full aircraft.

The American Airlines announcement accelerates an industry shift that is now unmistakably one-directional. United Airlines displaced geostationary operators for Starlink across its fleet. Southwest came next. Alaska, Hawaiian, Qatar Airways, British Airways, and Lufthansa Group — which announced in 2026 a deal to retrofit its entire fleet of more than 850 aircraft — have all committed. The carrier roster now spans nearly every major global airline, and Viasat, which listed commercial aviation as a core revenue stream, faces the prospect of contract losses it may struggle to offset elsewhere.

American's deal is specifically for its Airbus narrowbody fleet. Its Boeing aircraft — a mix of 737 and 787 variants — remain under Viasat and Panasonic contracts for now. That carve-out matters: it tells you something about the complexity and cost of fleet-wide retrofit programmes, and it means American is hedging while it absorbs the logistics of transitioning 500 planes. The airline confirmed it plans to offer Starlink service free of charge, following the industry's shift toward bundled connectivity as a competitive differentiator in premium cabins.

"The addition of Starlink solidifies American as a leading airline in keeping passengers connected in flight," said Heather Garboden, the airline's Chief Customer Officer. Jason Fritch, VP of Starlink Enterprise Sales, described the service as enabling "a fully connected experience gate to gate."

The IPO case: revenue depth at the right moment

The timing of the American Airlines announcement was not accidental. SpaceX filed its S-1 prospectus with the SEC on May 20, 2026, targeting a Nasdaq listing under the ticker SPCX with a roadshow expected in late May and a trading debut currently pencilled in for June 12. The valuation range being discussed runs from $1.75 trillion to $2 trillion, which would make it among the largest IPOs ever attempted.

Starlink is the financial core of that pitch. While SpaceX's rockets — the Falcon 9, Falcon Heavy, and Starship development programme — capture the headlines, Starlink is where the company's revenue actually accumulates. Enterprise contracts with airlines, cruise operators, and maritime carriers represent the high-margin, long-tenure business that institutional investors want to see in a company asking for a multi-trillion-dollar valuation. The American Airlines deal, announced four days before the expected start of the roadshow, is essentially a late-stage slide in a prospectus — a proof point that the satellite internet division can land and retain legacy enterprise customers at scale.

The index question: fast pass or fair process?

The IPO itself has already sparked a secondary debate about the architecture of financial indices. S&P Dow Jones Indices is weighing rule changes that would allow megacap newly-listed companies to join the S&P 500 on an accelerated timeline. Under current rules, SpaceX would fail three of five inclusion criteria at the moment of listing: it would not have twelve months of public trading history, its public float (with Elon Musk holding approximately 42% of shares) would fall below the 50% threshold, and it would not meet the GAAP profitability requirement — last-quarter positive earnings plus a positive trailing four-quarter sum.

The proposed revisions would reduce the trading-history requirement from twelve months to six for megacap companies, waive the float requirement for companies with market caps above $200 billion, and eliminate the profitability screen for companies that would rank in the top 100 of the S&P Total Market Index. Critics argue that the changes are written around a single company.

"There should be no fast pass to index inclusion, full stop," said Jay Woods, Chief Market Strategist at Freedom Capital Markets, who has spent more than 25 years on Wall Street. Woods contends the proposed modifications constitute "a fundamental rewrite of what the S&P 500 has always stood for," not a minor procedural adjustment. The practical stakes are significant: Bloomberg Intelligence estimates that S&P 500 index funds alone could be forced to acquire 19% of SpaceX's available float within six months of inclusion — a volume of forced buying that critics say would inflate the share price independent of underlying performance.

The counter-argument from index providers is straightforward: indices that exclude the largest companies by market cap lose their claim to representing the actual market, and institutional investors tracking the S&P 500 are already exposed to index drift. Neither argument is wrong. The question is whether S&P Dow Jones rewrites decades-old inclusion criteria in visible proximity to a single high-profile listing — and whether that precedent holds when the next megacap arrives at the gates.

What to watch

Three things will determine whether this story changes shape in the months ahead. First, American Airlines' Boeing fleet decision: a follow-on Starlink deal for its 737 and widebody aircraft would confirm a full-carrier commitment and represent a deeper hit to Viasat's revenue. Second, the SpaceX IPO pricing and opening trade on the expected June 12 date — a weak debut would complicate the valuation narrative that Starlink deals like this one are meant to reinforce. Third, S&P Dow Jones Indices' final ruling on the fast-track inclusion proposal, which will set the template for how OpenAI, Anthropic, and whichever private unicorn follows SpaceX through the public markets will be treated when they arrive.
