Majors bet on Alaska as Middle East supply risk persists

Aerial view of oilfield facilities at Prudhoe Bay on Alaska's North Slope, the backbone of the state's existing production infrastructure. — U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Record bids at Alaska's May lease sale signal a major-company shift toward the North Slope as a supply hedge — but key projects don't deliver barrels until 2026–2029.

A government lease sale last month attracted a record $163 million in bids from Exxon, Shell, ConocoPhillips, Santos, and Repsol — the strongest signal yet that major producers are treating Alaska's North Slope as a long-term hedge against Middle East instability, not merely an opportunistic play.

The auction covered 625 tracts across 5.5 million acres in the National Petroleum Reserve. It came as the ongoing Middle East conflict has removed an estimated 8 to 10 million barrels per day from global supply and disrupted roughly 20 percent of world LNG trade. With Strait of Hormuz risk reshaping how buyers and producers price supply security, geographically remote but politically stable basins have acquired a durable premium.

The numbers behind the optimism

Alaska's case as a supply alternative rests on two anchor projects. ConocoPhillips' Willow development — roughly 50 percent complete and now projected to cost between $8.5 billion and $9 billion, up from initial guidance of $7 billion to $7.5 billion — is on track for first oil in early 2029, with peak output around 180,000 barrels per day. Capital spending runs at roughly $1.7 billion annually through 2028 before falling to approximately $500 million a year once production begins.

Santos' Pikka project is the nearer-term addition. The company launched commercial production recently and expects to reach a plateau of 80,000 barrels per day by the third quarter of this year.

The return of Exxon and Shell to Alaska after a hiatus of nearly a decade adds further weight. The Trump administration's loosening of environmental permitting and expansion of lease territory provided the regulatory opening; the Middle East crisis provided the commercial logic. ConocoPhillips CEO Ryan Lance described the moment as "back to the future," adding: "When you think about strategic importance of where conventional oil comes from, people return to places like Alaska."

Alaska currently produces below 600,000 barrels per day, down from a peak of roughly 2 million two decades ago. The new investment, if it runs on schedule, would arrest that decline — but not reverse it dramatically in the near term. Willow's 180,000 bpd represents a meaningful increment; it arrives in 2029, three years from now, and markets are pricing supply risk today. Pikka's 80,000 bpd is real supply arriving soon, but modest against the scale of what the Middle East conflict has taken offline.

That gap between capital commitment and production timeline is the central tension. Santos VP Bruce Dingeman described the lease sale as showing "confidence in geology and responsible regulatory development" — a statement about long-term fundamentals, not near-term relief. A $40 billion Alaska LNG export project is separately pursuing final investment decisions in late 2026 and early 2027, pushing first LNG shipments further still.

The proximate catalysts to watch: whether Pikka reaches its 80,000 bpd plateau by September, and whether ConocoPhillips' $1.7 billion in annual Willow spending keeps the 2029 first-oil date intact. A schedule slip at either project would test how much of Alaska's supply premium is already built into current valuations.
