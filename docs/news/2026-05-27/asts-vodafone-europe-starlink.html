Europe's push against Starlink draws attention to AST SpaceMobile's Vodafone venture

A communications satellite in low Earth orbit — the arena where European regulators are now drawing jurisdictional lines. — Cesarhenriquebrandao / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
The EU's plan to ringfence 66% of key satellite spectrum for European firms sent AST SpaceMobile stock up 13%, but its Vodafone joint venture's commercial timeline remains unspecified.

The European Commission was set to adopt a decision this week reserving two-thirds of the EU's 2 GHz mobile-satellite spectrum for European operators — a move explicitly designed to limit expansion by Starlink and Amazon's Project Kuiper. The announcement landed neatly alongside the Commission's forthcoming Tech Sovereignty Package, and traders found a ready beneficiary: AST SpaceMobile, whose shares surged 13% on Tuesday, closing at $119.70, with retail message volume on Stocktwits spiking 744% in 24 hours.

The specific mechanics matter. The 2 GHz band — the only radio frequency harmonized at the EU level for mobile satellite services — has sat with two US-listed incumbents, Viasat and EchoStar, since 2009. Those licenses expire in May 2027. The Commission's decision would reserve 66% of the band for firms registered in the EU, UK, or Norway when the spectrum is reopened for direct-to-device use — the technology that lets a standard smartphone connect directly to a satellite without specialized hardware. Starlink and Kuiper would compete for the remaining third. Named beneficiaries include SES, Eutelsat, Hispasat, and the SpaceRISE consortium building the IRIS2 constellation, which targets governmental services by 2030.

Thomas Regnier, the Commission's spokesperson for tech sovereignty, framed the rationale plainly: "Satellite connectivity is a key piece of our technological sovereignty, our security, and our defence." The FCC's Brendan Carr was rather less warm. "If Europe insists on going down a path of satellite sovereignty that excludes providers that are not based in the continent," he said, "the US will have to be taking that into account."

A joint venture in search of a timeline

Where AST SpaceMobile enters the picture is through Satellite Connect Europe — a joint venture with Vodafone, headquartered in Luxembourg, that formally launched on 27 February 2026. The entity is structured to keep network control and customer data within European jurisdiction, with ground stations under construction in Spain and the UK and three further sites being finalized. A satellite operations center, focused on encryption and command, is based in Germany. Mobile network operators in 21 EU member states have reportedly expressed interest in the wholesale direct-to-device service.

What remains opaque is the business case. The ownership split between AST SpaceMobile and Vodafone has not been disclosed. A commercial launch is described as "2026," with no tighter guidance. The company's satellite pipeline — BlueBirds 8, 9 and 10 — targets a mid-June launch, and AST has said it aims for 45 satellite deployments this year. None of that is specific to the European venture or its spectrum positioning.

The stock's roughly 400% rise over the past year means Tuesday's 13% move tells you more about retail sentiment than the venture's readiness. Satellite Connect Europe is real — its path from ground-station construction to meaningful revenue under a spectrum regime that currently favors larger, established European operators is not.

The clearer catalysts to watch: the mid-June BlueBird launch, any commercial service announcement from Satellite Connect Europe, and how the Commission's allocation shapes the competitive field when Viasat and EchoStar's licenses lapse in May 2027.
