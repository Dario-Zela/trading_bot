USTR signals a permanent tariff premium on China and escalating pressure on Mexico

US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer, whose remarks at the Council on Foreign Relations on May 26 confirmed the permanence of elevated China tariffs — United States Trade Representative / Wikimedia Commons (public domain)
In one sentence
USTR Greer declared at CFR that US tariffs on China will permanently exceed those on other nations — structural separation, not a negotiating phase — while tying Mexico tariffs to deficit resolution.

On Tuesday, at the Harold Pratt House on Manhattan's Upper East Side — the stately home of the Council on Foreign Relations since 1945 — US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer delivered what amounts to a formal declaration of the new trade order. The setting was fitting: an institution built for grand strategic thinking, hosting a speech that closed off, rather than opened up, a generation's worth of liberalisation assumptions.

Greer's remarks, spanning China tariffs, managed trade, and Mexico's ballooning deficit, did not announce a policy shift so much as confirm one that has been accumulating for years. The message, stripped of diplomatic texture, was this: the tariff wall around Chinese goods is permanent, Washington has stopped trying to change Beijing's economic model from the outside, and the hemisphere is being reorganised around US content requirements. Companies and investors still pricing in a normalization path will need updated assumptions.

In one sentence: USTR Greer confirmed at CFR that US tariffs on Chinese goods will permanently exceed those on other nations — structural separation, not a negotiating phase.

A permanent floor, not a ceiling

The most consequential line of Greer's remarks arrived almost as an aside. Asked whether reduced tariffs might coax multinationals into rebuilding supply chains through China, he was direct: lower duties on roughly $30 billion in Chinese goods — the figure earmarked under the US-China "Board of Trade" framework — will not swing supply chains back. And, he added, US tariffs on Chinese goods will likely always be higher than tariffs on goods from other countries.

That formulation deserves a pause. Not currently higher, not elevated until conditions improve — likely always higher. For businesses that have treated the tariff premium on China as a cost to be managed until political winds shift, the USTR has now signalled that wind is not coming.

The $30 billion figure is real but deliberately narrow. Total US imports from China run roughly $450 billion annually; tariff modifications on 6-7% of that trade is a procedural acknowledgement, not a strategic reversal. To formalise even those limited adjustments, the USTR will issue a Federal Register notice soliciting public comment under Section 301 — the standard mechanism for any proposed tariff changes. The comment window, when published, will be the first concrete signal of which sectors are actively lobbying for relief.

From confrontation to containment

The more consequential admission was about what Washington has given up. Since 2018, the official American rationale for tariffs was never purely about trade balances — it was about leveraging China into structural reform: away from state subsidies, forced technology transfer, and currency management that systematically undercuts foreign competitors. That project, Greer acknowledged at CFR, is "mostly" over.

In its place: managed trade. The administration now accepts China's economic model as a fixed variable and is negotiating which specific goods can flow between the two countries at agreed volumes. The Board of Trade mechanism, emerging from Paris talks earlier this year between Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Chinese Vice Premier He Lifeng, formalises roughly $30-40 billion in mutually acceptable non-strategic trade, with both sides retaining high tariffs on everything else. One analyst watching the talks described the resulting structure as a "tariff canyon" — deep, settled, and not expected to drain.

Greer's characterisation of the Trump-Xi summit outcome was revealing in its minimalism: the US "got what we came for" — "stability and continued tariffs." Not market access pledges. Not reform commitments. Not liberalisation benchmarks. Stability, and preservation of the existing tariff architecture. That China "expects a certain level of US tariffs that we agreed to" is, in this framing, not a concession — it is the settlement.

For investors, this consolidation cuts both ways. Confirmation that Washington has formally accepted Beijing's economic structure reduces one species of uncertainty — the fear that tariffs might escalate further without warning — but replaces it with another. Managed trade is stable until it isn't, and the governance mechanisms are still being constructed.

Mexico, USMCA, and the hemisphere

If China policy is one of structured containment, the approach to the Western Hemisphere is more overtly transactional. Greer was explicit: tariffs on Mexican goods will remain "as long as we have a giant trade deficit." Last year the US goods deficit with Mexico rose nearly 15% to $196.9 billion — even as overall US trade deficits declined over 30%. The administration treats that as structural imbalance, not comparative advantage at work.

USMCA renegotiation will target rules of origin — the provisions governing how much content must originate within North America to qualify for preferential treatment. The administration wants those rules tightened in ways that shift production toward the United States specifically. Formal bilateral talks with Mexico began this week in Mexico City.

One number stands out in the broader tariff landscape: only Canada and China have formally retaliated against Trump administration tariffs. That narrower-than-expected coalition is partly explained by backroom deal-making — but it also reflects leverage. For Canada, Greer described problems "extending well beyond typical trade disagreements," and the administration's posture is bilateral negotiation rather than trilateral accommodation.

What to watch

The Federal Register notice will be the first procedural signal. Automotive, consumer electronics, and specialty chemicals are likely to dominate the comment filings, each with a different calculation about whether tariff relief on a narrow slice of Chinese inputs serves their interests or merely disadvantages domestic competitors who restructured supply chains at considerable cost.

For USMCA, the 2026 joint review has a genuine deadline, and rules-of-origin changes in the automotive sector would directly affect cross-border supply chains that took years to optimise. Any signal that US content thresholds are being ratcheted upward will move capital allocation decisions quickly.

The broader implication of Greer's CFR remarks is policy consolidation after years of tariff volatility: a permanently elevated floor on China, a managed-trade framework that accepts rather than challenges Beijing's economic structure, and explicit deficit conditionality on Mexico. That is not the end of trade tension — it is the formalisation of a new baseline. The question for companies now is not whether conditions will revert; it is how to operate efficiently under conditions that won't.
