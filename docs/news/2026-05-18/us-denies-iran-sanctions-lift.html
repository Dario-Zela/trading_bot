US official denies Iranian state media claim that oil sanctions are being lifted during talks

The Abadan oil refinery in southwestern Iran, the country's largest and one of the world's oldest. — Unknown photographer / Wikimedia Commons (public domain)
In one sentence
A U.S. official told CNBC the Iranian state media report that Washington agreed to lift oil sanctions during talks is false, deflating a brief oil-market spike.

A U.S. official pushed back on Sunday against a claim from Iranian state media that Washington had agreed to lift oil sanctions while nuclear talks are ongoing — a denial that arrived in time to prevent traders from building positions on what appears to have been a mischaracterisation of the American negotiating stance.

The denial was relayed by CNBC correspondent Megan Cassella, who reported that the official described the Iranian state media account as false.

What Tasnim reported

The original claim came from Tasnim News Agency, one of Iran's semi-official news outlets. Citing a source described as close to Iran's negotiating delegation, Tasnim reported that "unlike its previous texts, the Americans had accepted in the new text to waive Iran's oil sanctions during the negotiation period." Crucially, the agency's own source conceded this meant a temporary waiver — specifically an Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) suspension until a final agreement is reached — rather than the permanent, comprehensive sanctions removal Tehran has demanded throughout the talks.

The distinction matters. Iran's standing position is that the U.S. must remove all sanctions permanently. An interim OFAC waiver — assuming it were ever on the table — would be a far narrower concession. The U.S. official's flat denial suggests it wasn't on the table at all.

The market moment

Oil prices were already elevated when the Tasnim report circulated. Brent crude had climbed to around $112 a barrel — a two-week high — driven partly by a separate development: a drone strike on a UAE nuclear facility that heightened regional risk perceptions. WTI touched $108.70 before the Tasnim report introduced an additional upward impulse, the prospect that Iranian barrels might return to the market with U.S. blessing.

After the US denial landed, both benchmarks pulled back. Brent fell roughly 1.4% to around $107.78 and WTI dropped nearly 1.8% to $103.52. The sequence illustrated a familiar dynamic in commodity markets: geopolitical headlines can whipsaw prices faster than facts can travel, and a well-timed official response can contain the damage.

Where the talks stand

Sunday's denial lands in the middle of a negotiating process that is simultaneously active and deeply stuck. Talks between Washington and Tehran are being mediated by Pakistan, and the two sides are trading draft texts on a framework that would address Iran's nuclear programme, the status of the Strait of Hormuz, and the lifting of sanctions — in some form, on some timeline.

The White House has already publicly rejected Iran's most recent updated proposal as insufficient. Iran's President Pezeshkian, for his part, has been careful to frame the negotiations as dialogue rather than capitulation, telling audiences domestically that concessions are not the same as surrender. Trump, meanwhile, delayed a planned military strike against Iran after intervention by Saudi, UAE, and Qatari leaders — though the administration has made clear its military options remain on the table.

The U.S. has also been tightening enforcement in parallel: the Treasury sanctioned 12 individuals and entities over Iranian oil sales to China just last week, and the State Department has separately warned banks about exposure to Chinese refineries processing Iranian crude through Malaysian intermediaries. Issuing an OFAC waiver in that environment — and doing so quietly, without announcement, as a goodwill gesture mid-negotiation — would represent a sharp reversal of posture that the denial makes clear has not occurred.

What to watch

The episode underscores how sensitive oil markets remain to anything that touches the Iranian supply question. With Brent holding above $100 and the Strait of Hormuz status unresolved, even unconfirmed reports can produce intraday moves of several percent. The next meaningful signal will likely come from Pakistan, which is hosting and mediating the current round of talks, or from any public statement by the two sides about whether a framework document — the so-called one-page memo discussed in earlier sessions — is any closer to being agreed.
