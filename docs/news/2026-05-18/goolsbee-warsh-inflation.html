Goolsbee: inflation must stay front of mind as Warsh takes the Fed chair

Kevin Warsh, incoming Federal Reserve chair, to be sworn in at the White House on Friday — Federal Reserve / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Goolsbee used a Fox Business appearance to warn that inflation — running at 3.8% — must stay the Fed's top priority as Warsh is sworn in Friday.

On the morning Kevin Warsh was scheduled to be sworn in as the next chair of the Federal Reserve, Chicago Fed President Austan Goolsbee went on Fox Business to deliver a pointed reminder: the inflation fight is not over, and the incoming leader needs to keep it front of mind.

The timing was not incidental. Warsh takes the oath at the White House on Friday — a venue that itself signals a departure from recent tradition, where swearing-in ceremonies have been held at the Fed's boardroom, without the president in attendance. The Senate confirmed Warsh earlier this month on a 54-45 vote, the slimmest margin in the modern era for a Fed chair, with only one Democrat — Pennsylvania's John Fetterman — crossing the aisle.

Goolsbee's message was not a rebuke. He has said separately that he is "excited" for Warsh's arrival and glad that outgoing Chair Jerome Powell will remain available. But the comments underscore a real constraint: whoever takes the chair inherits an inflation problem that has resisted resolution for years.

The numbers are uncomfortable

U.S. annual inflation stands at 3.8%, its highest reading in nearly three years, driven by a confluence of tariff pressures, an oil price spike tied to Middle East tensions, and — more troubling to Goolsbee — services inflation that was rising before any of those factors entered the picture. "Inflation hasn't been great, and it's been going the wrong way lately," Goolsbee said in recent remarks, noting that the deterioration extends well beyond energy and tariff-sensitive categories. The Fed's preferred gauge, the PCE index, was running at a 3.5% annual rate in March — well above the 2% target.

The job market, by contrast, has held together. Goolsbee described it as "stable without being good" — not collapsing, but not robust. April employment data showed no significant deterioration. "There's not a lot of evidence the job market is falling apart," Goolsbee said.

That stable-labor, rising-prices combination is precisely the environment that limits the Fed's room to cut. It forecloses the easy argument — that softening employment warrants rate relief — and forces the committee to weigh inflation on its own merits.

A debate already forming

Goolsbee's inflation-first framing also foreshadows a substantive argument with Warsh over how to think about the price outlook.

Warsh has argued that a wave of AI-driven productivity gains could prove disinflationary, providing analytical cover to lower rates without fueling prices. He is also skeptical of the Fed's rigid 2% point target — arguing that inflation measurement lacks the precision to justify such specificity and that committing to a fixed number has led central banks to overstimulate in the past. During his confirmation process, Warsh said the Fed needs a "different framework" and that its preferred gauge offers only a rough take.

Goolsbee has cautioned explicitly against "front-running productivity growth" — treating disinflationary tailwinds as real before they show up in the data. That tension, between Warsh's structural optimism about AI and Goolsbee's insistence on what the numbers actually say, is likely to define the early months of the new chairmanship.

Political backdrop

The political context adds pressure from another direction. President Trump, who nominated Warsh after a prolonged pressure campaign against Powell, has made no secret of his expectation that rates will fall. The confirmation process was turbulent: a Justice Department investigation of the Fed delayed proceedings, and Republican Sen. Thom Tillis of North Carolina initially blocked the committee from advancing Warsh's nomination — dropping his opposition only after prosecutors agreed to drop the probe.

Warsh would have to persuade a majority of the Fed's 12-member policy committee that cutting rates would not risk reigniting prices. With inflation at 3.8% and moving in the wrong direction, that argument is harder to make on day one.

What to watch

Warsh will chair his first Federal Open Market Committee meeting with inflation still running well above target, oil above $100 a barrel, and a labor market that is holding but not accelerating. The next core PCE reading arrives before the June FOMC meeting and will be the first real test of whether the trajectory is bending. If it isn't, Goolsbee's Fox Business warning — delivered on the morning of the swearing-in — will look less like a courtesy reminder and more like an early marker in a policy debate that is only beginning.
