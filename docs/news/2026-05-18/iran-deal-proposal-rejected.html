White House rejects Iran's updated nuclear proposal as insufficient

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi, who has led Tehran's side of the negotiations with the United States. — Iranian government / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Iran's latest peace proposal was dismissed by the White House as insufficient on nuclear specifics, keeping the ceasefire fragile and military options on the table.

Iran delivered an updated proposal Sunday night — relayed through Pakistani intermediaries — for a deal to end the ongoing war with the United States. By Monday, the White House had concluded it wasn't worth much.

A senior U.S. official told Axios the offer was "not a meaningful improvement" over earlier drafts and fell well short of what Washington needs to reach an agreement. "We are really not making a lot of progress," the official said. "We are at a very serious place today."

What Iran put forward — and what it left out

The updated proposal added language reaffirming Iran's commitment not to pursue a nuclear weapon, but contained no concrete steps on the two issues Washington has made central to any deal: suspension of uranium enrichment and surrender of Iran's existing stockpile of highly enriched uranium. Tehran holds an estimated 460 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60% — material that, under the right conditions, could be processed into weapons-grade fuel.

U.S. officials have sought a moratorium on enrichment lasting at least a decade, full dismantlement of key nuclear sites including Fordow and Natanz, and transfer of the enriched stockpile to a third country. Iran has rejected each of these demands outright, insisting enrichment is a sovereign right and the facilities are not for negotiation.

A ceasefire already under strain

The rejection lands at a perilous moment. The war itself began February 28, when the United States and Israel launched strikes on Iran following the collapse of earlier talks. A fragile ceasefire took effect April 7, but it has never held cleanly — both sides have exchanged fire in the Strait of Hormuz since it came into effect.

Less than two weeks ago, Trump publicly declared the ceasefire "on massive life support" after rejecting a separate Iranian counterproposal, calling it "totally unacceptable." Sunday's updated offer was Iran's attempt to revive the framework; Washington's verdict is that it moved the needle barely at all.

Trump is expected to convene his national security team in the Situation Room on Tuesday to review military options. The senior U.S. official issued a stark warning: "If that's not gonna happen, we will have a conversation through bombs, which will be a shame."

Mediators working overtime

Pakistan has emerged as the primary channel for this round of diplomacy, with Qatar, Oman, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt also engaged in parallel efforts to prevent the ceasefire from collapsing. The breadth of the mediating coalition reflects how much regional governments want to avoid a return to open conflict — and how little direct traction the two sides have made on their own.

Iran's position has not softened meaningfully. President Pezeshkian has said publicly that "dialogue does not mean surrender," signalling Tehran's view that concessions on the nuclear program are a political ceiling, not a negotiating floor. Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf has said Iran is "prepared for every option."

A separate dispute over sanctions has added to the friction. Iranian state media reported over the weekend that the U.S. had agreed to waive some oil sanctions as a gesture during the talks. Washington flatly denied this, with the senior official saying no sanctions relief will occur "for free" without reciprocal Iranian action.

What to watch

Tuesday's Situation Room meeting is the next concrete marker. If Trump signals renewed military pressure, energy markets — already pricing oil above $106 — will react. If mediators manage to extract any sign of movement on the enrichment question before then, the odds of another ceasefire extension improve. For now, the pattern remains: Iran submits, the U.S. rejects, intermediaries shuttle between capitals, and the clock ticks toward the next possible escalation.
