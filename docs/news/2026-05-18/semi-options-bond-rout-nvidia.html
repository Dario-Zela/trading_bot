Options traders pile into semis as bond markets flash caution ahead of Nvidia

Nvidia's headquarters campus in Santa Clara, California, where the company will report fiscal Q1 FY2027 earnings on May 20. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Options traders are piling into semiconductor calls ahead of Nvidia's May 20 results while bond markets price rising inflation and rate hike risk—a divergence that signals crowding, not conviction.

Two markets are telling different stories about the same week.

In equity derivatives, traders have spent the past month loading up on semiconductor exposure ahead of Nvidia's fiscal first-quarter results on Wednesday evening. Near-dated Nvidia options are carrying implied volatility above 68% — against a 30-day realized volatility closer to 30% — with the options market pricing a move of roughly 8% in either direction off a stock trading near $236. Broader semiconductor ETFs have pulled in a record $5.5 billion in April alone, and the sector is now priced at roughly 25 times forward earnings, a premium of more than six turns above its 10-year average of 19x. One options desk summary circulating on Benzinga this week described a "dual mania of extremely aggressive buying of semiconductors and call options by the momo crowd."

Bond markets, meanwhile, are raising their hand.

What the rates market is pricing

The 10-year Treasury yield finished Friday at 4.59% and pushed above 4.60% in early Monday trading — the highest since February 2025 and a 12-basis-point surge on the week, matching the pace of the April 2025 tariff shock. The 2-year closed at 4.09%. Rate futures, which a week ago implied a 24% probability of a Federal Reserve rate hike this year, now assign that chance at 52%. Options on TLT, the iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF, traded at more than three times their 30-day average volume on Friday, with a heavy bias toward puts — bets on further price declines, and thus further yield increases.

The catalysts are not subtle. Back-to-back CPI and PPI readings have surprised to the upside. Oil prices above $100 are feeding into broader inflation. In Japan, the 10-year government bond yield rose to 2.8%, a 29-year high, with rate futures now implying a 52% chance of a Bank of Japan hike this year — up from 24% the week before. Global sovereign bond markets are re-pricing risk in the same direction simultaneously.

Higher long rates compress the discount rate applied to future earnings. That matters more for growth stocks — particularly those, like chip designers, whose valuations rest on projected cash flows years out. Semiconductors at 25x forward earnings have no margin for a rates surprise.

The crowding risk

What makes the setup uncomfortable is how one-directional positioning has become. Analyst consensus on Nvidia sits at a $269 price target with 57 buy ratings against two holds and a single sell. Polymarket traders were pricing a 97% probability of an earnings beat as of early this week. Nvidia's composite sentiment score has nonetheless slipped roughly 14 points over the past 30 days even as the stock rallied 20%.

That divergence — falling sentiment, rising price, maximum bullish consensus — is consistent with crowding rather than fresh conviction. The historical pattern reinforces the concern: Nvidia has declined on the day of its earnings release in three of the last four quarters, despite beating consensus each time. The stock repeatedly runs into whisper numbers that exceed the published estimate, and results that beat the consensus but miss the whisper have been enough to trigger a sell-off.

IV Rank on Nvidia stands at roughly 61, meaning front-month implied volatility is elevated relative to the stock's own 52-week range. The ATM straddle on the May 23 expiry is pricing approximately $18.80 in premium — call it $9.90 on the call side and $8.90 on the put. That's a rich price to pay if the thesis is merely that Nvidia will beat again.

The divergence that matters

The bond market's message is structural: inflation is not yet contained, rate cuts are off the table for 2026, and the risk premium on long-duration assets is rising. The options market's message in semiconductors is the opposite — that this week's print will validate an expensive positioning, and that the macro backdrop is either irrelevant or already priced in.

One of them is wrong. Sentiment divergences of this kind — aggressive options buying in a high-multiple sector while the long end of the rates curve sells off — have historically resolved in the direction bond markets pointed. That doesn't mean Nvidia misses. It means the asymmetry is less favorable than a 97% beat probability implies, and that the risk of a crowded unwind has risen.

Wednesday's number — Wall Street expects $78 billion in revenue and $1.77 in non-GAAP earnings per share, implying roughly 78% year-on-year revenue growth — will be the first real test. Watch also what management says about data-center demand trajectory and Blackwell ramp timing; guidance has consistently mattered more than the backward-looking print.
