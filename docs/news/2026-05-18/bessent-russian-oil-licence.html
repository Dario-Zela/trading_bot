Bessent issues 30-day licence for vulnerable nations to access stranded Russian oil

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent announced the GL 134C extension via X on May 18, 2026 — U.S. Department of the Treasury / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Treasury's GL 134C gives buyers until June 17 to complete stranded Russian crude deliveries — the third such 30-day extension since March, driven by Iran-war supply disruptions.

The U.S. Treasury on Monday issued a second renewal of its temporary exemption from Russian oil sanctions, giving buyers another 30 days to take delivery of crude already loaded on tankers before U.S. restrictions fully apply. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent announced the measure via X, describing it as a 30-day general license letting "the most vulnerable nations" access Russian oil "currently stranded at sea."

The post was thin on details. Bessent named no qualifying nations, specified no volume caps, and gave no legal citation. In practice, the mechanism is OFAC General License 134C — published the same day — which supersedes GL 134B and extends the authorization window through June 17, 2026. Like its predecessors, GL 134C is limited to cargoes already loaded onto vessels before the prior waiver expired on May 16; it does not authorize fresh purchases of Russian crude under sanctions. Transactions involving persons subject to Iran, North Korea, or Cuba sanctions, or tied to occupied Ukrainian territories, remain blocked.

A pattern of short-term extensions

This is the third iteration of a policy that began in mid-March 2026. OFAC first issued GL 134A covering vessels loaded by March 12. GL 134B extended the cutoff to April 17 and expired May 16 — a two-day lapse preceded Monday's announcement. The prior April extension came after Bessent had publicly stated the administration would not renew the waiver, before reversing course days later. Monday's renewal was quieter, though it arrived hours after India's Ministry of Petroleum said publicly it would keep buying Russian oil regardless of any U.S. license status.

"Whatever waiver or no waiver, it will not affect our supplies," joint secretary Sujata Sharma said Monday, noting that India had imported Russian crude before, during, and after the original waiver.

India is on pace to import a record 1.9 million barrels per day in May. The waivers have collectively allowed over 250 million barrels of Russian crude to reach market. The current extension is estimated to free up another 45–50 million barrels.

Iran war drives the policy

The underlying pressure is the U.S.-Israel campaign against Iran, which has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint for roughly one-fifth of global seaborne oil and gas supply. With that disruption pushing crude above $106 a barrel and U.S. gasoline prices more than 50 percent above pre-war levels, the administration has found it politically difficult to enforce Russian oil restrictions that would tighten supply further.

Bessent's stated rationale is twofold: helping energy-vulnerable nations access supply, and — notably — limiting China's ability to stockpile discounted Russian crude by keeping prices in motion.

The logic has critics. EU Commission President Ursula von der Leyen said it is "not the time to relax sanctions against Russia." Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky has objected to each extension, given the direct revenue benefit to Moscow; Russia's oil export earnings nearly doubled in March against year-earlier levels.

What to watch

GL 134C runs through June 17. The administration will face the same decision again in roughly four weeks — with the Iran conflict unresolved and oil prices showing no sign of retreating. If a ceasefire or deal materializes before then (see related coverage on U.S.-Iran nuclear talks), the political case for another extension weakens considerably. If it does not, a fourth renewal looks like the path of least resistance.
