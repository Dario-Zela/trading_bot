Small-cap ETFs quietly outperform while investors stay crowded in mega-cap AI

The NYSE trading floor — where capital concentration in mega-cap AI names has left small-cap performance largely unrewarded by investor flows. — Asymptote Architecture / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Small-cap ETFs are outpacing the S&P 500 by ~5 percentage points in 2026, yet IWM has bled $6B in outflows as capital stays locked in mega-cap AI.

Small-cap equity ETFs are running ahead of both the S&P 500 and the Nasdaq by a meaningful margin in 2026, yet the money hasn't followed. The S&P 600 small-cap index is up roughly 12% year-to-date; the S&P 500 has gained about 7%; the Nasdaq is closer to 4%. That's a five-percentage-point spread over large caps — and it's happening largely without the capital flows that would normally accompany such outperformance.

The flow data tells the story plainly. IWM, the iShares Russell 2000 ETF and the most liquid small-cap vehicle in the market, has seen approximately $6 billion in net outflows so far this year. The Vanguard Small-Cap Growth ETF (VBK) has shed roughly $357 million despite posting returns of around 9%. Meanwhile, iShares Core S&P Small-Cap (IJR) has attracted $2.1 billion in net inflows — one of the few bright spots — and State Street's SPDR Portfolio S&P 600 Small Cap (SPSM) has added $455 million. The Vanguard Small-Cap ETF (VB) sits near flat, with just $292 million in net inflows.

In aggregate, it looks less like rotation and more like neglect. Bloomberg Intelligence ETF analyst Eric Balchunas has described small caps as having effectively become "Ignored Caps," noting that the segment's share of total ETF industry assets has fallen from roughly 10% to around 4%. Small-cap mutual funds have suffered an additional $25 billion in outflows on top of that.

Why the divergence exists

The structural case for small caps is not new, but it has sharpened. On a forward price-to-earnings basis, small caps are trading at roughly 15x versus approximately 22x for large caps — a valuation gap near its widest in three decades. Their predominantly domestic revenue base insulates them from geopolitical friction and dollar volatility, both of which have flared this year. And several small-cap sub-sectors — energy infrastructure, electrification, contract manufacturing — sit at the intersection of reshoring and AI-driven demand, without carrying the premium multiples of the "Magnificent Seven."

The irony is structural. Passive flows into broad indices naturally overweight the largest stocks; the bigger a mega-cap gets, the more index-tracking money chases it. Small caps, by contrast, require either active selection or deliberate allocation. In a market where AI narratives have dominated ETF marketing and media coverage for two-plus years, small caps have simply been off the agenda.

Performance without confirmation

The central interpretive question is whether this outperformance reflects genuine mean reversion — valuation compression catching up to reality — or whether it's noise that will evaporate when macro conditions shift. Small-cap stocks carry more floating-rate debt and are more sensitive to credit conditions, which means any resumption of rate hikes would hurt them disproportionately. Their thinner liquidity also makes the outperformance harder to capture cleanly at scale.

Still, the data points in one direction: small caps are generating returns, and very little capital is positioned to benefit from it. If investors eventually rotate even a fraction of the assets concentrated in mega-cap AI names, the flow impact on a much smaller segment could be amplified considerably.

The next test will come in June, when the Federal Reserve's rate path becomes clearer and Q2 earnings season begins to reveal whether small-cap revenue is holding up domestically. A dovish Fed stance, combined with another quarter of relative earnings stability, would provide the catalyst that current valuations and flows conspire to make meaningful.
