RPC shares fall 5.7% despite Q1 earnings beat as costs squeeze margins

Oil pump jacks near South Heart, North Dakota — the kind of oilfield activity RPC services across the US basin. — Acroterion / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
RPC beat Q1 estimates on revenue and EPS, but net income fell 92% year-over-year as Pintail acquisition costs and softer oil prices crushed margins.

RPC Inc. shares dropped 5.7% to $6.92 on May 7 after the oilfield services company reported first-quarter 2026 results that beat Wall Street estimates on both the top and bottom lines — yet left investors unimpressed. The disconnect is worth examining, because the numbers tell a company running faster just to stay in place.

Revenue for the quarter came in at $454.8 million, a 36.6% jump from a year earlier and roughly $59 million ahead of the $396 million consensus. Adjusted earnings per share of three cents beat the Zacks estimate of one cent by a wide margin. On the surface, that looks like a clean beat.

Beneath the surface, profitability collapsed. Net income fell to $0.9 million from $12.0 million in Q1 2025 — a decline of more than 92%. Operating income dropped to $2.6 million from $12.4 million a year earlier. Adjusted EPS, while above estimates, was still down 50% year-over-year. Beating a low bar is still beating a lower number than you used to clear.

Pintail inflates the top line, costs follow

The headline revenue growth is almost entirely attributable to Pintail Completions, which RPC acquired in the second quarter of 2025. Pintail contributed $97.8 million in Q1 revenue — and if you strip that out, organic growth looks far less impressive. Cost of revenues rose to $355.6 million from $243.9 million the prior year, an increase of $111.7 million, much of it Pintail-related. SG&A climbed to $48.2 million from $42.5 million. Together, these erased nearly all of the revenue gain at the operating income line.

Management noted that job mix during the quarter included direct operator supply of materials and fuel — a factor that inflated costs but is unlikely to repeat consistently.

Macro headwinds compound the pressure

The operating environment did RPC no favors. The average domestic rig count fell 6.8% year-over-year during the quarter, and crude oil averaged $70.54 per barrel — down 1.9% from the same period in 2025. Natural gas fared better, up 16.2% to $4.81 per thousand cubic feet, but RPC's business is weighted toward oil-directed activity.

Management described operators as "cautious and concerned about the duration of higher crude prices" and their implications for capital budgets. On fleet reactivation, the message was blunt: "We have no plans to reactivate fleets at current pricing levels" without clear visibility into pricing, duration, and volume. Spot market pricing, while nudging upward, is "not broad-based yet" — and spot exposure covers only 31% of RPC's overall revenue, concentrated in pressure pumping.

Pintail, as the largest wireline provider in the Permian Basin, is expected to track large Permian operator activity closely. That dependency means Pintail's growth trajectory rests heavily on decisions made in the boardrooms of major oil companies — decisions being made cautiously, given market signals.

Balance sheet strong; margins less so

RPC ended the quarter with $200.7 million in cash, no debt, and a $100 million revolving credit facility untouched. Operating cash flow was $31.2 million, down from $39.9 million a year ago. Capital expenditure guidance for 2026 was nudged up to a $160–$180 million range, reflecting opportunistic equipment purchases.

The company's financial position is solid. The question is whether the cost structure — inflated by the Pintail integration and by the kind of job mix that may not persist — can be brought back in line with margins that investors actually want to own.

What to watch

The next test comes when RPC reports Q2 results, likely in early August. By then, Pintail will be further integrated, and the comparison period will include a full quarter of the acquisition for the first time on a like-for-like basis. If oil prices hold — they have moved considerably since Q1 averaged $70.54 — pressure pumping utilization could pick up, along with spot pricing. But until operators open their capital budgets more broadly, any improvement is likely to be incremental. Pricing discipline, not volume, is what management says it is watching most closely.
