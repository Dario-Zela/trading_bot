White House set to announce TrumpRx expansion alongside Mark Cuban

Mark Cuban speaks at a campaign event in Phoenix, Arizona in October 2024. His Cost Plus Drug Company is now integrated into the White House's TrumpRx platform. — Gage Skidmore / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 2.0)
In one sentence
The White House added 600+ generics to TrumpRx with Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs as a partner, targeting uninsured Americans and high-deductible plan holders.

The Trump administration used a White House event Monday to roll out a major expansion of TrumpRx.gov, its direct-to-consumer drug pricing platform, adding more than 600 generic medications and bringing in an unlikely partner for the announcement: Mark Cuban.

Cuban, whose Cost Plus Drug Company sells mail-order generics at transparent, fixed markups, joined the event despite having campaigned for Democrat Kamala Harris in the 2024 presidential election. The billionaire investor has nonetheless been vocal in his support for TrumpRx, arguing that the platform's price-transparency push aligns with what his own company has been doing since 2022. Cuban had previously stated that adding Cost Plus Drugs to the government platform would strengthen it. Monday's event made that partnership official.

From 40 drugs to 640-plus

TrumpRx launched in February 2026 with roughly 40 medications, initially skewed toward brand-name treatments including Ozempic and Wegovy. Critics — including Democratic Senator Elizabeth Warren — pointed out during congressional hearings that the site was light on generics, the medications most Americans rely on for everyday conditions. Monday's expansion is the administration's answer to that criticism. Common drugs now available through the platform include atorvastatin for cholesterol, metformin for diabetes, lisinopril for blood pressure, and clopidogrel, a blood thinner.

The platform now integrates discounts from three pharmacy networks: Amazon Pharmacy, GoodRx, and Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs. TrumpRx functions as a comparison and routing layer — it does not sell drugs directly but directs users to whichever partner offers the lowest cash price, and it now includes a tool to surface the cheapest nearby pharmacy as well as a home-delivery option.

Who actually benefits

Health policy experts have noted a persistent caveat: for the roughly 90 percent of Americans who carry insurance, their co-pays will often still beat the cash prices shown on TrumpRx. The expansion is most meaningful for the uninsured and for people with high-deductible plans who pay out of pocket before coverage kicks in. Cost Plus Drugs operates on a cost-plus-15% model, adding a $5 pharmacy service fee and $5.25 shipping — a structure that can undercut traditional pharmacy chains significantly for generic medications.

Trump described the announcement as "the greatest breakthrough in lowering health care costs in modern history," framing the rollout as a signature domestic win ahead of the midterm elections this fall.

Political backdrop

The timing is not accidental. The administration is under pressure on healthcare affordability following Medicaid cuts and the expiration of enhanced Affordable Care Act subsidies that pushed premiums higher for millions of enrollees. Cuban's presence — a recognizable face who made drug pricing a public crusade before Trump took office — lends the announcement bipartisan texture the White House is keen to project.

The White House has been building toward this moment since May 2025, when an executive order on Most-Favored-Nation drug pricing kicked off a chain of negotiations with pharmaceutical manufacturers. Seventeen separate deals with major drugmakers followed by late 2025.

The next test is whether TrumpRx's traffic numbers reflect genuine consumer adoption, or whether price comparison tools remain underused as they have historically been. Cuban's direct involvement — and his company's integration — gives the platform a built-in distribution channel that the earlier version lacked.
