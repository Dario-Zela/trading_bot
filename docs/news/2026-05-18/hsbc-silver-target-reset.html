HSBC raises silver price targets while warning upside may already be capped

A 1,000-troy-ounce silver bullion bar — the standard contract unit in wholesale silver markets. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
HSBC lifted its 2026 silver average to $75/oz but set a year-end target of $70 — below current spot — as shrinking supply deficits and demand substitution cap the upside.

HSBC raised its silver price forecasts this week — and simultaneously told the market that the metal's best days in this cycle are probably behind it. Both statements are accurate, and understanding how they coexist matters more than either one taken alone.

James Steel, HSBC's chief precious metals analyst, lifted the bank's average silver price forecast for 2026 to $75 per troy ounce, up from $68.25. The 2027 average was raised to $68 from $57. Those are substantial revisions. The detail that undermines any bullish read: HSBC's year-end 2026 target is $70 per ounce. Silver was trading around $77 Monday morning. The bank's year-end target sits roughly 9% below current spot.

The arithmetic of a front-loaded year

The apparent contradiction resolves once you look at the calendar. Silver opened 2026 aggressively, touching a nominal record of $121 per ounce in late January — driven by gold's rally, constrained supply, and safe-haven buying tied to tariff uncertainty and geopolitical tension. That January spike was sharp enough to pull the full-year average well above where any analyst had previously pencilled. HSBC's average upgrade is largely a recognition that the first quarter already happened.

From here, the bank's base case is a grind lower. Year-end at $70 implies a roughly 10% decline from present levels over the next seven months. The 2027 year-end target of $65 extends the slide further.

The structural case for a ceiling

The limiting factor HSBC identifies is supply-side normalization, compounded by demand erosion at high price levels.

On supply: the global silver market deficit is projected to shrink from approximately 143 million ounces in 2025 to 73 million ounces this year and just 25 million ounces in 2027. Mine production is forecast at 848 million ounces in 2026, rising to 868 million in 2027. Recycling adds another 216 million ounces to available supply. The tightness that drove the January spike is loosening, quarter by quarter.

On demand: industrial consumption — which accounts for more than half of total silver use — is expected to slip from 657 million ounces in 2025 to 642 million ounces this year and 618 million in 2027. Steel attributed the decline to manufacturers substituting away from silver or redesigning processes to reduce it, a rational response to prices that are roughly 140% higher than a year ago. Jewelry demand is also falling, from 189 million ounces in 2025 to a projected 157 million this year.

Steel's framing of the combined picture: "Moderating deficits, in our view, will not be sufficient to propel silver sharply higher for prolonged periods."

The solar caveat

The industrial demand story has a notable asterisk: solar. Photovoltaic panels now account for roughly 30% of industrial silver demand — up from about 11% a decade ago — and solar's share of total mined silver has grown to roughly 15–20%. HSBC's forecasts embed declining aggregate industrial demand despite that structural tailwind from renewables. The implicit read is that substitution pressure and slower manufacturing activity elsewhere is large enough to offset continued solar growth. Whether that call holds depends in part on how aggressively panel manufacturers adapt silver-reduction technologies, a line of engineering work that has accelerated precisely because prices ran so high.

What the framing signals to buyers

For industrial buyers managing procurement and hedgers setting positions, the HSBC view translates to a clear posture: the supply squeeze has likely peaked, near-term prices are already at or above the bank's year-end expectation, and the multi-year path is lower. Strategies built around continued upward momentum are working from a different base case than HSBC's.

The relevant watch points from here: Q2 mine output data (which will indicate whether supply growth is tracking the forecast), Federal Reserve guidance on rate direction (precious metals broadly are sensitive to real rates), and movement in the gold-to-silver ratio. If gold gives back gains — a plausible scenario given current geopolitical uncertainty showing signs of resolution — silver loses its primary tailwind without a compensating supply shock in view.
