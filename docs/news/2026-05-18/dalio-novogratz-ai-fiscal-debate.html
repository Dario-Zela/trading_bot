Dalio sees AI as America's fiscal escape hatch; Novogratz says Washington will fumble it

Ray Dalio, founder of Bridgewater Associates, speaking at Web Summit 2018 in Lisbon — Harry Murphy/Web Summit via Sportsfile (Wikimedia Commons)
In one sentence
Dalio says AI productivity can close America's fiscal gap if Washington acts; Novogratz says Washington won't act, and a robot tax is more likely than not by 2028.

Two investors who have spent careers making macro bets are staring at the same set of facts — an American government that spends roughly $7 trillion a year while collecting about $5 trillion, a national debt that has now crossed 100 percent of GDP for the first time since the brief COVID crash and before that World War II, and a technology wave that could, in theory, change the math. They have arrived at opposite conclusions about what happens next.

Ray Dalio, founder of Bridgewater Associates, has positioned AI as potentially the most significant productivity event in a generation — one that, if policymakers move correctly, could bend the country's fiscal trajectory. Mike Novogratz, CEO of Galaxy Digital, thinks policymakers will not move correctly. He is putting a probability on it.

The size of the problem

The numbers are not in dispute. Gross federal debt sits around $39 trillion. Debt held by the public has crossed $31.27 trillion, now slightly above GDP. The Congressional Budget Office projects that figure rising to 120 percent of GDP by 2036 absent significant course correction — surpassing the previous all-time high of 106 percent set in 1946. Annual interest payments are already running into the hundreds of billions, competing for budget space with defense, entitlements, and everything else.

Dalio has made the structural case repeatedly, most recently before the House Budget Committee. His framing: "debt and debt service costs rise faster than the incomes needed to service them" — and that dynamic, once entrenched, tends to end badly. His historical analogy is arterial plaque, not a heart attack, but building toward one.

The fiscal math requires reducing the annual deficit by roughly half, to around 3 percent of GDP — a target that Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent embedded in his so-called 3-3-3 framework: 3 percent deficit, 3 percent GDP growth, 3 million extra barrels of daily oil output, all by 2028. Dalio has publicly endorsed the deficit target. The question is what fills the gap between aspiration and arithmetic.

Where Dalio sees the opening

AI is, in Dalio's telling, a potential multiplier that could make the math more forgiving. A genuine productivity acceleration — the kind that has historically come only a handful of times in the modern era — would mean higher incomes, higher tax receipts, and a denominator that grows faster than the numerator. That is the only version of "growing out of debt" that actually pencils.

Research from the Yale Budget Lab puts rough numbers on the scenario. If AI lifts productivity growth to 2.5 percent annually over the next five years — above the 1.8 percent average of the past decade but not outside historical precedent — the federal deficit could narrow to 3.7 percent of GDP by 2035, versus the 6.2 percent baseline. Debt stabilizes near 100 percent of GDP rather than compounding toward 118 percent. The Penn-Wharton model estimates AI-driven productivity could reduce cumulative deficits by $400 billion through 2035. The CBO's base projection is more conservative: a 1 percent total productivity boost over ten years, with its own caveat that the estimate is "highly uncertain."

Dalio has been careful to distinguish between the potential and the certainty. He told Fortune in May that productivity growth "will likely improve in 2026," but acknowledged that how much flows to company profits versus workers and tax revenues "remains uncertain." He is enthusiastic about AI's capacity to improve decision-making across the economy — an argument he has made emphatically, including through his own AI-modeled persona — but his optimism is conditional on fiscal discipline that has so far proved elusive.

The policy gap

This is where Novogratz parts company.

The Galaxy CEO's core argument is not that AI won't deliver productivity gains. It is that Washington will not convert those gains into fiscal repair. It will spend them. His evidence: the Bessent 3-3-3 framework was announced as a marker of newfound fiscal seriousness, complete with DOGE efficiency promises and a newly installed Fed chair in Kevin Warsh. What actually emerged in April was a White House budget requesting $1.5 trillion in defense spending for fiscal year 2027 — one of the largest military budget proposals in American history — while nondefense cuts amounted to $73 billion, less than one-third of the increase on the other side of the ledger.

"Donald Trump likes to spend money," Novogratz said in a recent interview. "Like, period, end story."

His conclusion is that a productivity windfall from AI, rather than being applied to debt reduction, will be claimed by defense spending, entitlement expansion, or simply absorbed into a wider deficit. The fiscal discipline required to actually close the gap — the spending cuts and revenue increases that Dalio argues are mathematically necessary alongside any productivity benefit — will not materialize in the political environment he sees.

The robot tax forecast

Novogratz has gone further, arguing that the displacement AI causes in the labor market will itself generate a political response that becomes a structural headwind for the technology sector. He puts the probability of some form of AI agent or robot tax being enacted in the United States by 2028 at above 70 percent.

The concept is not new. Bill Gates proposed taxing robots nearly a decade ago as a mechanism to fund worker retraining when automation reduced the payroll base. South Korea has already explored similar legislation. And in April of this year, Sam Altman's OpenAI published a 13-page policy document that, among other things, proposed shifting the tax base from labor toward capital gains and corporate income — and explicitly floated levies on automated labor — precisely because AI-driven displacement would hollow out the payroll taxes that fund Social Security.

The Yale Budget Lab's Martha Gimbel framed the structural tension clearly: focusing only on AI productivity gains "can give you an overly rosy view" because "there are costs that come with that productivity growth that we as a society will be expected to manage."

Managing those costs, in the policy vocabulary Novogratz anticipates, means taxing the machines that caused them. For equity markets, the implication is direct: if AI agents become a taxed input rather than a free productivity multiplier, the discounted value of the AI bull case contracts. Novogratz has said he remains long AI infrastructure and capex — Galaxy Digital's own Helios data center campus in West Texas, valued at more than $15 billion, is a bet on the buildout continuing — but he is simultaneously buying protective puts on the S&P 500. He is hedging the policy response, not the technology.

What the market has priced

The current AI equity narrative runs roughly as follows: large-scale productivity gains justify elevated technology multiples; higher trend growth reduces real debt burdens over time; the Fed can stay on a normalization path rather than reverting to emergency accommodation. That is Dalio's scenario, more or less, and it is what the market has been pricing since late 2024.

Novogratz's scenario — an administration that spends through any productivity windfall, layered with a 70-percent-probability robot tax by 2028 — is not priced in the same way. A robot tax that materially raised the cost of AI deployment would hit enterprise software adoption curves, data center utilization rates, and the long-tail of companies whose margin expansion assumptions depend on labor cost substitution. The downside scenario for AI-exposed growth equities, in other words, is not just a tech correction. It is a policy correction arriving after the productivity gains are already in earnings models.

What to watch

The near-term signal is the budget reconciliation process. If the "One Big Beautiful Bill" makes it through Congress with the full $1.5 trillion in defense spending and minimal structural offsets — which current vote counts suggest is plausible — Novogratz's argument that Washington cannot convert AI productivity into fiscal discipline will have gained a concrete data point. The CBO score of the final bill will be the first numerical test.

On the productivity side, the 2026 Q2 and Q3 output-per-hour numbers — due to land later this year — will be the earliest statistical evidence of whether an AI-driven acceleration is materializing in aggregate productivity figures or remaining concentrated in the tech sector's own accounts. If the broader numbers stay near the decade average of 1.8 percent, the fiscal math Dalio is counting on does not improve.

Dalio and Novogratz agree on the diagnosis. They disagree on the treatment, and they disagree, sharply, on whether the government is capable of administering it.
