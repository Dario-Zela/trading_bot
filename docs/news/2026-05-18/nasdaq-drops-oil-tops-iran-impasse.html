Nasdaq slides 1% on AI capex fears as oil tops $106 and Iran talks stall

The Nasdaq MarketSite tower in New York's Times Square, where the exchange's stock tickers scroll continuously. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nasdaq 100 drops 1.1% as AMAT, MU, ORCL and Vertiv crater on AI capex slowdown fears, with WTI at $107 and the 10-year yield near 4.6% adding cost and financing pressure.

Technology stocks sold off sharply on Monday as investors rotated out of AI hardware names, sending the Nasdaq 100 down 1.1% — its worst two-session stretch since late March — while oil pushed past $106 a barrel and the 10-year Treasury yield sat unmoved near 4.60%, a level that has not been sustained since early 2025.

The S&P 500 slipped 0.4% to 7,381. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed nearly flat at 49,488. The Russell 2000 fell 0.7% to 2,773. The damage was concentrated in a specific corner of the market: companies whose earnings depend on sustained AI capital expenditure.

The capex trade unwinds

Applied Materials dropped 5.3%. Micron Technology fell 4.2%. Oracle slid 3.8%. GE Vernova shed 5.0%. Vertiv Holdings, the data-center power-management company that hit an all-time high as recently as May 14, tumbled 8.9% with no fresh company-specific news on the tape — the move read as concentrated profit-taking after a strong run.

The common thread is exposure to the AI infrastructure buildout. Applied Materials supplies the chip-fabrication equipment that depends on fab investment staying elevated. Micron sells high-bandwidth memory into the same data-center market. Oracle has pivoted aggressively toward cloud and AI workloads. Vertiv's entire thesis is that AI servers require more power per rack than conventional compute, so more data-center construction means more Vertiv revenue.

All four names work only if hyperscaler capex keeps compounding. Recent data suggests the pace is decelerating. Hyperscaler capital-expenditure growth ran at roughly 75% year-over-year in the third quarter of 2025, slowed to 49% in the fourth quarter, and consensus now puts the figure closer to 25% by the end of 2026. Absolute spending levels remain enormous — the four largest cloud operators are on track to collectively deploy close to $700 billion this year — but the second-derivative turn is what matters for valuation multiples in capital-equipment and infrastructure names.

Oil and Iran

The energy picture complicated an already difficult session. WTI crude rose 1.5% to $106.96 a barrel. Brent added 1.6% to $110.97. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed to most commercial shipping following the outbreak of the US-Iran conflict, and weekend diplomatic news offered no reason for optimism.

Talks between Washington and Tehran appear to have broken down again over the sequence and substance of any nuclear concessions. Iran's latest proposal — an immediate ceasefire, Hormuz reopening, and parallel nuclear discussions deferred to a later stage — was rejected by the White House as insufficient. President Trump, who earlier this month said Iran had come close to an agreement "four or five times" only to reverse course, reiterated on Sunday that any deal would require Tehran to issue what he described as documents acknowledging US primacy. Iranian state media framed the American position as demanding unconditional surrender; Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said publicly that Tehran doubted Washington's "seriousness" about reaching a settlement.

Elevated oil prices feed directly into semiconductor manufacturing costs — fab operations are energy-intensive — and into freight and logistics expenses across the supply chain. For companies like AMAT and MU, $106 WTI is not an abstract macro backdrop; it is a line item.

Rates hold the vice closed

The third leg of the squeeze is financing costs. The 10-year Treasury yield held at 4.60% on Monday, near 16-month highs. The 30-year has climbed to 5.13%. Traders are now pricing a meaningful probability of a rate increase before year-end under incoming Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh, after a week of inflation data that showed energy costs feeding into broader CPI and PPI readings.

High long rates make capital expenditures more expensive to finance. They also raise the discount rate applied to future earnings, compressing the multiples that AI infrastructure stocks have been trading on. The combination of slower capex growth (demand headwind), higher oil (input cost headwind), and sticky yields (financing headwind) is a rare alignment of three pressures at once — and it landed on stocks that had priced in a more benign scenario.

Energy and insurance sectors gained on the day, as investors sought yield and commodity exposure. Small-cap industrials also held up relatively well.

What to watch

Nvidia reports earnings later this month, and the market will treat the guidance language on data-center demand as a referendum on whether Monday's repricing was justified or premature. Any signal that hyperscaler purchase orders are being deferred or stretched would confirm the capex deceleration thesis; a beat-and-raise would likely trigger a partial reversal in the names sold off today.

On the geopolitical side, a fifth round of US-Iran talks has not been publicly scheduled. The Hormuz closure is the primary mechanism linking the Iran conflict to energy markets and, through energy, to technology sector margins. A credible ceasefire framework would remove roughly $15-20 per barrel of risk premium from crude almost immediately, taking pressure off input costs and shifting rate expectations in a more accommodative direction. Until one materialises, the three-way squeeze on AI hardware names is the base case.
