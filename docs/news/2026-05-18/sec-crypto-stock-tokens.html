SEC readies framework to allow trading of crypto-linked stock tokens

The SEC's headquarters at 100 F Street NE in Washington, D.C. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
The SEC is drafting rules to allow blockchain-based trading of tokenized U.S. stocks, extending beyond exchange-level approvals already in place for Nasdaq and NYSE.

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission is preparing a formal framework to allow blockchain-based versions of American stocks and other securities to trade, Bloomberg reported Monday — the clearest signal yet that the agency intends to put a regulatory roof over an industry that has been operating under a patchwork of no-action letters and exchange-specific approvals.

The reported framework would extend the SEC's recent exchange-level tokenization approvals into broader territory. In March, the SEC greenlit a Nasdaq rule change allowing eligible participants to settle trades as blockchain tokens alongside traditional shares, with the same ticker, the same CUSIP, and T+1 settlement routed through the Depository Trust Company. NYSE followed in April with its own SEC-approved rule change (SR-NYSE-2026-17), and subsequently named Securitize — a tokenization specialist backed by BlackRock — as the first digital transfer agent eligible to mint blockchain-native securities on its platform.

Those approvals kept tokenized shares firmly inside the existing clearinghouse system. The framework now in preparation would go further, potentially covering permissionless blockchains and decentralized protocols — which is where the real regulatory novelty lies.

The groundwork

SEC Chair Paul Atkins signaled the direction on April 21, telling the Economic Club of Washington that the Commission was "on the verge" of releasing what he called an Innovation Exemption: a 12- to 36-month regulatory sandbox that would let qualified issuers and trading venues bring tokenized securities to market under lighter-touch compliance conditions, with KYC/AML and anti-fraud requirements in place throughout. Firms exiting the sandbox would need to either demonstrate sufficient decentralization or achieve full registration.

The agency laid conceptual groundwork in January, when the Divisions of Corporation Finance, Trading and Markets, and Investment Management issued a joint statement on tokenized securities. Its core message was unambiguous: blockchain changes the infrastructure, not the legal classification. A tokenized stock is still a stock. Disclosure rules, custody requirements, and registration obligations travel with it.

The Investor Advisory Committee backed the broader push in March, though it flagged unresolved questions around ownership clarity, private-key custody standards, and voting rights — issues that any formal rulemaking will need to address.

Industry already moving

Market participants have not waited. The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation announced on May 5 that it plans initial limited production trades of tokenized real-world assets in July, with a full service launch in October. The asset scope covers Russell 1000 constituents, major index ETFs, and U.S. Treasury instruments. More than 50 firms — BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Bank of America, Citi, Charles Schwab, Morgan Stanley, and crypto-native firms including Circle and Ripple — have been involved in shaping the system. DTCC's Depository Trust Company obtained a three-year SEC no-action letter in December 2025 authorizing the service.

Nasdaq, meanwhile, signed a deal with Kraken to issue tokenized equities for European and other international customers. Securitize and Jump Trading launched regulated on-chain trading for tokenized equities earlier this month.

Not everyone is on board

The enthusiasm is not uniform. In December 2025, Nasdaq and CME Group led a coalition of major exchanges in a joint letter urging the SEC to reject exemptions that would let crypto-native firms trade tokenized equities without full securities oversight — a sign that incumbents are watching their turf carefully. SIFMA has pressed for a transparent public comment process. Senators Elizabeth Warren and Chris Van Hollen wrote to Atkins in March pressing him not to exempt broad swaths of the crypto market from U.S. securities law.

The World Federation of Exchanges warned that broad exemptions could "dilute" investor protections, a concern that becomes sharper if trading migrates to permissionless protocols where intermediary accountability is less clear.

What comes next

The full Regulation Crypto Assets rulemaking package — which would address not just tokenized equities but digital commodities, stablecoins, and DeFi broadly — is expected in the second half of 2026. DTCC's July pilot will be an early real-world test of whether the plumbing works under operational conditions. Any formal SEC rulemaking would trigger a public comment period, so binding rules are unlikely before 2027. The immediate question is whether the Innovation Exemption arrives before or after the broader rulemaking, and how tightly the two are coupled.
