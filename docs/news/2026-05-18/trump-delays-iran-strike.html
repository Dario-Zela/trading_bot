Trump delays Iran military strike after Saudi, UAE and Qatari leaders intervene; military told to stand ready

President Trump with Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman at King Khalid International Airport, Riyadh, May 2025. MBS was among the three Gulf leaders who asked Trump to delay a planned Tuesday strike on Iran. — White House / Daniel Torok
In one sentence
Trump has delayed a planned Tuesday strike on Iran at Gulf allies' request, but ordered the military to stay ready for an immediate assault if talks fail.

President Donald Trump announced on Monday that he was standing down from a military strike on Iran planned for the following day, posting on Truth Social that the leaders of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the United Arab Emirates had asked him to allow negotiations to continue. The announcement came with an explicit caveat: he had ordered Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth and Joint Chiefs Chairman General Daniel Caine to keep the military prepared for what he called "a full, large scale assault" on Iran "on a moment's notice" if those talks collapse.

"I have been asked by the Emir of Qatar, the Crown Prince of Saudi Arabia and the President of the United Arab Emirates to hold off on our planned Military attack," Trump wrote, citing "serious negotiations" now underway. Asked by reporters how long the pause might last, he was characteristically imprecise: "I put it off for a little while, hopefully, maybe, forever."

The Gulf Intervention

The three leaders — Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, Qatari Emir Sheikh Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, and UAE President Sheikh Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan — each made contact with Trump within 24 hours of his announcement. Their argument was straightforward: talks were at a delicate stage, and a deal was achievable on the central US demand of no Iranian nuclear weapons capability. Trump's Truth Social post suggested he found that framing persuasive.

But the intervention also reflected the Gulf states' own vulnerability. Since the conflict began in late February, Iranian missile and drone strikes have targeted energy infrastructure across the Arabian Peninsula, with total damage to Arab nations assessed at over $120 billion by late March. A fresh American strike risked triggering Iranian retaliation against the same oil and gas facilities that these economies — and, through them, global energy markets — depend on.

Gulf states occupy an awkward position in this conflict: they are America's primary regional partners, yet they share a waterway, a neighbourhood, and complex historical ties with Iran. Pushing Trump to pause serves their immediate security interests. It also preserves whatever diplomatic credibility they retain with Tehran, should they need to serve as go-betweens in any eventual settlement.

How the Conflict Got Here

The US-Iran war began on February 28, when American and Israeli forces launched coordinated strikes on Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and several other senior officials. Iran responded with mass missile and drone barrages against Israel, US military installations across the region, and neighbouring Arab states. Simultaneously, Tehran blockaded the Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint through which roughly 20% of global oil trade passes — effectively holding the world's energy supply hostage.

A two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan took effect on April 8, after Trump threatened to destroy Iranian power plants and bridges. Iran agreed to the terms and signalled it would reopen Hormuz. The truce did not hold. Both sides have exchanged fire in the Strait since; Iran subsequently established a "Persian Gulf Strait Authority" to formally institutionalise its control over the waterway, a move that suggests Tehran is preparing for prolonged confrontation rather than a near-term deal.

By early May, Trump said the ceasefire was "on massive life support." White House officials were described as increasingly frustrated with what they saw as fractured Iranian leadership that was preventing any substantial concession on nuclear talks. Iran submitted an updated peace proposal through Pakistani intermediaries; the White House rejected it as insufficient. Iran's President Masoud Pezeshkian offered his own public framing: "Dialogue does not mean surrender."

The Negotiating Gap

The distance between the two sides remains substantial. Trump's stated demands include a complete ban on Iranian nuclear weapons, strict caps on uranium enrichment, the severing of Tehran's ties with regional proxy forces, and the dismantling of Iran's missile arsenal and naval capabilities. Iran wants frozen assets released and comprehensive sanctions lifted. There is no agreement — or even apparent clarity — on sequencing: whether Hormuz reopens before or after a final deal, and whether enrichment is paused as a precondition or as part of an end state.

For Gulf states, notably, the nuclear issue is not the only priority — some officials have signalled that re-opening Hormuz and curtailing Iran's missile programme are equally pressing. That may create some flexibility in how a deal is structured, but it also means more variables to resolve.

Monday's announcement is, by most counts, the seventh time Trump has postponed military action or extended a deadline since the conflict began. The pattern — escalation, a hard deadline, a last-minute reprieve — is familiar enough that markets now treat each new threat with more caution than full credence. What distinguishes this episode is that the intervention came from Washington's own partners, not from Tehran.

Economic Pressure Points

Oil was trading above $106 per barrel as of Monday, already elevated well above pre-conflict levels. Analysts have floated worst-case scenarios approaching $200 a barrel if a full resumption of hostilities closes Hormuz completely and for an extended duration. US military costs have reached an estimated $29 billion since February; at least 13 American service members have been killed. A New York Times poll found 64% of Americans opposed to the war — a political pressure the administration will feel acutely as November midterm elections near.

What to Watch

The most consequential unknowns are the timeline and substance of any new talks. Trump set no deadline for this pause — only the threat that the military remains ready to move. Whether Pakistan can bring both sides to a concrete framework, whether Iran signals any movement on enrichment or Hormuz, and whether the Gulf states can keep Trump's military calculus in check are the questions that matter most in the coming days.

For markets, every signal from Truth Social and every statement from Iranian officials carries outsized weight. The pause is real. So is the trigger finger.
