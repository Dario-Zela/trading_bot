House plan would impose $130 annual fee on electric vehicle owners

An Electrify America DC fast-charging station in Valdosta, Georgia. — Michael Rivera / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
House highway bill proposes a $130 annual EV fee rising to $150, framed as road-funding equity but landing above what most gas-car drivers pay in federal gas tax.

The House Transportation Committee has unveiled a $130 annual registration fee on electric vehicle owners as part of the BUILD America 250 Act — a $580 billion, five-year highway reauthorization bill that Congress must pass before current surface transportation funding expires on September 30. The fee would increase by $5 every two years, capping at $150. Plug-in hybrid owners would face a separate $35 charge, rising to $50.

The rationale is structural. The federal gasoline tax — fixed at 18.4 cents per gallon since 1993 — finances the Highway Trust Fund, which pays for road maintenance and construction. EV drivers bypass it entirely. Committee Chairman Sam Graves (R-MO) framed the provision as parity: the bill "ensures that electric vehicle owners begin paying their fair share for the use of our roads."

The math, however, runs against that framing on the numbers alone. Consumer Reports data puts the average federal gas tax paid by a typical American driver at $73 to $89 a year — well below the proposed EV floor. Albert Gore of the Zero Emission Transportation Association acknowledged the Trust Fund's structural problem but called the fee "a punitive tax that would disproportionately impact adopters of electric vehicles." The Sierra Club's Katherine García termed it "irresponsible." Neither group disputes that EV drivers contribute less to road funding; both argue the premium over what gas-car drivers actually pay is punitive rather than equitable.

A shifting policy climate

The bill emerged from bipartisan negotiations inside the Transportation Committee, which separates it somewhat from the more overtly ideological debate over EV subsidies happening elsewhere in Congress. Still, the fee sits alongside a separate House reconciliation package that would eliminate the $7,500 consumer EV tax credit. The two measures are unconnected legislatively, but they reinforce the same signal: the federal policy architecture that accelerated EV adoption since the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 is being dismantled piece by piece.

For automakers, the aggregate effect matters more than any single provision. Ford and General Motors have each committed tens of billions of dollars to EV capacity, then spent the past two years walking back production targets as retail demand lagged. An additional $130 annual fee is unlikely to tip a purchase decision at the margin — it represents less than half a month of the average car payment — but it adds to a list of headwinds that includes higher interest rates, reduced credits, and persistent range anxiety. Tesla, which carries the heaviest concentration of U.S. EV registrations, faces the most direct exposure if sentiment on EV economics continues to deteriorate.

States already there

Congress would not be pioneering new territory. Michigan currently charges EV owners $267 in additional annual registration fees; New Jersey, $270; Illinois, $100, with proposals in that state to raise it to $320. A federal $130 fee would stack on top of existing state charges in those markets, bringing the total surcharge for a Michigan EV owner to nearly $400 a year — against the $73 to $89 that a comparable gas-car driver pays the federal government.

The bill now heads into the full legislative process. The Senate has not released its version, and the fee provision is a natural target for amendment or elimination in conference. With the September 30 deadline four months out, there is time for the provision to be revised — the question is whether it survives that process intact, softened, or dropped entirely. The Senate's response will be the clearest indicator of whether this is durable policy or a bargaining chip.
