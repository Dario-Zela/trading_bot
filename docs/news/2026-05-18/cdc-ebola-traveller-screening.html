CDC enhances Ebola screening for travellers from affected regions

Colorized transmission electron micrograph of Ebola virus virions, showing the characteristic filamentous morphology of the pathogen. — Cynthia Goldsmith / CDC / Public Domain
In one sentence
WHO declared an Ebola global health emergency May 17 as the U.S. imposed its first-ever Ebola travel ban, restricting entry from DRC, Uganda, and South Sudan.

The United States moved Monday to restrict travel from three Central and East African countries and enhance screening at its ports of entry, as a fast-moving Ebola outbreak that the World Health Organization declared a global health emergency the previous day continues to expand.

The outbreak is concentrated in Ituri Province in northeastern Democratic Republic of Congo, with cases now confirmed across the border in Uganda — including in Kampala and Fort Portal. As of May 18, health authorities have recorded 12 confirmed cases and more than 395 suspected cases, with over 100 deaths reported. The case count has risen sharply since the WHO was first notified on May 5; laboratory confirmation of the pathogen came on May 14.

A strain without approved countermeasures

The pathogen driving the outbreak is the Bundibugyo ebolavirus — not the more familiar Zaire ebolavirus responsible for the catastrophic 2014–2016 West Africa epidemic and the 2018–2020 DRC outbreak. That distinction carries direct clinical weight. Every approved countermeasure — the rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine (Ervebo) and the monoclonal antibody therapies Inmazeb and Ebanga — targets Zaire ebolavirus specifically. None holds regulatory approval for use against the Bundibugyo variant.¹ Supportive care remains the primary treatment. Historical Bundibugyo outbreaks have recorded case fatality rates between 25% and 50%; this is only the third confirmed Bundibugyo outbreak in recorded history, following Uganda in 2007 and DRC in 2015.

An American physician among those infected

Among those infected is Dr. Peter Stafford, a U.S. medical missionary with the evangelical relief organization Serge who has served at Nyankunde Hospital near Bunia since 2023. Stafford tested positive for Bundibugyo ebolavirus on or around May 18, reportedly after treating infected patients at the hospital. His wife and a fellow missionary physician, Dr. Patrick LaRochelle, were also exposed and are under monitoring. U.S. officials have arranged for Stafford and exposed contacts — including Stafford's four children — to be transferred to Germany, selected for its advanced infectious disease treatment capacity and shorter flight time from the region.²

What the CDC is doing

CDC officials outlined the following measures at a Monday briefing, effective immediately:

  • Enhanced public health screening and traveler monitoring for individuals arriving from DRC, Uganda, and South Sudan.
  • Entry restrictions on non-U.S. passport holders who have traveled to any of those three countries within the previous 21 days. The order runs for 30 days and exempts U.S. citizens, lawful permanent residents, and active-duty military personnel.
  • Coordination with airlines and port-of-entry officials to identify and manage travelers who may have already been exposed.
  • Expanded domestic contact tracing, laboratory testing capacity, and hospital readiness nationwide.

The CDC has approximately 25 staff stationed in DRC through its country office and is deploying a senior technical coordinator from its Atlanta headquarters. The agency's Emergency Operations Center is activated. Officials characterized the immediate risk to the general U.S. public as low, citing Ebola's transmission route — direct contact with bodily fluids — rather than airborne or casual spread.

According to multiple reports, this is the first time the United States has imposed entry restrictions specifically in response to an Ebola outbreak.³

What to watch

The gap between confirmed cases (12) and suspected cases (395+) — a ratio of more than 30 to one — strongly suggests that surveillance and contact tracing in Ituri Province remain incomplete. The disease's 21-day incubation window means travelers who left DRC, Uganda, or South Sudan before Monday's restrictions were announced could still develop symptoms into early June. The critical near-term questions: whether the outbreak reaches additional high-traffic hubs beyond Kampala, whether the WHO's PHEIC designation mobilizes meaningful international surge capacity, and whether the Bundibugyo variant's spread prompts accelerated emergency-use authorization processes for cross-protective vaccine candidates currently in trials. The CDC's 7- and 14-day monitoring checkpoints for newly identified contacts will be the next substantive data points.
