French court convicts Airbus of manslaughter over the 2009 Rio-Paris crash, reviving legacy liability questions

Air France Airbus A330-203 registered F-GZCP — the aircraft that operated as Flight 447 on June 1, 2009, killing all 228 aboard — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Paris appeals court convicts Airbus and Air France of involuntary manslaughter over the 2009 AF447 crash, overturning a 2023 acquittal and fining each company €225,000 as both head to France's highest court.

On the morning of June 1, 2009, Air France Flight 447 — an Airbus A330-203 en route from Rio de Janeiro to Paris — disappeared over the South Atlantic with 228 people aboard. The aircraft hit the ocean in a full stall. It took two years to find the black boxes, and seventeen years to produce a criminal conviction.

On May 21, 2026, the Paris Court of Appeal found both Airbus (ENXTPA: AIR) and Air France guilty of involuntary manslaughter, overturning a 2023 acquittal in which the lower court had found negligence but concluded there was no provable causal link to the deaths. The appeals court rejected that reasoning in full. In its ruling, it held both companies "solely and entirely responsible" for the crash — and described the disaster as a "foretold catastrophe" that competent risk management could have prevented.

Each company was ordered to pay €225,000, the maximum fine available under French law for corporate manslaughter. Both plan to appeal.

"Airbus acknowledges the judgment delivered by the Paris Court of Appeal and the conviction of the company following the accident of Air France flight AF447 between Rio and Paris. Flight safety is the absolute priority for Airbus." — Airbus statement, May 21, 2026

A Foretold Catastrophe

The technical failure at the heart of AF447 was relatively well-understood long before the aircraft went down. Pitot tubes — external sensors that measure airspeed — were prone to icing at high altitude under certain atmospheric conditions. Airbus had recommended replacing the Thales-manufactured C16195AA sensors with an upgraded model as early as September 2007. Between May 2008 and March 2009, nine separate incidents of temporary airspeed loss were recorded on A330 aircraft across various operators. The aircraft that became AF447, registered F-GZCP, was scheduled for its tube replacement upon returning to Paris — a return it never made.

When the sensors iced over over the mid-Atlantic on the night of June 1, inconsistent airspeed readings caused the autopilot to disengage. The crew, presented with degraded and conflicting instrument data at cruise altitude, inadvertently entered a stall. They had not been trained for the precise failure mode they encountered. The aircraft entered an unrecoverable descent.

The court was precise on pilot culpability: the three-person crew "tried everything" and "nothing can be held against them." The failure, in the court's reading, belonged to the companies that put them in a situation for which they lacked the tools and training to recover.

For Airbus, the specific finding was that it underestimated the severity of the pitot tube risk and failed to adequately communicate operating hazards to airlines. For Air France, the finding was inadequate pilot training — the airline had not prepared crews for the high-altitude emergency that AF447's cascade of failures produced.

Symbolic Fine, Material Risk

The €225,000 fine levied on each company is, by any measure, immaterial to companies operating at their scale. Airbus's revenues run into the tens of billions of euros annually; Air France-KLM (EURONEXT: AF) is similarly sized. Even the court acknowledged the penalties are largely symbolic.

What is not symbolic is the conviction itself.

A criminal finding of liability creates a stronger foundation for civil claims than a mere finding of negligence. Air France paid initial compensation of roughly €17,500 per victim's family in the days following the crash, but families who have not fully settled — and any claims conditional on a definitive finding of fault — now stand on firmer legal ground. The civil exposure is harder to quantify precisely because so many individual settlements were reached privately, but the criminal conviction removes a significant barrier that the 2023 acquittal had maintained.

For investors, the conviction adds what one analyst framing characterised as a layer "in the regulatory and legal bucket rather than the core earnings story" — but that framing understates the second-order risks. Airlines, aircraft lessors, and government customers evaluate safety culture and governance when selecting suppliers. A criminal manslaughter conviction — however long in arriving — registers in procurement conversations. Tighter regulatory requirements in response to the ruling could also add compliance friction to Airbus programs already running under supply-chain constraint.

No individual Airbus or Air France executive faces criminal charges. That outcome drew explicit disappointment from families, particularly Brazilian relatives of victims. The absence of individual accountability limits the direct governance fallout, but it does not shield the companies from the reputational arithmetic of the verdict.

The Battle Continues

Neither company is accepting the ruling as final. Airbus confirmed it will lodge an appeal with the Cour de Cassation — France's highest court for criminal matters — and noted that the appeals court decision "contradicts the submissions of the Public Prosecutor's Office," the conclusions of a 2019 investigating judge's dismissal order, and the 2023 acquittal. The Cour de Cassation does not retry facts; it reviews whether the appeals court correctly applied French law. The process could extend two years or more.

Until that decision, the conviction stands. Airbus carries a criminal record in France for a disaster that killed 228 people, even if the legal status remains provisional.

The specific judicial question the Cour de Cassation will be asked to resolve — whether the appeals court was right to find a provable causal chain between the companies' failures and the deaths, where the lower court did not — is a narrow one. But its resolution will be consequential. A confirmation of the conviction would close seventeen years of legal proceedings with the verdict intact and potentially accelerate any remaining civil claims. A reversal would restore the 2023 acquittal, ending criminal liability while leaving open the broader reputational question that no court ruling can cleanly settle.

For Airbus shareholders, the next material date is not the filing of the appeal — that is already announced — but the Cour de Cassation's decision on whether to accept the case for review, a procedural threshold that will determine whether this enters a further phase or moves toward a definitive conclusion. Given the appeals court's explicit reversal of an earlier acquittal, that threshold may arrive with more uncertainty than markets currently appear to be pricing in.
