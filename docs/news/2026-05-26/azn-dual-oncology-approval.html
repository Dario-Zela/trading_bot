AstraZeneca secures dual regulatory wins with Enhertu and Datroway in a single day

AstraZeneca's Discovery Centre on the Cambridge Biomedical Campus, the company's global R&D headquarters since 2021 — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo land three regulatory wins in one day — tumor-agnostic EU backing for Enhertu, a CHMP nod for camizestrant, and FDA approval for Datroway in TNBC.

On May 22, a single day produced three separate regulatory verdicts for AstraZeneca and its Japanese partner Daiichi Sankyo: two positive opinions from Europe's Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use (CHMP) and one FDA approval, each targeting a different cancer type and a different patient population. The sequence is unusual by any measure, and it reflects a deliberate strategic architecture — the two companies have spent several years building a portfolio of precision-targeted antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), and regulators are now validating that bet across multiple fronts simultaneously.

The commercial stakes are not trivial. Enhertu (trastuzumab deruxtecan), the first and largest of the collaboration's ADC programs, generated $2.78 billion in revenue last year — a 40% increase year-on-year — within an AstraZeneca oncology franchise that reached $25.6 billion in 2025. The EU decision announced May 22 stretches that franchise into territory it had not formally occupied.

Enhertu steps beyond organ-by-organ approvals

The CHMP recommended Enhertu as a monotherapy for adults with unresectable or metastatic HER2-positive (IHC 3+) solid tumors who have received prior treatment and have no satisfactory remaining alternatives — regardless of where in the body the cancer originated. That tumor-agnostic framing matters. Enhertu already carried approvals in Europe for HER2-positive breast cancer, non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), and gastric cancer. The new opinion, which covers biliary tract, bladder, cervical, endometrial, ovarian, pancreatic, and colorectal tumors, does not so much add indications as collapse the need for them: one label tied to a biomarker rather than a tissue of origin.

If the European Commission ratifies the opinion — a step that typically follows within two to three months and is rarely refused — Enhertu would become the first HER2-directed therapy approved for a tumor-agnostic indication in the EU.

The clinical evidence rests on three phase 2 trials. DESTINY-PanTumor02 showed a 51.4% objective response rate across 111 previously treated patients with six different solid tumor types. DESTINY-Lung01 produced a 52.9% response rate in pretreated NSCLC. DESTINY-CRC02 showed 46.9% responses in colorectal cancer. These are meaningful numbers in populations where prior chemotherapy has already failed and alternatives are sparse.

"This positive CHMP opinion underscores the importance of precision oncology and marks an important step toward bringing a new targeted option to more patients," said Susan Galbraith, AstraZeneca's executive vice-president for oncology R&D. John Tsai, chief medical officer at Daiichi Sankyo, described Enhertu as offering "meaningful responses for patients with advanced cancers that overexpress HER2, who have limited treatment options."

Datroway: first survival win in a notoriously resistant cancer

While the CHMP was finalizing its Enhertu opinion, the FDA approved a separate AstraZeneca-Daiichi ADC on the same day. Datroway (datopotamab deruxtecan) — a TROP2-directed DXd conjugate rather than a HER2-directed one — was cleared for first-line use in adults with unresectable or metastatic triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) who are not candidates for PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitor therapy.

Triple-negative breast cancer is defined by what it lacks: estrogen receptors, progesterone receptors, and HER2 amplification. That combination has historically placed it beyond the reach of targeted therapies that have transformed care in other subtypes. For the subset of TNBC patients who are also ineligible for immunotherapy — the current backbone of first-line treatment in immunotherapy-eligible TNBC — cytotoxic chemotherapy has been the only available option.

The TROPION-Breast02 phase 3 trial enrolled 644 patients. Those receiving Datroway achieved a median overall survival of 23.7 months versus 18.7 months for investigator's choice of chemotherapy — a five-month gap that was statistically significant. Progression-free survival ran 10.8 months against 5.6 months; objective response rates were 64% versus 30%. The trial is the first in this patient population to demonstrate a statistically significant overall survival improvement over chemotherapy.

Dr. Tiffany A. Traina, a clinical investigator involved in the program, called the approval "a much-needed treatment option for these patients." Datroway's label notes risks consistent with the DXd payload class — interstitial lung disease, ocular adverse reactions, stomatitis, and embryo-fetal toxicity — that require monitoring protocols.

The TROP2 program originated from AstraZeneca's separate partnership agreement with Daiichi Sankyo, under which AstraZeneca committed up to $6 billion for co-development rights. That investment is now generating its first major regulatory return in a setting with few prior approved targeted agents.

Camizestrant: a transatlantic divergence worth watching

The third decision on May 22 is the most textured. The CHMP recommended approval for camizestrant — under the brand name Etcamah — in combination with a CDK4/6 inhibitor (palbociclib, ribociclib, or abemaciclib) for adults with ER-positive, HER2-negative locally advanced or metastatic breast cancer who develop an ESR1 mutation during first-line endocrine therapy, before disease progression.

The pivotal SERENA-6 phase 3 trial, presented at the 2025 ASCO Annual Meeting and simultaneously published in The New England Journal of Medicine, showed camizestrant reducing the risk of progression or death by 54% compared with continued aromatase inhibitor plus CDK4/6 inhibitor (median PFS: 16.6 months versus 9.2 months; hazard ratio 0.46; P < 0.00001). The two-year progression-free survival rate was 32.2% versus 14.3%. Discontinuations due to adverse events ran at just 1.3%.

The CHMP's endorsement is significant partly because the FDA moved differently. On April 30, the FDA's Oncologic Drugs Advisory Committee voted 3 to 6 against the benefit-risk profile of camizestrant in the same indication, expressing reservations the European panel apparently did not find decisive. Advisory committee votes are non-binding, and the FDA has not issued a final ruling; AstraZeneca has said it will continue working with the agency through the review process. The split is a reminder that tumor board and regulatory science can arrive at genuinely different conclusions from the same dataset — particularly when the intervention is early (switching therapy ahead of progression) and the surrogate endpoint (PFS rather than OS) is the primary anchor.

What the franchise adds up to

The cumulative read across these three decisions is that AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo are executing on a strategy that began with a single ADC — Enhertu — and has since expanded into a multi-target, multi-indication platform. The tumor-agnostic Enhertu opinion, if converted to a formal EC decision as expected, extends a single manufacturing and commercial line into cancers that were previously addressed separately or not at all. Datroway carves into a breast cancer subtype that has long resisted precision medicine. Camizestrant adds a combination hormone-therapy angle that, in Europe at least, regulators now consider adequately supported.

The next hard milestone is the FDA's final decision on camizestrant, for which the agency faces a statutory deadline to act. Whether Washington follows Brussels — or holds to the ODAC majority's skepticism — will determine whether camizestrant becomes a transatlantic franchise or a European-only one. For Enhertu, the European Commission clock starts now.
