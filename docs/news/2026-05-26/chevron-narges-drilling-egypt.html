Chevron spuds new well at Egypt's Narges gas field

Offshore jacket installation in the Eastern Mediterranean, 2012 — the basin where Chevron is now advancing development at the Narges gas field. — BoH / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Chevron spuds Egypt's Narges gas field via the Stena Forth drillship — one of two 2026 Egyptian wells targeting a 50% regional capacity increase alongside Eni and Mubadala.

Chevron has begun drilling a new well at Egypt's Narges natural gas field, deploying the Stena Forth drillship to a concession in the country's Eastern Mediterranean offshore acreage. The move converts a planned expansion into active capital deployment at a field carrying roughly 3.5 trillion cubic feet of estimated reserves.

Egyptian Petroleum Minister Karim Badawi was on hand to witness the spud on May 23, framing the start of operations as central to a government push to "motivate international oil companies to accelerate the exploitation of undeveloped gas discoveries" — a phrase that doubles as an endorsement and a mild pressure campaign on a sector that has been slow to commit in the region.

A 50% capacity target, one well at a time

The Narges field is operated by Chevron, which holds a 45% working interest, alongside Eni and its subsidiary IEOC (also 45%) and Egypt's Tharwa Petroleum Company (10%), with the Egyptian Natural Gas Holding Company, EGAS, also party to the concession structure. The consortium is long-established; the active drilling is not.

Clay Neff, Chevron's president of exploration operations, had set out the ambition in March: raise regional production capacity by as much as 50% over five years, with Egypt at the centre of that effort. Narges is one half of a two-well Egyptian drilling programme for 2026 — the second well is planned in the Western Mediterranean in partnership with Shell.

That programme, taken together, reads as a deliberate vote of confidence in Egypt's upstream regulatory environment at a moment when the broader MENA region is harder to read. Extended ceasefire negotiations and ongoing noise around Gulf shipping lanes have unsettled some energy market participants, but Chevron's capital commitment suggests the Egyptian offshore is, for now, operating in a different risk register. (The U.S.-Iran ceasefire extension and its implications for Hormuz traffic are covered separately in this edition.)

What the market is watching

The immediate question for energy traders is timing. Commercial operations at Narges were originally scheduled to begin in 2025, which means the programme is already running roughly a year behind the initial plan. The current well is a development drill rather than a wildcat, which limits the upside surprise from a reserves standpoint but also reduces the binary risk.

Egypt's position as an LNG transit hub adds downstream optionality. The Idku and Damietta liquefaction terminals give incremental Narges volumes a direct route to European markets, where demand for non-Russian supply remains structurally supported. That route is one reason international operators continue to prioritise Egyptian acreage over other Eastern Mediterranean alternatives that lack comparable export infrastructure.

The next concrete markers: completion timeline for the Stena Forth at Narges, and a confirmed spud date for the Western Mediterranean well. If the second well slips into 2027, the 50% capacity growth target will start to look more aspirational than operational.
