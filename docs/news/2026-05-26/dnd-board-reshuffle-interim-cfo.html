Dye & Durham reshuffles board again and names interim CFO

Bay and King Streets in Toronto's financial district, where Dye & Durham is headquartered. — George Socka / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Dye & Durham named its second consecutive interim CFO and a third board chair in six months, extending a governance churn that has coincided with a 58% stock decline.

Dye & Durham (DND.TO), the Toronto-listed provider of cloud-based legal-practice-management software, on Monday named Steve Waszak as interim chief financial officer — replacing Sandra Bell, who was herself an interim appointment — while simultaneously shuffling its board chair and welcoming co-founder Tyler Proud back to the boardroom.

The triple-change announcement, made May 25, adds another chapter to a governance saga that has become something of a Canadian corporate running joke. "I think we're now seeing the final season of my favourite daytime soap opera," David Barr, chief executive of PenderFund Capital Management, said earlier this year as the churn accelerated.

A CFO seat with no permanent occupant

The revolving door on the CFO title is the starkest signal of organizational strain. Avjit Kamboj was rehired into the role in June 2025, having previously held it from 2019 to 2022. He was dismissed without cause by late July — barely two months into his second stint — reportedly amid friction with incoming chief executive George Tsivin, who joined from LexisNexis in the same month. Bell stepped in as interim that July. Now, with Waszak's appointment, Dye & Durham has burned through two consecutive interim CFOs in under twelve months without naming a permanent one. The company says the permanent search continues; no timeline has been set.

Waszak's professional background was not disclosed in Monday's press release.

Board reshuffle, again

On the board, Mary Filippelli succeeds Eddie Smith as chair. Smith was installed in the role only in December 2025, as part of a settlement agreement Dye & Durham struck with OneMove Capital Ltd. — the investment vehicle of Tyler Proud. Smith has now resigned. Proud himself takes the seat vacated by Wendy Cheah, also an OneMove nominee, completing what amounts to a proxy pivot that puts the co-founder back at the table.

Filippelli brings a credible governance résumé: former vice-chair and managing partner at Deloitte Canada, with senior roles at Lloyds Banking Group and KPMG Canada, and current board service at Sylogist Ltd., Canadian Western Bank, and Fidelity Investments Canada.

As part of the arrangement, Proud and OneMove have committed to support the company's shareholder rights plan at a June 9 meeting and to back the full director slate at the 2026 annual general meeting — for now, the most visible activist pressure point appears contained.

The operating backdrop remains difficult. First-half fiscal 2025 revenue fell 7% year-over-year to C$215.3 million; adjusted EBITDA dropped 24% to C$100.8 million. The stock has shed roughly 58% of its value over the past 52 weeks, trading at approximately C$2.94 on Monday. Dye & Durham confirmed it is conducting a strategic review of non-core assets to reduce debt, and was placed on the block for the second time in a year before Monday's changes — without a deal materialising either time.

The June 9 shareholder meeting is the immediate watchpoint. Beyond it: the 2026 AGM vote on directors, and — still absent after nearly a year — a permanent CFO.
