The Fed put is dead: strategists warn structural forces will keep yields high and the Mag-7 cycle may be turning

Mohamed El-Erian, chief economic adviser at Allianz, has argued the central bank backstop that underpinned markets for two decades is structurally compromised. — World Economic Forum / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
El-Erian says the Fed put is gone; with yields structurally high and Mag-7 carrying the market, there is no policy safety net if tech earnings disappoint.

For more than a decade, every serious market selloff came with an implicit question: how long before the Fed intervenes? The answer, in 2008, 2020, and the mini-panics in between, was reliably: not long. That backstop — informally called the "Fed put," a reference to the options contract that limits downside — became so embedded in market practice that it shaped how investors sized risk, priced volatility, and structured portfolios. Long equity, short volatility, expect rescue: a strategy that worked until it didn't.

Mohamed El-Erian, economist and Allianz chief economic adviser, says that era is over. Most advanced economies now face structural policy constraints that prevent central banks from rushing to markets' aid whenever equities slide. In place of that backstop, investors have substituted something else: the earnings power of big technology companies and the transformative promise of artificial intelligence. The substitution may hold — until it doesn't.

The structural yield floor

The clearest evidence that the old regime has changed is in the bond market. The 10-year US Treasury yield hit nearly 4.70% earlier this month before easing slightly to 4.56% on Friday — and strategists across Barclays, Bank of America, ING, and Goldman Sachs have each concluded, in varying terms, that yields will remain elevated even if the Iran conflict resolves entirely. The 60-day ceasefire extension has done nothing to move the Treasury market.

"The bond market is not reacting to one headline," said Mark Malek, chief investment officer at Muriel Siebert & Co. Jonathan Hill, Barclays' head of US inflation strategy, put the analytical tension plainly: "The argument that duration is selling off globally due to inflation fears is hard to square with market pricing." What's actually driving yields higher is real rates, not inflation expectations. US 10-year breakeven inflation sits roughly 50 basis points below its first-half 2022 peaks; the 5-year, 5-year forward breakeven is near 2.2% — levels that don't signal panic. The move is structural: fiscal deficits, a changing supply-demand profile for Treasuries, and an inflation floor that central banks are reluctant to fight too aggressively.

Goldman's Phillip Lee was unambiguous: "I think rates are going higher." ING's Padhraic Garvey added that "Treasury yields don't collapse lower as many currently anticipate." Geopolitical resolution, in other words, is not the bond relief trade that bulls are waiting for. The driver is domestic and durable.

The Mag-7 lifeline — and its limits

Into the gap left by a constrained Fed has stepped big tech. The Nasdaq is up 13.38% year-to-date; the S&P 500 has gained 8.97%; the Dow, more exposed to the broader economy, trails at 4.54%. That spread tells the story. Performance has concentrated sharply in the seven largest technology and technology-adjacent names — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Tesla — with AI-driven capital expenditure cycles providing the earnings narrative that replaced the monetary backstop.

Tom Lee, head of research at Fundstrat Global Advisors, crystallized the tension last week with a call that landed on both sides of the argument simultaneously. The Mag-7 bear market, he said, is over. "I think we've already had a bear market in Mag-7 and software" — the names have bottomed, and AI-driven demand will keep them supported.

But the next sentence carries more weight for risk managers: "I think there's going to be a bear market in other stocks later this year." A "day of reckoning" is coming for non-Mag-7 names — the rest of Wall Street that has ridden the tide without comparable earnings momentum. Lee's primary near-term trigger is an energy crunch tied to thin petroleum inventory, but the structural point is broader: "stocks that are working are the ones that are selling something that's scarce." In 2026, that means AI infrastructure suppliers and Big Tech. Everything else is exposed.

Concentration risk cuts in both directions. When Nvidia and Microsoft carry an index, the index is only as stable as their earnings and their multiples. That's precisely the scenario El-Erian's warning anticipates. If AI spending cycles disappoint — if hyperscalers blink on capex, or if the physical constraints on the $8 trillion buildout prove binding sooner than expected — there is no policy backstop waiting to absorb the selling. The Fed, constrained by above-target inflation and a deficit that limits fiscal room, cannot pivot quickly. The safety net that conditioned two decades of risk-taking has been structurally compromised.

What to watch

For portfolio managers, the next test arrives in two waves. Nvidia's late-May earnings will serve as the live read on AI capital expenditure appetite — any softness in data center guidance will reverberate through the concentration trade immediately. Beyond that, the Treasury market's response to upcoming CPI data matters more than usual: if real yields continue to climb, the premium required to hold equities at current multiples rises with them, compressing the very tech earnings story that replaced the Fed put.

The market has found a new foundation. The open question is whether it's load-bearing — and who discovers the answer first.
