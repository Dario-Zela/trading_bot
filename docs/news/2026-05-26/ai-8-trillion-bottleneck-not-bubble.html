AI's $8 trillion buildout is a physical-world bottleneck, not a bubble, analyst argues

Server racks at a U.S. data centre facility — only 12–18% of the projected $8 trillion AI infrastructure buildout is in place, Visser estimates. — Carl Lender / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 2.0)
In one sentence
22V Research's Jordi Visser argues AI's $8T capex cycle is physically constrained — power grids, transformers, copper — setting up a 1970s-style inflation regime, not a bubble pop.

The conventional argument against AI spending is familiar by now: too much money chasing too little return, a valuation bubble dressed up in compute. Jordi Visser, who heads AI Macro Nexus Research at 22V Research, thinks that framing is simply wrong — and that getting it wrong is costing investors meaningful money.

Visser's thesis, laid out in research published this week, is that the AI buildout is not a demand-constrained cycle waiting to hit a wall of disappointing returns. It is a supply-constrained cycle — a physical-world capex wave that will be throttled not by fading enthusiasm but by power grids, high-voltage transformers, copper supplies, and gas turbines. The distinction matters enormously for how the next several years play out.

"The deeper point is that this is a physical-world capex cycle, not a software one."
— Jordi Visser, 22V Research

His starting number: $8 trillion in projected AI infrastructure spending. Of that, Visser estimates only 12% to 18% has been deployed so far. The stress appearing across supply chains, in other words, is arriving at the very beginning of the build — not near the end.

The bottleneck argument

The evidence that physical constraints rather than demand are the binding factor is accumulating. Nearly seven gigawatts of planned U.S. AI data centre capacity for 2026 has been delayed or cancelled — not because hyperscalers ran out of appetite, but because the power is not there. Utilities require three to seven years to deliver grid interconnections at the scale that Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta need. Lead times for high-voltage transformers, the workhorses of substation buildout, have stretched from a pre-AI-boom 12 to 18 months to as long as 36 to 48 months in some cases. Roughly 40% of announced AI data centre projects are sitting behind construction delays attributable to power infrastructure, not chips.

The four major hyperscalers spent more than $80 billion in Q1 2026 alone — an annualised rate above $320 billion from just four companies — against combined 2026 capex guidance approaching $725 billion. Nvidia's Jensen Huang has cited a $90 trillion physical AI upgrade cycle globally; cloud service backlogs already exceed $2 trillion. Visser's point is that this spending is real, contracted, and being funded by companies with enormous free cash flows. It is nothing like the dot-com era, when spending outran revenue by years and was funded by equity issuance into frothy markets.

What is different — and what constitutes the analytical update — is that the bottlenecks are in the physical economy, not in software licensing agreements. High-bandwidth memory, liquid cooling systems, copper, fibre, substations, gas turbines: these are the rate-limiters now. The constraint is no longer capital or chips. It is electrons.

A 1970s regime in disguise

This is where Visser's analysis becomes genuinely provocative for equity investors. If the binding constraint is physical rather than financial, the macroeconomic regime that follows looks less like the 2010s software boom and more like the 1970s industrial cycle — a period most active fund managers have never traded.

The hallmarks are already visible. CPI has climbed to around 3.8% year-over-year, the largest print excluding COVID since 2012. The Federal Reserve, in Visser's reading, has been repriced roughly 100 basis points more hawkish since the Strait of Hormuz closure; the 10-year Treasury yield is up approximately 75 basis points. With 3-month T-bill yields likely to fall below CPI for the first time since 2023, real rates on cash are turning negative — a classic inflation-regime signal.

Growth equities tend to underperform in that environment. The 1970s analogue is not perfect — it never is — but the mechanism is: when physical scarcity, not soft demand, sets the pace of investment, pricing power shifts upstream toward commodity producers and infrastructure builders, and away from asset-light software names.

Where the money goes from here

Visser's investment conclusions follow from the diagnosis. He has sold two-thirds of his Micron position. Memory — the early-cycle AI semiconductor winner — has, in his view, largely run its leg. The "five-layer AI cake," as he calls it, is shifting from the chip layer toward the energy and commodities layer.

Silver is his most publicised conviction — trading near $80 an ounce after a 140% year-over-year gain, it is a structural input to advanced packaging, optical fibre, and battery systems. Caterpillar, whose $62 billion backlog includes guidance for power sales to triple by 2030, represents the power infrastructure layer. Specialty chemicals — Visser singles out The Chemours Company, up 88% year-to-date — feed the same advanced-manufacturing chain. Independent power producers and grid equipment firms fill out the picture.

The unifying concept he calls "benchmark arbitrage": passive index funds, built for the software age, are mechanically overweight old beneficiaries and underweight the actual value creators of the AI infrastructure era — power, copper, silver, chemicals, packaging, and fibre. An active manager benchmarked against the S&P 500 sits in roughly the same position of structural mismatch.

The specific catalyst to watch: the trajectory of CPI over the next two quarters. If inflation continues to drift above bill yields and physical bottlenecks sustain rather than ease — transformer lead times, grid interconnections, power-plant permitting — the 1970s-regime thesis strengthens considerably. The alternative, a sudden easing of supply constraints through policy action or technological substitution, would relieve pressure on both yields and commodity prices. Neither outcome is yet locked in; only 12 to 18 cents of the $8 trillion dollar has been spent.
