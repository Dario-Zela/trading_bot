Voyager Technologies surges 30% after joint venture secures LEO expansion capital

An artist's rendering of Starlab, the commercial space station targeting a 2029 launch to replace the retiring International Space Station — Starlab Space / Space.com
In one sentence
Starlab, Voyager's commercial LEO joint venture, secured an undisclosed investment from 1789 Capital, lifting VOYG shares 30% as the race to succeed the ISS tightens ahead of its 2030 deorbit.

Voyager Technologies (NYSE: VOYG) shares rose 29.6% on the week after its joint-venture unit, Starlab Space Stations, secured a strategic investment from 1789 Capital — a Washington-backed growth fund whose thesis sits at the intersection of patriotic capital and uncorrelated returns. Financial terms of the deal were not disclosed.

The investment adds a fresh institutional name to the Starlab consortium, which already includes Airbus, Mitsubishi Corporation, MDA Space, Palantir Technologies, and Space Applications Services, with Hilton, Northrop Grumman, and The Ohio State University as strategic partners. Starlab is designed as the primary U.S.-led successor to the International Space Station, which NASA plans to deorbit in 2030.

Filling the gap before 2030

The strategic logic is not complicated: when the ISS comes down, something has to go up. Starlab — an 8-metre-diameter, 450-cubic-metre station capable of housing four permanent crew, launching atop a SpaceX Starship — is one of a handful of commercial platforms NASA is backing to fill that void. It completed its 28th milestone under a NASA Space Act Agreement in February, passing a commercial critical design review and formally moving from design validation into fabrication, testing, and assembly. A launch date of 2029 is the current target.

Matt Kuta, Voyager's president, framed the stakes with an eye toward Washington rather than Wall Street. "As the ISS approaches retirement, we cannot afford a gap in American presence in low-Earth orbit while the Tiangong is expanding," he said in the May 21 announcement. "Starlab is how the United States answers that challenge, not just for the commercial market, but for every national security and scientific mission requiring assured access to LEO."

1789 Capital's founder Omeed Malik, who co-founded the firm in 2022 with Chris Buskirk, matched the register: "America built the space age and must lead the next one." The firm describes its mandate as targeting the Entrepreneurship, Innovation & Growth economy — a category it defines as encompassing critical infrastructure from the digital to the orbital. It is currently reviewing more than 20 targets across AI and software, defense-tech, fintech, space, and data infrastructure.

The absence of a disclosed investment amount makes the funding story harder to evaluate on its merits. A 30% weekly move on an undisclosed cheque from a three-year-old growth fund is, at minimum, a market reading momentum as much as fundamentals.

Voyager's underlying position, though, has been improving independently. First-quarter 2026 results showed a record backlog of $275.3 million — up 54% year-on-year — and the company raised full-year 2026 revenue guidance to a range of $230 million to $255 million, implying 38% to 53% growth over 2025. NASA also selected Voyager for a private astronaut mission to the ISS, designated VOYG-1, targeting launch no earlier than 2028.

The next market test comes quickly. Voyager is scheduled at three investor conferences in the next four weeks: KeyBanc Capital Markets in Boston on May 31, Wells Fargo Industrials in Chicago on June 10, and the Deutsche Bank Defence Conference on June 22. The last venue says something about how management wants institutional capital to think about the platform — less sci-fi, more sovereign-infrastructure.
