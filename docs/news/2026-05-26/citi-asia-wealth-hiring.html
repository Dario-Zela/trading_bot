Citigroup accelerates Asia wealth push with near-500-hire expansion

Citi Tower at One Bay East, Kwun Tong — Citigroup's principal Hong Kong office and a hub for its Asia wealth operations. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Citigroup is adding ~500 staff to its Asia wealth business — 100 private bankers plus 400 specialists — as Asia now generates 35% of the bank's global wealth revenue.

Citigroup is moving to substantially expand its wealth management operations across Asia, with plans to hire roughly 100 private bankers and approximately 400 supporting specialists — the bulk of the recruitment concentrated in the region that now generates nearly 35% of the bank's global wealth revenue.

The numbers emerged from comments made by Andy Sieg, Citigroup's global wealth head, during a visit to Hong Kong. Sieg described Asia as "the fastest-growing and most productive segment within the private bank" and said the hiring push would be meaningfully anchored in the region, without specifying an exact market-by-market breakdown. The expansion spans Citi's full Asia footprint: Japan, the Asia North cluster, Singapore and South-East Asia, and Australia. Hong Kong and Singapore — the two markets where Citi retained its consumer and wealth operations after exiting 14 others under CEO Jane Fraser's restructuring — are expected to absorb the heaviest concentration of new hires.

The returns case

Citi is at pains to frame the move as a profitability play, not simply a geographic rebalance. The wealth unit posted net income of $1.5 billion in 2025, up nearly 50% year-over-year, with Asia alone contributing roughly $3 billion of total wealth revenues. In the first quarter of 2026, the wealth unit's income metric surged 126% against the prior-year quarter — a trajectory that makes the case for further capital deployment relatively direct.

The bank has set return on tangible common equity targets of 15–20% for the wealth unit in 2027–28, rising above 20% over the medium term. Reaching those thresholds through organic growth means deploying bankers where productivity per head is highest — which, by Sieg's own accounting, is Asia.

The macro tailwind is substantial. The Asia-Pacific wealth management market carried an estimated $27–34 trillion in assets under management in 2025, forecast to expand at a compound annual rate of roughly 7–8% through 2031. Asia is the only region where new client money in the high-net-worth and ultra-high-net-worth segments is still growing at a double-digit pace, driven by rising cross-border wealth creation and a surge in demand for investment advisory services among clients who want global banking access alongside local relationships.

Citi is not alone in this conviction. UBS, HSBC, and DBS have all signalled expanded private bank headcount in the region this year — a synchronised hiring trend that functions as a leading indicator of where the industry collectively expects asset flows to concentrate. For Citi specifically, the practical test will be whether a near-500-strong hiring wave translates to durable market share gains in a market where talent competition is fierce and client portability is high. The next clear data point: Q2 2026 wealth revenues, due in mid-July.
