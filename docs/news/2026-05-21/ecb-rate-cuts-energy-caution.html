ECB flags caution on further cuts as Middle East energy shock clouds eurozone outlook

The ECB's twin-tower headquarters in Frankfurt, where policymakers held rates at 2% for the third consecutive meeting on 30 April 2026. — Thomas Pohl / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
ECB held rates at 2% as Iran-war energy shock drove eurozone inflation to 3%, swinging OIS pricing from expected cuts to ~40bp of hikes by year-end.

The European Central Bank left its deposit rate at 2.00% on 30 April — its third consecutive hold — but the meeting's significance lay less in the decision itself than in what ECB President Christine Lagarde said must change before cuts are back on the table: not just inflation figures, but the energy market.

"The war in the Middle East has led to a sharp increase in energy prices, pushing up inflation and weighing on economic sentiment," Lagarde told reporters after the Governing Council's unanimous vote. She was candid about the scale of what Frankfurt is up against. "War, ceasefire, peace talks, their collapse, a naval blockade, its lifting, its reinstatement — makes it exceptionally hard to gauge the duration and depth of the consequences."

The numbers back her caution. Energy price inflation jumped to 10.9% in April, up from 5.1% in March, pulling headline eurozone HICP to 3.0% — a full percentage point above the ECB's target. Core inflation, which strips out food and energy, ticked down slightly to 2.2%, suggesting the shock remains primarily external rather than embedded in domestic wage dynamics. No second-round effects are visible yet. That is the one piece of comfort available to Frankfurt.

From Data-Dependent to Geopolitically Contingent

The framing shift matters for anyone trading European rates or the single currency. The ECB's deposit rate had been cut to 2% through an easing sequence that ended in late 2025, and forward guidance had pointed toward further reductions if CPI continued cooling toward target. That calculus has been overwritten by the Iran conflict.

The International Energy Agency described the resulting supply disruption as the largest in the history of the global oil market — the net loss of oil estimated at around 13 million barrels per day. Brent briefly surpassed $126 per barrel in late April. ECB staff revised their 2026 oil price assumptions upward by nearly 30% and gas assumptions by 57% relative to December projections. Lagarde, speaking in March, put the policy constraint plainly: "Monetary policy cannot bring down energy prices. But we must identify when higher energy costs risk spilling over into broad-based inflation."

A 6 May address by Executive Board member Piero Cipollone sharpened the picture. "We are facing the second major energy shock in just four years," he said, adding that "the current situation seems to be drifting away from our March baseline projections, which increases the likelihood that we may need to adjust our policy rates." Notably, he framed adjustment as potentially going in either direction — a signal that the ECB's next move is genuinely open.

Rate expectations have repriced sharply. Overnight index swap markets, which embedded at least one cut before the conflict began, now price roughly 40 basis points of tightening by year-end. Prediction markets put the probability of any 2026 rate cut at around 31%, down from near-certainty earlier in the year. The euro has benefited modestly: it rose 0.2% against the dollar to $1.17 after the April decision, as higher-for-longer expectations provide a carry floor. Ten-year Bund yields fell 3 basis points to 3.06% on the day, reflecting the growth headwind in the same shock — the ECB's own staff projections now see eurozone GDP averaging just 0.9% in 2026.

Lagarde's signal at the press conference was precise: "We believe that in six weeks we will be able to make a more informed decision, either because the conflict will have an outcome or the consequences will be clearer." The June 11-12 meeting is thus explicitly framed as a reassessment point, with current market odds placing the probability of a 25-basis-point hike at better than even. Whether energy prices stabilise — or whether an Iran-US settlement relieves Hormuz pressure before then — is the variable that no central bank model was built to forecast.
