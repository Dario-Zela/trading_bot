UK planning reform bill clears key parliamentary hurdle

The Palace of Westminster: the government's planning reform bill cleared its first substantive Commons vote this week — Arpingstone / Wikimedia Commons (public domain)
In one sentence
Labour's new Planning and Infrastructure Bill cleared Commons Second Reading, advancing its flagship housebuilding agenda — but Royal Assent is 18 months away at minimum.

The government's new planning reform bill cleared its second reading in the House of Commons this week, handing the Starmer administration a domestic policy milestone and formal parliamentary approval to advance housing acceleration legislation it first began architecting after taking office nearly two years ago.

The new Planning and Infrastructure Bill — the flagship legislative piece from last week's King's Speech — passed its second reading with the government's parliamentary majority intact. The vote does not signal the end of a difficult journey; the bill faces committee scrutiny, a Lords passage, and secondary regulations that could stretch implementation well into 2028. But the approval marks the bill's first meaningful parliamentary test and, politically, confirms that dissent on its core principles is contained.

The second wave

The new legislation is deliberately sequenced to build on what the Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025 — which received Royal Assent in December — left unfinished. That predecessor act focused on nationally significant infrastructure: it capped judicial review attempts, streamlined grid connection approvals, and established a Nature Restoration Fund to let developers offset environmental obligations at scale rather than site by site. The government projects those reforms will boost GDP by up to £7.5 billion over the next decade.

The 2026 bill turns attention to the local planning system, which the government regards as a persistent brake on delivery. Its headline provisions: funding 300 additional planning officers across local authorities, reforming planning committees to concentrate scrutiny on significant developments rather than householder extensions, and — most consequentially for developers — amending compulsory purchase compensation rules to limit hope value payments on sites assembled for affordable housing, schools, and health facilities.

The context is forbidding. Planning permissions in England fell to a 15-year low in the year to September 2025, when just 209,781 new homes were approved — 38 per cent below the peak recorded in early 2022. Over one million homes that have already cleared the planning system remain unbuilt. England completed roughly 200,000 homes in 2025, a long way from the 300,000-plus annual run-rate the government's 1.5 million-homes target implies.

Housing and Planning Minister Matthew Pennycook, speaking at the UKREiiF property investment forum in Leeds on Monday, described the effort as "the most rapid, holistic and radical overhaul of the housing and planning system in decades." He acknowledged growing economic headwinds but said the government intends to "stay the course and finish the job."

Priced in, or a genuine catalyst?

For listed housebuilders, the Second Reading is incremental rather than transformative. Persimmon, Taylor Wimpey, Bellway and Vistry already carry optimism about falling mortgage rates and easier consenting in their forward multiples, after a year in which the sector was essentially flat against a rising broader market. Planning reform adds another support, but industry surveys flag weak buyer confidence, rising build costs, and a skills deficit as more binding near-term constraints than the consenting regime itself.

The compulsory purchase provisions are arguably the clause most likely to reprice landowner expectations — and, in turn, make more brownfield sites economically viable. That repricing, if it occurs, would take years to propagate through the development pipeline.

The bill moves to committee stage in June. Lords scrutiny is expected in autumn. Pennycook's office has not put a Royal Assent date on record; late 2027 is a reasonable working assumption. The next meaningful parliamentary indicator to watch is whether the government accepts or resists amendments on hope value at committee — that clause has drawn the most industry and landowner opposition, and its fate will signal how far ministers are willing to defend the bill's redistributive intent.
