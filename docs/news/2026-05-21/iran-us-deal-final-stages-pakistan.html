Iran-US deal enters final stages with Pakistan brokering the text — tanker standoff simmers

A vessel transits the Strait of Hormuz, the waterway at the centre of Iran's sovereignty demands and the U.S. naval blockade that has choked Iranian ports since February. — Mass Communication Specialist 3rd Class Andrew Waters / U.S. Navy
In one sentence
Trump declared Iran talks in 'final stages' as Pakistan shuttles a 14-point MOU draft between capitals — even as US Marines boarded an Iranian tanker hours later.

On the same day that U.S. Marines boarded an Iranian-flagged oil tanker in the Gulf of Oman, Donald Trump told reporters that negotiations with Tehran to end a war that has gripped the Middle East since February were in their "final stages." The juxtaposition—coercive enforcement and claimed diplomatic progress operating simultaneously—captures exactly where this long-running crisis now stands. A deal that seemed unreachable a fortnight ago is suddenly within sight, with Pakistan playing the unlikely role of textual midwife.

Pakistan's Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi made his second trip to Tehran in a single week on May 20, carrying revised Iranian positions to share with Washington.

Markets noticed. U.S. Treasuries rallied sharply on the "final stages" signal; Brent crude has shed ground as traders price out some of the geopolitical risk premium that has inflated energy costs since hostilities began in late February.

The shuttle diplomat

Pakistan's emergence as broker in what is arguably the most consequential diplomacy of the decade was not accidental. Islamabad sits at a unique intersection: a close partner of Washington's on counterterrorism and defence, while maintaining deep historical, religious, and economic ties with Tehran. When direct US-Iran talks collapsed and Oman's capacity to bridge the gap proved insufficient, Pakistan stepped in.

Field Marshal Asim Munir, Pakistan's army chief, flew to Tehran in mid-April to meet President Masoud Pezeshkian, Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, and senior military commanders. That visit established a working channel that has since operated almost continuously. Pakistan's interior minister, Mohsin Naqvi, has become a near-permanent fixture in both capitals, ferrying documents, softening language, and testing red lines in real time. On May 16, Naqvi was in Tehran to discuss the "resumption of negotiations" with his Iranian counterpart; four days later, he was back again.

The framework now taking shape is anchored in a 14-point memorandum of understanding, with Trump envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner negotiating the American side while Iranian officials engage both directly and through intermediaries. In its current form, the document would declare a formal end to hostilities and open a 30-day intensive negotiation window on the three core issues: the Strait of Hormuz's legal status, limits on Iran's nuclear programme, and the lifting of U.S. sanctions.

The note worth making here is the document's origin. Iran submitted a 14-point counter-response to a 15-point American framework drafted in late March—a shift from the earlier dynamic, in which Tehran had largely responded to Washington's framing with maximalist public rhetoric. Iran's willingness to engage in a structured, numbered exchange of proposals is itself a signal. It suggests both sides have moved from posturing to the harder work of bridging specific differences.

Fourteen points, many sticking points

Iran's proposal, as described by its deputy foreign minister, conditions everything on three parallel tracks: lifting the U.S. naval blockade that has choked Iranian ports since the conflict began; releasing frozen Iranian assets held abroad; and guaranteeing that any nuclear limits come with binding sanctions relief rather than phased promises. Tehran also demands recognition of its sovereignty claims over the Strait of Hormuz—a point the United States has resisted, since accepting it would effectively legitimise Iran's wartime toll collection on commercial shipping.

Trump's public preconditions, stated in mid-May, run in a different direction. Washington wants Iran to hand over 400 kilograms of enriched uranium to U.S. custody, to mothball all but one nuclear facility, and to accept a moratorium on further enrichment—without any war reparations and with only partial release of frozen funds. The gap between those positions and Iran's 14 points is not trivial.

What appears to have shifted in the past week is less the substance than the tone. On May 20, Pezeshkian posted a statement that circulated widely in diplomatic circles: "Iran has consistently honored its commitments and explored every avenue to avert war; all paths remain open from our side. Forcing Iran to surrender through coercion is nothing but an illusion. Mutual respect in diplomacy is far wiser, safer, and more sustainable than war."

The phrasing—calibrated, not defiant—was read in Washington and in Islamabad as a deliberate signal of continued engagement rather than a door-slamming ultimatum. Iran's foreign minister has simultaneously made sharper public demands, calling on Washington to end what he described as "piracy" against Iranian vessels and to release frozen billions as a goodwill gesture before any deal is signed. The dual-track messaging—conciliatory president, hardline foreign minister—is classic Iranian negotiating practice, and experienced diplomats in the region say it suggests the leadership is genuinely trying to find a face-saving path to signature.

Pressure on all sides

The M/T Celestial Sea boarding on May 20 illustrated how much is at stake in getting the timing right. U.S. Marines from the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit intercepted the Iranian-flagged tanker in the Gulf of Oman after it was suspected of attempting to reach an Iranian port in violation of the American blockade. The vessel was searched, the crew redirected, and the ship released—a measured operation rather than an escalatory one. U.S. Central Command confirmed the details; it was the 91st commercial ship redirected since the blockade began.

Earlier in May, the posture was less restrained. On May 8, U.S. Navy F/A-18 Super Hornets disabled two Iranian tankers by firing precision munitions into their smokestacks. That escalation, which came amid a temporary breakdown in negotiations, has since been wound back as talks resumed. The Celestial Sea operation suggests Washington is calibrating its enforcement to avoid handing Tehran a pretext to walk away from the table.

Trump's own language has tracked the diplomatic temperature. He warned in early May that Iran would be bombed "at a much higher level" if it rejected his terms, and as recently as May 11 called the ceasefire "on massive life support" after dismissing Tehran's latest counter as "totally unacceptable." The pivot to "final stages" nine days later represents either a genuine breakthrough in the back-channel text or a calculated burst of optimism designed to pressure Tehran into concessions before any momentum dissipates.

For energy markets, the stakes are clear. Iranian oil, largely locked out of international markets by sanctions, represents meaningful spare capacity at a moment when supply constraints have pushed prices higher and fed inflation across import-dependent economies. A deal that allows sanctioned Iranian barrels back into the market would reshape the supply picture, with downstream effects on everything from European central bank rate paths to emerging-market current accounts.

What to watch in the coming days: whether Pakistan's army chief makes a return trip to Tehran—the benchmark described by multiple sources as the moment the agreement text would formally be announced as complete—and whether both governments can hold their domestic coalitions together long enough to sign. Iran's supreme leader Ayatollah Khamenei has given Pezeshkian political space to negotiate; in Washington, any final text will face scrutiny from hawks who view any enrichment-retention arrangement as an unacceptable precedent. The 30-day negotiating window, if it opens, is where the hardest compromises will have to be made.
