Pfizer pivots oncology strategy to Padcev as patent cliff looms

In one sentence
Padcev sales rose 39% in Q1 2026, but with $15–18B in patent losses looming by 2028, the bladder-cancer drug must now anchor Pfizer's entire oncology future.

The anatomy of Pfizer's post-COVID reinvention is becoming clearer: one bladder-cancer drug is being asked to carry a great deal of weight.

Pfizer has designated Padcev — the antibody-drug conjugate acquired as part of its $43 billion purchase of biotech Seagen in 2023 — as the "anchor" of its oncology portfolio. In the first quarter of 2026, the drug recorded $591 million in revenue, up 39% year-on-year, making it the fastest-growing asset in a segment that already accounts for roughly 27% of the company's total revenues. Full-year 2025 sales came in at $1.94 billion, and analyst projections put the figure at $3.6 billion by 2032 across eight major markets.

The cliff below

The urgency behind the Padcev pivot is straightforward. Pfizer faces a compressed run of exclusivity expirations between 2026 and 2028 that analysts estimate could strip $15 billion to $18 billion from its annual revenue base. Eliquis, its blockbuster anticoagulant, loses US patent protection in late 2026; Ibrance, the breast-cancer standby that generated $4.1 billion in 2025, faces generics around 2027; Xtandi, a prostate-cancer therapy that contributed $2.19 billion last year, follows on a similar timeline. Ibrance revenue was already moving in the wrong direction — down 6% in 2025 — a preview of the steeper erosion ahead.

CEO Albert Bourla has framed the company's response in expansive terms. "We saved the world from Covid," he told Fortune in January 2026. "Now we'll save the world from cancer." Pfizer now directs more than 40% of its annual R&D budget toward oncology and has set a target of eight or more blockbuster oncology medicines by 2030.

Padcev's commercial runway hinges on a single near-term regulatory decision. The FDA has set August 17, 2026, as its target action date for a supplemental approval covering Padcev combined with Merck's Keytruda in cisplatin-eligible muscle-invasive bladder cancer — a substantially larger patient population than the cisplatin-ineligible group the combination already covers following FDA approval in November 2025. Phase 3 EV-304 data showed the regimen reduced recurrence risk and improved overall survival against standard chemotherapy. A positive ruling would, in practice, position the combination as the default perioperative treatment for muscle-invasive bladder cancer regardless of cisplatin eligibility.

The pipeline beyond Padcev includes sigvotatug vedotin, a further ADC in development, and PF-08634404, a dual PD-1/VEGF inhibitor for which Pfizer has four pivotal studies planned in 2026. Lorbrena, a lung-cancer drug, contributed $1.02 billion in 2025, up 40% for the year. At ASCO 2026 — whose annual meeting opens in the coming days — the company is expected to present updated data across Lorbrena, Braftovi, Talzenna, and Tukysa.

Still, at a forward price-to-earnings ratio of roughly 8.7 — well below the sector average near 16.7 — the market is plainly sceptical. The concentration risk is not abstract: Adcetris, another Seagen-heritage drug, fell 17% in 2025 as competitors gained ground. Pfizer's oncology segment grew 7% in Q1 2026, a decent clip, but not yet sufficient to replace what the patent cliff will remove. The August FDA verdict on Padcev's expanded bladder-cancer indication is the first genuine test of whether Pfizer's $43 billion bet on Seagen has secured a durable growth engine — or simply bought time.
