AI data-centre investment sharpens competition for bond market capital, Warsh warns

Kevin Warsh, sworn in as Federal Reserve chair on 18 May, inherits a bond market straining under competing capital demands from AI infrastructure and federal borrowing. — Federal Reserve / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
With tech giants financing $725B in AI capex via bond markets at the same time Washington needs to borrow $2T, long yields are the pressure valve.

The week Kevin Warsh took the Federal Reserve chair, the bond market sent him an invoice. The 30-year Treasury yield climbed to 5.19% on 19 May — its highest since July 2007 — while the 10-year note touched 4.69%, a 16-month peak. The proximate cause was renewed inflation pressure tied to energy prices. The structural cause is the one that will define his tenure: two enormous capital demands are now competing for the same pool of long-duration money.

Two floods, one drain

The federal government needs to borrow roughly $2 trillion this fiscal year. The Congressional Budget Office puts the FY2026 deficit at $1.85 trillion; the White House's own Office of Management and Budget has it at $2.06 trillion, with more than $88 billion in interest going out every month. That borrowing requires a steady stream of buyers for Treasury securities across every maturity.

At the same time, four hyperscalers — Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, and Meta — plan a combined $725 billion in capital expenditure in 2026, up 77% from 2025's $410 billion. Most of it is AI infrastructure. Tech firms have already sold more than $300 billion in debt to US investors to fund the buildout; Bank of America projects a further $140–$300 billion in hyperscaler bond issuance for 2026 alone.

The scale of what that adds to bond markets is not trivial. Dallas Fed researchers estimated earlier this year that AI-related investment-grade issuance of around $300 billion generates roughly $360 billion in 10-year equivalent duration supply — about an eighth of total annual US duration supply. Their formulation: the market impact is "roughly equivalent to what would happen if the supply of longer-dated Treasury bonds jumped by more than 10%." Buyers absorbing that new supply will require higher yields to do it.

The composition shift is structural, not cyclical. AI-related debt now accounts for around 15% of the entire US corporate bond universe — a share that has already surpassed the large banks that traditionally dominated investment-grade issuance. Unlike bank debt, borrowed to lend and recycled through the economy at a spread, tech infrastructure bonds finance data centres, chips, and energy contracts that must be serviced from operating cash flows or future AI revenues. Data-centre net debt-to-earnings ratios are projected to rise from 3.5 to 4.5 times as capex mounts — a trajectory harder to sustain at 5% long rates than at 4%.

Warsh arrives with a stated belief that AI will ultimately prove a "significant disinflationary force" — that productivity gains will give the Fed room to ease. The bond market is testing that thesis in real time. Five-year breakeven inflation expectations have risen to 2.7%, the highest since 2023, up from 2.2% at year-end. Christoph Rieger of Commerzbank takes the opposing view, calling AI a net contributor to higher prices in the years ahead; the chip prices powering the buildout offer partial evidence — DRAM costs rose roughly 17-fold over the past year.

Ed Yardeni of Yardeni Research argues that a hawkish Warsh — willing to hike rates — might paradoxically bring long yields down by convincing bond investors inflation will be contained. He has pencilled in a July increase. UBS economist Jonathan Pingle identifies the internal tension in Warsh's framework: hiking risks "restraining the investment today that might represent tomorrow's disinflation." Both cannot simultaneously be right.

Nvidia reports earnings this week. The results will indicate whether hyperscalers' appetite for chips — and the debt financing their buildout — remains as strong as their capex guidance implies, and whether the capital competition bearing on the long end is about to intensify further.
