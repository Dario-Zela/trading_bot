US contracts small biotech for experimental Ebola treatment as outbreak severity signals mount

In one sentence
Washington enlisted Mapp Biopharmaceutical to ship experimental Ebola doses as the Bundibugyo outbreak hits 650 suspected cases — signalling deeper alarm than officials have stated.

The US government has contracted Mapp Biopharmaceutical — a small, San Diego-based biotech — to supply doses of an experimental antibody treatment for individuals potentially exposed to Ebola. The company has already shipped doses, according to Bloomberg, which first reported the arrangement. The procurement runs through BARDA, the Biomedical Advanced Research and Development Authority, and draws in HHS, the FDA, the State Department, and the Administration for Strategic Preparedness and Response — a coalition the government describes as a "whole-of-government approach."

The treatment, MBP134, is an antibody cocktail. No regulator has approved it. Its two constituent antibodies were isolated from a survivor of the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic — the largest on record — and preclinical studies demonstrated the cocktail can protect ferrets and rhesus macaques against three ebolaviruses, including the Bundibugyo strain now driving the current outbreak. What that protection means in human clinical terms, at the doses being deployed, is a question without a clinical-trial answer.

The outbreak behind the procurement

The Bundibugyo virus is, in a practical sense, the version of Ebola the global health system was least equipped to fight. There is no approved vaccine against it; there is no approved therapeutic. The Zaire strain — the one most stockpiled against — has both. Animal data suggest Ervebo, Zaire's licensed vaccine, offers only marginal cross-protection against Bundibugyo.

The outbreak traces back to a cluster of severe illnesses among healthcare workers in Bunia, Ituri Province, DRC, around April 24. WHO was not notified until May 5. Lab confirmation arrived May 15 — by which point 246 suspected cases and 80 suspected deaths were already on record. By May 20, those figures had more than doubled: 653 suspected cases, 51 confirmed, 144 deaths, spread across at least three health zones in Ituri — with two independent confirmed cases now in Kampala, Uganda. WHO's Director-General declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on May 16 without first convening the emergency committee — a procedural departure that itself signals urgency.

The gap between action and messaging

US government communications have framed the response as precautionary. The 30-day prohibition on non-Americans entering from affected regions, announced May 18, was presented as a measured step. But invoking emergency procurement mechanisms for a pre-approval compound — from a company without commercial-scale manufacturing capacity — suggests a threat assessment that is running ahead of the official narrative.

This is Mapp Biopharmaceutical's second Ebola moment. In 2014, its earlier cocktail ZMapp was administered to two American aid workers before any clinical trial had completed, raising unresolved questions about experimental access and allocation that are now operative again, with fewer licensed alternatives on hand.

What to watch

The clearest forward signal is the FDA's decision on expanded access or compassionate use authorization for MBP134. If additional governments approach Mapp Bio for doses — as happened with ZMapp in 2014 — that trajectory will confirm what the procurement itself implies: that the Bundibugyo outbreak is materially more serious than official statements have communicated, and that experimental medicine is being asked to fill a gap that licensed stockpiles cannot.
