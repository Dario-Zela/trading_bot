Nvidia earnings will test whether the AI infrastructure boom is intact — and the supply chain is already straining

Nvidia's headquarters campus on San Tomas Expressway, Santa Clara, California — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nvidia posted $81.6B in Q1 FY27 revenue and guided to $91B for Q2, confirming the AI capex boom is intact — even as Samsung's 45,000-worker strike puts HBM supply under immediate pressure.

When Nvidia closed its books on the three months ending April 2026 and published the figures on Wednesday evening, the semiconductor sector got its clearest answer in months to the question the whole market has been asking: is the AI infrastructure spending cycle still running, or has it peaked?

The answer was unambiguous. Revenue hit $81.6 billion for the quarter — an 85 percent increase year-on-year and $2.8 billion ahead of the Wall Street consensus. Non-GAAP earnings per share came in at $1.87, beating the $1.77 estimate. Q2 guidance was set at $91 billion, a figure that landed $4.2 billion above what analysts had pencilled in. The company also announced a 25-fold hike in its quarterly dividend, from one cent to 25 cents a share, and authorised an $80 billion buyback.

Nvidia's stock dipped briefly in after-hours trading before recovering to close around 1.4 percent higher — a muted reaction to a spectacular print, but one that reflects how much of the good news had already been priced in. The stock was sitting at $220.61 before the report, not far from its all-time closing high of $235.74 set just a week earlier.

For trading algorithms and portfolio managers who use Nvidia's quarterly results as a real-time proxy for the health of the entire AI capex cycle, the takeaway was clean: hyperscale cloud operators and enterprise buyers alike are still spending at accelerating rates. Cautious outlooks on AI infrastructure — the deceleration thesis that has surfaced periodically since early 2025 — took another blow.

The Blackwell machine meets the moment

The standout number inside the report was data centre revenue: $75.2 billion, up 92 percent year-on-year. That figure now accounts for more than 90 percent of Nvidia's total business, and the company chose this earnings report to restructure how it discloses those results — a detail that tells its own story.

Starting this quarter, Nvidia splits data centre into two buckets: hyperscale (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta and comparable cloud platforms, at $37.9 billion) and what it calls ACIE — enterprise, industrial, and sovereign AI deployments, at $37.4 billion. The near-even split matters because it undercuts one of the more persistent bearish arguments: that Nvidia's customers are dangerously concentrated in a handful of hyperscalers who might cut capex simultaneously. A $37 billion enterprise and sovereign AI segment says the demand base has widened substantially.

The Q2 guidance of $91 billion carries an important caveat: it assumes zero revenue from Chinese data centres. The export control regime that has constrained H20 shipments to China since April 2025 remains in place, and Nvidia has written that revenue line down to nil for planning purposes. Without that constraint, forward numbers would presumably be higher. CEO Jensen Huang's recent visit to Beijing attracted attention, and any relaxation of export restrictions remains an unquantified upside that the guidance deliberately excludes.

Gross margin came in at 74.9 percent — within striking distance of the company's 75 percent long-run target, and a signal that Nvidia retains pricing power even as Blackwell-generation chip volumes scale.

Samsung's timing problem

The data centre numbers confirmed that demand for AI compute remains ferocious. But as markets opened Thursday, a second, complicating storyline was already unfolding 9,000 kilometres away in South Korea.

On May 21, some 45,000 unionised Samsung Electronics workers began an 18-day strike — the largest work stoppage in the history of the semiconductor industry. The dispute centres on profit-sharing: the union is demanding 15 percent of operating profit plus removal of a bonus cap and a 7 percent wage rise; management offered roughly 13 percent as a one-time 2026 payment, without committing to structural change. Talks collapsed on May 12 after a 17-hour mediation session under South Korea's Ministry of Employment and Labor. The Korean prime minister has called an emergency meeting.

The financial arithmetic is sobering. During a single-day walkout in April — a dress rehearsal of sorts — Samsung's foundry output fell 58 percent and its memory fabrication dropped 18 percent. JPMorgan estimates the full 18-day action could reduce Samsung's operating profit by between $14 billion and $20.8 billion, with daily sales losses running at roughly $700 million.

What makes the timing particularly awkward is the specific product lines at risk. Samsung's HBM4 chips — which began mass production in February 2026 and are already sold out for the year — are the same chips that sit stacked atop every high-end AI accelerator. The same goes for HBM3E production, where Samsung has faced quality and yield problems that have prevented it from qualifying its memory for Nvidia's most demanding specifications — a situation that has already pushed much of Nvidia's HBM sourcing toward SK Hynix.

A prolonged strike would make a tight market tighter still. High-bandwidth memory now consumes roughly 23 percent of total DRAM wafer capacity globally; any further reduction in Samsung output flows directly into longer lead times and higher prices for data centre operators building AI clusters. The immediate market signal on Thursday was unambiguous: Micron Technology and SanDisk — the two major non-Samsung HBM suppliers — surged as traders priced in diverted demand. Samsung's difficulties are, paradoxically, a windfall for its competitors.

The Strait of Hormuz also factored into the day's semiconductor rally. Two Chinese tankers and one Korean vessel were reported exiting the strait on Thursday morning, raising expectations that oil transit could resume and that energy-cost pressure on chip fabs — heavy consumers of power — might ease. Vice President Vance has been publicly backing an Iran deal, and the prospect of lower oil prices pulling down yields added a macro tailwind to the sector's gains.

From cycle to structure

The most revealing analytical move of the past fortnight came not from Nvidia itself but from Bank of America's semiconductor team. On May 13, analyst Vivek Arya nearly doubled his price target on Micron Technology from $500 to $950, maintaining a Buy rating. The revision was underpinned by an upward revision to BofA's 2030 AI data centre systems total addressable market — now $1.7 trillion, up from $1.4 trillion previously.

Arya's framing was pointed: high-bandwidth memory, he argued, "ships with every AI accelerator system," and as inference scales and the cost of generating AI tokens becomes a competitive variable, better memory systems directly reduce that cost. The implication is that HBM demand is not a temporary side-effect of AI hype but a structural feature of the economics of AI deployment. Micron had already sold out its entire HBM3E and HBM4 inventory for 2026 under binding contracts before the year began, giving the company a level of revenue visibility unusual in the historically volatile memory sector.

If that framing holds, the investment thesis for the AI hardware complex shifts considerably. Memory suppliers are no longer priced as cyclical commodity makers whose margins collapse at the first sign of oversupply; they become infrastructure plays with contracted demand curves. Whether that assessment survives the next downturn in AI capex remains the open question — but for now, Wall Street is willing to pay for the reclassification.

What comes next

Not every participant in the AI supply chain is benefiting equally. Super Micro Computer, a primary manufacturer of high-density AI servers, has been navigating a federal securities investigation after a Department of Justice grand jury indictment — unsealed in March — alleged a $2.5 billion scheme to divert restricted GPU servers to Chinese buyers through a Southeast Asian shell entity. Class action lawsuits are active. The company's most recent quarterly revenue beat analyst estimates by 23 percent, but GAAP gross margins compressed to 6 percent from 12 percent a year earlier — a sign that legal and compliance costs, combined with competitive pricing pressure, are eating into the business even as AI server volumes surge.

The aggregate picture that emerges from Thursday's cross-currents: the AI infrastructure supercycle is not only intact but accelerating, at least by the metric that matters most — Nvidia's forward revenue guidance. The complicating factor is on the supply side. Constrained HBM availability, Samsung's industrial dispute, and export restrictions that have severed the Chinese market all point toward a supply chain that is strained rather than slack. Whether spending growth eventually runs into a hardware bottleneck — or whether Samsung's workers and management reach a settlement before the 18-day window closes — are the questions that will shape the next leg of positioning across semiconductors, cloud infrastructure, and enterprise AI.

The next hard data point arrives in Q2 FY2027 results, due in August. Nvidia has given itself $91 billion to hit. The supply chain has 17 days to get back to work.
