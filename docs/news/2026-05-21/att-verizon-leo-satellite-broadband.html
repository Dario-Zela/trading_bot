AT&T and Verizon jointly commit $1.2 billion to low-earth-orbit satellite broadband

BlueWalker 3 satellite traces its path over Kitt Peak National Observatory, Arizona — a prototype for the commercial constellation now backed by AT&T and Verizon — KPNO/NOIRLab/IAU/SKAO/NSF/AURA/R. Sparks
In one sentence
AT&T and Verizon anchor a $1.2B collective carrier commitment to AST SpaceMobile, betting LEO satellite supplements rather than threatens their 5G networks.

America's two largest wireless carriers have settled a question that has hovered over the telecom industry for years: is low-earth-orbit satellite broadband a competitive threat or a useful tool? Their position — as anchor partners in a collective $1.2 billion in contracted revenue commitments to a Texas startup — lands firmly on the side of the latter.

AT&T and Verizon are among nearly 60 mobile network operators globally that have signed commercial agreements with AST SpaceMobile, a Midland, Texas company building large LEO satellites designed to deliver 4G and 5G connectivity directly to ordinary, unmodified smartphones. The aggregate $1.2 billion in contracted revenue — accumulated across a series of partnership deals — is the most concrete public signal yet that established carriers intend to slot satellite into their network hierarchies as a supplement, not fight off a disruptor.

Verizon went further than most, taking a $100 million equity stake alongside its commercial agreement. AT&T signed up to contribute a segment of its 850 MHz spectrum for satellite-to-cell coverage, targeting full geographic coverage across the United States. The FCC has since authorised AST SpaceMobile to operate up to 248 LEO satellites using 700 and 800 MHz low-band spectrum, coordinated with both carriers and FirstNet, the public-safety broadband network.

The logic is less about threatening 5G than filling the spaces it cannot economically reach. Rural stretches, oceans, national parks, disaster zones — places where a ground-based tower is uneconomic — are the target. AST SpaceMobile's BlueBird satellites, which unfurl large flat antenna arrays in orbit, have demonstrated peak data speeds of nearly 100 megabits per second to standard smartphones during testing. The company currently has six satellites operational and is targeting approximately 45 in orbit by year-end 2026. Full-year revenue guidance sits at $150–200 million, with an ambitious $1 billion target for 2027. The stock (Nasdaq: ASTS) trades near $88, implying a market cap of roughly $26 billion — a substantial premium for a business still in early commercial ramp.

The hedge, not the threat

The practical implication for AT&T and Verizon is capital efficiency: satellite coverage for customers without building a constellation themselves. It is also a defensive manoeuvre. T-Mobile began offering Starlink direct-to-device service under its T-Satellite brand in July 2025 — a coverage lead the other two carriers are still working to close.

On May 14, all three carriers announced a joint venture to standardise technical specifications and spectrum coordination for satellite-based direct-to-device services across their networks. AST SpaceMobile's chairman and CEO, Abel Avellan, welcomed it, saying the industry is "preparing to enable space-based cellular broadband connectivity to every American" and positioning his company as "a key enabler of this transformation."

Not everything has gone to plan. The BlueBird 7 mission failed earlier this year, leaving the constellation potentially short of the density needed for continuous nationwide service. BlueBird satellites 8 through 10 are in production and expected to ship within weeks, but the setback nudges the service timeline. A potential SpaceX IPO — which could force Alphabet, a 25% ASTS shareholder, to trim its position — is an additional overhang the market is pricing.

For investors reading the carrier behaviour as a signal, the key inference is that AT&T and Verizon no longer treat LEO satellite as an existential threat to terrestrial mobile. The question that follows is whether the $1.2 billion in contracted revenue — structured as future service payments spread across 60 operators, not upfront cash — will convert into real throughput and real income once the constellation reaches operating density. That answer arrives with AST SpaceMobile's second-quarter results this summer.
