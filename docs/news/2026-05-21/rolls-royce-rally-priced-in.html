Rolls-Royce recovery rally: analysts ask whether the turnaround is fully priced in

A Rolls-Royce Trent 1000 engine under the wing of a Boeing 787 Dreamliner — the widebody recovery that underpinned the re-rating is now a known quantity rather than a catalyst — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Rolls-Royce shares are up roughly tenfold since early 2023, but at 33–45x forward earnings, analysts are openly asking what drives the next leg higher.

When Tufan Erginbilgiç took the helm at Rolls-Royce in early 2023, shares were trading around 120 pence. Today they sit near 1,200 pence — up roughly tenfold in three years, and having already touched a 52-week high of 1,420 pence earlier in the year. The transformation he was hired to engineer is largely behind him. What analysts are debating now is whether the market has already paid for all of it.

The results case for the re-rating is settled. Full-year 2025 figures, published in February, showed underlying operating profit of £3.5 billion — up from £2.5 billion the prior year — free cash flow of £3.3 billion, and an operating margin of 17.3%. The company upgraded its 2028 mid-term targets to £4.9 billion–£5.2 billion in operating profit and £5.0 billion–£5.3 billion in free cash flow, and announced a £7 billion–£9 billion share buyback programme across 2026–2028. Erginbilgiç described the results as proof of a "transformation continuing with pace and intensity."

The valuation question

The consensus remains constructive. Of 19 analysts tracked on major platforms, 14 carry buy ratings, five hold, and none sell, with the average 12-month price target at approximately 1,411 pence — around 18 percent above current levels. But the nature of the remaining upside is attracting sharper scrutiny.

Forward P/E ratios are running between 33 and 45 times 2025 earnings, depending on the base used — elevated for a UK industrial by historical standards. Berenberg, maintaining a Hold with a 1,200 pence target, argues that expected growth is already reflected in the multiple, with limited room for further expansion unless results exceed guidance materially. Simply Wall St's discounted-cash-flow work, which strips out premium growth assumptions, places intrinsic value closer to 900 pence — a significant discount that largely reflects disagreement over whether operating margins above 20 percent can be sustained through the back half of the decade.

The core driver of the re-rating — the civil aerospace recovery — is, by now, a known variable. Widebody flight hours have normalised as long-haul travel returns to trend; the engine-hours revenue model that underpins civil earnings is generating predictable cash flows rather than surprise upside. Defence spending remains elevated but is already embedded in most models.

Finding the next catalyst

Two candidates are in focus. The first is nuclear. In April 2026, Rolls-Royce SMR — a separately financed entity with Rolls-Royce Holdings as a major shareholder, alongside investors including the Czech utility ČEZ and the Qatar Investment Authority — signed a contract with Great British Energy to design and deliver the first small modular reactors in the UK, sited at Wylfa in Anglesey. Growing demand from AI data-centre operators seeking reliable baseload power has elevated the narrative around SMR deployment. Meaningful revenue, however, remains a 2030s story.

The second catalyst is nearer: the half-year results due July 30. Analysts will scrutinise whether long-term service agreement billings confirm that widebody cash conversion is holding at 2025 rates, and whether the buyback timetable is proceeding as announced. A stumble on either front — particularly if airline capacity softens under current geopolitical pressure — would test a valuation that prices in continued execution without error.

At roughly 1,200 pence, the stock is a judgment on whether Erginbilgiç's upgraded mid-term targets are deliverable. The transformation narrative has been told and believed. What comes next requires new evidence rather than renewed confidence.
