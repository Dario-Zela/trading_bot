Fed minutes signal majority willing to hike again; gilt yields and BoE easing path repriced

The Marriner S. Eccles Federal Reserve Board Building in Washington, D.C., headquarters of the Federal Reserve System. — Britt Leckman / Federal Reserve (public domain)
In one sentence
Fed minutes show most FOMC members willing to raise rates if inflation stays above 2%, shattering easing expectations and forcing a sharp reprice of BoE's own cutting path.

The release of the Federal Reserve's April meeting minutes on Wednesday confirmed what rates markets had been pricing with growing conviction for weeks: the easing cycle is, at best, suspended — and a significant cohort of Federal Open Market Committee members now regard the next move as potentially being a hike, not a cut.

Minutes from the April 28-29 FOMC meeting, published on May 20, showed that a majority of members indicated some degree of policy firming would likely become appropriate if inflation continued to run persistently above the 2% target. The committee held its benchmark rate at 3.50%-3.75%, but the vote was far from tranquil: four dissents were recorded, the most since 1992. Three members objected to what they regarded as excessively dovish language in the post-meeting statement; one — Stephen Miran — went the other direction and pushed for an outright cut. That configuration — a committee divided between hawks and hawkier hawks, with a lone dove — is not how the easing narrative was supposed to unfold.

Most pointedly, the minutes noted that "many participants indicated that they would have preferred removing the language from the post-meeting statement that suggested an easing bias regarding the likely direction of the Committee's future interest rate decisions." That is careful central bank prose for a pointed message: the committee does not want markets assuming the next move is down.

The hawkish floor

The internal FOMC debate has a structural logic that transcends any single monthly print. Since late 2025, energy prices have risen sharply — oil is up more than 50% from its pre-conflict levels — driven by the disruption to Gulf supply flows that followed the US-Israel-led campaign against Iran. The International Energy Agency has characterised the resulting shock as the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market. That is not a backdrop against which a central bank can credibly signal that rate cuts remain the likely direction.

The minutes reflected this reality bluntly. Near-unanimously, participants identified the Middle East conflict as a factor that could "significantly" reshape the policy path. The committee articulated two discrete risk scenarios. In the first, a prolonged war amplifies energy costs and holds inflation above target through 2026, requiring a sustained hold. In the second — the scenario that attracted the most hawkish commentary in the minutes — war-driven energy inflation embeds in wages and services, triggering second-round effects that would push the Fed into an active hiking cycle.

Neither scenario is the stated base case. But the committee's stated tolerance for above-target inflation has clearly narrowed. The FOMC that spent much of 2024 characterising inflation as "gradually" moving toward 2% is now explicitly conditioning its forward guidance on whether inflation behaves — and signalling that, if it does not, the rate path is up rather than flat.

The political context sharpens the picture. The April 28-29 meeting was Jerome Powell's last as chair. Kevin Warsh — confirmed 54-45 by the Senate on May 13, the most divisive Fed confirmation vote in history — is due to be sworn in on May 22. Warsh inherits a committee already fractured between hawkish and more hawkish positions, and his own public record suggests he is unlikely to anchor the institution dovishly: he argued for years that the Fed was too slow to tighten in the post-pandemic cycle and has been consistently sceptical of the premise that neutral rates remain low. The minutes, in a sense, read like a welcome letter.

What the market repricing looks like

For much of the first quarter, US interest rate futures still priced two or three cuts by December 2026. That view had been fraying since March; the minutes provided the formal mechanism for its collapse. The two-year Treasury yield — the maturity most sensitive to near-term Fed expectations — had already climbed from roughly 3.40% in late February to above 4.10% by the eve of the minutes' release. The 10-year note reached 4.67%, its highest since early 2025. The Reuters poll of economists now shows fewer than half projecting any rate cuts by year-end, down from two-thirds a month earlier.

The US repricing did not stay domestic. UK 10-year gilt yields moved to 5.07% in London trading on Wednesday, with two-year gilts at 4.95% — and the US-UK two-year differential widened in a session that caught a number of carry positions off-guard. The arithmetic of the move is mechanical: if the Fed holds or tightens while traders had been positioned for Fed easing, dollar assets gain relative yield attractiveness, squeezing the premium embedded in sterling-denominated debt. UK rates either follow the move, or sterling weakens to compensate — and a weaker pound imports more inflation, making the BoE's job harder still.

The Bank of England's narrowing corridor

The Bank of England enters the Fed repricing in an already-constrained position. Its Monetary Policy Committee held Bank Rate at its April meeting, with the lone dissenter arguing for a hike rather than a cut — itself a signal that the internal balance of the committee had already tilted away from easing. The institution was until recently widely expected to be on a slow path toward normalisation; that expectation is now being revised in real time.

The constraints are layered. UK inflation remains above the BoE's 2% target, amplified by the same energy shock affecting the US, and the pass-through of higher oil prices into transport, energy bills, and manufactured goods has been persistent. If the BoE were to cut ahead of the Fed, the rate differential would shift against sterling — incentivising carry traders to unwind long-GBP positions, weaken the currency, and import further inflation. The BoE is effectively hemmed in: it cannot move dovishly without risking a sterling feedback loop that undoes the very conditions that would justify the cut.

MPC members Megan Greene and Catherine Mann had already signalled hawkish caution ahead of Wednesday's release. Governor Sarah Breeden struck a more sanguine tone earlier in the week, but the Fed minutes supplied the external reference point that made Breeden's framing harder to sustain. Sterling rate futures repriced lower immediately following the minutes, with traders reducing their bets on BoE easing through 2026. Money markets had been pricing roughly 40-50 basis points of cuts over the next 12 months before the release; that estimate has since contracted sharply.

The geopolitical dimension runs through the whole picture. With the Iran conflict showing no clear sign of resolution — and with oil prices still carrying a substantial risk premium — both the Fed and the BoE face an external variable that neither institution controls and that cuts directly against the conditions required for easing. As one source close to the deliberations put it, the committee sees the conflict not merely as a supply shock but as a test of whether inflation expectations can be held in check when households are paying 50% more for energy than they were a year ago.

What to watch

The next critical data points arrive quickly. UK CPI for April is due before the BoE's June 18 meeting — any upside surprise would make a June hold the only credible option and likely cement hawkish bias in the committee's forward guidance. On the US side, May CPI will be the first inflation print landing in Kevin Warsh's tenure and will set the tone for his initial policy communications; if it comes in above 3%, the conversation shifts from "would the Fed hike?" to "at which meeting?" The Fed's July meeting follows in short order.

For now, the minutes have performed their function: they have broken the complacency that had settled into rates markets through April and established clearly that the Fed's hawkish floor is real, not rhetorical. The question is no longer whether the easing cycle continues. It is whether it was ever really underway.
