S&P 500 edges higher ahead of Nvidia earnings despite hawkish Fed and Middle East tension

The New York Stock Exchange on Broad Street, where equities advanced as traders positioned ahead of Nvidia's closely watched quarterly results. — Jean-Christophe BENOIST / Wikimedia Commons / CC BY 3.0
In one sentence
S&P 500 gained 1% as oil's 5%-plus drop on Iran deal progress offset hawkish Fed minutes, while Nvidia's $82B revenue beat and $91B Q2 guide barely moved its stock after-hours.

Wall Street closed Wednesday broadly higher, with the S&P 500 adding 1.08% to 7,432.97 and the Nasdaq Composite gaining 1.54% to 26,270.36, as a sharp fall in oil prices outweighed Federal Reserve minutes that flagged an explicit rate-hike scenario for the first time in this cycle. The Dow Jones Industrial Average added 645 points, or 1.31%, to close at 50,009.35. The equity market's composure had a simpler explanation than the macro backdrop: Nvidia reported after the bell, and few traders were prepared to get aggressively short in the hours beforehand.

The minutes — from the FOMC's April 29 meeting, released mid-session — showed that a majority of policymakers regarded some degree of policy firming as likely appropriate if inflation continued to run persistently above the 2% target. "Many" officials pushed to strip the committee's residual easing bias from the post-meeting statement; three governors — Beth Hammack, Neel Kashkari, and Lorie Logan — formally dissented on those grounds. The federal funds rate remains in the 3.50%–3.75% band, but rate-futures markets now put a 65.7% probability on no cuts at all in 2026, a repricing that would have seemed implausible at the start of the year. Incoming chair Kevin Warsh will inherit a committee that, on the evidence of the minutes, is edging toward its next move being a hike rather than a cut.

The blunt instrument softening the blow was oil. Brent crude fell 5.63% to $105.02 a barrel and West Texas Intermediate shed 5.66% to $98.26, as diplomats signalled that US-Iran negotiations had entered a final stage. Energy has been the primary transmission channel from the three-month-old US-Israel military campaign against Iran into consumer prices — and any dialling-back of that pressure gives the Fed room it currently lacks. Treasury yields dipped; equities followed.

The AI Gravity Test

Into this cross-grained backdrop stepped Nvidia, whose quarterly results have become, in the estimation of much of the Street, as market-moving as a Federal Reserve decision. The chipmaker's market capitalisation stands at approximately $5.3 trillion, a figure that has pulled the technology sector's year-to-date return 15.4 percentage points clear of the broader S&P 500. With concentration at those levels, Nvidia's guidance does not merely reflect the AI trade — it sets the terms for it.

The options market priced a move of roughly 8% in either direction, implying a $355 billion swing in market value. The VIX closed at 17.44, down 3.43% on the session — apparently measured — but that index-level reading obscures elevated single-stock volatility and a dispersion spread that Michael Kramer of Mott Capital Management described as sitting near the first percentile on a five-year lookback: concentrated risk dressed up as calm.

The results cleared the bar. Revenue for the fiscal first quarter — ending April 2026 — came in at $81.62 billion, up 85% year-on-year and above the $78.86 billion consensus. Data-centre revenue of $75 billion nearly doubled from a year earlier, driven by Blackwell architecture demand; networking within that segment nearly tripled. Earnings per share of $1.87 beat the $1.77 estimate. Nvidia raised its quarterly dividend to 25 cents from one cent and guided second-quarter revenue to $91 billion — well above the roughly $87 billion the Street had anticipated, and representing a new sequential record if achieved.

The stock whipsawed near flat in after-hours trading. Nvidia has beaten consensus estimates in 18 of the past 20 quarters; it has also fallen following three of its four most recent reports. The dynamic is structural: when positioning is uniformly bullish, perfection is the floor, not the ceiling. Whether the $91 billion Q2 guide breaks that pattern will become clear when pre-market trading opens Thursday — and whether a single stock's result can continue to override the cumulative weight of a hawkish Fed and a Middle East that has yet to fully deescalate.
