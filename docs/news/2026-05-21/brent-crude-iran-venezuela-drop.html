Brent crude sheds 5% as Iran deal nears and Venezuelan oil returns to US refineries

A US Navy ultra-large crude carrier — tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has been disrupted since March 2026, with resolution now hinging on the Iran-US peace framework — US Navy / Wikimedia Commons (public domain)
In one sentence
Brent shed 5% after Trump signaled an Iran ceasefire and Venezuelan crude confirmed its return to US Gulf refineries, unwinding the war-risk premium.

In oil markets, the premium attached to geopolitical risk is invisible until the moment it isn't. On Wednesday, three months of accumulated war pricing became very visible — and proceeded to exit in a single afternoon.

Brent crude futures fell roughly 5%, settling near $105.61 a barrel, after President Trump told reporters that the United States would end the Iran war "very quickly." Separately, confirmation firmed that Venezuelan crude was flowing in meaningful volumes to US Gulf Coast refineries, reversing a sanctions-era drought and quietly adding several hundred thousand barrels per day to the hemisphere's effective supply. The combination was enough to collapse what had been, for three months, the most expensive geopolitical risk premium oil markets had ever priced.

From $72 to $126: how the premium built

The war now tentatively being unwound began on February 28, when US and Israeli forces conducted a joint operation against Iran that culminated in the death of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Within three weeks, Brent had cleared $100 a barrel for the first time since 2022. By mid-March the International Energy Agency was tracking regional production shut-ins approaching 10 million barrels per day — what the agency described as the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." By late April, Brent had reached $126 as hedgers priced in a prolonged Hormuz closure.

The arithmetic behind that premium was straightforward: roughly 20% of global seaborne crude and LNG exports pass through the Strait of Hormuz. Vitol chief executive Russell Hardy estimated that approximately one billion barrels of production would ultimately be lost to the conflict. Markets priced accordingly, with Goldman Sachs forecasting Brent above $100 through year-end even under a deal scenario.

Two supply shocks, one afternoon

Trump's remarks on Wednesday were the more immediate catalyst. Since postponing a planned military strike on May 18 — citing "very good chances" of a resolution — the president has shifted steadily toward signaling imminent closure. The negotiating framework, developed through Pakistani mediation, envisions a 14-point memorandum: a formal end to hostilities, a 30-day window for nuclear talks, a gradual sanctions unwind, and an enhanced UN inspections regime. Jared Kushner and Special Envoy Steve Witkoff are leading for Washington; Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi is managing Tehran's position.

Markets had already demonstrated they could move sharply on Iran headlines. On May 6, when the MOU framework first leaked, Brent fell almost 8%, briefly dipping below $101. Wednesday's 5% decline is the second leg of the same trade — and with each iteration, the market's estimate of the residual war premium shrinks further.

The Venezuelan thread is quieter but structurally significant. The Trump administration struck a supply arrangement with Caracas in January — a roughly $2 billion pact that eased some trade restrictions and redirected cargoes previously destined for Chinese buyers toward US ports. By February, Venezuelan crude imports had climbed to approximately 339,000 barrels per day, their highest since late 2024. S&P Global CERA estimates that Gulf Coast refiners — historically configured for the heavy, high-sulfur grades Venezuela produces — can absorb an additional 300,000 to 400,000 bpd. Valero Energy confirmed it ramped Venezuelan purchases materially in the first quarter; Chevron, the only US major still producing inside Venezuela, is supplying multiple buyers alongside trading houses Vitol and Trafigura.

This is not new supply in a technical sense. It is, however, the normalisation of supply that markets had learned to discount. That normalisation is now being re-priced into the forward curve.

London's oil majors absorb the hit

The sector consequences landed most clearly in London. BP and Shell both fell 4–5% in FTSE 100 trading on Thursday, tracking the overnight Brent decline closely enough to make the implication obvious: both stocks had been carrying an identifiable war premium, and that premium is now being liquidated.

The question now is whether the repricing is complete or only beginning. Energy stocks served, through the first quarter of 2026, as an effective stagflation hedge — rising when inflation stayed sticky, growth softened, and tail risks were elevated. Strip the geopolitical layer out and the underlying story for both companies is more complicated. BP entered the crisis with a higher gearing ratio and less financial headroom than Shell, meaning its capacity to sustain buyback programmes compresses materially if crude retraces toward pre-war fundamentals. Shell's stronger downstream exposure — refining, LNG trading, petrochemicals — gives it more structural earnings support if crude weakens.

For traders running cross-sector energy positions, the unwind raises a sharper question: how much of the sector's relative strength since March was fundamentals-driven, and how much was a pure tail-risk trade? The answer determines where hedge ratios need adjusting — and at what speed.

What the market is still pricing in

The important caveat is that headlines are not barrels. LSEG research analyst Emril Jamil noted that prices "are likely to still exhibit some upside potential even if a deal is concluded, given that supply will likely not return to pre-war levels immediately." Infrastructure damage to Iranian production facilities, the tanker backlog at Hormuz, and the logistics of restoring QatarEnergy and other blocked exporters to full output all imply a normalisation timeline measured in months, not weeks.

Swissquote's Ipek Ozkardeskaya offered a more pointed version of the same reservation: "I'll clap when Iran confirms."

Brent was holding above $105 in early Thursday trading — still up more than 45% from its pre-war level of $72.48, and still pricing meaningful residual risk. The direction of that residual will be set by three specific things: the formal text of any Iran-US memorandum, expected through Pakistani channels; weekly EIA inventory data showing whether Venezuelan barrels are being drawn down at Gulf Coast refineries or accumulating in storage; and, for equity investors, whether BP and Shell's second-quarter earnings guidance calibrates to a $100 oil world — or still assumes a geopolitical floor that is quietly disappearing beneath it.
