Small-cap ETFs are beating the S&P 500 in 2026, but earnings quality is quietly eroding

The New York Stock Exchange on Wall Street — small-cap indices have outrun the large-cap benchmarks in 2026, but the earnings data are telling a quieter story. — Elcobbola / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Small-cap ETFs are outpacing the S&P 500 in 2026, but with 40% of Russell 2000 firms unprofitable and EPS forecasts falling 7%, the rally rests on eroding foundations.

Small-cap exchange-traded funds have spent much of 2026 running ahead of the S&P 500 by a margin that commands attention. As of early February, the Russell 2000 was up 6.5% year-to-date while the large-cap benchmark had returned just 1.1% — a gap of 540 basis points in under six weeks. Through the spring, broad small-cap indices have extended their lead: the Russell 2000 has gained roughly 13% year-to-date against approximately 8% for the S&P 500. Specific vehicles have done better still — the Invesco S&P SmallCap 600 Revenue ETF (RWJ) returned 11.6% through early February, and quality-screened funds such as the iShares Core S&P Small-Cap ETF (IJR) have drawn roughly $2 billion in fresh inflows.

The rotation has had a coherent logic behind it. Investors rotated out of mega-cap technology names at the start of the year, wary of concentration risk and stretched valuations. Domestically-oriented small caps — less exposed to geopolitical friction over trade and international revenues — looked like a rational shelter. The thesis was tidy. The problem is that the earnings data are now quietly complicating it.

The quality problem prices haven't caught up with

A Benzinga analysis citing Wells Fargo research, published this month, compresses the contradiction into two figures: consensus earnings forecasts for Russell 2000 companies fell 7% between January and May 18, while S&P 500 earnings estimates rose 8% over the same period. Prices and earnings are moving in opposite directions — which is the mechanics of a valuation expansion with no fundamental backing.

The structural weakness runs deeper than headline revisions. Nearly 40% of Russell 2000 constituents currently generate no earnings at all, a proportion that has roughly doubled from 17% two decades ago. The cohort's trailing return on equity for the 12 months through Q1 2026 came in at under 1%, against approximately 20% for large-cap peers. Net profit margins average 4.4% in the small-cap universe versus 14.5–14.8% for large caps.

Debt is the second pressure point. Small-cap net debt-to-EBITDA now stands at 4.5 times — three times the 1.5x ratio for large-cap companies. Several small-cap industrials and retailers attempting to refinance debt issued during the zero-rate era of 2021 have found new coupons running 150–200 basis points above the old ones, effectively wiping out the first-quarter earnings growth analysts had projected. The approximately 40–41% of Russell 2000 names that cannot cover interest expenses from operating income — broadly defined as "zombie" companies — face the sharpest exposure to this refinancing arithmetic.

The signal in the flows is consistent with that read. IWM, the broad passive Russell 2000 vehicle, has shed $8 billion in net outflows year-to-date. IJR, which applies profitability screens to its constituent pool, has absorbed inflows. Investors are not abandoning small caps wholesale; they are sorting for quality — which is, in itself, a warning about the broad-index vehicles that do not.

The next meaningful test arrives with Q2 earnings reports, when analysts will discover whether the 17–22% full-year EPS growth consensus for the small-cap cohort — already being revised lower — can survive another quarter of elevated refinancing costs and thin margins. If it cannot, the gap between prices and fundamentals will close by one of two routes: earnings recovery, or the correction that quality-adjusted valuations have been signalling for months.
