ECB governors counsel patience as the June policy meeting approaches

The European Central Bank's twin-tower headquarters in Frankfurt, Germany, where the June 10–11 policy meeting will be held. — Thomas Wolf / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
ECB governors including Villeroy warn they need more wage and core-inflation data before committing to June rate cuts, even as oil prices collapse.

The European Central Bank meets on June 10–11 with markets in an unfamiliar position: unsure, after weeks of whipsaw repricing, which direction the deposit rate is heading. Following the outbreak of the Middle East conflict that drove euro-area headline inflation to 3.0% in April, rate-hike expectations briefly dominated the forward curve. Then oil prices began unravelling — down around 19% over the course of May — and the cut camp returned, betting that the energy shock was transient and that the ECB would resume its easing cycle before summer. Several governing council members have now publicly complicated that bet.

François Villeroy de Galhau, the Bank of France governor, has been the most articulate voice for patience. In separate statements on May 13 and May 25, he said the ECB lacks a "critical mass of data" on wages, underlying inflation, and expectations before it can commit to any rate action, adding bluntly that "the ECB's next move should be guided by the data, not the date." Crucially, he flagged that the bank has not yet detected second-round effects from the energy shock — a reference to the wage-price spiral that would turn a supply-side jolt into a persistent inflation problem — but said the council must "remain extremely vigilant" about the possibility. Core inflation at 2.2%, and wage growth expectations that have slipped to 2.8% from 3.1%, give the patient camp a defensible baseline. But those are backwards-looking figures, and the officials want forward-looking confirmation.

What the council is waiting for

The June meeting will be accompanied by fresh ECB staff macroeconomic projections — the first since the energy shock hit, and the vehicle through which the council will update its three-scenario analysis (baseline, adverse at $119/bbl, severe at $145/bbl) against where oil actually stands. For the June projections to unlock a cut, Villeroy and sympathisers need May flash inflation data to confirm that falling energy prices are already mechanically pushing the headline number lower, while core and wage metrics hold steady. That flash print is due in early June, just before the blackout period.

Not all of the council reads the pause as a prelude to easing. Isabel Schnabel, the ECB executive board member most closely associated with inflation vigilance, told Reuters on May 26 that the bank should raise rates in June. Slovakia's Peter Kazimir called a June hike "all but inevitable" at the start of the month. Their argument is insurance: even if the worst scenarios don't materialise, delivering one pre-emptive move shields the ECB's credibility against a scenario where oil prices stabilise at levels still well above pre-war norms. President Christine Lagarde, at the April 30 press conference, declined to endorse either camp, noting only that six weeks of incoming data would inform an "informed decision."

For rates traders, the practical consequence is that a June cut — which was barely imaginable in April and briefly fashionable in mid-May as oil sold off — now requires a sharp downward surprise in the flash CPI print and a shift in the balance of council opinion that currently seems unlikely. EUR/USD positioning and the German two-year Schatz, the rate most sensitive to ECB near-term expectations, are the immediate instruments to watch. The early June data calendar is the first scheduled catalyst.
