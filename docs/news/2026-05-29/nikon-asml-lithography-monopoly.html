Nikon challenges ASML's chip equipment monopoly with a low-cost lithography push

ASML's headquarters in Veldhoven, the Netherlands — home to the world's only manufacturer of EUV chip-printing machines. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nikon's CEO says the company is developing budget lithography tools to chip away at ASML's near-monopoly, spurred by a collapse in Intel orders that left Nikon with its worst losses in 109 years.

The machines that print the world's most advanced chips — the ones inside every smartphone, AI server, and electric car — are almost all made by one company. ASML, based in Veldhoven, the Netherlands, holds a 100% share of the extreme-ultraviolet (EUV) lithography market and roughly 83% of the global lithography equipment market overall. Its nearest rival, Japan's Nikon, sold nine systems in six months. All were older models.

Now Nikon's new president wants to change that calculus — not by out-engineering ASML, but by undercutting it on price.

In an interview with Nikkei Asia, Yasuhiro Ohmura, who took over as Nikon's president and chief executive this year, said the company is developing low-cost chipmaking equipment targeted directly at ASML customers. "Even with low prices," Ohmura said, "there is still plenty of profit to be had in lithography equipment." The trigger for the push, he acknowledged, is blunt: Nikon has been dangerously dependent on a single buyer — Intel — and Intel is no longer buying.

The scale of the problem

For the fiscal year ending March 2026, Nikon now expects a net loss of roughly ¥85 billion ($580 million) — its worst financial result in 109 years of existence. Operating losses are projected at ¥100–105 billion. The precision equipment business, which houses the lithography unit, saw revenues fall 8.4% year-on-year to approximately ¥185 billion, dropping below ¥200 billion for the first time in years.

The explanation is almost entirely Intel-shaped. When Intel began slashing capital expenditure in 2024 following massive losses of its own, Nikon's order book collapsed. Nikon had never cultivated the broad foundry relationships that ASML developed over decades — TSMC and Samsung each barely feature in Nikon's customer mix. The company responded with plant closures, including its Yokohama facility, and a restructuring that amounts to a rethink of the entire equipment division.

The contrast with ASML borders on the startling. The Dutch company posted €32.7 billion in net sales for 2025, a 15.6% rise over the prior year, with an order backlog of €38.8 billion and guidance for €34–39 billion in 2026. It sold 327 lithography systems in 2025, including 48 premium EUV tools worth roughly €150 million apiece. Nikon sold nine. ASML's monopoly on EUV is the geopolitical chokepoint around which US-Dutch-China export controls have been built — EUV machines have never been licensed for export to China at all.

The bet: price, compatibility, and the ArF market

Nikon is not attempting to revive its EUV ambitions. That ship sailed quietly during the 2010s, when ASML secured foundry partnerships, supply-chain lock-in, and the most critical optical components, leaving Nikon unable to match its R&D investment at scale. What Ohmura is targeting instead is the deep-ultraviolet (DUV) market for mature and mid-tier nodes — particularly argon fluoride (ArF) immersion systems used in packaging, 3D DRAM, image sensors, and power semiconductors.

Nikon has a concrete plan here, of sorts. The company is co-developing a new ArF immersion scanner with semiconductor manufacturers, with a prototype expected by fiscal 2028. The system is being designed to accept ASML-format photomasks, reducing the friction for fabs considering a second-source supplier. Industry analysts have projected the ArF segment could grow 40–50% over the next decade, driven largely by the 3D chip packaging surge underpinning AI computing.

The China angle matters too. Export restrictions have largely cut Chinese chipmakers off from ASML's advanced DUV tools, opening a gap that Nikon — which is not subject to the same Dutch restrictions — may be able to fill in mature-node equipment, particularly for the automotive and consumer electronics segments Beijing is prioritising.

Nikon has also opened a legal front. The company has filed IP claims against ASML alleging that ASML used Nikon's DUV laser technology without proper licensing when developing its EUV systems — a charge ASML rejects and has countered with its own claims on more than ten Nikon patents.

Whether price is enough

The pricing argument has intuitive logic. ASML's near-monopoly position allows it to command premium margins; a credible lower-cost alternative, even for non-frontier tools, would give procurement officers at mid-tier fabs genuine negotiating leverage. Nikon's legacy in the sector — the company pioneered photolithography in the 1960s and held approximately 40% of the global market as recently as 2001 — means it is not starting from a blank page technically.

But the obstacles are serious and the timeline is long. A FY2028 prototype means no production-ready system before fiscal 2029 at the earliest, assuming co-development proceeds smoothly. Nikon will need to rebuild foundry relationships that were never its strength, at a moment when its balance sheet is severely stretched. And ASML is not standing still: it is already moving into High-NA EUV tools for sub-2nm nodes and projecting revenues of €71 billion by 2030.

Ohmura's statement is a direction, not a roadmap. Whether the ArF-segment push produces a durable second-supplier position in chipmaking equipment, or simply a tidier wind-down of Nikon's semiconductor ambitions, will depend less on the CEO's conviction than on the prototype specs that emerge in 2028 — and on whether fabs are willing to qualify a new supplier in a cost environment that still favours proven yield over cheaper kit.
