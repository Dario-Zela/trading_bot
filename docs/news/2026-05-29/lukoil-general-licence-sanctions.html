US grants general licence for Lukoil contract talks in notable Russia sanctions easing

A Lukoil office building. The Russian energy company's international assets are held via an Austrian subsidiary, Lukoil International GmbH. — Wikimedia Commons / Panoramio
In one sentence
OFAC's sixth extension of the Lukoil divestment licence keeps negotiations alive through June 28 but still bars any actual sale—raising questions about sanctions enforcement credibility.

Washington has quietly extended its carve-out licence for Lukoil divestment negotiations for the sixth time since October, issuing General Licence 131F just before the previous authorisation was set to lapse on May 30. The move keeps the door open for potential buyers to negotiate terms for Lukoil International GmbH — the Austrian holding company through which Russia's second-largest oil producer holds most of its overseas operations — through June 28, 2026.

What the licence does not do is more instructive than what it does. Parties may conduct due diligence, engage advisers, and sign letters of intent or executory contracts. They may not complete any transaction. Every contract executed under GL 131F must contain an express clause making performance contingent on a separate, specific OFAC authorisation. Sale proceeds would be frozen in US jurisdiction pending sanctions removal. Treasury explicitly reserves the right to revoke any licence in the series "at any time" if it determines Lukoil is not negotiating in good faith.

Repeatedly deferred, never delivered

The pattern here is notable. The GL 131 series now spans seven versions across roughly seven months — negotiation windows narrowed from 42 days at their widest (GL 131B, January 2026) to the current 29 days. GL 131E, the version just expiring, was signed by OFAC Deputy Director Lisa Palluconi rather than Director Bradley Smith — a small administrative detail compliance lawyers read as evidence the process has been routinised.

No buyer has been publicly named. Gunvor SA, the commodity trader, was reportedly under consideration before the original November 2025 licence was even published, only to be dropped by the administration before ink was dry. Since then, official silence. The underlying asset — refineries, retail stations, and exploration interests across roughly 14 countries — remains operational under parallel licences (GL 128C and GL 130A) that run through October 2026, creating an asymmetry: the infrastructure stays live while deal talks inch forward on a tight monthly clock.

Any eventual buyer faces demanding structural conditions: proceeds frozen in a US-controlled account, complete severance from Lukoil post-close, and an obligation to seek fresh OFAC approval before any material subsequent divestiture. That framework likely deters risk-averse trade buyers and favours distressed-asset specialists willing to accept payment delay in exchange for a steep discount on strategically located energy infrastructure.

Policy signalling, sanctions substance

The framing matters. Calling these extensions a sanctions "easing" overstates what is happening. Lukoil remains on the SDN list. OFAC designated it in October 2025 specifically to degrade Russia's capacity to finance its war effort — the licences are a mechanism for managed divestment, not a rehabilitation of the sanctioned party.

The credibility risk is cumulative. Six extensions with no named buyer, running alongside the Trump administration's March decision to temporarily suspend enforcement against seaborne Russian crude during the Iran-driven price spike, builds a pattern that markets may read as broader policy softening. Each individual action has a rational technical explanation; the series, taken together, is harder to characterise as unambiguous pressure.

The next concrete decision point is June 28, when GL 131F lapses. OFAC can issue GL 131G and extend again, or allow the authorisation to expire — forcing operational wind-down across Lukoil's non-Russian portfolio and imposing real commercial disruption on the counterparties holding those retail stations and refinery stakes. A seventh extension, absent a signed conditional agreement, would place the enforcement credibility of the entire exercise under considerably more scrutiny.
