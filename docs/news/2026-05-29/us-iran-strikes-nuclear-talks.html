US and Iran trade strikes as nuclear talks hang by a thread

Iran's Bushehr nuclear power plant on the Persian Gulf coast, where air defences engaged a US drone on Thursday — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
US and Iran traded strikes near Bandar Abbas and Kuwait on Thursday as back-channel MOU talks collapsed into conflicting statements and oil jumped 3%.

The pattern is now grimly familiar: strikes in the early hours, a retaliatory salvo before dawn, and then — before the smoke clears — back to the negotiating table. That rhythm played out again on Thursday when the United States and Iran carried out the most serious exchange of fire since a fragile April ceasefire took hold, while Pakistani and Qatari mediators worked the phones in search of a memorandum of understanding that neither side can agree was ever finished.

Oil jumped three percent. Stocks fell. Then oil pulled back when ceasefire-extension reports surfaced. It was, in miniature, the story of a conflict that has spent three months defying resolution.

The exchange

American forces struck near Bandar Abbas airport in the early hours of Thursday, targeting what the Pentagon described as a site posing an imminent threat to US troops and commercial shipping in the Gulf. The strike — described in Pentagon terms as a "self-defense" action, destroying a ground control station that was preparing to launch a fifth Iranian drone — was the second such US operation in as many days, following an earlier strike on Wednesday that downed four IRGC drones.

Within hours, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps announced it had responded in kind, claiming its forces targeted a US airbase "as the origin of the aggression" at 4:50am local time. That claim resolved, partially, into something more specific. The IRGC had fired a ballistic missile toward Ali Al Salem air base in Kuwait, home to a significant contingent of US forces. Kuwait's military confirmed it had intercepted "hostile missile and drone attacks"; no hits on American facilities were reported. The IRGC called the strikes "a serious warning" and promised a "more decisive" response if Washington pushed further.

Separately, Iran's air defenses engaged what officials described as an incoming American drone over Jam, a city in Bushehr province on the Persian Gulf coast. The district governor confirmed to state television that "an aircraft was shot down over the skies of Jam by the Armed Forces." No state had publicly claimed the drone by the time of publication.

Nuclear shadow over Bushehr

The Jam intercept matters beyond its tactical significance. Bushehr province is home to Iran's only operational commercial nuclear reactor — a 1,000-megawatt plant built with Russian assistance and transferred to Iranian control in 2013. The reactor sits roughly 20 kilometres from the province's coastal capital.

No damage to the nuclear facility was confirmed. But air-defense activation in the province is itself a significant signal: it means the airspace around Iran's most symbolically loaded civilian nuclear site is being contested. The IAEA has reported no rise in radiation levels throughout the conflict, and Tehran has been at pains to keep the reactor outside the frame of active hostilities. Any sustained disruption to Bushehr's operations would affect the regional energy balance and sharpen uranium-market risk pricing at an already elevated moment. Iran has acknowledged the area around the plant was struck four times earlier in the conflict, killing at least one plant employee; Thursday's episode did not reach that threshold, but markets priced it as though it might.

Duelling accounts from the table

The strikes land against a backdrop of active, if internally contradictory, diplomacy. Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner have been negotiating for the US side, working both directly with Iranian counterparts and through Pakistani and Qatari mediators — Pakistan having stepped into the central role vacated by Oman, whose back-channel to Tehran was disrupted when Iran targeted Omani ports and shipping during the conflict's early phases.

Trump said as recently as last week that a memorandum of understanding was "largely negotiated" and would be announced soon. The proposed framework would declare an end to hostilities and open a 30-day negotiating window covering the Strait of Hormuz, Iran's nuclear programme, and sanctions relief. Reports from the Financial Times last weekend described the two sides closing in on a 60-day ceasefire extension bundled with a nuclear framework.

Then the accounts diverged sharply. Iran's Tasnim News Agency — state-aligned, and therefore significant as a channel for Tehran's official thinking — cited a source close to the negotiating team saying the text of the MOU "has not been confirmed." The White House hit back, calling an Iranian-sourced version of the document "a complete fabrication." Iranian state television then released what it presented as the MOU text; Washington dismissed it.

The central sticking point is uranium. The White House has insisted any deal must include long-term limits on Iran's nuclear development and the confiscation and destruction of its enriched-uranium stockpile. Tehran, via Tasnim, has claimed the first round of peace talks did not address the stockpile at all, focusing instead on reopening commercial transit through Hormuz. That contradiction — if it reflects genuine positions rather than tactical messaging — is why Thursday's strikes were not simply a breakdown in a ceasefire, but a signal delivered into the middle of open negotiations.

Markets on a hair-trigger

Oil had fallen more than five percent on Wednesday on ceasefire optimism. When the exchange of strikes landed early Thursday, Brent crude futures gained more than three percent to $97.29 a barrel, while West Texas Intermediate climbed 3.42 percent to $91.71. Equities fell; the dollar rose. By the afternoon session, both moves had largely reversed as ceasefire-extension reports circulated — leaving investors with another whipsaw session and no cleaner read on where the conflict heads.

The volatility points to a structural problem: oil markets have spent much of May pricing in a swift resolution that has not arrived. Brent is on course for a monthly decline of close to 19 percent — a collapse that reflects both the scale of earlier risk-premium unwinding and the near-impossibility of holding a position confidently when the headline alternates between escalation and deal within a single trading day.

What happens next turns on two signatures. Pakistani and Qatari negotiators held talks with Iranian counterparts through Thursday and stayed in regular contact with Witkoff. But no deal reaches a close without endorsement from Trump and, on the Iranian side, from Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — who has not publicly affirmed the negotiations' direction. The first concrete test will be whether the ceasefire in force since April 8 survives the week's exchange intact, or whether Thursday's ballistic missile fired toward Kuwait marks the beginning of a sharper phase — one that would make an MOU, finalised or not, much harder to salvage.
