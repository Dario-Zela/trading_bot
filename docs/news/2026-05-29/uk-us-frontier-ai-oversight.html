UK and US signal tighter frontier AI oversight, but enforcement detail remains thin on both sides

The Palace of Westminster: Labour's AI governance ambitions are taking shape in legislation that has yet to reach Parliament. — Daniele Vaghini / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
UK formalises frontier AI pre-deployment vetting and the US edges toward a review order — then Trump pulls back, leaving enforcement detail thin on both sides.

Two governments published or retreated from AI oversight measures in the same week, producing a picture that is simultaneously more active and less decisive than the headlines suggested.

The UK's Department for Science, Innovation and Technology announced what it is calling the next phase of its frontier AI safety regime. Developers of the most capable models will be required to submit systems for pre-deployment evaluation by the AI Security Institute (AISI) before public release — a formalization of what has, until now, been a voluntary arrangement. The institute, rebranded last year from its original AI Safety Institute title, has been running assessments of frontier models since late 2023 and has covered more than 30 systems from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta and Mistral. A recent partnership with Microsoft, framed as "sustained two-way collaboration between government and companies developing and deploying frontier AI," signals the direction of travel.

The shift in language from voluntary to required carries weight — but the new measures stop well short of a regulatory framework with teeth. The announcement does not specify which labs or capability thresholds trigger the requirement, and there are no penalties attached to non-compliance. The government's stated ambition is to put the AISI on a statutory footing through forthcoming legislation — a Frontier AI Bill that would grant the institute legal powers to compel pre-release testing. The 2026 King's Speech, however, contained no standalone AI Act. Instead, AI governance is being threaded through sector-specific reform and a Regulating for Growth Bill whose centrepiece is not rule-setting but "AI sandboxes" — spaces where existing rules can be relaxed, not added.

Across the Atlantic, the signal ran the other way

On the US side, the Trump administration appeared briefly to be converging on analogous territory. A draft executive order circulating in mid-May would have established a voluntary framework under which Anthropic, OpenAI and Google would submit advanced models for a federal security review of up to 90 days before public release. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, separately warning Wall Street chief executives about the cybersecurity risks posed by frontier models, was among officials characterising the administration as being in "close collaboration" with leading AI laboratories to finalise implementation.

Then Trump pulled back. He said he had postponed the signing because he "didn't like certain aspects" of the order, and that the US was "leading China, leading everybody" and he did not want anything to "get in the way of that lead." His AI adviser David Sacks characterised the draft as something "doomers wanted." The order was subsequently cancelled.

The practical result: in one week, the world's two largest AI-producing jurisdictions signalled interest in pre-deployment oversight and then collectively failed to formalise any of it. The UK's measures have institutional backing but no enforcement muscle. The US measures did not survive contact with presidential impatience.

What labs actually do will depend on whether the UK's forthcoming legislation clears Parliament with specific capability thresholds and penalties intact, or arrives diluted by pro-growth lobbying. The next concrete inflection point is the Frontier AI Bill's introduction — if it mandates pre-deployment evaluation with defined triggers and consequences, it would raise the bar for any developer seeking UK market access. If it doesn't, the global baseline stays where it is: voluntary, and therefore largely optional.
