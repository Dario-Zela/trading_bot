HSBC forecasts two Bank of Japan rate hikes in 2026 on sticky inflation

Bank of Japan headquarters in Nihonbashi, Tokyo — the neo-Baroque building completed in 1896 that houses the institution setting the rate path HSBC expects to accelerate. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
HSBC calls for two BoJ hikes this year — June and December — to 1.25%, citing a 2.8% trend inflation gauge and yen weakness as explicit policy drivers.

HSBC strategists have revised their Bank of Japan outlook to include two 25-basis-point rate increases this calendar year — one at the June meeting and a second in December — which would push the short-term policy rate from its current 0.75% to 1.25% by year-end. The call, circulated via Investing.com aggregation of HSBC's research, makes explicit what the central bank has avoided saying outright: that yen weakness is now a constraint on policy, not a tolerated side-effect of it.

The inflation case rests on a specific measure. Standard core CPI — excluding fresh food but including energy — fell to 1.4% in April, its lowest since March 2022 and well below the BoJ's 2% target, partly because government fuel subsidies are masking energy-cost pass-through from the Iran war. HSBC is not using that number. Instead, the bank points to the BoJ's own trend inflation gauge, which strips out those institutional distortions and came in at 2.8% year-on-year in April, up from 2.5% in March. That measure has now run comfortably above target for several consecutive months, suggesting the disinflationary window the BoJ had been quietly banking on has closed.

The yen framing is the more notable element of the call. HSBC argues that prolonged rate holds risk "renewed yen weakness," and that a narrower interest-rate differential with G10 economies "should at least help stabilise the yen." This language treats exchange-rate management as an explicit justification for tightening — framing the BoJ itself has deployed only obliquely. USD/JPY sat near 159–160 in late May, with the US-Japan rate spread still around 300 basis points, and an estimated ¥35 trillion in outstanding hedge-fund yen forward positions.

Carry-trade exposure

That figure concentrates the stakes. A faster-than-expected normalisation path compresses the funding advantage that has kept yen-carry positions persistently attractive — and the hike timetable matters more than the destination rate. An earlier June hike gives markets roughly six months to absorb the first move before December; delay into July or beyond, and a more compressed unwind cycle becomes likelier. Japan was already selling US Treasuries at its fastest quarterly pace in four years during Q1 2026 — roughly $30 billion in three months — a signal that some repatriation is underway regardless of policy timing.

OIS markets currently price in approximately two 25bp hikes for the year, so HSBC's view is not, strictly, a contrarian call. The differentiation is in the June timing — several analysts still favour July as the earliest date — and in the explicit acknowledgement of yen risk as a policy driver rather than an afterthought.

The BoJ next meets on June 16. The board voted 6-3 in April to hold, with the three dissenters pushing for an immediate hike to 1%. Board member Junko Nakagawa's term expires on June 29; her replacement, Ayano Sato, is considered more dovish. HSBC notes, however, that if other hawkish members align with the three existing dissenters, a 6-3 majority for action remains within reach. The trend inflation gauge and the June vote count are now the two numbers that matter.
