Iran shock halts the bull run at dawn; soft PCE data reignites it by the close

The New York Stock Exchange trading floor — site of Thursday's dramatic intraday reversal from Iran-shock lows to an all-time record close — Eduard Hueber / Asymptote Architecture / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
An Iran drone exchange sent S&P futures lower at dawn on Thursday, but an April PCE monthly print softer than feared lifted the index to a record close of 7,558 as the AI trade roared back.

Thursday's session opened with every indicator pointing the wrong way. Overnight, the United States military struck Iranian drone-control stations near Bandar Abbas — a measured response, the Pentagon insisted, to Iranian drone attacks on commercial vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz. By early morning in New York, Brent crude had jumped nearly four percent to around $97 a barrel, Asian equity indices had retreated across the board, and Bitcoin had fallen below $73,000, touching a six-week low. S&P 500 futures implied a down open of roughly 0.3 percent.

By 4 p.m. in New York, the index had closed at a fresh all-time record of 7,557. The distance between those two data points tells a good deal about which variables the market currently treats as load-bearing.

When the Strait rattles the tape

The exchange between Washington and Tehran was the second round of direct military action in a week. Iran's Revolutionary Guard vowed retaliation; President Trump dismissed reports of a proposed shipping agreement for the strait while simultaneously signalling openness to negotiations. The calculus for risk assets was simple: higher energy prices, fatter tail risks, reason to reduce exposure.

Asian markets absorbed the shock first. Hong Kong's Hang Seng fell close to two percent; South Korea's KOSPI lost 1.1 percent; Japan's Nikkei shed 0.1 percent after touching an intraday record earlier in the week. In London, the FTSE 100 dropped 0.9 percent — some 104 points — a move that masked a pronounced internal split. Defence names rallied hard: BAE Systems gained 1.55 percent to 1,995p, Babcock 1.50 percent, Melrose 1.19 percent. Energy sector losses cancelled those gains and then some. The index's structural over-weight in oil-sensitive stocks makes it a more direct casualty of Hormuz volatility than the technology-tilted S&P 500. When crude surges on a supply-risk spike, UK energy names catch a bid; but if the spike also portends demand destruction and a prolonged conflict discount, the FTSE ends up worse off than indices that earn more of their living from software.

In the cryptocurrency market, the divergence was equally legible. Bitcoin's slide below $73,000 confirmed the established pattern of the asset repricing as a risk-off outlet when geopolitical uncertainty spikes. AI-linked tokens ran a different playbook: the CoinDesk Computing Select Index added 1.9 percent on the day, led by RENDER and FET. Even within crypto, the AI narrative was trading on a separate register from the geopolitical one.

The reading that reset risk appetite

At 8:30 a.m. Eastern time, the Bureau of Economic Analysis published its April Personal Consumption Expenditures report. In absolute terms, the numbers were not comfortable. Headline PCE rose 3.8 percent year-over-year — its highest since early 2023 — as the Iran conflict's pass-through into gasoline prices, up more than 50 percent since hostilities began in late February, filtered into the consumer gauges. Core PCE, which strips out food and energy, edged up to 3.3 percent year-over-year from 3.2 percent in March.

What moved the market was the monthly trajectory rather than the annual level. Core PCE on a month-on-month basis came in at 0.2 percent, below the 0.3 percent consensus. Headline monthly was 0.4 percent versus 0.5 percent expected. Neither number signals disinflation in any meaningful sense — 3.3 percent core is still well above the Fed's 2 percent target — but both suggest the underlying pace of price gains is not accelerating in the way that would force the central bank into fresh tightening. That distinction, between elevated and deteriorating, was enough. Fed funds futures repriced modestly toward an earlier cut path.

By mid-session, reports emerged that Washington and Tehran had tentatively agreed to a 60-day memorandum of understanding extending the ceasefire and launching a phased reopening of Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic, pending President Trump's formal endorsement. The combination of benign monthly inflation momentum and geopolitical de-escalation proved decisive. The S&P 500 finished at a record 7,557.85, up 0.5 percent. The Nasdaq 100 closed at 30,089, also a record. The Dow added 32 points to 50,676.

Snowflake supplied the session's starkest single-name verdict, surging 37 percent on its best trading day ever after the cloud data platform reported what its management called an inflection quarter — beat on earnings, strong guidance, and a $6 billion five-year commitment to AWS infrastructure. Oracle advanced six percent, Palantir 5.7 percent, ServiceNow 5.3 percent, AMD 4.5 percent. The day that began with an Iran shock ended with the AI trade in full acceleration, a combination that would have seemed improbable at the open.

The gilt market's revised priorities

The UK sovereign bond market added its own testimony. Ten-year gilt yields, which had spiked sharply on the initial Iran escalation headlines, fell back to five-week lows by the London close, with the 30-year settling around 5.55 percent. The proximate driver was the same PCE print that supported equities: a softer monthly reading in the United States shifted rate-path expectations, removing some of the pressure on the Bank of England to match a potentially more hawkish Fed. Traders priced out roughly one anticipated BoE rate rise over the remainder of the year.

The pattern inverts a long-standing reflex. Historically, military escalation in a region supplying a quarter of global crude would prompt a flight to gilt safety, compressing yields. Thursday ran the opposite logic: Iran headlines pushed yields up, not down, because the dominant market read of a sustained conflict is inflationary — sustained high oil prices, persistent supply-chain disruption, potential stagflation. When PCE data reasserted itself and demonstrated that the monthly inflation pace was, at least, not worsening faster than feared, the rate-path outlook reclaimed primacy. Gilts rallied on a data release, not on the news that strikes had paused.

That inversion captures the day's governing theme. The market's current hierarchy runs: Fed policy trajectory first, AI growth thesis second, headline geopolitical shock a distant third. Thursday's session tested that ordering under fairly extreme conditions — live military exchanges, crude price volatility, a six-week low in Bitcoin — and the ordering held. Snowflake's 37 percent surge happened on the same afternoon as an Iranian retaliation threat. Whether that hierarchy reflects genuine structural change or accumulated risk complacency is a question the next data cycle will begin to answer.

The immediate test is whether the ceasefire MoU survives the weekend with Trump's signature. Brent fell more than one percent on Friday, heading for its steepest weekly decline since April, as markets priced in a deal. If the Strait of Hormuz reopens on schedule, May's energy-driven PCE spike may prove to be the peak in year-on-year headline inflation. The next formal read on that question is June 25, when the BEA publishes May's PCE data. Between now and then, the structural question is whether the market's current priorities — data over drama, software over crude — hold through what remains of a very active spring.
