UK household energy bills face renewed upward pressure as Middle East volatility feeds through

A domestic gas meter — the hardware through which the Strait of Hormuz's disruptions eventually reach British household bills. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Ofgem's July price cap hits £1,862 — a 13% jump blamed squarely on Middle East wholesale gas disruption, with October likely to push higher still.

British households are heading into summer facing energy bills at their highest since early 2024. Ofgem confirmed on 27 May that the quarterly price cap will rise by 13% from 1 July, adding £221 to the typical annual dual-fuel bill and lifting it to £1,862. The regulator was direct about the cause: higher wholesale gas prices driven by the ongoing conflict in the Middle East.

"Today's price change reflects continued volatility in global energy markets," said Tim Jarvis, Ofgem's chief executive. "Higher wholesale gas prices, driven by ongoing conflict in the Middle East, is impacting the price we pay for energy."

The pass-through is uneven. Gas bills rise by 24% — reflecting the UK's direct exposure to global LNG markets — while electricity increases by only 5%, cushioned by growing domestic renewables output. For the roughly 60% of accounts still on standard variable tariffs, the full increase arrives on the July statement. The 40% locked into fixed contracts are unaffected until renewal.

How the shock fed through

The pricing mechanism traces to events at the end of February, when Israel-US military strikes against Iran disrupted Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic. QatarEnergy, one of the world's largest LNG exporters, declared force majeure in early March after Iranian drone strikes forced production halts at its main facilities. UK wholesale gas prices rose roughly 75% between late February and late March. Brent crude oscillated between $97 and just above $100 a barrel at the end of May — well up from roughly $70 before the conflict started, though down from early-March peaks.

The oil-and-gas linkage in UK import contracts means that lag feeds directly into Ofgem's quarterly reference-price window. April's CPI reading of 2.8% offered a brief reprieve: that cap was set before the full wholesale shock registered. Energy inflation within the April basket, however, rose to 7.1% from 4.9% in March — a signal of what was still in the pipeline.

The Bank of England, which held Bank Rate at 3.75% in April, has already revised its inflation projections upward, warning that the July cap increase will push headline CPI back to around 3.3% in Q3 — some 1.4 percentage points above its February forecast. Household inflation expectations have risen notably, the Bank noted, which risks sustaining firms' pricing power into 2027.

The October price cap — the first to be set over a full winter demand period — is currently projected at around £1,923. Independent forecasters polled by HM Treasury put Q4 CPI at roughly 3.5%, in part because last year's one-off removal of Renewables Obligation subsidies from consumer bills cannot be repeated as a downward offset.

The outstanding variable is Hormuz. If shipping volumes recover before the August window that sets the October cap, some relief is possible. If they do not, energy analysts have warned that the trajectory through 2026-27 could embed higher bills well into next year — reopening, at considerable political cost, the cost-of-living debate the government had been moving to close. The next quarterly Ofgem announcement is due in late August.
