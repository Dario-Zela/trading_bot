S&P 500 closes at a record high as Snowflake surges 37% on AI data beat

In one sentence
Snowflake surged 37% on AI-driven data-warehouse revenue growth, lifting the S&P 500 to a record while concentration concerns quietly mount beneath the surface.

Snowflake's stock closed up 37% on Wednesday — erasing most of a year's worth of underperformance in a single session — after the data-cloud company posted Q1 fiscal 2027 results that beat on every major line and then raised its full-year outlook, fuelled almost entirely by enterprise demand for AI-powered data tools. The move helped push the S&P 500 to a fresh all-time closing high, extending a run of records that has become a fixture of the spring trading calendar.

Snowflake posted its strongest sequential dollar revenue growth on record; the stock's 37% jump reflected both the beat and a substantial raise to full-year guidance driven by AI product adoption.

What Snowflake actually reported

Product revenue for the quarter came in at $1.33 billion, up 34% year on year — the strongest sequential dollar gain in the company's history. Total revenue of $1.39 billion cleared the $1.32 billion analyst consensus by roughly 5%. Net revenue retention held at 126%, meaning existing customers kept expanding their spend materially.

The headline driver was enterprise adoption of Snowflake's AI stack. More than 7,100 accounts are now actively using Cortex Code — the company's AI coding assistant, known internally as "CoCo" — and accounts using Snowflake Intelligence, a broader agentic data-analysis layer, more than doubled quarter over quarter. Chief executive Sridhar Ramaswamy said CoCo "had the largest driver" impact on the guidance upgrade, calling the quarter "the strongest sequential dollar growth in our history" and describing Snowflake as "at the center of the enterprise AI revolution."

Full-year product revenue guidance was raised to $5.84 billion — implying 31% growth, up from a prior 27% target. Snowflake simultaneously disclosed a five-year, $6 billion expanded commitment to AWS infrastructure, more than double its previous deal, as it leans into Amazon's custom AI chips to reduce compute costs.

A record — but an uneven one

The Snowflake surge contributed to yet another S&P 500 all-time close, continuing a recovery from March lows that has taken the index toward 7,500. But the rally's internal architecture is worth examining alongside the headline number.

Analysis of the index's most recent 28-session advance found that just 10 stocks generated 69% of the S&P 500's total gains — Alphabet, Nvidia, Amazon, Broadcom, and Microsoft among the primary contributors. The top ten index constituents now account for roughly 40% of total market capitalisation, a concentration that exceeds the peak reached during the dot-com era.

That context makes Snowflake's blowout quarter legible in two ways simultaneously. One reading is straightforwardly constructive: AI spending is converting into auditable revenue at enterprise scale, and a 34% product-revenue acceleration with a 126% net retention rate is not easy to dismiss as hype. The second is more cautious. A market climbing on a narrow set of names can reverse sharply if those names stumble — and at Snowflake's post-earnings multiple, the margin for error has narrowed considerably.

For now, Wall Street chose the first reading.

What to watch

The next material test arrives in mid-August, when Snowflake reports Q2 results against guidance it has now meaningfully raised. The key question is whether Cortex Code's 7,100-account user base — which grew faster than revenue — translates into proportionate dollar expansion, or whether usage is widening without lifting average spend per customer. For the broader market, the ratio of advancing to declining stocks and the Russell 2000's performance relative to the S&P 500 will indicate whether record closes are gaining depth — or whether the same few names are simply marking time higher, again.
