Japan expands the eligible population for GSK's Arexvy RSV vaccine

In one sentence
Japan has approved Arexvy for at-risk adults aged 18–59, making GSK's RSV vaccine the first in Japan to cover all higher-risk adults from age 18 upward.

Japan's health regulator has broadened the authorised use of GlaxoSmithKline's Arexvy vaccine, clearing it on 18 May for adults aged 18 to 49 who carry conditions that raise their risk of severe respiratory syncytial virus disease. The decision by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare makes Arexvy the first RSV vaccine in Japan to cover all adult at-risk populations from age 18 upward — sitting alongside existing indications for adults aged 50–59 at increased risk, and for all adults aged 60 and older. The updated prescribing information now explicitly names immunocompromised patients, including transplant recipients, as a qualifying risk group, giving clinicians clearer guidance on who to vaccinate.

The commercial read matters as much as the regulatory one. Japan has the world's highest share of citizens aged over 65, and the newly eligible cohort adds further scale: more than 42 million Japanese adults fall into the 18–49 bracket, a meaningful proportion of whom carry the chronic conditions — cardiovascular disease, COPD, asthma — that regulators associate with severe RSV outcomes. For GSK, which counts Japan among its most strategically important markets, successive expansions of the Arexvy label represent incremental revenue from an already-approved asset rather than the riskier economics of a new product launch.

What the clinical data support

The expanded indication rests principally on a Phase IIIb trial enrolling 1,458 participants across 52 sites in six countries (NCT06389487), which showed Arexvy producing a non-inferior immune response in adults aged 18–49 compared with those aged 60 and older — the population in which the vaccine's efficacy was first established. A separate Phase IIb study in 386 organ-transplant recipients (NCT05921903) demonstrated robust immunogenicity from a single dose, underpinning the immunocompromised designation in the label update. Safety data across the trials were consistent with earlier experience: mostly mild-to-moderate injection-site reactions, myalgia, and fatigue.

RSV commands less public attention than influenza but carries a comparable clinical toll in vulnerable adults. The virus infects an estimated 64 million people globally each year and can precipitate pneumonia, COPD exacerbations, and — a finding that has shifted clinical thinking in recent years — major adverse cardiovascular events including heart attack and stroke. In Japan specifically, a database study spanning 2015–2018 estimated RSV-attributable cardiorespiratory hospitalizations at between 100 and 124 per 100,000 person-years in adults aged 60 and above, a burden that rises steeply with age.¹

Sanjay Gurunathan, GSK's head of vaccines and infectious diseases R&D, described the approval as "the first covering all at-risk adults in Japan" and said it should help clinicians reduce severe RSV outcomes across a broader population.

The immediate forward marker is the European Medicines Agency, which is currently reviewing a comparable application to extend Arexvy's label to at-risk adults under 60 across the EU. A positive ruling there — expected in the coming months — would close the last major regulatory gap in the vaccine's developed-market footprint and sharpen the commercial case for sustained investment in the RSV category. In Japan itself, uptake of adult vaccines has historically trailed public awareness; how quickly the newly eligible population converts to actual vaccination will be the metric that moves the revenue needle.

¹ PLOS One / medRxiv: Estimation of RSV-attributable hospitalizations among older adults in Japan, 2015–2018.
