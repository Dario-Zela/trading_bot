Musalem puts a rate hike back on the table as Bessent signals confidence in the Fed

Alberto Musalem at his appointment as St. Louis Fed President, January 2024 — Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis / Axios
In one sentence
St. Louis Fed's Musalem revives the rate-hike option and backs demand-side balance-sheet discipline, as Bessent signals quiet confidence in the new Warsh Fed.

At an economic conference in Reykjavik on Thursday, St. Louis Federal Reserve President Alberto Musalem left markets in no doubt that monetary easing is not the only direction still on the table. Speaking with Bloomberg's Stephanie Flanders at a joint event hosted by the Central Bank of Iceland and Northwestern University, Musalem said flatly that "the possibility that we would consider an interest rate increase is greater than zero" — language that consensus had largely stopped pricing for.

That is not the signal markets wanted. Since the Fed's last rate move, the working assumption has anchored on a next step of cuts. But with U.S. PCE inflation at 3.8% year-over-year in April — the fastest pace in three years, amplified by Hormuz-linked gas price spikes — Musalem argued the calculus has shifted decisively. "The balance of economic risks have shifted toward the inflation side," he said, before laying out his trigger: "If we don't see disinflation in the next one to two quarters, that would concern me."

The fed funds target currently sits in the 3.50%–3.75% range. Unemployment stands at 4.3%. On those numbers, rate cuts look premature at best; a hike, in Musalem's framing, is a live option if inflation does not cooperate. Financial markets are now pricing better-than-even odds of at least one hike before year-end — a striking reversal from the cut-cycle expectations that dominated early 2026.

The balance sheet, done differently

Beyond the rate signal, Musalem drew a distinction between two paths to a smaller Fed balance sheet — and came down firmly on one. Reducing the banking system's demand for reserves, he argued, offers a smoother route than compressing reserve supply through active quantitative tightening. The difference is material: demand-side reduction lets financial conditions tighten gradually as banks adjust, while supply-side contraction can produce abrupt liquidity stress. It is a preference for patience and market discipline over blunt-force normalization.

Musalem also pushed back, implicitly, on the new Fed chair's more optimistic framing. He warned that it would be risky to rely on the prospect of AI-driven productivity gains to solve today's inflation problem — a direct counter to Kevin Warsh's view that a technology-led disinflation may be in train.

In Washington, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent offered a deliberately low-key counterpoint at the White House press briefing. Bessent confirmed he had breakfast Thursday with Warsh — the first meeting between the two since Warsh was sworn in on May 22, succeeding Jerome Powell in a 54–45 Senate vote. Asked whether he had pressed the new chair on rates, Bessent demurred: "I had breakfast with Chair Powell 41 times, and I never did that." He said only that he believes Warsh "will do the right thing to balance inflation and growth."

The signal from the Treasury was alignment without instruction — studied restraint that left the Fed's autonomy formally intact while telegraphing White House comfort with the new leadership. Whether that comfort extends to patience on rates, or eventually shades into pressure for cuts, will depend heavily on the data. The Fed's next policy meeting is in mid-June; between now and then, one more inflation print lands.
