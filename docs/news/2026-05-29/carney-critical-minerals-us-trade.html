Carney positions Canada's critical minerals as leverage in US trade talks

Prime Minister Mark Carney at the White House, May 2025. — Daniel Torok / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Carney used a New York speech to frame Canada's minerals as indispensable to the US, but offered no new demands ahead of the July CUSMA review.

Speaking to a room of New York financiers on Thursday, Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney did what politicians do well before high-stakes negotiations: he reminded the other side what it stands to lose.

In a speech at the Economic Club of New York — timed deliberately to the approaching July 1 review of the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement — Carney catalogued Canada's resource endowment in terms calibrated for a Washington audience. With its reserves of potash, nickel, copper, and uranium, he said, Canada can be "the most reliable supplier that America needs to put affordable food on the table, to strengthen its national defence and meet exploding demand to power AI." He noted that Canada already supplies 99 percent of American natural gas imports, 85 percent of electricity imports, and 60 percent of crude oil imports.

"A country that cannot feed, fuel or defend itself is not truly sovereign."

The line drew applause. It was also directed at Washington as much as Ottawa.

The semantic game

Carney's position contains a studied ambiguity. Asked in early May whether Canada would use energy and critical minerals as leverage in negotiations, he rejected the framing: "I reject that characterization of, 'It's leverage.' It's looking at, is it in our mutual interest to trade more? Where is it? And if it's not there, we have other options."

The distinction matters politically — U.S. Trade Representative Jamieson Greer had explicitly warned visiting Canadian officials that Washington would not accept Canada using natural resources as a negotiating cudgel. Carney's formulation threads that needle: Canada's minerals are presented not as a threat to withhold, but as an asset Washington would be foolish to alienate. The effect, strategically, is much the same.

What is missing from the picture — and what the Economic Club speech did not supply — is any concrete new offer, timeline, or concession. Canada's trade minister Dominic LeBlanc is scheduled to travel to Washington next week, but formal bilateral talks under the CUSMA review process have barely begun. LeBlanc has held just one day of in-person meetings with Greer in seven months. The U.S. and Mexico, meanwhile, have multiple negotiating rounds already scheduled.

What the clock says

The CUSMA review deadline of July 1 is weeks away, and almost nobody expects a deal by then. Canada enters that window with a genuine structural asset — its mineral and energy position is not imaginary — but also without a clear articulation of what it wants in exchange for deeper supply-chain integration. Asserting indispensability is the beginning of a negotiating posture, not the posture itself.

Canada holds cobalt reserves ranked third globally, nickel reserves ranked seventh, and lithium deposits large enough, in theory, to supply roughly half of cumulative global demand from 2030 to 2050. These are real cards. Whether Ottawa knows how to play them is the question that Thursday's speech left unanswered.

The next indicator will be LeBlanc's Washington meetings. If they produce a joint statement on minerals cooperation — even a framework document — the posture graduates from rhetoric to process. If not, Carney's resource pitch remains what it was this week: a reminder, not a move.
