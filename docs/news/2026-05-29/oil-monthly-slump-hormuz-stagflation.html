Oil heads for a 19% monthly collapse even as Hormuz risk embeds quietly into inflation forecasts

A NASA MODIS satellite image of the Strait of Hormuz and Musandam Peninsula — the chokepoint through which roughly a quarter of the world's seaborne oil trade passes. — NASA / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Bessent secured a public assurance from Oman's ambassador that Iran won't toll Hormuz, lifting the tail risk that had embedded a stagflationary premium into crude markets now down ~17% in May.

The fact that a U.S. Treasury Secretary felt compelled to telephone Oman's ambassador — and then announce the outcome to reporters — says more about what markets had been pricing than the assurance itself.

On May 28, Scott Bessent disclosed that he had received direct confirmation from Oman's ambassador that Iran has no plans to introduce transit fees in the Strait of Hormuz. The phrasing was precise: "I had a phone call with the Omani ambassador this morning, and he assured me that there are no plans to introduce a fee for passage through the strait." A diplomat's words, delivered as market intelligence, by a Treasury Secretary who had hours earlier sanctioned the Iranian authority already charging ships more than $1 million per transit of the waterway.

The picture is of a real and recent fear — not abstract energy-security anxiety, but live concern, at the highest level of the U.S. executive, that a toll on roughly a quarter of the world's seaborne oil trade was imminent enough to warrant a call and a public statement.

What the Call Actually Meant

The Strait of Hormuz has been the central fault line of the 2026 oil shock. Since Operation Epic Fury on February 28 — the U.S.-Israel air campaign against Iran that prompted the strait's effective closure — crude markets have endured what the IEA characterised as the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Brent hit $126 per barrel in the panic buying. Dubai crude reached a record $166 on March 19. U.S. CPI climbed to 3.8% year-over-year by April, its highest since 2023, driven by a 17.87% surge in energy prices.

Against that backdrop, Iran established the "Persian Gulf Strait Authority" and began levying transit charges — over $1 million per ship, according to multiple reports. Iranian officials also floated a joint mechanism with Oman to institutionalise these fees permanently. Oman, historically a diplomatic go-between in U.S.-Iran dealings, was simultaneously offering its deep-water ports at Duqm, Salalah, and Sohar as bypass routes, though Iranian drone strikes damaged infrastructure at at least some of those facilities.

Bessent's response was direct: any Omani support for a tolling mechanism would be "a non-starter," and the U.S. retained the option to sanction Omani citizens and financial institutions. The Treasury simultaneously sanctioned the Persian Gulf Strait Authority. Within hours, the ambassador called back with the all-clear.

The signal matters independent of the substance. Someone in markets — or in the policy apparatus that reads those signals — had been treating an Iran-imposed Hormuz toll as a non-trivial outcome. You do not threaten a historically friendly country with secondary sanctions, then go public with the assurance, unless the threat had a credible near-term form.

The Price of Fear, Quantified

Even without a single barrel formally taxed, the toll threat had already worked its way into prices. War-risk ship insurance premiums for Hormuz transits roughly tripled — from 0.125% to between 0.2% and 0.4% of the ship's insured value per voyage — adding approximately $250,000 per leg for a very large crude carrier. Barclays and Goldman Sachs both flagged the risk of sustained elevated crude if the disruption ran into the second half of the year.

The supply accounting is stark. Global oil output fell a further 1.8 million barrels per day in April alone, leaving total output at 95.1 mb/d — roughly 12.8 mb/d below pre-war levels. Gulf producers dependent on Hormuz access were running 14.4 mb/d below their pre-conflict baseline. European central bankers warned that a protracted conflict risked pushing Germany and Italy into technical recession via stagflation. The Fed's options narrowed: tighten into the oil shock and risk growth, or hold and watch inflation print above 3.5%.

Nineteen Percent, and Falling

Brent crude has shed roughly 17% since the start of May, tracking toward one of its worst monthly performances in several years — from around $116 per barrel on May 1 to roughly $96 on May 28. A move of that scale, in that direction, while the underlying conflict remains active, reveals what traders were actually carrying: an outsized geopolitical risk premium, now being unwound.

The proximate trigger was OPEC+, which met without the UAE for the first time since Abu Dhabi's departure from the group on May 1. The remaining seven members — Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman — approved a 188,000 barrel-per-day increase in June production, slightly below May's 206,000 bpd addition. That taper path, combined with growing confidence that ceasefire negotiations would eventually reopen Hormuz flows, prompted traders to rapidly strip out the disruption premium embedded in prices since March.

The IEA projects that even if traffic through the strait gradually resumes from June onward, global oil supply will average 3.9 mb/d below 2025 levels across the full year — a meaningful structural shortfall. But the market is evidently prepared to look through it, or at least to price in a more benign resolution.

What the Bessent call removed was the worst-case scenario: a formalised Iranian toll that would have embedded a permanent risk premium into every cargo transiting the Persian Gulf. What it did not remove was the supply disruption, the inflation it has already produced, or the war-risk insurance lines unlikely to reset until sustained traffic data confirm the all-clear.

The credibility of the oil market's new, lower level rests on two variables: whether the ceasefire talks that have been circling reach something enforceable by mid-June, and whether ship-traffic data — published monthly by Lloyd's and the IEA — begin to show the volume recovery that the current price already implies.
