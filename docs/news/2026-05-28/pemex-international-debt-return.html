Pemex bondholders urge Mexico's state oil giant to re-enter international debt markets

The Torre Ejecutiva Pemex in Mexico City, headquarters of the state oil company — JEDIKNIGHT1970 / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 2.5)
In one sentence
Foreign bondholders including BlackRock are pressing Pemex to issue international bonds for the first time since 2023, citing improved EBITDA and $13bn in near-term maturities.

For the first time in more than three years, Pemex's foreign bondholders want the company back in the international debt markets — and are making their case loudly enough that Mexico City is listening, even if it is not yet convinced.

A group of investors that includes BlackRock, the world's largest asset manager, has argued that Petroleos Mexicanos is finally positioned to tap global bond markets again. The last time the state oil company sold a cross-border deal was in 2023; since then, a combination of chronic cash deficits, political caution and an aggressive government-led liability management programme has kept Pemex confined to domestic peso paper and federal bailout mechanisms.

That dynamic began shifting in February, when Pemex raised MX$31.5 billion — roughly $1.8 billion — on the Mexican Stock Exchange, ending a six-year absence from its home market. For bondholders watching from New York and London, that was a sign. The logical next step, they argue, is a return to the dollar market.

The financing math

The pitch rests partly on need and partly on improved optics. Pemex faces approximately $13 billion in debt maturities in 2026 alone, and roughly 46% of its total outstanding obligations — some $79 billion at the end of March, the lowest level since 2014 — come due between now and 2029. Against those rollover pressures, domestic peso issuance is a rounding error.

BlackRock's director of active investments in Mexico, José Luis Ortega, has said the company's "very high financing needs" make a return to international capital markets likely, and that BlackRock would consider participating. The qualifier is that Pemex still requires federal government support "for several years." That caveat matters: Pemex's standalone free cash flow remained negative in the first quarter of 2026 even after a 58.3 billion peso cash injection from Mexico City, and the company posted a net loss of 45.99 billion pesos for the period. Moody's projects negative free cash flow of roughly $7 billion per year through 2029.

Still, the trajectory has moved in the right direction. Adjusted EBITDA recovered to $6.71 billion in the first quarter, up from a weak fourth-quarter 2025 base. Government support has reduced total financial debt by more than $5 billion in the past year. Fitch upgraded the company one notch to BB+, leaving it a single step below Mexico's sovereign rating — and Pemex's 10-year spread has compressed 136 basis points since July 2025, to around 355 basis points, trading inside the sovereign on some metrics following the government's extraordinary liability management push.

What a return would signal

For emerging-market credit investors, the symbolism of a Pemex international deal extends beyond the single issuer. Pemex is widely treated as a quasi-sovereign proxy for Mexican risk, and its last extended absence from the dollar market was read as a sign of systemic stress. A successful new issue would signal that Mexico's most complex credit story — a company that carries a debt-to-EBITDA ratio of more than 12 times, by Moody's reckoning — can find price-sensitive buyers without a sovereign guarantee wrapped around every tranche.

It would also test whether the broader EM credit bid that has been compressing spreads across the asset class will hold for names at the riskier end of the quasi-sovereign spectrum. Demand for Pemex's 2025 P-CAPs transaction, a $12 billion government-backed instrument, reached $23 billion in orders — a data point that bondholders are using to argue that the investor base is there.

President Claudia Sheinbaum's administration has so far resisted the push, preferring a mix of federal cash injections, tax relief and domestic issuance to manage the balance sheet. The government's concern is not just pricing: every dollar of international debt that Pemex takes on complicates Mexico's own sovereign borrowing calculus.

But the maturities arriving in 2026 and 2027 may leave the administration with little room. With crude exports running at 405,000 barrels per day in the first quarter — down 38.8% from a year earlier — the operating cash engine is not producing the liquidity buffer the government would need to refinance entirely from its own balance sheet. The next indicator to watch is whether Pemex mandates banks for an international roadshow before the summer credit window closes.
