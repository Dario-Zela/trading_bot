Seaport calls GE Aerospace selloff overdone, citing engine aftermarket strength

A CFM LEAP engine, the turbofan at the heart of GE Aerospace's aftermarket growth thesis — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Seaport initiated GE Aerospace at Buy with a $375 target, arguing the ~10% pullback from its March high ignores the durability of engine aftermarket demand.

Seaport Research Partners initiated GE Aerospace with a Buy and a $375 target, arguing the stock's recent retreat has outrun the damage to its actual business.

GE Aerospace shares have pulled back roughly 10% from their March peak of $348, even as the company's engine aftermarket — its highest-margin segment — continues to grow. Seaport Research Partners analyst Richard Safran initiated coverage on Wednesday with a Buy rating and a $375 price target, calling the slide a buying opportunity rather than a signal of fundamental deterioration.

The case rests on a structural dynamic that has quietly extended the aerospace upcycle well past initial expectations. Because Boeing and Airbus have struggled to ramp narrow-body production — both still face supply-chain bottlenecks on fuselages, engines, and interiors — airlines have been forced to keep older aircraft in service longer than planned. That, in turn, means more shop visits, more spare parts, and more demand for the kind of maintenance contracts on which GE makes its best margins. Safran put aftermarket revenue growth at above 10% annually through 2030, a notch above the Wall Street consensus of roughly 9%.

The near-term numbers support the optimism selectively. Q1 2026 earnings per share came in at $1.86 against estimates of $1.60 — a 16% beat — and new orders surged 87% year-over-year. Yet management held full-year EPS guidance unchanged at $7.10–$7.40, which disappointed investors priced for an immediate raise. The stock dropped around 5% on earnings day in late April. Geopolitical noise added to the pressure: the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps publicly named GE among a list of U.S. companies it threatened, an item that moved Reddit more than fundamentals warranted but still rattled sentiment.

The aftermarket engine

Central to Seaport's thesis is the LEAP engine cycle. CFM's LEAP-1A and LEAP-1B, which power the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX families respectively, are now entering their first major overhaul windows after accumulating thousands of flight cycles. GE has committed $1 billion to expand its MRO network to meet that demand, with roughly half directed at the LEAP aftermarket specifically. Seven Premier MRO partners — including Iberia and Delta TechOps — are being brought up to LEAP certification.

The $190 billion order backlog underpins the longer runway. Seaport's earnings model — $7.55 adjusted EPS in 2026, $8.67 in 2027, $10.25 in 2028 — sits meaningfully above the 2028 consensus of $9.80, implying that the market is not yet pricing in the full compounding effect of aftermarket growth as the installed fleet ages. The firm also cited GE's $1.58 billion in 2025 R&D spend as a competitive moat against RTX Corporation and Rolls-Royce.

The honest caveat: Seaport's case is largely an analyst constructing a model from publicly available production data and management commentary. There is no proprietary shop-visit count, no independent read on LEAP utilization rates, and no fresh data on airline MRO spending intentions. The argument is coherent and internally consistent — it just rests on extrapolation rather than primary evidence.

Seven of the 19 analysts covering GE Aerospace already had Buy ratings before Seaport's initiation; the consensus price target sits near $350. Safran's $375 is the high end of a crowded bullish camp. The next catalyst that would actually test his thesis: GE's midyear guidance update, expected alongside Q2 results in late July.
