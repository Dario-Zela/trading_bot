Fed's Cook puts rate hike back on the table, warns AI displacement will arrive before job gains

Fed Governor Lisa D. Cook, who signalled readiness to raise rates if the disinflation trajectory fails to materialise — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Fed Governor Cook warns at Stanford SIEPR that a rate hike is live if disinflation stalls, citing oil prices, tariff passthrough, and AI investment as upside inflation risks.

Fed Governor Lisa Cook stepped to the podium at Stanford's SIEPR on Tuesday and delivered a message that markets, priced for rate cuts this year, may not have wanted to hear: the door to higher rates is still open.

"I am prepared to raise rates if the expected disinflation does not appear in a timely manner."

— Governor Lisa Cook, Stanford SIEPR, May 27, 2026

Cook, speaking at the Stanford Institute for Economic Policy Research, said that holding the federal funds rate steady at its current 3½–3¾ percent band "remains the right policy for now" — but she framed that hold as conditional, not conclusive. After five years of above-target inflation, she said, the risk of price pressures becoming embedded in wage- and price-setting behaviour is too significant to dismiss.

Inflation's wrong direction

The governor did not use soft language. Inflation, she said, is "clearly moving in the wrong direction" — and risks, in her assessment, remain "tilted toward higher inflation." The culprits are familiar by now: last year's tariff waves, elevated energy costs since the outbreak of the Iran war on February 28, and an AI-driven investment surge that has stoked demand for chips, software, and the skilled construction labour needed to build data centres at scale.

That last point has an inflationary arithmetic of its own. Projected data centre investment stands at roughly $1.5 trillion, according to figures Cook cited. Electricity and water prices have each risen around 5 percent annually as grid infrastructure strains to keep up. Specialty construction wages are climbing. These are not transient shocks — they are capacity constraints being pressed by a structural investment boom, and Cook appears to regard them as durable unless demand or supply conditions shift.

With the April unemployment rate at 4.3 percent and the labour market described as "broadly stable," the Fed lacks the easy alibi of a cooling economy to justify rate cuts. Markets have been pricing in two 25-basis-point reductions over the next year, per Desk survey medians as of late April. Cook's remarks are a direct challenge to that timeline.

The oil variable

If there is one variable Cook watches above the others right now, it is crude. Oil prices have already shifted significantly since February 28 — the Iran war's start date — and Cook was explicit that further moves in the wrong direction would be "problematic" for the Fed's policy path.

The mechanism is direct: energy costs feed into headline CPI quickly and, over a lag, into core measures via transportation and manufacturing inputs. A sustained rise would undercut the disinflation trajectory Cook said she is counting on to avoid raising rates. The corollary is equally clear — the disinflationary scenario the market has been trading depends, in part, on Hormuz remaining open and crude supplies staying orderly. That is a geopolitical bet, not a macroeconomic certainty, and Cook's language flags the Fed is aware of the exposure.

She did not name a price threshold. But describing an adverse oil move as a potential trigger for policy reconsideration puts commodity traders on notice that the Fed's patience is energy-price-contingent.

When AI creates jobs — and when it doesn't

Cook broke from the more sanguine readings of AI's labour market trajectory. Worker displacement, she said, will arrive before new roles are created — the transition will not be smooth, and the Fed is watching the asymmetry.

"We could be approaching the most significant reorganisation of work in generations," she said, pointing to already-visible cracks: declining demand for entry-level coders, softer hiring of recent graduates as firms deploy AI for tasks previously assigned to junior staff, and a broader pattern of "job displacement may precede job creation" that could push participation rates lower before any productivity dividend shows up in employment figures.

The policy implication is uncomfortable. The Fed's standard toolkit — adjusting the cost of borrowing — cannot easily distinguish between unemployment caused by deficient demand and unemployment caused by structural technological displacement. If AI accelerates labour shedding while productivity gains keep output buoyant, the unemployment rate could rise without signalling the kind of slack that would justify rate cuts. Cook acknowledged as much in February, noting that in a productivity boom "a rise in unemployment may not indicate increased slack," and that "education, workforce, and other policy that is nonmonetary may be better suited" to address AI-related hardship.

Her Stanford remarks carried the same structural caution into the near-term inflation debate. AI investment is itself inflationary right now — through energy demand, chip prices, and construction — even as it promises eventual productivity gains. The timing mismatch cuts both ways: costs arrive before benefits in wages and output, just as job losses arrive before new roles.

What to watch next

For traders recalibrating after Cook's remarks, the next hard data points are the June CPI release and the subsequent FOMC meeting on June 17–18. If headline inflation prints above expectations — particularly if energy or shelter components re-accelerate — Cook's language gives the committee clear cover to remove or qualify any easing bias entirely.

On the labour side, the May payrolls report (due June 6) will be scrutinised for signs that the entry-level softness Cook flagged is broadening into wider weakness. A deteriorating jobs picture could push Cook toward rate-cut readiness, but she was careful at Stanford to frame cuts as contingent on unemployment "beginning to rise" — not merely stalling at 4.3 percent.

The Fed held at its April 29 meeting amid internal dissent, and Cook's Stanford appearance confirms that the debate inside the institution is not between cutting now or cutting later — it is, at least for her, between holding and the possibility of tightening further. Markets pricing in two cuts this year are trading a different Fed than the one Cook described on Tuesday.
