Deutsche Bank weighs Rio Tinto's copper pipeline against near-term China softness

Bingham Canyon Mine, Utah — Rio Tinto's Kennecott operation, among the deepest open-pit copper mines in the world — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Deutsche Bank kept its Hold on Rio Tinto, lifting the target to 6,900p, but the miner's copper upside remains years away while China's steel and aluminium weakness is already here.

Deutsche Bank raised its price target on Rio Tinto from 6,200 pence to 6,900 pence on May 15, maintaining a Hold rating — a nudge upward that did little to resolve the fundamental tension hanging over the stock. The bank's analyst Liam Fitzpatrick acknowledged the miner's long-dated copper growth pipeline as a structural positive while noting that near-term demand softness in Chinese steel and aluminium remains a genuine headwind.

The result is a piece of institutional commentary that says, in effect: we like the direction, but not yet.

The copper argument, and its time problem

Rio Tinto's case as a copper play is not hard to make in broad strokes. The company is targeting production of 800–870 kilotonnes this year, with a stated ambition to reach one million tonnes per year by 2030. The Oyu Tolgoi underground mine in Mongolia — the cornerstone of that target — is ramping toward an average of around 500 ktpa between 2028 and 2036, making it among the highest-grade large-scale copper developments anywhere on earth.

Beyond Oyu Tolgoi, the pipeline extends further still. Resolution Copper, a 55/45 joint venture with BHP in Arizona, completed a land exchange earlier this year that unlocked a further $500 million of pre-development spending. Resolution has the theoretical capacity to supply up to 25 percent of US copper demand for decades — a statement that contains, embedded within it, the timeline problem. Decades. Resolution will not contribute material production this decade.

For equity investors pricing assets on two- to three-year horizons, that copper optionality looks better on a slide deck than it does in a discounted cash flow model. The payoff is real; the timing is not.

China softness is not a new story — which makes it harder to dismiss

The near-term headwind Deutsche Bank flags is by now a well-worn macro theme. China's steel demand fell roughly 2 percent in 2025, the property sector's multi-year contraction the primary driver, and analysts expect a further 1 percent decline in 2026 before any stabilisation. Rio Tinto's Pilbara iron ore operations — still the company's largest earnings contributor — are exposed directly to that dynamic, despite strong Q1 production figures (up 13 percent year-on-year, the second-highest first-quarter result since 2018).

Aluminium complicates the picture in the opposite direction. China's state-mandated production cap of 45 million tonnes is beginning to tighten global supply, and demand from electric vehicles, solar panels and power infrastructure is absorbing enough volume to offset construction weakness. Rio Tinto's integrated aluminium business delivered solid Q1 results, and the Chalco joint venture in Brazil positions it for further exposure to that segment.

But a Hold rating priced around 6,900p implies that the iron ore drag and the copper timeline together justify patience over conviction. That is a defensible position — it just is not a particularly useful one for investors trying to decide whether long-cycle commodity plays have a role in a slowdown environment.

What would change the thesis

The clearest near-term catalyst would be a material improvement in Chinese property data, which would lift iron ore sentiment and close some of the gap between Rio's current valuation and its copper-adjusted intrinsic value. Alternatively, a meaningful acceleration at Oyu Tolgoi — production reaching the upper end of guidance consistently — would demonstrate that the 2028–2036 ramp is on track.

Rio Tinto's half-year results, due in late July, will be the next proper read on whether Q1's iron ore volumes were a turning point or a statistical blip. Until then, Deutsche Bank's view captures the trade-off accurately, if unenthusiastically.
