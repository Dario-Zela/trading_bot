Trump draws two red lines on Iran: no HEU to Moscow or Beijing, and Hormuz stays open to all

The Strait of Hormuz, the 21-mile-wide chokepoint through which roughly 20 percent of global oil transits. Iran has kept the waterway closed since March 4, 2026. — NASA/GSFC, MODIS Rapid Response — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Trump's demand that Iran's HEU be destroyed rather than stored by Russia or China, paired with a refusal to offer sanctions relief, complicates the path to a nuclear ceasefire deal.

Three months into a shooting war with Iran, the United States finds itself navigating a peculiar diplomacy: one where the same president who launched strikes that killed Iran's supreme leader is now drawing red lines to define the shape of a ceasefire deal. On Wednesday, at a White House Cabinet meeting convened in the shadow of unresolved negotiations, Donald Trump added two explicit conditions to any peace framework — conditions that narrow the space for an agreement even as both sides signal readiness to move forward.

The uranium problem

The first condition concerns Iran's stockpile of highly enriched uranium. Before the February 28 strikes that opened Operation Epic Fury, Iran held approximately 440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60 percent — short of weapons-grade but sufficient, if further refined to 90 percent, for roughly ten crude nuclear devices. The strikes damaged several Iranian nuclear facilities, but significant portions of the stockpile are believed to have survived, with material reportedly transferred to Isfahan before the attacks.

Russia had floated a practical solution: Moscow would accept the stockpile for safekeeping as a confidence measure, in the spirit of an arrangement that dates to early Obama-era diplomacy. Trump rejected that framing without ambiguity on Wednesday. The uranium would be "turned over to the United States to be brought home and destroyed, destroyed in place, or at another acceptable location" — but it would not travel to Moscow or Beijing. "I am not comfortable with Russia or China having that material," he said.

Iran's foreign ministry responded that the stockpile "will under no circumstances be transferred anywhere" — a formulation that, at face value, rules out both the American and the Russian options simultaneously. According to a senior US official speaking to CBS News, Iran has agreed "in principle" to HEU disposal, but the mechanism for doing so remains unresolved.

The structural difficulty runs deeper than the mechanics. In the same week, Trump told PBS News that Iran would receive no sanctions relief in exchange for surrendering its enriched uranium. That position is a significant departure from the architecture of the 2015 JCPOA, in which sanctions relief was the central inducement that persuaded Tehran to cap its enrichment program. Remove that lever — while simultaneously demanding outright destruction rather than the third-party storage Russia proposed — and Iranian negotiators face the unenviable task of explaining to their leadership why they gave up a strategic deterrent and received nothing comparable in return.

The Kremlin's willingness to absorb the stockpile also hints at Moscow's interest in retaining leverage within any postwar Middle East order. Trump's red line blocks that avenue, which consolidates Western control over the deal's terms — but only if there is a deal to speak of.

The shipping lane and the sovereignty dispute

The second condition concerns the Strait of Hormuz, the 21-mile-wide chokepoint through which roughly 20 percent of global oil and a third of liquefied natural gas normally transits. Iran sealed the strait on March 4, mining its approaches and attacking merchant vessels; the International Energy Agency described the resulting supply disruption as the largest in the history of the global oil market. Brent crude spiked past $120 per barrel before ceasefire talks began pulling prices back.

Trump declared Wednesday that the strait must reopen under any deal — to everyone, with no party controlling or tolling passage. "The strait's gotta be open to everybody. Nobody's going to control it," he said. "We'll watch over it, but nobody's going to control it."

Iran's state news agency Fars pushed back almost immediately, insisting the strait "remains under Iran's management" and calling Trump's framing "incomplete and inconsistent with reality." The disagreement is not merely semantic. Iran views the Hormuz corridor as partly within its territorial waters and has long treated the threat of closure as one of its primary strategic cards — the one asset that makes its adversaries calculate twice. Asking Tehran to formally cede that deterrent, even implicitly through language enshrining universal access, touches a sovereignty question that no Iranian leadership can easily concede in public.

The broader deal framework that Axios reported earlier in the week had offered a more pragmatic middle path: the strait would reopen during a 60-day ceasefire window, Iran would clear its mines, the US would lift its naval blockade on Iranian ports and issue limited sanctions waivers for oil sales, and final-status arrangements would be negotiated later. That approach left the sovereignty question deliberately ambiguous. Wednesday's statements from Trump — delivered publicly, at a Cabinet meeting — appear to harden the American posture in advance of those final-status talks, foreclosing the constructive ambiguity that such negotiations typically require.

What needs to happen next

The gap between the two positions is real but not inherently terminal. Iran has signaled, through its in-principle agreement on HEU disposal and its engagement with a Hormuz reopening, that it is prepared to trade leverage. The question is whether the terms on offer add up to enough for Iranian negotiators to defend domestically — particularly with a weakened supreme leadership structure following Khamenei's death, and a population that absorbed three months of strikes.

The specific mechanism to watch is whether special envoy Steve Witkoff can translate Trump's publicly stated red lines into language that Tehran can present as something other than unconditional surrender. An HEU destruction framework using the Atomic Energy Commission as witness — as Trump suggested — would also require International Atomic Energy Agency inspectors to return to Iranian facilities; Iran terminated all IAEA access on February 28, and restoring that access is itself a negotiation within the negotiation.

On Hormuz, any mine-clearing operation that allows even partial commercial traffic to resume would be the most concrete confidence measure available to oil markets, which have priced in ceasefire probability but remain exposed to a rapid reversal if talks collapse. The next substantive session between negotiating teams — not a public Cabinet statement — is the data point that matters.
