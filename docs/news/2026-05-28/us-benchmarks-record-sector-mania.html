Space-sector mania and AI rotation drive US benchmarks to simultaneous record highs

The NYSE trading floor, where options-driven momentum across space and semiconductor sectors pushed US equity benchmarks to simultaneous record highs — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
SpaceX's June 12 IPO is bleeding space mania into semiconductor options, amplifying a Micron gamma squeeze and pulling the S&P 500 toward Wall Street's 8,000 target.

On Wednesday, US equity benchmarks settled at record levels in a session that had the texture of a coordinated multi-sector squeeze more than an organic repricing. The S&P 500 closed at 7,520, up fractionally but extended from its prior record. The Russell 2000 had already printed a fresh all-time high of 2,886 earlier this month; the Nasdaq-100 was carried along. The thread connecting them: a pair of correlated manias — space and semiconductors — that have made it difficult to separate genuine fundamental demand from momentum contagion.

The SpaceX Catalyst

The proximate trigger for the space sector's ascent is the scheduled June 12 debut of SpaceX on Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX. The company filed a prospectus pricing its valuation at $1.75 to $2 trillion, describing itself as a "vertically integrated space, connectivity, and AI conglomerate" — language engineered to capture multiples from several sector buckets simultaneously. The market has answered in kind.

AST SpaceMobile has gained 78% year-to-date, trading near $129. Rocket Lab has done better, up roughly 95% from January to hit an all-time high of $146 this week; Q1 2026 revenue came in at $200 million, up 64% year-over-year, with a record $2.2 billion backlog. Planet Labs and Intuitive Machines moved 15% and 16% respectively in a single Monday session as SpaceX IPO coverage intensified.

The notable mechanic is how space enthusiasm has crossed sector lines. Options traders appear to be using semiconductor calls — principally on Micron — as a leveraged proxy for space-themed AI infrastructure plays. The logic: space requires compute, compute requires memory, Micron makes memory. That inferential chain, stretched as it is, has been enough to sustain elevated options volume in semis during peak space-stock sessions, breaking the historical assumption that the two sectors trade on unrelated fundamentals.

Micron and the Mechanics of a Squeeze

Micron Technology has become the semiconductor most visibly caught in this feedback loop, though it also carries a genuine fundamental story. The company reported 62% year-over-year revenue growth in its most recent quarter, with data-center revenue up 150%. High-bandwidth memory — the stacked DRAM chips embedded directly into AI accelerators — is sold out through most of 2026, and Micron is one of only three global suppliers alongside Samsung and SK Hynix.

Wall Street has been catching up emphatically. UBS raised its price target to $1,625 — three times its prior target of $535 — implying a market cap above $1.8 trillion. Barclays followed on May 27 with $1,175. Melius Research published $1,100. Shares have moved from around $576 in early May to $917 at Wednesday's close, a gain of nearly 60% in less than a month. The company has crossed $1 trillion in market capitalisation.

The options mechanics are the more revealing tell. Micron experienced what traders describe as a gamma squeeze — aggressive call buying pushed market makers to continuously delta-hedge by purchasing shares as prices breached successive strikes. One session produced a near-19% single-day move. The upgrades that followed are not manufactured, but they complicate interpretation: when analyst revisions draw in call buyers who then create their own squeeze, price action validates the original thesis in ways that are structurally circular.

Small Caps Confirm the Broadening

One of the more striking features of May's rally is that it has not been confined to mega-cap technology. The Russell 2000 hit an all-time high of 2,886 on May 6 and has held the level, delivering a 16.3% gain year-to-date — more than double the S&P 500's 7.6% and ahead of the Nasdaq's 11.2%. Within the index, semiconductor names have contributed a 133% year-to-date return on a 5.6% index weight. Applied Optoelectronics is up 417%. Bloom Energy has gained 234%. DigitalOcean, up 224%, has been repriced as a small-cap AI infrastructure play.

The prevailing description is that "the AI trade has moved downmarket" — that capital once concentrated in Nvidia and Microsoft is trickling into data-center suppliers, power-systems firms, and optical networking companies that populate the Russell 2000. That reading is partly accurate. It also flatters the move: small-cap AI names carry higher volatility, thinner institutional coverage, and more compressed earnings bases, making them susceptible to the same momentum-amplifying options dynamics visible in Micron. The breadth is real; the durability is unproven.

The 8,000 Question

The larger backdrop is what is happening to the S&P 500's own positioning as major banks converge on 8,000 as a year-end destination. Goldman Sachs raised its forecast from 7,600 to 8,000 on May 26, citing 84% earnings-beat rates in the latest reporting season and expectations of 12% full-year EPS growth. Ed Yardeni moved to 8,250. At 7,520, the index sits roughly 6.4% below the target — plausible distance for a seven-month run, which is precisely why options positioning around that strike has become a market-moving consideration.

Round-number milestones function as gravitational centres in options markets. Call open interest clustered at the 8,000 strike creates hedging constraints for dealers: as the index drifts higher, delta-hedging requirements mechanically pull the underlying with it. That reflexivity is a structural feature of large index options markets, not a conspiracy — but it does mean that whether the index reaches 8,000 depends not only on earnings or macro data, but on the geometry of the options book. Bullish analyst targets reinforcing the milestone tend not to advertise that feedback loop.

Against this, the breadth data offers a counterweight. Only 54% of S&P 500 components are in an uptrend despite the index trading at record levels. Narrow leadership driving a headline index higher is a recurring precondition for momentum reversals, particularly when the sectors doing the leading — semiconductors, space, leveraged AI infrastructure — are also the most optioned-up.

The next hard catalyst is June 12, when SpaceX begins trading. If SPCX prices cleanly and opens well, the space-to-semis narrative gets another extension and the 8,000 target shifts from aspiration to near-term trading level. If the debut disappoints — oversubscription in a roadshow does not guarantee post-listing price stability — the correlated unwind in space names, Micron calls, and small-cap AI momentum could be swift. Between now and then, the breadth data will tell more than the headline level.
