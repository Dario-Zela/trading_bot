SLS chief points to leukaemia trial data as path to NCCN guideline recognition

A blood smear showing myeloblasts characteristic of acute myeloid leukaemia; GPS targets the WT1 antigen expressed on such cells. — Wikimedia Commons / VashtiVivant
In one sentence
SELLAS Life Sciences CEO says imminent REGAL trial readout could put GPS immunotherapy on a path to NCCN guideline inclusion in AML — but no data has been disclosed.

Shares of SELLAS Life Sciences (Nasdaq: SLS) extended an overnight gain on Wednesday after CEO Angelos Stergiou told investors he was "very bullish" about forthcoming Phase 3 trial data for the company's lead cancer immunotherapy, and said strong results could position the drug for inclusion in National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) treatment guidelines — a milestone with direct implications for how widely oncologists can prescribe and insurers will pay for it.

The stock rose roughly 6% on the day, part of a year-long run that has now carried SLS up more than 345%. No trial results have been publicly disclosed. The REGAL Phase 3 study, which tests Galinpepimut-S (GPS) as maintenance therapy for acute myeloid leukaemia (AML) patients in second complete remission, remains blinded. What has drawn investors is the pace at which the trial is accumulating the clinical events required to trigger its final analysis.

Seventy-eight of eighty

REGAL is event-driven: final analysis begins once 80 patient deaths have been recorded across the two trial arms. As of 11 May, 78 events had been logged. Stergiou's argument — and it is an inferential one — is that longer-than-expected survival in the GPS arm is slowing event accrual. "80 events have not occurred yet," he told listeners, "which obviously indicates longer survival" than the trial's original design assumed. The company had projected a median overall survival of 12.6 months for GPS-treated patients against 8 months under best available therapy.

GPS targets the Wilms Tumor-1 (WT1) antigen, activating T cells to attack leukaemic stem cells. The appeal of the mechanism is that it is mutation-agnostic — relevant in AML, where targeted therapies face the constant threat of resistance through tumour evolution. Phase 2 data in first-remission patients showed a median overall survival of approximately 68 months, a figure Stergiou has described as "quite unprecedented" for the indication. Elderly patients — median age 74 — showed 21 months in earlier studies, against 5.4 months without GPS.

Commentary as catalyst

What Stergiou specifically flagged for investors was the possibility that strong REGAL results could win GPS guideline recognition not only in the AML second-remission setting the Phase 3 trial directly tests, but in the adjacent first-remission (CR1) context as well. NCCN inclusion in that broader population would substantially widen the commercial case for a drug currently on the cusp of its first regulatory test.

The cautionary note writes itself. Wednesday's move was driven entirely by forward-looking executive commentary, with no trial data, no peer-reviewed manuscript, and no regulatory filing in view. Studies of NCCN panel behaviour suggest a median of roughly 15 months between a regimen's initial data presentation and guideline inclusion — and that clock does not start until topline data are actually in hand. Biotech investors know this pattern well: a CEO at a conference or investor forum provides just enough specificity to move a stock without furnishing the evidence that would allow independent scrutiny.

The next verifiable catalyst is the 80th event itself, which Stergiou has described as imminent, followed by topline data release. That readout will either validate a year's worth of pre-data optimism or compress those gains sharply. No formal timeline has been committed to by the company.
