South Korean equities outpace the Nasdaq 100 by the widest margin in 25 years

In one sentence
South Korean equities have beaten the Nasdaq 100 by 85 percentage points in 2026, the widest gap since 2001, driven by an AI memory supercycle and surging defense exports.

South Korean equities have pulled so far ahead of the Nasdaq 100 this year that the gap now measures 85 percentage points — a divergence not seen since the dot-com bubble was still inflating in 2001. As of May 27, the iShares MSCI South Korea ETF (EWY) had returned 104% year-to-date while the Invesco QQQ Trust, the standard Nasdaq 100 proxy, had gained 18.75%. The KOSPI index itself closed at a record 8,228 on May 27, briefly touching an intraday high of 8,450 that triggered a circuit-breaker halting algorithmic trading.

The scale invites skepticism — a 100% gain in five months sounds like bubble arithmetic — yet the numbers behind it are harder to dismiss. At the center of the move are two memory chip makers whose position in the global AI supply chain has proved more consequential than almost any investor model assumed at the start of the year.

The memory supercycle

SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics together account for 52.9% of EWY's portfolio and have contributed the overwhelming share of its returns — roughly 7,264 of the ETF's 10,405 basis points of year-to-date gain. SK Hynix is up 245% this year; Samsung has gained 157%. In May, both crossed the $1 trillion market capitalization threshold, making South Korea the first country outside the United States to simultaneously host two publicly traded companies at that valuation.

The engine is high-bandwidth memory. HBM chips sit alongside Nvidia's H100 and H200 accelerators in AI data centers, and demand has outrun supply by a widening margin. SK Hynix, which pioneered HBM and locked in Nvidia as a primary customer, has sold its entire 2026 production capacity with shortages now projected into 2027. Samsung is expanding its own HBM output by approximately 50% this year. Nomura Securities, which raised its KOSPI year-end target to 11,000 in late May, described memory as being in a "supercycle" and forecast that demand would expand by orders of magnitude over the next five years while supply grows at a fraction of that pace.

Goldman Sachs went further. The bank's chief Asia Pacific equity strategist Timothy Moe declared Korea its "highest-conviction" regional call and forecast 300% earnings growth for KOSPI constituents in 2026 — "the strongest for any market in Asia ever, with the exception of the 1999 recovery from the Asian Financial Crisis." Goldman subsequently raised its 12-month KOSPI target to 9,000.

Defense and the secondary rotation

The semiconductor story is well-known by now, but a parallel rally in Korean defense equities has added a geopolitical dimension to the outperformance that matters for how long the trade holds together.

South Korean defense stocks surged sharply earlier in the year as tensions in the Middle East escalated, with Hanwha Aerospace and LIG Nex1 each posting single-session gains above 20% on the sharpest news days. Hanwha Group's combined defense subsidiaries climbed into Korea's top five companies by market capitalization. Korea's Big Four defense firms — Hanwha Aerospace, Hyundai Rotem, KAI, and LIG Nex1 — are projected to reach combined revenue of 50.5 trillion won (roughly $37 billion) in 2026, up from 40 trillion won last year. Defense exports hit $15.4 billion in 2025 and are tracking higher; South Korea has become the second-largest arms supplier to NATO member states in Europe, trailing only the United States.

The defense rally reflects a structural shift rather than a single incident. European rearmament is a multi-year procurement cycle, and Korean hardware — from K9 self-propelled howitzers to Chunmoo rocket artillery systems — has proven competitive on price, delivery speed, and battlefield track record. For global investors, it provides diversification within the Korea trade: the thesis is not solely predicated on AI capex cycles.

Broader rotation has since spread to batteries, robotics, and automobiles as the semiconductor leaders have paused. The Korea Exchange's record session on May 27 saw 826 stocks decline against only 77 gainers, a configuration that underscores how top-heavy the rally remains — but also signals that rotation, when it occurs, is already looking for the next leg.

Tactical noise or regime shift?

The structural question that most matters to systematic and global macro traders is whether the spread represents a durable regime change in relative equity performance or a supercycle trade that will revert once AI capex moderates.

The concentration risk is real. Samsung and SK Hynix together now account for more than half of the entire KOSPI by market cap — a distortion that mechanically forces Korea-dedicated passive funds to trim when weightings breach internal limits. Foreigners have net-sold 40.05 trillion won from the KOSPI in May alone, a record pace of outflow. What has prevented the index from breaking is a domestic counterflow: retail investors, high-net-worth buyers rotating from property, and pension funds channeled through ETFs have absorbed the supply in near-equal measure. Foreign ownership of the KOSPI hit a six-year high earlier in 2026 before the profit-taking wave began.

The test for regime-shift bulls is whether sector diversification — defense, shipbuilding, power infrastructure, autos — picks up enough of the index weighting to reduce single-stock dependence before institutional selling intensifies. Goldman's structural thesis depends on that broadening happening. Nomura's 11,000 target implies roughly 34% additional upside from current levels; the more cautious framing from US-based analysts is that the memory-driven phase of the rally is mature.

The next concrete data points: SK Hynix finalizes its 2026 HBM order book by end of June, which will either confirm the demand picture or introduce the first credible supply-demand recalibration. Samsung's mid-year capacity guidance, expected in its second-quarter earnings call, will follow shortly after. Those two disclosures will do more to determine whether the Korea-US spread widens further or begins a controlled mean reversion than any amount of macro positioning.
