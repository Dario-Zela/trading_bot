Ciena's optical networking valuation rises as hyperscaler AI build-outs drive demand

Fiber optic patch connections in a data center networking cabinet — the physical backbone of the high-capacity optical interconnects driving demand for Ciena's equipment. — Robert.Harker / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Ciena's stock has surged 600% on AI infrastructure demand, but analysts' bullish targets assume hyperscaler capex stays elevated — a bet that is far from certain.

Optical networking has spent years waiting for a moment like this. Ciena, the Maryland-based maker of high-speed optical interconnect systems, has seen its stock climb roughly 600% in twelve months as Wall Street concluded that building AI infrastructure runs directly through its product line — and a string of analyst target raises is now making that thesis explicit.

The trigger for the latest round was Ciena's fiscal first-quarter 2026 results, reported in March. Revenue hit $1.43 billion — a company record, up 33% year-on-year — with its core optical networking segment growing 41% to $1.02 billion. Order momentum pushed the company's backlog up by $2 billion in a single quarter to roughly $7 billion. Management raised full-year fiscal 2026 revenue guidance to $5.9 billion to $6.3 billion, implying growth of around 28% at the midpoint. Bank of America, which had already upgraded Ciena to Buy in April and lifted its price target from $355 to $550, subsequently raised that target again to $660, calling it "the heart of an optical super-cycle" it expects to extend through at least 2027.

The demand logic is not complicated. The four largest hyperscalers — Amazon, Microsoft, Alphabet and Meta — have collectively budgeted close to $700 billion in capital expenditure for 2026, up roughly 65% year-on-year. AI training clusters are growing so large they now span multiple buildings and campuses; linking them requires coherent optical technology of precisely the kind Ciena builds. Its WaveLogic 6 Extreme modem, capable of transmitting 1.6 terabits per second on a single wavelength, secured 49 customers within two quarters of launch — unusually fast adoption for infrastructure hardware.

Where the risk lives

The bull case is also, almost entirely, the bear case. Ciena's valuation expansion rests on a single assumption: that hyperscaler AI capex continues to grow, or at least holds. Strip that out and the numbers change quickly. At trading levels near $585, the stock has expanded well past most conventional valuation anchors. Simply Wall Street's DCF model puts fair value at around $96; its more generous narrative-based model arrives at roughly $341 — still a steep discount to where the stock trades today.

The capex figures themselves carry internal tension. Amazon's free cash flow is projected to turn negative this year from infrastructure spending. Analysts at Barclays project Meta's free cash flow could fall by nearly 90%. That is sustainable only if AI revenue scales alongside it — and the evidence is patchy. Fewer than 40% of companies have scaled AI workloads beyond pilot programmes, according to widely cited surveys. That gap between infrastructure commitment and demonstrated returns is precisely the kind of thing that causes capex guidance to be revised.

If even one major hyperscaler pulls back spending guidance in the next two earnings cycles, the effect on Ciena's backlog conversion rate — and through it, on the valuation multiples that assume the cycle extends indefinitely — would be immediate. The next concrete test arrives in late June, when Ciena reports its fiscal second quarter. Investors will be looking at whether the $7 billion backlog is converting at pace, or whether lead times are stretching in ways that suggest demand is softer than the headline order figures imply. The super-cycle thesis and the extrapolation-from-a-single-boom-year thesis look identical right now; that distinction should be considerably clearer then.
