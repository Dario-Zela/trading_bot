American Airlines confirms Starlink deal, raising the stakes for rivals on legacy systems

An American Airlines Airbus A321neo — the model class at the center of the carrier's Starlink rollout, which begins in early 2027. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
American Airlines is deploying Starlink on 500+ Airbus jets from Q1 2027, lifting shares 7% and widening the connectivity gap with Delta, which is stuck on Viasat until 2028.

American Airlines announced on Tuesday that it will install SpaceX's Starlink satellite internet across more than 500 of its narrowbody Airbus aircraft, beginning in the first quarter of 2027. The market took notice: AAL shares jumped nearly 7% on the day, putting the stock on track for a four-month high.

The deal covers American's Airbus fleet — A321neo and A321XLR variants — but conspicuously leaves its Boeing aircraft untouched for now. Those planes will continue running a patchwork of Viasat and Panasonic hardware. Starlink's Aero Terminal is rated at up to 1 gigabit per second per antenna, a meaningful step up from current satellite systems that typically deliver a fraction of that speed at significantly higher latency.

"The addition of Starlink solidifies American as a leading airline in keeping passengers connected in flight," said Heather Garboden, the carrier's chief customer officer. SpaceX's Jason Fritch, vice president of Starlink enterprise sales, called the partnership a path to "a fully connected experience gate to gate."

The legacy gap

The competitive picture around American's announcement is arguably more significant than the deal itself. United Airlines has been rolling out Starlink since 2024 and expects to have 800 aircraft equipped by the end of 2026 — a lead American will spend years closing. Southwest and Alaska Airlines are also in the Starlink camp.

Delta Air Lines is the conspicuous holdout. After talks with SpaceX reportedly broke down over a branding dispute — Delta wanted to maintain its proprietary Delta Sync portal rather than surface the Starlink name — the carrier signed with Amazon's Project Kuiper instead. That bet carries a material timing problem: Amazon's Kuiper constellation has fewer than 250 satellites in orbit against the roughly 10,000 in Starlink's network, and Delta's installations are not expected to begin until 2028. Until then, Delta passengers fly on Viasat hardware rated at 12 to 150 megabits per second and latency between 500 and 700 milliseconds — a different product class than what United and, eventually, American will offer.

For investors, that gap translates into a valuation narrative. Airlines capable of marketing a premium, reliable connectivity product have an asset that is difficult to replicate quickly. Delta's two-year window of competitive disadvantage is a known risk, and the AA announcement extends the timeline over which that risk compounds.

The next data point to watch is American's Boeing fleet. The carrier has not indicated when — or whether — it intends to extend Starlink to those planes, leaving the rollout picture incomplete. With installations not starting until early 2027, the first real-world passenger experience numbers are still roughly a year away.
