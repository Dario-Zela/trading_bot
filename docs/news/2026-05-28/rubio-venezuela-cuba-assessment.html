Rubio offers cautious optimism on Venezuela while issuing blunt warning to Cuba

Secretary of State Marco Rubio at a White House cabinet meeting where he assessed Venezuela's transition as proceeding and called Cuba a national security concern. — US Department of State / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Rubio called Venezuela's post-Maduro transition 'on track' and Cuba's communist leadership a national security threat, signalling deliberate US hemispheric recalibration.

At a White House cabinet meeting on May 27, Secretary of State Marco Rubio delivered two sharply contrasting assessments of US hemispheric policy: Venezuela's post-Maduro transition was proceeding on course, while Cuba was sinking deeper into crisis of its own making. The dual framing was deliberate — and carries distinct implications for energy markets and geopolitical risk pricing across the region.

Rubio described Venezuela's "stabilization, recovery, and transition" as on track, citing measurable progress. More than 10 million barrels of oil have been delivered to the United States since January 3 — the day US special operations forces captured former President Nicolás Maduro in a two-hour operation. Venezuelan petroleum is now sold at market rates, with revenues deposited in a US Treasury-controlled account and independently audited by KPMG. The money, Rubio told cabinet members, is not being stolen. Acting President Delcy Rodríguez, sworn in within days of Maduro's arrest, signed a sweeping hydrocarbons reform law in late January that opened Venezuela's oil sector to private investment for the first time in a generation. Washington subsequently lifted personal sanctions on Rodríguez in April, a significant diplomatic signal.

On Cuba, the tone was blunt. "Cuba is in a lot of trouble because, unfortunately for them, it's run by a bunch of incompetent communists," Rubio said, with President Trump seated alongside him. He pointed to GAESA — the Cuban Revolutionary Armed Forces' sprawling business conglomerate — as controlling roughly 70 percent of the island's economy while funnelling little of that to ordinary Cubans. A failing state ninety miles from US shores, he argued, is not merely a humanitarian concern but a direct national security threat.

A model and an implicit demand

The contrast is not rhetorical coincidence. Venezuela, post-Maduro, has met Washington's terms: an internationally audited revenue framework, oil-sector privatisation, cooperation on the extradition of former allies such as Alex Saab. Cuba has offered nothing comparable. By publicly differentiating the two countries, the administration is laying out a template — and signalling, without spelling it out, what accommodation would require.

For energy traders, Rubio's Venezuela read matters directly. Venezuelan crude exports have rebounded substantially under the post-January framework, contributing to the supply increases that have helped push oil prices lower in recent weeks. A continued stabilisation signal from Washington compresses the risk premium attached to a return of sanctions-era supply disruption. Any deterioration in that assessment — a breakdown in the Treasury audit mechanism, a political reversal in Caracas — would reprice that risk quickly.

Cuba's framing is less immediate in commodity terms but significant for regional capital flows. GAESA's economic stranglehold, combined with Rubio's national-security framing and the US continuing diplomatic talks with Havana, suggests Washington is methodically building a pressure campaign — potential further sanctions, restrictions on GAESA-linked entities, or tightened conditions on remittance flows.

The clearest near-term catalyst: Treasury's expected review of the Venezuelan oil-revenue audit framework in June. Any revision to that arrangement — tighter controls, evidence of leakage, or political friction with the Rodríguez government — would test the cautious optimism Rubio expressed this week and force a reassessment of how durable the post-Maduro settlement actually is.
