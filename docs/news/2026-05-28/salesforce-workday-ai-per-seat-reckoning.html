Salesforce and Workday face a structural reckoning as AI agents erode the per-seat model

Salesforce Tower in San Francisco, headquarters of the company at the centre of the enterprise AI pricing debate — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Salesforce beat Q1 estimates but soft Q2 guidance left investors unconvinced that Agentforce task-pricing can offset the structural erosion of per-seat CRM revenue.

On the evening of May 27, Salesforce reported one of its cleaner quarters in recent memory: $11.13 billion in first-quarter revenue, up 13 percent year-over-year, and adjusted earnings per share of $3.88, against Wall Street's expectations of $11.06 billion and $3.13 respectively. Clear beat, clean numbers. The stock fell anyway.

The after-hours decline — about two percent — was modest in absolute terms. In context it tells a bigger story. Salesforce shares have lost roughly a third of their value in 2026, their worst run on the Dow Jones Industrial Average this year. Investors are not disputing the quarterly arithmetic. They are disputing the business model.

The problem with per seat

For three decades, enterprise software sold like office furniture. Every worker who touched a system needed a licence — one human, one seat, one annual fee. Salesforce built a $30-billion revenue engine on that premise. So did Workday, which now serves 65 percent of the Fortune 500 on the same logic.

That logic is under pressure. When AI agents can handle the tasks that previously required a licensed human — routing support queries, updating CRM records, running payroll workflows — fewer humans need to log in. Fewer logins mean fewer seats. Fewer seats mean lower recurring revenue.

Bank of America analyst Tal Liani framed it plainly in a note reinstating Workday coverage with a Neutral rating and a $140 price target: "AI decouples the value of the solution from the number of seats." Workday's gross revenue retention rate sits at 97 percent — customers are not walking out the door — but the concern is that they will quietly downsize their seat counts as AI takes up the slack. Workday's revenue growth has already slowed from roughly 30 percent annually to the mid-to-low teens; analysts at Jefferies, who downgraded the stock from Buy to Hold in late February and sent shares down more than eight percent in a single session, believe the deceleration has further to run.

Workday has not stood still. The company is shifting toward a hybrid model — pairing traditional seat-based billing with consumption credits it calls Flex Credits — that charges for usage rather than headcount. It is a sensible hedge and, read another way, an acknowledgement that the original model needs reinforcing.

Benioff's pivot, and its limits

Salesforce is further along in its response than most. Chief executive Marc Benioff has spent the past year restructuring Agentforce — the company's AI platform — around a per-task pricing unit called an Agentic Work Unit. Rather than billing for a user licence, Salesforce now charges for resolved tickets, completed workflows, closed service cases. The pitch is that a business deploying AI agents could pay more in aggregate than it did under the seat model, because agents process volume around the clock that humans never handled.

The numbers so far are encouraging in isolation. Agentforce annual recurring revenue has exceeded $800 million, growing at more than 200 percent year-over-year. The platform has closed 29,000 deals, up 50 percent quarter-on-quarter, and Salesforce processed 28.6 trillion AI tokens during the quarter.

The problem is substitution speed. Benioff acknowledged that Salesforce itself cut its customer-support workforce from roughly 9,000 to around 5,000 as Agentforce took over routine cases — a vivid demonstration of what the platform can do, and a self-inflicted preview of the risk it poses to the seat model everywhere else. The question investors were asking after Wednesday's guidance is whether the task-pricing revenue ramp will outpace seat-pricing attrition across the broader customer base. The Q2 outlook — $11.27 to $11.35 billion, slightly below consensus — did not answer that question. It sustained the anxiety.

What the repricing implies

The unease around Salesforce and Workday extends well beyond those two companies. It goes to the valuation framework for the entire enterprise software sector. The per-seat model was the foundation of the SaaS revenue multiple: predictable, scalable, sticky. If it is structurally eroding, multiples need to compress to reflect it. Analysts estimate the correction has already wiped roughly $285 billion in market capitalisation from the sector in 2026 alone.

The next concrete data points will be Workday's upcoming earnings — which will test whether Flex Credits are gaining genuine traction or simply replacing foregone seat revenue at a lower margin — and Salesforce's own Q2 results, due in late August. That report will need to show an Agentforce growth curve steep enough to offset any seat-count decline appearing in the same figures.

The structural question underneath all of this has not changed: when AI agents do the work, who pays for the software, and how much? Every major enterprise software vendor has an answer. None of them has proved one yet.
