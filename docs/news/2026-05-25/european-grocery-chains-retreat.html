Morrisons and Rewe's Penny retreat point to a margin squeeze across European grocery

A Morrisons supermarket at Portsmouth's Victory Retail Park — the chain now looks to sell products through competitors' shelves as debt costs mount — Editor5807 / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 3.0)
In one sentence
Morrisons is turning its food manufacturing arm into a wholesale supplier to rivals as £3.1bn in PE debt forces a fundamental rethink of the mid-tier UK grocery model.

For decades, Morrisons' integrated model — growing, processing, and selling its own food — was its competitive advantage. Now it is being turned into a lifeline. The Bradford-based supermarket chain is in advanced talks to supply own-brand pies, meat, and eggs to rival British grocers through its Myton Food Group manufacturing division. It is an admission, dressed up as opportunity, that Morrisons cannot generate enough margin from its retail operation alone.

The weight of debt

The financial context is inescapable. Clayton, Dubilier & Rice's £10bn leveraged buyout of Morrisons in 2021 left the chain carrying £3.1bn in net debt — a burden that now costs £281m a year in interest alone. For the year to February 2025, Morrisons posted a pre-tax loss of £381m despite £15.8bn in revenue and an EBITDA of £835m. The gap between those two figures illustrates how completely the interest bill consumes operating profit.

CEO Rami Baitiéh, a former head of Carrefour France who took charge in November 2023, has tried to arrest the decline through aggressive price cuts on more than 2,900 product lines, a revamped loyalty scheme, and the closure of 100 convenience stores. Like-for-like sales are growing at 2.8%, and market share has stabilised at around 8.2%. But stabilisation is not recovery — and the Myton wholesale plan signals that management has concluded the retail business alone will not service the debt.

Myton is an unusual asset: 17 production facilities across the UK employing roughly 6,700 people, producing everything from meat and pies to eggs and flowers. It already supplies independent retailers. The new ambition is to put its products on the shelves of Tesco, Sainsbury's, or other major chains — representatives from Sainsbury's have already visited Myton facilities. A Morrisons spokesman described the push as "expanding its customer base across retail, food service and manufacturing sectors both in the UK and overseas," careful language that avoids naming what the manoeuvre actually is: a mid-tier grocer quietly becoming a manufacturer-for-hire to the competition.

Continental echoes

The pressure on Morrisons is not a uniquely British phenomenon. Across the North Sea, Germany's Rewe Group is weighing whether to sell part or all of its Penny Market chain in Italy — nearly 500 stores generating close to €2bn in annual revenue — according to Bloomberg, citing Italy's Corriere della Sera.

Penny has operated in Italy since 1994 and is one of the country's larger discount chains. But the strategic logic for Rewe holding on has weakened. Penny sits awkwardly in a market where Lidl and, to a degree, Aldi have steadily undercut it from below, while consumers with more disposable income have drifted toward premium formats. That squeeze — hard discounters on one side, premiumisation on the other — has compressed industry-wide EBITDA margins across European grocery from an average of 6.9% in 2019 to 6.2% in 2024, according to McKinsey. A full exit from Italy would represent a significant retrenchment for Rewe. Potential buyers are expected to include the same German discounters that made Penny's position untenable in the first place.

The mid-tier trap

Taken together, Morrisons and Penny illustrate a structural problem that no amount of operational discipline fully resolves. European grocery is evolving into a barbell: scale players at the value end — Aldi and Lidl are on course to surpass a combined 20% share of the UK market by late 2026 — and premium or specialist formats at the other. The middle is where margins go to die.

Morrisons, at 8.2% UK market share, sits squarely in that middle. So does Asda, which has fallen to 11.9% — a historic low. Both chains are working through the same arithmetic: high fixed costs, relentless pricing pressure, and balance sheets shaped by the leveraged-buyout era. The PE model demands returns that the underlying retail business has struggled to generate.

Morrisons' Myton pivot could work on its own terms. Spare factory capacity is a genuine asset, and supplying own-label products to rivals is an established model elsewhere in European food manufacturing. But if it remains primarily a patch over a retail business bleeding margin rather than a structural transformation, it will be remembered as an early warning rather than a solution.

The next data points to watch: whether any formal supply agreement emerges from the Myton discussions, and whether Rewe announces a transaction for Penny Italy before the end of the year. Either outcome would confirm that the barbell has snapped — and that Europe's mid-tier grocers are now in the business of managed retreat.
