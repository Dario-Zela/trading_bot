Alaska's North Slope draws fresh investment as Arctic oil revival gathers pace

The Trans-Alaska Pipeline System, which carries North Slope crude some 800 miles south to the port of Valdez — Luca Galuzzi / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 2.5)
In one sentence
Alaska's North Slope is drawing record investment as Pikka starts producing and a $164M federal lease sale marks the broadest industry re-engagement in decades.

For nearly a generation, America's biggest oil field looked like a sunset industry. When John Kurz left the North Slope in 2009, crude flowing through the Trans-Alaska Pipeline had slumped to 567,000 barrels a day — barely a quarter of the 2 million barrels the field had pumped at its 1988 peak. "The industry was dying," he said. "We could see the end of TAPS coming."

Kurz now runs Alyeska Pipeline Service Co., which operates that same infrastructure. The view from his desk is considerably more optimistic. This spring, Santos and Repsol began pushing first commercial oil from the Pikka project — the North Slope's most significant new development in a generation. In March, a federal auction drew ten companies and a record $163.7 million in bids for leases in the National Petroleum Reserve-Alaska. ConocoPhillips, Shell, and ExxonMobil — a company that had not bid on North Slope acreage in years — were all at the table.

From Prudhoe Bay to the Nanushuk

The turnaround traces its roots to a well drilled in 2013, when Colorado wildcatter Bill Armstrong and Spain's Repsol tapped into the Nanushuk formation — a shallow, geologically forgiving layer of rock that proved far richer than anticipated. A series of appraisals from 2015 to 2017 confirmed the formation's extent. The 2016 discovery of the Willow deposit, a reservoir holding an estimated 600 million barrels, reinforced the case for renewed exploration.

Armstrong's companies have since built positions across the formation, partnering with Santos and APA Corp. on the Sockeye prospect — a play some geologists estimate could hold more than 700 million barrels. Walter Hufford, a 68-year-old geologist who heads US government affairs at Repsol, has spent decades studying the North Slope's stratigraphy and describes the Nanushuk as one of the most significant lower-cost reservoir systems in North America.

A pipeline with something to prove

The most tangible marker of the revival arrived in May, when Santos and Repsol declared commercial production at Pikka, a project that consumed more than $3 billion and years of Arctic construction. The first drill site is expected to reach 80,000 barrels per day, with capacity expandable to 120,000 as additional pads are developed. Repsol's chief executive has said the nearby Quokka appraisal — results from which came back positive — will be "something equivalent" to Pikka when it reaches full output.

For TAPS, the timing is material. The pipeline is currently moving roughly 470,000 barrels daily — a fraction of its 2.14 million-barrel design capacity. Low throughput means slower flow, longer transit times, and growing risks of gel formation in cold temperatures. State forecasters estimate that Pikka and ConocoPhillips' Willow project — expected to begin production in early 2029 at roughly 180,000 barrels per day, with the company investing about $1 billion annually in its development — would together lift Alaska's output around 40 percent, to approximately 665,000 barrels daily. That would push TAPS back toward the flow rates that allow stable, cost-effective operation.

The March lease sale reinforced the commercial momentum. The Bureau of Land Management offered more than 600 tracts covering approximately 5.5 million acres in the NPRA, a reserve the USGS estimates holds 8.7 billion barrels of recoverable oil. Repsol and Shell partnered to win several large blocks, submitting more than $90 million in combined bids; ExxonMobil secured 23 tracts. The sale is the first in a planned series of five over the next decade. Mark Oberstoetter of Wood Mackenzie notes that the regulatory environment — the Trump administration lifted Biden-era restrictions on NPRA drilling and has directed agencies to streamline permitting — has removed uncertainty that kept companies cautious in recent years.

A contested boom in warming terrain

The environmental case against the expansion is not trivial. Alaska is warming at roughly four times the global average, and the permafrost underpinning North Slope infrastructure is actively degrading. Research published in Nature found that well pads on permafrost accelerate that degradation persistently, even after remediation. ConocoPhillips has disclosed that Willow alone will emit an estimated 284 million metric tons of CO₂-equivalent over its 30-year lifespan.

Bobby McEnaney, director of land conservation at the Natural Resources Defense Council, has argued that opening the NPRA at the scale now contemplated would lock in decades of emissions from one of the planet's most climate-sensitive ecosystems. The NRDC has challenged several NPRA permitting decisions in federal court.

Advocates for development counter that the North Slope's Indigenous-owned corporations depend on oil revenues for basic services. Andy Mack, chief executive of Kuukpik Corporation — which operates near the Pikka site outside Nuiqsut — acknowledged the tension directly: "The projects don't come without impacts, so we're always focused on that." His corporation supports Santos's operations.

Two near-term indicators will test the durability of the revival. ConocoPhillips must commit to Willow's major construction phase if the 2029 production target is to hold, and the BLM must complete its review of March auction bids before new leases can formally issue. How quickly those leases translate into drill bits — and whether courts uphold the permits — will say more about this boom's staying power than any lease auction ever can.
