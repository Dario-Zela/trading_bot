IRAN DEAL · By M. Halloway · 2026-05-25 · 5 min read

Iran deal sends equities to records while bond market signals yields will stay high

The Strait of Hormuz photographed from 35,000 feet — the 21-mile chokepoint through which roughly a fifth of global oil supply passes, and the central sticking point in US-Iran ceasefire negotiations — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
S&P 500 perpetual futures hit a record $7,610 on Hyperliquid as Trump's Iran ceasefire deal emerged, but bond strategists say structural forces will keep yields elevated regardless.

The announcement arrived on a Saturday. President Trump declared a deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz was "largely negotiated" — his administration's chosen phrase — and promised specifics before the end of the week. By Sunday, risk markets had moved. S&P 500 Index perpetual futures on Hyperliquid climbed to an all-time high of $7,610, then pulled back to $7,540. Crude oil futures moved in the opposite direction.

The simultaneity of those moves makes for a compelling headline. The causation is considerably harder to establish.

An Uneasy Ceasefire Reaches for Permanence

The conflict's arc matters here. Operation Epic Fury — the US-Israel air campaign launched February 28, 2026 — struck roughly 5,000 targets in its first two months, killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in its opening hours, and cost the United States approximately $25 billion before concluding on May 5. A fragile ceasefire took hold on April 8, punctuated by skirmishes over who controls the Strait of Hormuz, the 21-mile-wide chokepoint through which roughly a fifth of global oil supply passes.

The framework announced this weekend would extend that ceasefire by 60 days while the parties negotiate a final agreement. Iran's state media said the deal provides for Tehran to continue managing the waterway — a formulation that immediately drew fire from Senate Republicans.

Senator Roger Wicker of Mississippi said the arrangement invalidated "everything that the US accomplished by Operation Epic Fury." Senator Lindsey Graham of South Carolina argued the deal would leave Iran as "the most dominant power in the region" and urged Trump to "finish the job" through additional military action. Senator Ted Cruz said he was "deeply concerned" about a deal he characterized as handing Iran billions of dollars, operational control of the Strait, and room to develop nuclear capability.

Trump has committed to releasing full terms before week's end. Until those details arrive, markets are trading on the headline, not the substance — and the headline is contested.

Oil Down, Equities Up — and the Limits of That Read

The immediate market logic runs as follows: Hormuz reopening implies lower oil prices, which implies lower inflation, which reduces the probability of Federal Reserve rate hikes, which lifts equity valuations. WTI and Brent crude perpetual futures both declined on the news. The S&P 500 perpetual on Hyperliquid hit $7,610 before softening to $7,540.

That chain of inference is not wrong in its broad direction. It is, however, thin on supporting data. The magnitude of the perpetual futures move is not independently reported, and volume and open-interest figures for the Hyperliquid contract are not publicly available at the time of writing. Whether the record reflects broad institutional repositioning or a thinner, more speculative move in a venue that operates outside regulated exchange infrastructure is impossible to determine from the price alone. An all-time high is a fact; what it signals about the underlying market is a different question.

The Republican opposition adds a second layer of uncertainty. If senators mount serious resistance to the deal's terms — or if this week's promised disclosures reveal provisions that reopen the political fight over Iranian sovereignty over the waterway — the geopolitical premium in energy could reverse quickly. The 60-day clock gives the market a relatively short window before the same negotiating pressures resurface.

Why Bond Markets Are Not Celebrating

The equity market's enthusiasm depends partly on an assumption that geopolitical resolution translates to lower Treasury yields, which in turn supports risk-asset valuations. Bond strategists are skeptical of that transmission mechanism.

The US 10-year yield approached 4.70% earlier this month before retreating to 4.56%. That pullback does not reflect optimism about the Iran deal; according to analysts at ING, Barclays, and Goldman Sachs, the forces driving long rates elevated are structural, not cyclical — and an Iranian ceasefire does not address them.

"Treasury yields don't collapse lower as many currently anticipate," said Padhraic Garvey of ING. Jonathan Hill at Barclays was blunter: the inflation thesis driving rate-cut expectations is "hard to square with market pricing." The data supports that skepticism. The five-year, five-year forward breakeven inflation rate sits around 2.2 percent, and 10-year breakevens are 50 basis points below their 2022 peaks — meaning the bond market's inflation pricing is already contained. The pressure in long rates is coming from real yields, not the price level.

Behind that real-yield elevation sits the fiscal picture. The Trump administration's tax-cut package is widening the deficit and expanding Treasury issuance at a moment when the AI investment boom is competing for long-duration capital. Phillip Lee of Goldman Sachs has said "rates are going higher" due to fiscal deficits and debt-sustainability concerns — a view aligned with JPMorgan's Jamie Dimon, who has warned repeatedly that government borrowing pressures could push rates significantly above consensus expectations. The upward revision to estimates of the economy's neutral rate reinforces the same conclusion: the Fed, under newly appointed Chair Kevin Warsh, is operating in an environment where the market has priced in the possibility of a rate hike before year-end, not cuts.

A deal over a narrow waterway in the Persian Gulf does not alter that arithmetic.

What the Week Ahead Will Resolve

Markets will have multiple opportunities this week to test whether Sunday's risk-on move has durable support. Trump's promised deal disclosures — specifically the language around Iranian management of the Strait, sanctions relief, and nuclear provisions — will determine whether congressional opposition hardens from rhetoric into legislative action.

On the earnings side: AutoZone, Zscaler, and Israeli defense contractor Elbit Systems report Tuesday; Marvell Technology, Salesforce, Snowflake, Costco, and Dell follow later in the week. Marvell and Dell provide a read on AI infrastructure spending, which feeds directly into the neutral-rate debate weighing on bonds. Elbit's results carry obvious relevance to the changed security calculus in the Middle East.

On the macro side: the Conference Board's consumer confidence index releases Tuesday. The more consequential print arrives Thursday — GDP and PCE inflation data. PCE is the Fed's preferred gauge. A soft reading would complicate the case for further hikes and could reprice the yield curve, the one scenario in which bond markets might actually validate what equity futures implied on Sunday morning.

Until then, the S&P 500 perpetual's record high rests on a deal whose terms remain undisclosed, backed by a geopolitical resolution whose durability is actively contested in the Senate, against a bond backdrop that strategists say will not soften regardless of what happens in the Strait.
