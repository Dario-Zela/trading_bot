AI MEGA-CAPS · By M. Halloway · 2026-05-30 · 6 min read

S&P 500 stretches its winning run to nine weeks on AI mega-caps while eight of eleven sectors close May lower

The New York Stock Exchange advanced trading floor — index records in May masked deteriorating breadth across eight of eleven S&P 500 sectors. — Eduard Hueber / Asymptote Architecture (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
S&P 500 hit a record 7,581 on a nine-week win streak — but 8 of 11 sectors closed May lower as Dell's 757% AI-server surge and Micron's best month since 1985 did nearly all the work.

The S&P 500 closed May at a record 7,581, capping nine consecutive weekly gains — a streak achieved fewer than ten times since the Second World War. The Nasdaq rose 8% for the month, semiconductor ETFs logged their strongest two-month run in history, and the financial press reached for superlatives. The narrative looks like a broad bull market.

Look one layer down and the picture is more complicated. Eight of the eleven S&P 500 sectors finished May in the red. Health care, energy, financials, materials, industrials, utilities, real estate, and consumer staples all lost ground. The record closes were, in effect, the work of a handful of companies — most of them touching artificial intelligence — that pulled the index upward while most of the market moved the other way. For anyone who manages risk by index exposure rather than individual stock selection, that gap matters.

Dell and Micron Drive the Tape

The two most consequential contributors to May's gains were textbook cases of AI-driven earnings momentum.

Dell Technologies reported fiscal first-quarter revenue of $43.8 billion Thursday — up 88% year over year — and non-GAAP EPS of $4.86 against a Wall Street consensus of $2.96. The number that moved the stock: $16.1 billion in AI server revenue, a 757% increase from the year-earlier quarter. Dell's shares rose 32.76% on Friday, the company's best single trading day since returning to public markets in 2018, adding roughly $62 billion in market capitalisation. The company raised its full-year AI server revenue forecast to $60 billion.

Micron Technology's month was arguably more striking over a longer horizon. The memory-chip maker gained 84% in May — its best single-month performance since November 1985 — crossing $1 trillion in market capitalisation for the first time on May 26. The catalyst was a global shortage of high-bandwidth memory used in AI training workloads, accelerated by a UBS price-target upgrade from $535 to $1,625, a record jump on Wall Street. Qualcomm nearly doubled over the two-month April–May period, its strongest consecutive-month rally since December 1999.

The iShares Semiconductor ETF gained 23% in May on top of April's 40% advance — the strongest two-month run in the fund's two-decade history. Snowflake added 36.5% in a single session after announcing a five-year, $6 billion commitment to AWS Graviton chips. By the end of the month, 14 public companies exceeded $1 trillion in market capitalisation.

Eight Sectors, All Lower

Against that list of winners, consider what did not participate.

Citi's director of U.S. equity strategy, Drew Pettit, estimated that more than half of the S&P 500's first-quarter earnings growth was directly attributable to AI infrastructure spending. That growth came in at 27% year over year — nearly double the 14% consensus forecast. Large-cap next-12-month EPS estimates have risen 14% in 2026, driven almost entirely by technology and AI-linked companies.

Smaller companies have no equivalent engine. The iShares Russell 2000 ETF slipped Friday while mega-cap growth vehicles closed higher. Elevated borrowing costs, compressed margins, and weak pricing power have kept small caps from mounting the kind of sustained recovery that analysts periodically forecast for them. Q1 GDP was revised down to 1.6% from an initial 2.0% reading. Core PCE inflation held at a 3.3% annual rate in April — below the 0.3% monthly forecast, but still too high for the Fed to give smaller borrowers meaningful relief.

The bifurcation shows up in the ETF flow: SPY has gained roughly 20% since late March, QQQ around 30% over the same window. But those are not evenly distributed gains. The concentration is narrow even within the mega-cap universe — on weeks when Dell and Micron can by themselves sustain an index advance, the benchmark is tracking a story that most of its constituents are not telling. Narrow leadership during index records is a classic warning sign in breadth analysis. The rally lacks depth, and any reversal in mega-cap AI enthusiasm would find few offsetting positions in the sectors currently in the red.

Anthropic Approaches the Trillion-Dollar Mark

The week's most significant private-market development arrived alongside the earnings parade. Anthropic — the AI safety company backed by Amazon and Google — closed a $65 billion funding round at a valuation of approximately $965 billion, overtaking OpenAI as the most valuable AI startup and pulling within reach of a threshold previously reserved for the largest public companies on earth.

The Series H was led by Altimeter Capital, Dragoneer, Greenoaks, and Sequoia Capital, with each lead investor committing more than $2 billion. A $15 billion portion of the round comprised previously committed investments from cloud hyperscalers, including $5 billion from Amazon. The company's annualised run-rate revenue has crossed $47 billion; the Wall Street Journal has reported it expects a 130% revenue surge that would bring it to its first operating profit. Google's earlier $10 billion investment, made when Anthropic was valued at $350 billion, is now worth more than $25 billion on paper.

The round is described as Anthropic's likely final private fundraise before an IPO — a process that, if it proceeds, would add a fifteenth name to the trillion-dollar public company list.

Oil, Orbit, and What to Watch

Two threads alongside the central story are worth noting.

A 60-day US-Iran ceasefire extension — pending formal approval — triggered a sharp repricing in energy markets. Brent crude has fallen roughly 19% from its end-April level to around $91.68 per barrel, a move detailed in this edition's oil and Hormuz piece. For energy and materials, that decline accounts for a meaningful share of May's sector-level losses.

In a separate development, Blue Origin's New Glenn rocket exploded on its launch pad, dragging down shares of AST SpaceMobile, Rocket Lab, Intuitive Machines, and Voyager Technologies. The incident reinforced SpaceX's position as the only launch provider with a reliable heavy-lift track record and highlighted how thin the capital base remains for publicly traded launch companies outside that incumbent.

The number that will test the AI-concentration thesis is Dell's $60 billion full-year AI server forecast — the first quarterly validation comes when fiscal Q2 results land this autumn. Micron's trillion-dollar valuation rests on a memory shortage that could shift if capacity additions outpace demand growth; the next Micron analyst day will be worth watching for any signal on supply-side additions. And the breadth question will not resolve on its own. Until smaller-cap earnings show they can grow without AI infrastructure as a direct driver, the nine-week S&P 500 rally remains a record built on a narrow ledger.
