Oil slides to a six-week low as US-Iran ceasefire extension raises Hormuz reopening hopes

The Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly 20% of global oil flows daily. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Oil shed ~17% in May to a six-week low as the US and Iran neared a 60-day ceasefire extension, raising—but not yet confirming—Strait of Hormuz reopening.

The most consequential variable in the oil market in 2026 has always been geography: whether roughly 20% of the world's daily crude supply could pass through the narrow passage between Iran and Oman. On Friday, traders finally put a number on the probability that it could.

Brent crude fell around 2% to approximately $91.20 a barrel — a six-week low — while West Texas Intermediate settled near $87.20, as reports circulated that the United States and Iran had reached a preliminary agreement to extend the current ceasefire by 60 days. May's damage totals nearly 17%, the sharpest monthly decline in Brent since the pandemic cratered demand in 2020. Against Brent's highs earlier in the year, the contract is down roughly 19% — a wipeout of the entire geopolitical risk premium that had accumulated when the conflict first threatened to shut the strait.

The sell-off reflects a swift repricing that the market is executing with more confidence than the underlying facts may yet justify.

60 Days to Reopen the World's Most Critical Chokepoint

The terms of the tentative agreement center on a memorandum of understanding extending the ceasefire for two months while negotiations on a broader settlement continue. Parties are described as having "mostly agreed" on the text. But President Trump has not yet signed off, and Iranian state media said no deal has been finalized. The gap between "mostly agreed" and "agreed" is precisely where the current market pricing lives.

If the agreement holds and the Strait eventually reopens, the supply implications would be significant. Tanker traffic through the waterway essentially collapsed after the conflict began — among the most severe disruptions to the route in modern memory. Restoring normal flows would mean unwinding months of rerouting, insurance surcharges, and supply-chain adjustments that the market has been absorbing at considerable cost. Energy traders and refinery margins both have substantial positions levered to whether that normalization happens in weeks or in quarters.

The magnitude of May's sell-off suggests the market had been pricing in an extended closure and is now, perhaps prematurely, pricing in resolution. Long positions in crude built on geopolitical risk have been systematically liquidated; the question is how much unwind remains and whether fresh selling materializes if the deal stalls.

Bessent Counts the Cost — and the Leverage

Speaking Friday at the Reagan National Economic Forum in Simi Valley, California, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent offered the most detailed public accounting yet of the economic pressure Washington has been applying to Tehran. The picture he painted was stark.

Iran's inflation rate is "probably over 200%," Bessent told Fox Business Network's Larry Kudlow. Between 40% and 50% of Iran's military forces are no longer receiving pay. Kharg Island — through which the bulk of Iranian crude exports historically flowed — has been shut down. Bessent framed the moment as Iran approaching "the end of their tether," with the economy and currency in what he described as free fall.

He also disclosed that the United States has seized approximately $1 billion in Iranian cryptocurrency holdings as part of what the administration calls "Operation Economic Fury." "We just outright grabbed the wallets," Bessent said, noting that some account holders may not yet realize their assets are gone. The seizure targets Iran's attempts to use digital assets as a workaround for dollar-denominated sanctions — an alternative financial channel that has grown in significance as traditional banking access has dried up.

The aggregate case Bessent is making is that Iran's negotiating position deteriorates with every week of delay. Whether Tehran's leadership reads the same balance sheet — and whether economic distress produces flexibility or intransigence — will shape everything that follows.

What Comes Off, and How Slowly

The most market-relevant remark Bessent made may also have been the most hedged. When asked about the prospect of sanctions relief and easing the naval blockade, he was deliberate: anything taken off will be taken off slowly. The administration is conditioning relief on Hormuz reopening, Iran's agreement to surrender highly enriched uranium, and acceptance of a permanent cap on its nuclear program.

That sequencing matters enormously for how quickly supply could actually return. Even if a 60-day MOU is signed, the timeline from agreement to normalized tanker traffic involves inspections, insurance market recalibration, and the gradual restoration of shipping confidence in a route that has been effectively closed. None of that happens overnight.

Bessent described three possible paths forward: a negotiated deal, a continuing blockade combined with escalating economic pressure, or a return to military action. The existence of options two and three explains why market enthusiasm is being held in check by hedgers who see a deal as plausible rather than certain.

Trump has not yet made his "final determination," administration officials confirmed Thursday. That decision — whenever it comes — is the next discrete catalyst for crude prices, energy equities, and downstream refining margins. For now, the market is trading the probability of yes, not the fact of it.
