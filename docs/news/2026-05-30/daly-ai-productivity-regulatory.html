Fed's Daly: regulatory friction, not technology, is the main brake on AI's productivity dividend

Mary C. Daly, President and CEO of the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, at a public appearance in October 2024. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
San Francisco Fed President Daly told the Reagan Forum that regulatory barriers — not the technology itself — are the main brake on AI's productivity dividend.

Mary Daly, president of the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, told a gathering of policymakers and business leaders on Thursday that the primary obstacle to extracting sustained productivity gains from artificial intelligence is neither the technology itself nor a wave of displaced workers — it is regulation.

Speaking at the 2026 Reagan National Economic Forum at the Ronald Reagan Presidential Library in Simi Valley, California, Daly appeared on a panel titled "New Ideas for Invigorating American Growth," moderated by Axios' Neil Irwin, alongside Business Roundtable president Kristen Silverberg. Her diagnosis was pointed: barriers to realizing AI's long-promised economic dividend are "largely regulatory."

The framing is a notable pivot from the more standard complaint that AI's productivity impact hasn't materialized in the aggregate data — a gap that Daly herself has documented. In a February address at San Jose State University, she noted that "most macro-studies of productivity growth find limited evidence of a significant AI effect" and that "even firms that say it's useful find little evidence of transformative gains." A survey of 5,000 executives at that time found "little impact of AI on either employment or productivity over the past three years."

But the February speech contained the seeds of Thursday's message. Daly drew an analogy to electrification — a technology that took decades to reshape measured productivity, and whose gains depended on firms that reimagined their processes from scratch rather than bolting electrical motors onto steam-era workflows. The machinery, in her telling, is now ready. What's missing is the operating environment.

By May, that implicit argument had become explicit: regulatory friction is what separates AI's potential from its performance.

Green shoots, no mass displacement

Daly's broader read of the economy skewed cautiously optimistic. She pointed to "green shoots" for near-term productivity gains from AI — early efficiency improvements beginning to filter through sectors — while firmly rejecting the fear of structural labor-market disruption. Mass unemployment or large-scale displacement from AI is not, she said, what she sees.

That assessment aligns with a broader Fed tendency to frame the current technology cycle as analogous to the late-1990s computing boom rather than to automation-era displacement events. Daly has invoked Alan Greenspan's willingness in the 1990s to look past lagging official data and hold rates steady while higher productivity from technological advance built up beneath the surface.

The significance of Daly's regulatory framing is in where it points. A Fed official attributing the productivity gap to regulatory failure — rather than to immature technology or workforce friction — implicitly frames deregulation as a growth lever. That argument has traditionally been Treasury's territory, and it lands at a moment when Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, also on stage at Simi Valley on Thursday, was making his own case that American industrial policy has consistently chosen efficiency over resilience at structural cost.

Fed policy currently sits at 3.50%–3.75% on the federal funds rate. The channel to watch is whether AI-productivity optimism begins to move the Fed's longer-run neutral-rate estimates upward — the path through which Daly's regulatory thesis would most directly affect both rate expectations and technology-sector valuations. The next scheduled FOMC meeting is in mid-June.
