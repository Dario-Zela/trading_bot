BP boardroom battle ran deeper than the public ouster suggested

BP's global headquarters at 1 St James's Square, London, where the board voted unanimously on May 26 to remove its chairman. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
BP's board ejected Albert Manifold after undisclosed Elliott meetings and bullying complaints exposed a chairman who governed like a CEO.

Albert Manifold lasted 239 days as BP's chairman. By the morning of May 26, when BP's board voted unanimously to remove him with immediate effect, the surface explanation — vague language about "governance standards, oversight and conduct" — was already giving way to something messier underneath.

The public framing was that the board had been "surprised and disappointed" to learn of unacceptable conduct. Manifold's own framing was blunter: he was removed "without warning and without explanation" and accused unnamed parties of telling lies. "I do not accept," he said, "that lies can be told about me."

Both versions were incomplete.

A chairman who acted like a CEO

Manifold arrived in October 2025 with credentials that made the appointment look bold at the time. He had spent a decade running CRH, the Irish building-materials group, compounding shareholder returns at a rate that drew consistent admiration in the City. BP, reeling through its third senior leadership change in as many years and under sustained pressure from activist investor Elliott Management — which had built a stake of roughly 5% — wanted a tough operator.

It got one. Almost immediately, Manifold moved to replace CEO Murray Auchincloss, accelerated the company's retreat from renewables, and signalled a new cost discipline that extended, by his own account, to making his own coffee and declining a chauffeur. He also turned down corporate sports hospitality — including, according to people familiar with the matter, Wimbledon tickets, a traditional City perk — as part of his broader austerity message.

The problem, as people inside BP came to see it, was that the line between driving change and running the company had dissolved. Multiple colleagues described Manifold as wanting to control everything, his style characterised as "shouty" and his manner as condescending across levels of seniority. Several shareholders who met him during the period reported difficulty securing access and, once in the room, found him prone to deflecting questions to the company secretary. One person at a leading shareholder described a colleague who came away from a meeting concluding Manifold was "condescending" and "arrogant." A City adviser put the governance misjudgement plainly: "I've never known a company take such a risk."

The Elliott problem

The flashpoint on governance — as opposed to conduct — was Manifold's engagement with Elliott. The chairman held meetings with the activist without formally notifying the full board. The interactions were known to BP's investor relations team, but that fell short of the disclosure standard the board expected. Sources characterised the Elliott meetings as not a formal breach of rules, but as symptomatic of a unilateral style that had become untenable.

Manifold's reading was different. He suggested publicly that his cost-cutting push — challenging excess, holding the organisation to higher standards — had made him enemies. "Is it possible that in my determination to drive change on costs, performance, the balance sheet and shareholder communications, I pushed hard and challenged people directly? Yes, it is," he said. The implication being that the conduct complaints were, at least partly, blowback from those he had inconvenienced.

The board rejected that reading. Amanda Blanc, BP's senior independent director and the figure who had led the search that produced Manifold's appointment, made the statement that closed the door. The board, she said, had taken "decisive action."

BP shares fell as much as 9% on the day of the announcement before paring losses to close around 6% lower. Ian Tyler, a non-executive director and former chief executive of Balfour Beatty, was named interim chairman. Ironically, during Manifold's seven-month tenure, BP's stock had risen roughly 29%, outpacing Shell — a fact that will complicate any neat narrative about the removal being performance-driven.

The search for a permanent chair is now underway. For a company that has cycled through three chief executives and three chairmen since 2023, the credibility of that process matters as much as the outcome.
