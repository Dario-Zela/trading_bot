AstraZeneca wins FDA approval for Imfinzi in high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer

AstraZeneca's global headquarters at the Cambridge Biomedical Campus, UK, where its oncology R&D is anchored — Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 4.0
In one sentence
FDA cleared IMFINZI + BCG as the first new bladder cancer therapy in 30 years — AstraZeneca extends a $6B franchise, though Merck's rival trial looms.

The US Food and Drug Administration on 28 May 2026 approved durvalumab — marketed by AstraZeneca as IMFINZI — in combination with Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) for adults with BCG-naïve, high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC). The agency's action marks the first immunotherapy combination cleared in this early-stage disease setting and, by AstraZeneca's count, the first genuinely new treatment option for this patient group in more than 30 years.

High-risk NMIBC is a common but often manageable form of bladder malignancy — tumours that have not penetrated the muscle wall and can, with prompt treatment, frequently be controlled without removing the bladder. BCG, a tuberculosis vaccine long adapted to prime the immune system in the bladder lining, has been the anchor of treatment for decades. "BCG-naïve" describes patients who have not previously received BCG, typically those presenting at initial diagnosis with high-risk disease features. The gap in the existing regimen: a meaningful proportion of patients still experience recurrence or progression on BCG alone, and until this approval no licensed agent existed in the US to improve on that baseline.

What the trial showed

The supporting evidence comes from POTOMAC, a Phase III trial that enrolled 1,018 patients across more than 120 centres in 12 countries. Adding one year of IMFINZI to standard BCG induction and maintenance therapy produced a hazard ratio for disease-free survival of 0.68 (95% CI: 0.50–0.93; p=0.0154) — a 32% reduction in the risk of high-risk disease recurrence, progression, or death — against BCG alone, at a median follow-up of 60.7 months. At two years, 87% of patients in the combination arm remained alive and disease-free versus 82% in the control arm. In a disease where recurrence frequently leads to cystectomy — surgical removal of the bladder — that difference is clinically consequential. The trial also found that BCG-unresponsive recurrences in the first year of treatment were roughly halved in the IMFINZI arm.

Dave Fredrickson, AstraZeneca's executive vice president of oncology, described the approval as marking "an important advance for patients at risk of early disease recurrence" that "signals a shift in the standard of care."

For AstraZeneca, the more precise framing may be franchise protection rather than franchise expansion. IMFINZI generated $6.06 billion in 2025 revenue, up 29% on the year, on the back of prior US approvals in muscle-invasive bladder cancer and small-cell lung cancer. The new NMIBC indication extends that commercial runway, but the BCG-naïve high-risk subset is a defined patient population — not the broad, indication-agnostic market that pushed Merck's pembrolizumab (Keytruda) to nearly $32 billion in 2025 sales across more than 40 approved indications.

The competitive context matters. Roche's atezolizumab already failed its primary endpoint in the comparable ALBAN combination trial, leaving IMFINZI as the only approved immunotherapy in this setting — for now. Merck is running the Phase III KEYNOTE-676 study evaluating pembrolizumab in BCG-naïve NMIBC; if it reads out positively, AstraZeneca's first-mover position will face direct challenge. Those results, alongside any European Medicines Agency action on the POTOMAC data, are the next meaningful watch points in this corner of the checkpoint-inhibitor market.
