Iran draws red lines on nuclear talks and claims co-ownership of Hormuz as Trump defers any new deal

The Strait of Hormuz, photographed by NASA's Terra satellite, December 2020. Iran insists its management is a matter for Tehran and Muscat alone. — NASA / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Iran will discuss a ceasefire but not its nuclear program, while Trump left a two-hour Situation Room meeting without signing off on any deal.

The diplomatic choreography coming out of Tehran this week reveals a deliberate attempt to manage two crises at separate speeds. Iran's Foreign Ministry Spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei told state television on Monday that current negotiations with the United States are focused exclusively on ending the war — and that Tehran's nuclear program is not on the table. Not now, and not at Iran's invitation.

"The focus of the negotiations is on ending the war, and at this stage we are not discussing nuclear issues," Baghaei said, a position Iranian officials have maintained with unusual consistency across the rounds of diplomacy that followed the ceasefire established in April. Meanwhile, in Washington on Friday, President Trump convened his national security team in the Situation Room for roughly two hours, emerging without a decision on whether to sign off on a memorandum of understanding that would extend the ceasefire, reopen the Strait of Hormuz, and begin a 60-day clock on separate nuclear talks. Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Vice President JD Vance were both present. The meeting was described by a senior administration official as focused on "making a final determination" — language that made the absence of any announcement all the more pointed.

A deliberate partition

The Iranian strategy has been to treat the two issues — the war and the nuclear file — as concentric circles to be tackled in sequence, not simultaneously. Baghaei framed this as diplomatic logic: end active hostilities first, then open space for the harder structural questions. That architecture also happens to serve Iran's negotiating interests. So long as the nuclear program is walled off from the immediate deal, Tehran can accept a ceasefire and partial economic relief without conceding anything on enrichment.

The difficulty is that meaningful sanctions relief — the economic prize Iran most wants — is precisely what the US is holding back. Baghaei acknowledged as much, saying that "lifting sanctions will not be discussed in this short timeframe" and that nuclear issues could be addressed in "whatever timeframe is ultimately agreed upon" — 30, 60 days or more. That sequencing preserves Iran's leverage over the second act even as it closes the first.

Senior Iranian lawmakers have been blunter. Ebrahim Azizi, a member of parliament, declared this week that uranium enrichment, sanctions removal, and control of the Strait of Hormuz are all non-negotiable red lines. The distinction between a spokesman's careful diplomatic language and a parliamentarian's open declaration is itself informative: Baghaei's version allows a degree of flexibility that Azizi's does not — and both can be true at once.

Hormuz as a sovereignty claim

The second signal from Tehran concerns the strait itself. Baghaei was explicit: "No country other than Iran and Oman is present in the Strait of Hormuz." Any arrangement for the waterway — which carries roughly a fifth of the world's traded oil — would be negotiated between those two coastal states, not with Washington. "How this region should be managed concerns the littoral states," he said.

That formulation is more than a procedural point. By designating Hormuz as a matter for Iran and Oman to manage jointly, Tehran is framing any American role in the strait's reopening as transactional rather than structural. Oman has played discreet go-between throughout the negotiating process, hosting multiple rounds in Muscat. Elevating its role to co-administrator of the strait formalises Oman's existing utility while excluding Washington from any long-term governance arrangement.

Iran disputed Trump's public account of the proposed deal sharply. After Trump posted on Truth Social that Hormuz "must be immediately open" and that Iran had agreed to allow the US to "recover and destroy" enriched uranium "in close coordination" with international inspectors, Iran's state-affiliated Fars News accused him of attempting to "project a fabricated victory" and "distort" the agreement's actual terms. The gap between the two sides' descriptions of the same emerging document is itself a material risk for the deal's survival.

Two hours, no decision

The disagreement over what has actually been agreed points to the difficulty Trump faces. The proposed memorandum — as broadly reported — would establish a 60-day ceasefire extension and begin formal nuclear negotiations. Trump laid out his own conditions publicly: Iran must commit to never acquiring a nuclear weapon, the strait must be open without tolls or restrictions, and "no money will be exchanged, until further notice."

That last condition matters most. Iranian officials have consistently linked any genuine progress on the nuclear file to "tangible economic benefits" — meaning real sanctions rollback, not a promise of future talks. Tehran estimates that relief on oil sales alone could generate nearly $10 billion in government revenue over 60 days. Without that prospect in sight, Iran calculates it has little reason to accelerate concessions on enrichment.

Trump had said he would make his "final determination" at Friday's meeting. He did not. Whether that reflects genuine deliberation among advisers, disagreement between hawkish and pragmatic camps in the room, or a posture aimed at extracting further Iranian concessions is not yet clear. What is clear is that the ambiguity costs both sides economically — and that it keeps the geopolitical risk premium alive in oil markets that had begun to price in a deal.

The next concrete catalysts are the fate of the ceasefire itself — which must be extended or allowed to expire — and whether a memorandum is formally signed or collapses under the weight of competing characterisations. If the MOU holds, the 60-day nuclear clock would begin, bringing the harder questions into focus: the permitted level of Iranian enrichment, the disposition of stockpiled material, and the pace of sanctions rollback.

Iran's insistence on sequencing the two tracks is not inherently unreasonable as a diplomatic structure. But it rests on an assumption that the US will stay at the table through the second act, after the immediate strategic pressure — the war, the closed strait — has been relieved. That is precisely what Trump's two-hour silence on Friday has left open.
