US general held talks with Cuban military officials at the edge of Guantanamo Bay

The Guantanamo fence line, with US territory on the right and Cuban territory on the left — the site of rare monthly military contacts that were suspended under Trump's second term. — US Navy / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
SOUTHCOM chief Gen. Francis Donovan met Cuban military officials at Guantanamo's perimeter — the first such senior-level contact since Trump suspended fence-line talks, as Washington pressures Havana.

A senior US military commander held a rare face-to-face meeting with Cuban military officials at the perimeter of Naval Station Guantanamo Bay on Friday — the first such exchange at that level in recent memory, and a notable signal of cautious communication amid one of the most confrontational periods in US-Cuba relations in decades.

General Francis Donovan, head of US Southern Command, met with Cuban Army Corps General Roberto Legrá Sotolongo, First Deputy Minister of Cuba's General Staff, along with other senior Cuban military leaders. The encounter took place at the physical fence line that divides the American naval base — a stretch of sovereign US territory on the eastern tip of Cuba — from the surrounding Cuban territory. Both sides described the meeting as covering "operational security matters" and "topics of interest to both parties." Cuba's Ministry of Defense, in a brief statement, called it "positive."

A fence line, long dormant

Lower-ranking US and Cuban military officers held routine monthly meetings at the Guantanamo perimeter for years — so-called fence-line meetings intended to manage the oddity of a US military base embedded in a country the United States has not had normal diplomatic relations with for over six decades. Those contacts were suspended when Donald Trump returned to the White House for his second term. Friday's meeting was therefore a resumption as much as a breakthrough, though Donovan's rank — he commands all US forces in Latin America and the Caribbean — made it unusually significant.

The meeting does not exist in isolation. CIA Director John Ratcliffe traveled to Havana in early May to meet with Cuban intelligence and interior ministry officials, conveying that Washington could offer expanded economic and security cooperation if Cuba made "fundamental changes" to its governance and foreign partnerships. State Department diplomats visited the island the month before. The accumulation of contacts, read together, suggests the Trump administration is running a dual-track posture: maximum economic pressure alongside exploratory back-channel dialogue.

That pressure has been severe. A US oil blockade, effectively in place since February, has tipped Cuba into acute crisis. Power cuts lasting up to 22 hours a day have become common across the island, food and fuel shortages are widespread, and protests have followed. The Trump administration has also indicted former Cuban President Raúl Castro, imposed new sanctions on Cuba's intelligence agency and interior ministry, and Secretary of State Marco Rubio has indicated further measures are coming.

Against that backdrop, Trump himself has escalated his rhetoric, expressing interest in "taking Cuba in some form" and warning — after the conclusion of the US-Iran confrontation — that "Cuba's going to be next." Cuba's President Miguel Díaz-Canel has replied that any US military action would produce a "bloodbath with incalculable consequences."

Friday's fence-line conversation — formal enough to involve a four-star-equivalent commander, limited enough to cover perimeter security rather than policy — does not resolve that standoff. What it may do is keep a thread of military-to-military communication open at a moment when miscalculation carries unusually high costs. The next indicator to watch is whether the monthly fence-line meetings resume at working level, and whether Ratcliffe's Havana visit produces any visible Cuban response to Washington's demand for "fundamental changes."
