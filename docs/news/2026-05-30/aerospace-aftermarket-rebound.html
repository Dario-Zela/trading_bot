Aerospace aftermarket rebounds unevenly as laggards present a potential entry point

Workers on the A321 final assembly line — the commercial aircraft backlog at Airbus and Boeing now exceeds 15,000 units, underpinning the long-duration demand thesis for suppliers like Woodward. — Wikimedia Commons / Airbus S.A.S.
In one sentence
Susquehanna opens WWD with a $423 target, framing the aerospace component supplier as an aftermarket laggard with a 19% recovery discount built in.

The aerospace aftermarket recovery that analysts have been calling for most of 2026 is happening — but not uniformly. A cluster of component suppliers and aftermarket specialists have rallied sharply off their late-2025 lows, while a second group of names tied to the same underlying demand story sits closer to multi-year valuation troughs. Susquehanna stepped into that gap on May 26, initiating coverage of Woodward Inc. (NASDAQ: WWD) with a Positive rating and a $423 price target — a figure that, at the time of writing, represents roughly 19% upside to where the stock is trading.

Woodward is not a household name outside industrial circles. The Fort Collins, Colorado company designs and manufactures fuel pumps, metering units, actuators, specialty valves, fuel nozzles, and thrust reverser actuation systems — the unglamorous plumbing that makes turbine engines work in demanding environments. It counts Boeing and Airbus as core customers, which matters more than it might sound: commercial aircraft backlogs at the two manufacturers now exceed 15,000 units, and those planes will need Woodward's components installed and serviced for decades.

Susquehanna's initiation note lays out a growth path that is notably specific: 12% annual revenue growth and 24% annual free cash flow growth through fiscal 2028. The FCF acceleration is the more interesting figure — it implies margin expansion and capital discipline running well ahead of the top line, which tends to be what re-rating stories are made of. The firm also flags the ongoing hydraulic-to-electric systems transition in commercial aviation as a secular tailwind that Woodward is positioned to capture.

The broader sector context explains the entry-point framing. Geopolitical tensions and elevated oil prices — the latter inflating airline operating costs — have dragged on aerospace equities since late 2025. Citi, in a recent sector note, flagged that commercial aerospace names tied to aftermarket services were trading near three- to five-year valuation lows despite solid first-quarter earnings. The bank identified the aftermarket segment as the most likely early mover in any recovery, given its recurring revenue profile and relative insulation from new-build production timing. WWD fits that template: while its stock touched $407 in 2025, it has spent much of the first half of 2026 well below that level.

The upside math and its assumptions

A $423 target from Susquehanna sits above even the 52-week high of $407, which makes the implied thesis more aggressive than a simple mean-reversion argument. It depends on execution against those fiscal-2028 projections — and on the commercial aviation cycle continuing to deliver volume, which Citi cautioned is not guaranteed absent a cleaner resolution to Middle East instability affecting travel demand and jet fuel supply chains.

Truist, for its part, had already confirmed its Buy view after Woodward's strong second-quarter earnings in early May, raising its target to $415 from $404. Two constructive notes in the same month, from different shops with slightly different targets, typically signals building institutional consensus rather than a lone-wolf call.

The specific catalyst worth watching is Woodward's fiscal third-quarter print, expected later this summer, which will be the first read on whether the demand momentum the company flagged in Q2 is sustaining. Any widening of full-year guidance — or commentary on the pace of aircraft deliveries from Boeing and Airbus — will either validate or complicate the Susquehanna thesis.
