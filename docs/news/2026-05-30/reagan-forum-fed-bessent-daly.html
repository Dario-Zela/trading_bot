At the Reagan Forum, Treasury and the Fed both called for a more accountable, productivity-focused era

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent at the 2026 Reagan National Economic Forum, where he endorsed Warsh's Fed independence while signalling a desire for institutional reform. — U.S. Department of the Treasury / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Bessent hailed a 'new day at the Fed' and a post-breakfast bond-yield observation, but offered no concrete mechanism behind his accountability push.

Scott Bessent had breakfast with Kevin Warsh before taking the stage at the Reagan National Economic Forum in Simi Valley on Thursday, and he wanted the room to know it. "It's a new day at the Fed," the Treasury Secretary told the audience at the Ronald Reagan Presidential Library. "Change is good, and I think we are going to see a new sheriff in town."

The remarks, delivered during a fireside conversation at the 2026 forum, were the most direct signal yet that Treasury views Warsh's appointment — the new chair was sworn in on May 22 — as a deliberate reset of the central bank's culture, not just its personnel. Pressed on the Fed's independence, Bessent was unequivocal: "100%." But he paired that endorsement with a pointed aspiration. "I also think we're going to get back to basics in terms of accountability, credibility."

What those basics entail, he did not specify. No legislative proposals, no structural review timeline, no metrics for success. The rhetorical pattern — values-laden, operationally empty — is one Bessent has repeated in congressional testimony and in a CNBC interview earlier this year, when he called for the Fed to "move back into the background like it used to do." The forum setting produced no new substance, only a warmer delivery.

The productivity thread

A few panels away, San Francisco Fed President Mary Daly offered a parallel framing from inside the institution. Daly told the forum that the Fed must restore full price stability — but not, she cautioned, at the expense of the broader economy. Her emphasis fell on productivity growth as the mechanism that allows wages and employment to rise without stoking inflation: a disinflationary tailwind, in her reading, rather than a tradeoff to be managed.

The remarks aligned with Daly's consistent line this year on AI and structural growth, and they offered no policy signal either. What they did do was illustrate how different the registers are: Bessent speaks of reform and regime renewal; Daly speaks of calibration and balance. The institution hums along regardless.

The concrete test of Bessent's enthusiasm will not come from a forum speech. Warsh has already stated publicly that he does not believe the Fed should offer forward guidance as currently practised — a long-held conviction that shaped his Senate confirmation hearings. Whether that translates into a formal communications overhaul, and on what timeline, is the next thing to watch. Until then, "back to basics" is a slogan in search of a rulebook.

Bessent also noted, with a studied air of neutrality, that bond yields peaked the day before Warsh was sworn in. "I'm not saying whether it's the causation or the correlation." The market is, apparently, supposed to draw its own conclusions.
