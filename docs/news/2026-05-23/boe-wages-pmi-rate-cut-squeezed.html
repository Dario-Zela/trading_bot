BoE rate-cut path narrows as 3.5% wage settlements and resilient PMIs squeeze the MPC

The Bank of England on Threadneedle Street, London. Bank Rate has been held at 3.75% at every MPC meeting in 2026. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
BoE Agents put median 2026 wage settlements at 3.5%, above the MPC's ~3.25% target-consistent floor, as stagflationary May PMI data forecloses any near-term rate-cut window.

The Bank of England's rate-cutting window is narrowing from both sides. From one direction comes the data its regional Agents gathered this spring: a median private-sector pay settlement of 3.5% for 2026, 0.1 percentage points above February's estimate and fractionally above the level the Monetary Policy Committee considers consistent with returning inflation to its 2% target. From the other comes May's flash PMI survey, which sent the UK services sector into contraction for the first time in thirteen months — in a manner that, paradoxically, does not strengthen the case for easing.

Bank Rate has been held at 3.75% since December. The MPC voted 8-1 to hold in April. The June 18 meeting looks set to be another hold.

The pay settlements the MPC can't unsee

The Agents' findings carry particular weight because of what they represent. Unlike forecasts or surveys of expectations, the quarterly pay-settlement data is drawn from the BoE's network of regional contacts — conversations with finance directors, HR leads, and industry bodies who have already signed agreements. As the April Monetary Policy Report noted, around four-fifths of 2026 settlements were agreed by the time of its publication, with the cumulative average sitting at 3.5%.

That figure is not alarming in isolation. Wage growth has been decelerating, and 3.5% sits closer to target than the 5%-plus readings of two years ago. The problem is where it sits relative to what the MPC needs to see. The Bank's own analysis suggests wage growth needs to settle at roughly 3.25% for services inflation — the most wage-sensitive component of the CPI basket — to sustainably return to target over the medium term. At 3.5%, the gap is modest but the direction is wrong.


Andrew Bailey chairs the MPC, which voted 8-1 to hold Bank Rate at 3.75% in April — a decision the wage data since has done nothing to complicate.

And the window to close that gap this year has effectively shut. Most of those settlements were reached before the Iran war energy shock reconfigured the cost-of-living backdrop. Firms are unlikely to reopen wage agreements downward now that energy bills have risen; some will have accepted marginally higher claims precisely because of the optics. Whatever the 2027 pay round brings, 2026's arithmetic is fixed. The MPC is reading last winter's decisions in May's data.

A services contraction that offers no relief

May's flash PMI at first glance looks like the economic deceleration the MPC needs to see before cutting rates. The S&P Global UK Services Business Activity Index fell to 47.9 — from 52.7 in April and well below the 50.0 no-change threshold — its steepest monthly drop since the post-pandemic reopening reversal. The composite output index slid to 48.5, a 13-month low, with S&P Global estimating the economy contracted at around a 0.2% quarterly rate based on the May reading.

But the mechanism matters. Services businesses cited not weakening domestic demand in any conventional sense, but specific shocks: hesitant client spending tied to Middle East geopolitical uncertainty, a sharp drop in international travel flows, and postponed investment decisions. These are supply-side disruptions dressed as demand weakness. And the price data running alongside the activity numbers tells a starkly different story: factory-gate input costs came in at their highest since July 2022, as supply-chain disruption and higher energy prices fed through manufacturers' books. The UK is not cooling into benign disinflation — it is slowing into a stagflationary squeeze.

Manufacturing held its ground at 53.7, matching April and sitting at its strongest level since May 2022. A substantial share of that resilience, though, reflects customers front-loading inventory ahead of expected further price rises or supply disruptions — borrowed demand, not evidence of a broad-based industrial recovery. The new orders sub-index tells a more cautious story than the headline figure.

The eurozone adds uncomfortable context. Eurozone services PMI dropped to 46.4 in May from 47.6 in April — the weakest reading in nearly five years — while manufacturing edged down to 51.4 from 52.2. The ECB, with its deposit rate at 2.00%, faces a structurally similar impasse: output softening under the weight of the Iran shock, costs rising because of it. Neither central bank has the clean data it would need to cut without risking entrenching the supply-side inflation.

June 18 and the summer calculus

Polymarket assigns an 81.5% probability to no change at the 18 June MPC meeting. More striking is the 17.5% attached to a 25-basis-point hike — a hawkish tail risk that reflects concern the BoE may eventually be compelled to lean against the inflationary half of the stagflationary equation rather than the growth half. Cut probabilities remain negligible.

UK CPI fell to 2.8% in April from 3.3% in March — softer than the Reuters consensus of 3.0%. But the drop was largely mechanical: energy price cap adjustments unwound the fuel-cost contribution that had built up over the preceding months. The Bank's own projections have CPI returning to roughly 3.3% in Q3 as those base effects fade and war-related energy costs pass through consumer prices. That trajectory, combined with wages locked at 3.5% until at least the 2027 pay round, leaves the MPC waiting for evidence that cannot arrive this year.

A first cut is a Q4 2026 story at the very earliest — and only then if some combination of Iran de-escalation, further labour market loosening, and encouraging autumn Agents' data on 2027 pay intentions aligns. For markets still pricing multiple cuts before December, the BoE's own fieldwork has just made that case considerably harder to sustain. The next catalyst to watch is the May CPI release, due mid-June: if the energy base-effect snapback arrives earlier than the Bank's models project, the hawkish tail on June 18 will look a good deal less theoretical.
