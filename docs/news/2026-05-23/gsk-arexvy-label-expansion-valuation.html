GSK's Arexvy label expansion and new licensing deals begin to close a persistent valuation gap

GSK's former global headquarters at Brentford, London — the company now holds RSV vaccine approvals in more than 70 countries. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Three multi-market label expansions and a Pfizer patent settlement give concrete form to the argument that GSK's persistent valuation discount on Arexvy is no longer fully justified.

Three regulatory approvals and a patent settlement have arrived in quick succession for GSK's Arexvy RSV vaccine, giving analysts something concrete to work with after years of arguing that the company trades at an unjustified discount to its pharmaceutical peers.

In March, the US Food and Drug Administration expanded Arexvy's label to cover adults aged 18 to 49 who carry at least one elevated risk factor — conditions including COPD, heart disease, diabetes and obesity. The vaccine had already been cleared for adults 60 and older, and for the 50–59 cohort at increased risk. The new indication unlocks roughly 21 million additional US adults who meet the clinical criteria. The annual RSV burden in this younger group is not trivial: an estimated 17,000 hospitalisations, 277,000 emergency department visits and nearly 2 million outpatient consultations each year, according to GSK's own submission data.

Europe followed. The European Commission approved Arexvy for all adults aged 18 and above — the broadest label of any RSV vaccine in the region. Japan's Ministry of Health moved in the same direction on 18 May, expanding its clearance to at-risk adults aged 18–59, explicitly including immunocompromised patients. GSK now holds some form of adult RSV approval in more than 70 countries.

The licensor angle

What has attracted less market attention is the parallel shift in GSK's IP position. In April 2025, Pfizer took a worldwide licence to GSK's RSV vaccine patents and agreed to pay undisclosed royalties, ending multi-jurisdictional litigation that had run since 2023. That outcome — converting a direct competitor into a royalty payer — carries strategic significance beyond the immediate cash flow. A separate settlement with CureVac and BioNTech in August 2025 will deliver $370 million upfront (of which $320 million in cash) plus a 1% royalty on US mRNA vaccine sales, demonstrating GSK's appetite to monetise its IP estate across the respiratory space. A potential Arexvy partnership with China's Chongqing Zhifei Biological Products, under a right-of-first-refusal arrangement, adds a further geographic option.

The valuation backdrop makes these developments worth examining. GSK has long traded at a forward P/E of roughly 13–15 times against a sector peer average above 25 times. Analysts point to several drag factors: an extended litigation overhang now in the process of being resolved; pipeline execution risk; and the 2024 ACIP decision restricting CDC recommendations on RSV vaccines to the 75-and-older population — a move that trimmed projected US market value from approximately $4.6 billion to around $1.7 billion annually by 2030. The label expansions do not overturn that ACIP guidance, but they add back younger, at-risk cohorts via a parallel regulatory route.

The commercial translation remains uncertain. Arexvy posted £100 million in Q1 2026 sales — down 18% year on year — reflecting low out-of-season demand and a harder-to-activate patient cohort. GSK has issued no revenue guidance on the expanded indication. Uptake among 18-to-49-year-olds will depend on physician awareness, payer coverage decisions and whether ACIP revisits its own guidance for this cohort.

The specific catalysts to watch: any ACIP deliberation on RSV vaccination in at-risk adults under 50, and the pace of uptake in Europe and Japan under the newly broadened labels. Until those data points arrive, the gap between GSK's current multiple and where analysts believe it should trade will remain a matter of inference rather than evidence.
