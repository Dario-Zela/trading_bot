China PMI below 50 hits iron ore and copper as dual-front demand squeeze deepens

Iron ore pellets stockpiled at a handling terminal — the primary steelmaking feedstock repricing on softening Chinese demand. — LightningVolt / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
China's May PMI hits 49.5, well below the 50 threshold and analyst forecasts, as weak exports and sluggish domestic demand simultaneously pressure iron ore and copper.

China's official manufacturing purchasing managers index for May came in at 49.5, missing the median analyst forecast of 50.5 by a full point and slipping back into contraction for the first time since February. The reading, released by the National Bureau of Statistics, sent iron ore futures on the Dalian Commodity Exchange below CNY 800 per metric tonne and pushed the benchmark Singapore iron ore contract toward $107 a tonne — a two-week low. Copper tracked lower in both London and Shanghai.

The miss was broad-based. The new orders subindex fell to 48.8, its weakest reading since August 2024; the production gauge contracted to 49.7 for the first time in six months. Neither subcomponent was close to consensus.

Two fronts, not one

What makes May's reading harder to dismiss than January–February's contraction is the character of the weakness. The earlier episode drew largely from external disruption: elevated tariffs, Iran-war energy-cost shocks, and strained shipping routes. Domestic production had held up. May removes that qualifier.

The NBS non-manufacturing PMI — a more domestically oriented gauge — eased to 50.3, barely expansionary. April's broader economic data had already signalled the same squeeze: retail sales grew just 0.2% year-on-year against a 2% consensus, their weakest print since late 2022, while factory output expanded 4.1% against expectations of 5.9%. Export orders and domestic consumption are now softening simultaneously.

For base-metals traders, that simultaneity matters. A China slowdown driven purely by export weakness still leaves some demand support from domestic construction and manufacturing. When internal consumption folds too, the entire demand thesis needs recalibrating. Iron ore stockpiles at 47 Chinese port cities stood at 177.5 million tonnes in the most recent weekly count — within 1% of the record set in March — suppressing restocking incentives at steel mills even if near-term sentiment firms.

Analysts at ING flagged the underlying divergence as early as April: the Caixin PMI, weighted toward private and export-oriented manufacturers, had outperformed the NBS survey. "External demand is the engine of China's current recovery while domestic demand remains the drag," the bank wrote at the time. May's NBS print suggests that engine has now stalled as well.

Stimulus is the obvious counter-narrative. Beijing's January cabinet meeting announced a household-consumption package, and further fiscal support is expected before the mid-year government work review. But transmission to commodity demand has been slow. Full-year iron ore futures consensus sits near $95 a tonne, well below current spot. Copper's muted response — despite long-run bullish arguments from AI infrastructure build-out and energy transition spending — reflects market scepticism that this stimulus round reaches construction and industrial channels quickly enough to shift 2026 balances.

The June PMI, due at the start of July, is the next clean read. If new orders and construction activity subindices remain soft, the case for structural rather than merely cyclical deceleration will be increasingly difficult to argue against.
