Iran rules out enrichment talks; Wicker's military call resets the oil risk premium

Satellite view of the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of globally traded oil passes — NASA / MODIS / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Iran rules out enrichment talks and says a deal is not close; Senator Wicker calls for military action, jointly resetting oil's fading conflict premium.

Oil markets spent much of this week systematically trimming the conflict premium baked in since February's US-Israel strikes on Iran. On Friday, two statements arrived in quick succession to complicate that trade.

Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei told reporters that uranium enrichment and the disposition of Iran's nuclear stockpile are not on the table in the current round of talks. "Details related to the nuclear issue are not being discussed at this stage," he said, characterising the gap between Tehran and Washington as "deep and significant." When pressed on whether a deal was within reach, Baghaei drew a clear line: Iran cannot say it "has reached a point where an agreement is close." The remarks deflate a specific market assumption — that the ongoing Pakistan-mediated channel between US envoy Steve Witkoff and Iranian negotiators was converging toward something that would address the nuclear file alongside the Hormuz shipping standoff.

Wicker's Capitol Hill intervention

Almost simultaneously, Roger Wicker — the Mississippi Republican who chairs the Senate Armed Services Committee — published a statement warning President Trump that he is "being ill-advised to pursue a deal that would not be worth the paper it is written on." Wicker called explicitly for US forces to "finish the destruction of Iran's conventional military capabilities and then reopen the strait," language that maps to an objective considerably more expansive than the parameters of any current diplomatic exchange. "We must finish what we started. It is past time for action," he wrote, dated May 22.

Wicker's position is not a fringe one within the Republican caucus; his committee chairmanship gives him a platform the White House cannot ignore. The statement adds institutional weight to the hawkish flank of the Iran debate at the precise moment talks appear to be stalling on their most substantive question.

Together, the two signals excise the near-term scenario oil traders had been tentatively pricing: an agreement that eases Hormuz shipping restrictions and opens the path toward sanctions relief. Brent crude, which had touched $106 early in Friday's session on optimism about Witkoff's latest visit, settled around $102.58 — down more than 2% on the day and off nearly 4% for the week. US crude ended at $96.35. The weekly move is a rough measure of how much deal optimism had been actively suppressing the geopolitical component of the price.

The broader stakes remain severe. Around 20% of globally traded oil transits the Strait of Hormuz, and the IEA has described the disruption since February as the largest supply shock in the market's recorded history; Brent surged roughly 65% at its peak before ceasefire diplomacy began to deflate the war premium. That premium never fully disappeared — it was right not to.

The next concrete catalyst is whether Baghaei's framing holds through the next round of talks or whether Tehran signals any flexibility on sequencing — enrichment and nuclear details now, Hormuz later, or vice versa. Trump has characterised the ceasefire as "on life support." With Wicker applying Senate pressure toward military resumption and Iran's Foreign Ministry declining to confirm progress, the direction of that premium is no longer obviously lower.
