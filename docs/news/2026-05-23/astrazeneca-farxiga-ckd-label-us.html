AstraZeneca's Farxiga wins expanded US kidney disease label, bolstering ex-China growth case

AstraZeneca's Academy House facility — the company's Farxiga franchise, now with a broader US kidney disease label, faces both generic competition at home and pricing pressure in China. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
A wider US CKD label for Farxiga adds addressable patients and bolsters AstraZeneca's case that ex-China growth can absorb pressure from Beijing's pricing mechanisms.

The US Food and Drug Administration has approved an expanded indication for AstraZeneca's Farxiga (dapagliflozin) in chronic kidney disease, broadening the range of patients eligible to receive the drug and adding incremental market runway to one of the company's largest revenue lines at a moment when that revenue is under notable pressure from another direction.

Farxiga generated $8.4 billion in global sales in 2025 — up 9% year-on-year — making it one of the few true blockbusters in AstraZeneca's cardiovascular and renal franchise. But 2026 has introduced friction. In the first quarter, the drug's revenue fell 3% to $2.2 billion, squeezed by two overlapping headwinds: the April 2026 US generic entry following patent expiry, and the implementation of China's volume-based procurement — the national tendering system that extracts sharply lower prices in exchange for guaranteed volumes. Volume-based procurement has been a recurring feature of AstraZeneca's China narrative for several years; Farxiga's inclusion in the latest round is not a surprise, but it has concentrated attention on what the ex-China business can deliver.

The label expansion addresses that question, at least at the margin. The original 2021 US approval covered adults with chronic kidney disease at risk of progression — specifically those with an estimated glomerular filtration rate between 25 and 75 mL/min/1.73m² and elevated urinary albumin — based on the DAPA-CKD phase 3 trial, which showed a 39% reduction in the composite endpoint of worsening kidney function or death versus placebo. The updated indication broadens patient eligibility, following a path already cleared by regulators elsewhere: the UK's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence removed its earlier eGFR restrictions in June 2025, and Australia's Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme expanded access in February 2026 to cover patients with eGFR as low as 20 mL/min/1.73m².

Adding bricks to the ex-China story

For investors, the calculus is straightforward if modest. China accounted for $6.65 billion — roughly 11% — of AstraZeneca's $58.7 billion in 2025 revenue, growing just 4%, a rate that lags the company's targets and reflects the structural compression that volume-based procurement imposes. The company has responded partly by expanding its footprint in other emerging markets, where revenues ex-China grew 16% in Q1 2026, and partly by defending its established drugs in developed markets through label extensions. A broader Farxiga CKD indication does not create a new product; it extends the addressable population of an existing one. In a market where generics will eventually erode branded Farxiga's premium — dapagliflozin generics entered the US in April — securing more patients on the branded version through widened indication language is a meaningful if incremental defensive measure.

The company's $80 billion revenue target for 2030 leaves limited room for individual product stumbles. Farxiga, now competing against generics in its home market while facing price suppression abroad, needs every available indication to sustain volume. Physicians have already been prescribing the drug below its previous label thresholds — real-world data suggest average discontinuation eGFRs below 15 mL/min, well under the former 25 mL/min floor — but formalising a broader label matters for formulary access and insurer reimbursement decisions.

Analysts will watch the company's Q2 2026 results — due in late July — for any sign that the expanded label is translating into prescription volume growth. The more immediate signal will be the pace of generic erosion in the US branded segment, which will define how much of the market expansion Farxiga's existing commercial team can capture before dapagliflozin becomes a commodity.
