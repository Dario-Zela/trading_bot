US and Sweden sign Technology Prosperity Deal on AI, quantum computing and frontier science

Swedish Foreign Minister Maria Malmer Stenergard signed the Technology Prosperity Deal with US Secretary of State Marco Rubio in Helsingborg on 22 May. — UK Foreign Office / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 2.0)
In one sentence
Sweden and the US signed a non-binding AI and quantum MOU at NATO's Helsingborg meeting — the fourth bilateral tech deal Washington has struck with individual allies in under a year.

The United States and Sweden signed a bilateral technology cooperation agreement on 22 May in Helsingborg — on the fringes of NATO's informal Foreign Ministers' Meeting — formalising joint ambitions across artificial intelligence, quantum computing, 5G/6G connectivity, biomedical research, and energy. Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Swedish Foreign Minister Maria Malmer Stenergard put their names to the Memorandum of Understanding, a document that functions as a political declaration rather than a contract: it commits no funds, imposes no legal obligations, and names no corporate partners or implementation deadlines.

The deal is the fourth of its kind in less than a year. Washington struck similar Technology Prosperity Deals with the United Kingdom in September 2025, and with Japan and South Korea in October 2025. Sweden now joins that short list — and is notable as the first EU member state to sign one. It also joined Pax Silica, a US-led initiative to secure the silicon supply chain from critical minerals through to AI infrastructure, in March 2026, again as the bloc's pioneer entrant.

Bilateral by design

The pattern is deliberate. EU-wide negotiations on technology standards, AI governance, and strategic industrial policy move at the pace of 27-member consensus. The bilateral approach lets Washington lock in alignment — on research security, export controls, shared standards for 5G networks, quantum ecosystems — without waiting for Brussels to agree. For Sweden, a NATO member only since 2024 and home to Ericsson, a central player in global 5G infrastructure, the deal carries clear industrial logic.

The MOU's text is broad. Cooperation targets AI and advanced connectivity, including alignment on trusted telecommunications standards and Arctic subsea cable infrastructure. On quantum, the two governments pledge to work through the Quantum Development Group toward open, secure standards and "explore additional opportunities" for commercialisation. Research security provisions — covering intellectual property protection, foreign funding disclosure, and talent integrity safeguards — reflect Washington's long-standing concern about technology leakage to strategic rivals.

What the text does not contain: dollar figures, named companies, timelines, or metrics for success. The document explicitly states that "nothing in this MOU commits the Participants to the expenditure of funds." These are aspiration, not appropriation.

What to watch

Rubio's remark that Sweden was the first EU state to sign Pax Silica hints at a wider ambition — a network of bilateral frameworks that, taken together, define a trusted technology sphere without formal EU-level architecture. Whether that framing sits comfortably in Brussels, which is simultaneously enforcing its AI Act and advancing its own semiconductor sovereignty agenda, is a question that will only sharpen as more EU member states are approached.

The next test is which country signs next, and whether the MOU language begins to harden into something with actual funding attached. Until then, it is a handshake in diplomatic clothing — meaningful as signal, inert as mechanism.
