Russia and China tripled spy presence in Cuba targeting US military sites, WSJ finds; Five Eyes partners scramble

Cuba and its capital Havana photographed from the International Space Station — about 160 kilometres from the Florida coast where US military commands are clustered — NASA / ISS Expedition 37 / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Moscow and Beijing have tripled intelligence staff at Cuban SIGINT facilities since 2023, building eavesdropping arrays targeting US military commands 160km across the Florida Straits.

A Wall Street Journal investigation published on 22 May has confirmed what American intelligence officials have warned about privately for months: Russia and China have roughly tripled the number of intelligence personnel stationed at Cuban facilities since 2023, investing in electronic-eavesdropping infrastructure specifically configured to monitor US military installations across the Florida Straits — roughly 160 kilometres of open water.

The Cuban coast places those facilities within reach of an unusually dense cluster of American military power. Florida hosts three of the ten US combatant commands — CENTCOM, SOCOM and SOUTHCOM — alongside the Navy's 4th Fleet, major naval aviation and pilot-training bases, and dozens of Air Force installations. For any adversary trying to understand how the United States trains, coordinates and plans for global conflict, Havana's outskirts offer a vantage point that most listening posts cannot match.

The Listening Posts

The Centre for Strategic and International Studies has identified at least twelve signals-intelligence facilities linked to Chinese operations across four primary Cuban sites: Bejucal, El Wajay, Calabazar and El Salao. Bejucal — the largest — sits in the hills above Havana on land that once housed Soviet nuclear weapons during the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis. Satellite imagery from early 2024 shows that the complex has undergone "major updates over the past decade," including multiple antenna fields and entrances to underground facilities.

The most consequential recent addition sits at El Salao, near Santiago de Cuba: a circularly disposed antenna array (CDAA) under construction since 2021, with a projected diameter of 130 to 200 metres. CDAAs of that scale can, according to CSIS, track signals from 3,000 to 8,000 nautical miles away — a radius that covers not just Florida but much of the eastern seaboard, the Caribbean, and the Atlantic approaches. Russia has followed a parallel track, adding more advanced equipment to its own Cuban posts to intercept maritime traffic and military communications. China is reportedly paying Cuba billions of dollars to host or upgrade these facilities, an arrangement that suits a Cuban government running out of fuel and enduring 20-hour daily blackouts.

What the WSJ investigation adds to the public record is both the scale of the personnel expansion and the degree of coordination between Moscow and Beijing. Both nations appear to have concluded that their separate Cuban posts are now central — not peripheral — to their intelligence posture vis-à-vis the United States.

Washington's Response

The Biden-to-Trump transition brought no reduction in concern on this file; if anything, the current administration has moved faster and more publicly.

On 28 April, Secretary of State Marco Rubio issued a blunt warning: "Washington will not allow any foreign military, intelligence, or security apparatus to operate with impunity just 90 miles from US shores." On 5 May, Rubio met SOUTHCOM commander General Francis Donovan, with a map of Cuba displayed behind them, to discuss what officials have labelled Operation Southern Spear. At a Senate appropriations hearing on 12 May, Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth told lawmakers that Russian nuclear submarines had recently used Cuban ports and that he regarded Cuba as a direct national security threat.

Two days later, CIA Director John Ratcliffe flew to Havana for a rare face-to-face meeting with Cuban Interior Minister Lázaro Álvarez Casas, the head of Cuban intelligence, and Raúl Guillermo Rodríguez Castro, grandson of former leader Raúl Castro. Ratcliffe's message, delivered against the backdrop of Cuba's economic collapse, was unambiguous: the "window of opportunity" for Havana to change course would not remain open indefinitely, and Cuba "cannot continue to serve as a platform for adversaries." Cuba's foreign ministry described the discussions as covering economic and security matters; Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lin Jian characterised Beijing's presence on the island as "legitimate, transparent, and in accordance with international law."

The Alliance Gap

The Five Eyes dimension of the WSJ story is, if anything, more structurally significant than the bilateral US-Cuba standoff. Partners in London, Canberra and Ottawa are now being briefed on the full scope of the electronic surveillance, raising questions about gaps in the alliance's collective threat-assessment protocols — not merely American operational security.

Those gaps have been widening. Britain quietly suspended intelligence sharing in the Caribbean after disputes over US counter-narcotics boat-sinking operations off South America. Excessive NOFORN (no-foreign-dissemination) classifications have restricted allied access to key intelligence threads. The trust deficit within the Five Eyes, documented in detail by analysts over the past year, means that a Cuban intelligence expansion of this scale could mature for longer than it should before the alliance's assessment machinery catches up.

The leadership vacuum at the top of the US intelligence apparatus compounds the timing problem. DNI Tulsi Gabbard announced her resignation on 22 May — the same day the WSJ investigation published — effective 30 June, with Deputy Director Aaron Lucas stepping in as acting DNI. Gabbard had already been excluded from key deliberations on Cuba policy in the weeks before her departure.

The Pentagon has been mandated to deliver a comprehensive report on adversary capabilities in Cuba by June 2026. That document — and whether its findings prompt a recalibration of Five Eyes sharing protocols or an acceleration of Operation Southern Spear — is now the clearest near-term signal of how seriously Washington intends to act on what the Straits of Florida have been quietly transmitting for years.
