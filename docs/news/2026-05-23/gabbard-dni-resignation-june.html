Tulsi Gabbard steps down as Director of National Intelligence, to leave on 30 June

Tulsi Gabbard, Director of National Intelligence, in her official portrait — a role she will vacate at the end of June. — Office of the Director of National Intelligence / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Tulsi Gabbard exits as DNI on June 30 to support her husband through a rare bone cancer diagnosis, with deputy Aaron Lukas named acting director.

Tulsi Gabbard will leave the Office of the Director of National Intelligence at the end of June, stepping away to care for her husband after his diagnosis with a rare form of bone cancer — making her the fourth member of President Trump's cabinet to depart this term.

The resignation, delivered in a letter to Trump after a Friday Oval Office meeting, was swift and personal. "Abraham has been my rock throughout our eleven years of marriage," Gabbard wrote of her husband Abraham Williams. "I cannot in good conscience ask him to face this fight alone while I continue in this demanding and time-consuming position." Trump responded on Truth Social, saying Gabbard had "done an incredible job," and named Principal Deputy Director Aaron Lukas as acting director.

A turbulent fifteen months

Gabbard arrived at ODNI as an unconventional choice for a spymaster — a former Democratic congresswoman from Hawaii who had crossed the aisle to back Trump in 2024 — and spent much of her tenure proving it. She clashed with the CIA over investigations into JFK files, COVID origins and Havana Syndrome incidents. She launched a Director's Initiative Group to root out what she called the politicisation of intelligence-gathering, then quietly shuttered it within a year after it generated more controversy than results. In April she released what she described as declassified evidence that elements of the intelligence community had manufactured the narrative behind Trump's 2019 impeachment — a move Democrats called a disinformation exercise dressed up as transparency.

On the ledger she would prefer cited: reductions in ODNI staffing she says saved more than $700 million a year, the dismantling of DEI programmes, and the declassification of more than 500,000 pages of records. Her office was still working, as recently as this week, to declassify a contested FISA court opinion — a project that now falls to her successor.

There was personal turbulence too. Reports from last month suggested Trump had nearly dismissed her before being persuaded to keep her; Laura Loomer, a prominent Trump ally, had been among her most persistent critics inside the orbit.

The immediate market read is thin. Aaron Lukas — an Arkansas native, 20-year intelligence community veteran and former CIA operations officer who served as chief of staff to acting DNI Richard Grenell and later as Deputy Senior Director for Europe and Russia at the NSC — is a career professional rather than a political statement. His elevation signals continuity, not a course correction.

The signal worth tracking is the permanent nomination. A hawkish successor with defence-sector ties could shift signals intelligence priorities and ripple into government IT and aerospace contractors. Gabbard's departure also removes a voice that had been sceptical of certain Iran threat assessments at a moment when that file is already in motion. Until Trump names a permanent director, Lukas holds the seat — quietly, which may be precisely what the intelligence community requires right now.
