UK, France and Germany push for Gaza humanitarian pause as US envoy runs a separate track

The UN Security Council chamber in New York, where divergent European and US positions on Gaza's transition came into open view on 21 May 2026. — Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 3.0
In one sentence
The E3 push for immediate aid access while the US insists on disarmament first, putting the Western alliance's Gaza strategy visibly out of sync.

Britain, France, and Germany have reiterated their joint call for a humanitarian pause in Gaza and immediate expansion of aid access — a position that sits at an awkward angle to the track being run by Washington's special envoys, who are focused on Hamas disarmament as the gate through which all other progress must pass.

The gap between the two approaches has been visible for months, but it sharpened again this week. On 21 May, the United Kingdom's chargé d'affaires to the UN, Ambassador James Kariuki, told the Security Council that Israel "must take urgent steps to address the humanitarian situation" and called for the United Nations and international NGOs to operate with "unrestricted humanitarian access in all of Gaza, in line with Israel's obligations under international law." France and Germany signalled the same priorities at the same session: French Foreign Minister Jean-Noël Barrot demanded that recovery begin without Hamas disarmament being imposed as a prior condition, a direct divergence from the US position.

The backdrop is a ceasefire that has held in name more than in practice. The Washington Accord, signed last October, and Security Council Resolution 2803 that endorsed it, gave the architecture for a transition to what the Trump administration calls its 20-point plan. But since Phase Two was announced in January 2026, progress has stalled. Nickolay Mladenov, the Board of Peace's High Representative for Gaza, told the Security Council this week that "the risk is that the deteriorating status quo becomes permanent" — a divided enclave, with Hamas retaining military and administrative control over roughly half the territory, and reconstruction financing frozen for as long as weapons have not been laid down. Over 850 civilians have been killed since the ceasefire began; 80 per cent of Gaza's buildings are damaged or destroyed; less than 13 per cent of the year's $4 billion humanitarian appeal has been funded.

A split over sequencing

The operational disagreement between Europe and the United States comes down to ordering. Washington, through both Witkoff and Mladenov, insists that Hamas must commit to full demilitarisation before reconstruction finance flows and governance structures are established. The E3 — the informal grouping of Britain, France, and Germany — argue that the humanitarian deterioration is itself destabilising the transition, and that aid access cannot remain hostage to a disarmament timetable that Hamas has so far refused to accept.

The three European powers also declined to sign the Board of Peace charter when it was unveiled at Davos in January, with Barrot stating plainly that France would support implementing the US peace plan but would not join a body structured to rival the UN Security Council's role. The UK and Germany made similar, if quieter, refusals. This week's Freedom Flotilla episode — French, Italian, and German officials summoned Israeli ambassadors after footage emerged of detained aid activists being mistreated — added further friction to a relationship already under strain.

The immediate test is whether the Mladenov roadmap, built on a principle of reciprocity — each verified step by one party triggering obligations by the other — can get traction at the Security Council. Mladenov has asked the Council to use "every means at its disposal" to press Hamas to accept the roadmap; he has asked equally that Israel uphold its ceasefire obligations. Europe wants humanitarian access to move now, on its own track if necessary. Washington wants the whole package to move together. The next Security Council vote on Gaza's transition timeline, expected before end of June, will show whether those two positions can be reconciled — or whether the divergence hardens into something more durable.
