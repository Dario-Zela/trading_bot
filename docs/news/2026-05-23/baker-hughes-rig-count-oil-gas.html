Baker Hughes: US oil rigs rise to 425 as gas drilling retreats again

Pump jacks working a California oil field — rig counts are a six-to-twelve month leading indicator of future US output — Damian P. Gadal / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
US oil rigs rose 10 to 425 — largest weekly gain since April 2022 — while gas rigs fell 3 to 125, as Hormuz-driven crude prices widen the gap over uninspiring Henry Hub economics.

US drillers added oil rigs for a second consecutive week on Friday, with the Baker Hughes weekly tally rising 10 to 425 — the largest single-week jump since April 2022 — while natural gas rigs slipped three to 125, sharpening the most visible divide in American energy drilling.

The split reflects a straightforward calculation: crude prices are elevated and gas prices are not. West Texas Intermediate traded around $97 a barrel on Friday, kept high by the geopolitical risk premium attached to ongoing Strait of Hormuz disruptions following the US-Iran conflict that flared earlier this spring. Henry Hub natural gas, by contrast, sat at roughly $3.02 per million BTUs — modestly improved from the sub-$3 prints that persisted through much of mid-May, but still well below the levels that historically unlock aggressive gas-directed drilling programs. The oil count came in nine units above the analyst consensus of 416, a beat that underlines how strongly the crude forward curve is pulling capital.

The Permian Basin and Eagle Ford Shale led the oil-directed acceleration, according to Baker Hughes data. Both formations have been the persistent engines of US crude growth, and the fresh rig additions suggest operators are beginning to convert drilled-but-uncompleted inventories as the price environment justifies incremental capital. Modern rigs carry materially greater output capacity than their predecessors — laterals running to 15,000 feet against the 5,000-foot historical standard — so even 425 rigs carries more production punch than the same number would have four years ago.

When gas economics don't pencil

On the gas side, the Haynesville and Marcellus — the two formations that dominate domestic supply growth — each dropped rigs during the period. The Haynesville's retreat matters in particular: it is the marginal swing supplier for LNG feedgas, so sustained softness there will eventually surface in export capacity assumptions.

The arithmetic is not subtle. US gas storage is running above the five-year seasonal average. The EIA's 2026 annual Henry Hub price forecast sits at $3.50/MMBtu — not catastrophic, but not the number that unlocks major new drilling commitments. Producers are not waiting for further confirmation; the rig count message is that capital is being rationed toward crude.

A necessary qualifier: the absolute moves are small. Ten additional oil rigs and three fewer gas rigs across a fleet of roughly 550 total active US units are single-digit percentage shifts. What makes them meaningful is their consistency — two consecutive weeks of oil-directed gains against a multi-week gas-rig retreat — and their direction, which maps cleanly onto the relative price signals operators are seeing.

Rig counts lead production by roughly six to twelve months, the time from deployment to first barrel. Friday's data, taken at face value, suggests US crude output should firm modestly into late 2026 and through 2027, provided the Hormuz risk premium keeps WTI supported. The next significant test comes Wednesday with the EIA's weekly US oil production report; the IEA's June Oil Market Report, due mid-month, will offer the top-down supply-balance view of whether the world can absorb the barrels these additional rigs will eventually deliver.
