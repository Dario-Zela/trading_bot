UK housebuilders rally as Planning Reform Bill passage opens the path to faster development

New-build housing under construction at Southwater, West Sussex — the type of site whose consent clock the reform is designed to compress — Acabashi / Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 4.0
In one sentence
Barratt Redrow, Taylor Wimpey and Persimmon shares rise as a third planning reform commencement order activates consent shortcuts—with output gains not expected before 2027.

The third commencement order under the Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025 landed on Thursday, activating provisions that materially compress the consent timeline for new residential development and bringing the Act's housebuilding framework substantially into operative law. Shares in the sector moved higher: Barratt Redrow, Taylor Wimpey and Persimmon all advanced as investors began pricing what analysts describe — almost uniformly — as a 2027 inflection point for output volumes.

Statutory Instrument 2026/549 brings into force the Nature Restoration Fund, the mechanism that allows developers to offset biodiversity obligations across a portfolio rather than negotiate them site by site. It also activates reforms to pre-application consultation requirements that the government estimates will cut average project timetables by up to twelve months, and commences the default-approval rule for housing within roughly 800 metres of a rail station served by at least two trains per hour — one of the Act's more commercially significant measures. Taken together, Thursday's order represents the most substantive tranche of planning deregulation to reach operative status since Royal Assent on 18 December 2025.

Output in 2027, not today

The market is buying a forward option. When Persimmon reported 2024 full-year results, management was explicit: the true benefit from planning reforms would come towards the end of 2026 and into 2027. The OBR's March 2025 Spring Statement forecast put macro numbers on it — an additional 170,000 homes by 2029-30, lifting annual completions to roughly 305,000, a four-decade high, and adding 0.2 percentage points to GDP. Ministers have separately claimed the reforms will inject £7.5 billion into the economy over the next decade.

Goldman Sachs, which initiated coverage of the sector earlier this year, projected 7% per annum volume growth for Barratt Redrow through FY26-30 against a 5% peer average — a premium attributed to the merged group's land-bank depth and post-Redrow cost synergies. UBS has maintained price targets implying 30-38% upside across the three names; Thursday's commencement narrows the residual risk that those targets depend on policy that never reaches operational status.

The execution risks, however, are structural. Local planning authority capacity has not kept pace with the legislative framework: just 1,410 sites with more than ten plots received approval between April and June 2025, the tenth successive quarterly decline. A thinner consent pipeline feeds thinner 2027 starts, which is the gap between the OBR's central scenario and the more conservative Savills forecast, which sees completions rising only modestly from 2026-27 and constrained by buyer affordability rather than supply law.

Mortgage rates have eased from 4.65% at the start of 2025 to around 4.2% now, and economists have pencilled in further Bank of England cuts through the year. But the MPC's rate-cut path has narrowed materially in recent weeks as wage settlements hold above 3.5% and PMI data stays resilient — precisely the conditions that argue for a longer pause. The April retail-sales data, due shortly, will be the first hard read on household confidence under continuing mortgage pressure, and will tell the market how much demand-side momentum exists to absorb the supply that the reform framework is now legally empowered to unlock.
