EU AI Act compliance crunch: ten weeks until the high-risk system deadline as guidance sinks in

The Berlaymont building in Brussels, seat of the European Commission, which published draft high-risk AI classification guidelines on 19 May 2026 — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Draft EU AI Act classification guidelines published on 19 May give financial firms their clearest map yet — but with ten weeks to August 2 and assessments taking up to six months, most are already behind.

Four days ago, the European Commission published draft guidelines on classifying high-risk AI systems under the AI Act. For compliance teams at European banks, insurers and fintech lenders, the arrival of practical classification examples — finally — would normally be welcome. The problem is the date: August 2, the hard enforcement deadline for high-risk AI system obligations, is ten weeks away.

Conformity assessments — the core certification exercise required before a high-risk system can legally remain in operation — take between three and six months to complete. The arithmetic is unambiguous.

The Commission's May 19 guidelines address who is in scope. Under Annex III of the Act, high-risk designation attaches to credit-scoring and creditworthiness tools, risk-assessment and pricing systems used in life and health insurance, and AI deployed for fraud detection and AML transaction monitoring. Once classified as high-risk, providers and deployers must implement a documented risk-management process, data governance procedures, automated event logging, human-oversight mechanisms and complete a full conformity assessment — then register the system in the EU AI database before deployment continues legally. Both the vendor building the tool and the institution running it carry obligations; deployers cannot outsource compliance to the supplier.

The guidelines arrived late. The Commission had targeted February for publication and missed by three months. The first harmonised technical standard relevant to the Act entered public enquiry in October 2025 — eight months behind schedule — compressing the implementation runway still further. Only eight of the EU's 27 member states have formally designated the national AI supervisory contact points the Act requires; 19 remain undesignated.

The Omnibus complication

A provisional political agreement reached on May 7, under the so-called Digital Omnibus package, would push the Annex III deadline back 16 months — to December 2, 2027. For financial services firms with credit-scoring and AML systems in scope, that looks like useful breathing room. In practice it is not yet law: the deal still requires formal endorsement by both the Council and the European Parliament, a process running into weeks at minimum.

Major law firms advising on compliance have been consistent: treat August 2 as the operative deadline. Firms that wait for the formal extension — if it passes — will still need three to six months for conformity assessments regardless. The extension buys time for quality; it is not grounds for inaction.

Enterprise readiness data supports the concern. More than half of organisations subject to the Act lack a systematic AI inventory — the foundational step without which no conformity assessment can begin. Around 40 per cent of deployed AI systems cannot be clearly classified under the Act's risk tiers. Initial compliance costs for large institutions range from $8 million to $15 million, with annual running costs on top. Non-compliance carries penalties of up to €15 million or 3 per cent of global annual turnover — whichever is higher.

The next concrete marker is June 23, when the Commission's public consultation on the classification guidelines closes. After that, the speed of formal legislative adoption for the Digital Omnibus deal will determine whether August 2 remains operative or quietly gives way to December 2027. Firms treating that question as spectator sport are likely to find the answer arrives faster than their remediation timeline.
