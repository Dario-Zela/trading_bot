UK April retail sales: first hard read on household sentiment under mortgage pressure

Inside a Tesco superstore in Sheringham, Norfolk — UK food retail held relatively stable in April, but clothing and fuel dragged the headline lower. — Wikimedia Commons / Geograph
In one sentence
UK retail sales fell 1.3% MoM in April — twice the expected decline — as a fuel-price reversal and softening clothing demand left YoY growth flat, deepening the BoE's stagflation bind ahead of the June 18 MPC meeting.

The Office for National Statistics reported on Friday that UK retail sales volumes fell 1.3% in April, roughly double the consensus forecast of –0.6% and the sharpest monthly decline since May 2025. Year-on-year growth came in at 0.0% against expectations of a 1.3% rise — a miss that reinforced concerns about the condition of the UK consumer heading into summer.

The headline number was distorted — though not flattered — by a collapse in fuel purchases. Automotive fuel sales dropped 10.2% on the month, the steepest fall since November 2020, as motorists who had filled up aggressively in March, when the Iran conflict first stoked fears of sustained oil disruption, sharply pulled back. Strip out fuel and the picture is still soft: sales excluding automotive fuel fell 0.4%.

Clothing stores bore the brunt of the underlying weakness, declining 2.4% month-on-month to their lowest volume since June 2025. Retailers attributed the fall to variable weather, weakening footfall, and rising consumer price sensitivity. Non-food stores fell 1.0% as a category. Online retail, which had shown resilience in the three-month trend — values up 2.2% against January — also slipped 2.3% on the month, though annual online growth remains solid at 6.6%.

The three-month picture provides a degree of cover: volumes in the three months to April ran 0.5% ahead of the three months to January, and 1.1% above the same period a year earlier. That context matters for the Bank of England, but it does not resolve the immediate question.

The BoE calculus gets harder

The data lands in an unusually uncomfortable position for the Monetary Policy Committee. Bank Rate has been held at 3.75% since the April 30 decision — a meeting at which one member voted for a 25bp increase rather than a hold — and CPI stands at 3.3%. Governor Andrew Bailey has said the path forward "depends on the size and duration of the energy price shock," a formulation that keeps all options open while delivering nothing the market can trade.

April's retail figures show demand deteriorating precisely as inflation remains elevated. That combination argues neither cleanly for cuts nor for hikes. Ruth Gregory of Capital Economics noted that the numbers, alongside a public borrowing overshoot, "highlight the deteriorating growth outlook and fragile fiscal backdrop." The borrowing data published alongside Friday's retail figures showed April's deficit at £24.3bn — the worst start to a fiscal year since 2020 — with debt interest payments reaching a record £10.3bn for the month.

Sterling dipped to around 1.3420 against the dollar in early European trading after the release, a measured move consistent with a data point that tightens rather than resolves the MPC's dilemma. Markets are now pricing two to three rate increases over 2026 — a sharp reversal from expectations of two cuts at the start of the year. The 10-year gilt was trading near 4.93% at the open.

The next hard test is the June 18 MPC meeting. Any shift in Bailey's forward guidance will land against a backdrop of weak consumer volumes, a fiscal position that narrows the government's room to respond, and an energy shock whose duration remains genuinely unclear.
