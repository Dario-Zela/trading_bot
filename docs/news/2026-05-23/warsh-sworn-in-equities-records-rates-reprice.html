Warsh sworn in as Fed chair; Wall Street hits records as global rate outlook reprices

The Marriner S. Eccles Federal Reserve Board Building in Washington, DC — headquarters of the institution Kevin Warsh now leads — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Dow and S&P 500 hit record closes on Friday as Kevin Warsh was sworn in as Fed chair, while bond markets worldwide pushed yields sharply higher on his reform-hawkish signals.

US equity markets closed at fresh all-time highs on Friday as Kevin Warsh was formally sworn in as the 17th chairman of the Federal Reserve — the first such ceremony held at the White House since Alan Greenspan took the oath in 1987. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 294 points to close at a record 50,707, while the S&P 500 added 0.65% to settle at 7,494, extending its winning streak to eight consecutive weeks, the longest run since late 2023. Bond markets told a different story: gilt and Bund yields pushed sharply higher after Warsh's remarks, as traders recalibrated expectations for the Bank of England and the European Central Bank along with those for the Fed itself.

A Ceremony Forty Years in the Making

The East Room setting was not incidental. No incoming Fed chair has been sworn in at the White House since Greenspan's arrival under Reagan, and the choice of venue underscored the unusual proximity between this administration and its preferred central banker. Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas delivered the oath. President Trump — who publicly insisted he wants Warsh to act independently while simultaneously demanding lower rates — looked on.

Warsh, confirmed by the Senate in a near-party-line vote on May 13, formally assumed the role as Jerome Powell's four-year term expired on May 15. His East Room remarks were measured and institutional in tone, but the substance was clear: "I will lead a reform-oriented Federal Reserve, learning from past successes and mistakes, both escaping static frameworks and models and upholding clear standards of integrity and performance."

The phrase "reform-oriented" carried the session. Warsh has signalled that his priorities include shrinking the Fed's balance sheet, overhauling its communications architecture, and abandoning what he views as overly mechanical forecasting models. He was explicit that the balance sheet reduction would be gradual — decades to build, years to unwind — but explicit nonetheless that it was coming. What he conspicuously avoided was any reassurance that rates were headed lower, and that omission drove a material wedge between equity markets and the bond market by late afternoon.

Wall Street's Eighth Week

The S&P 500's eight-week streak encompasses a roughly 12% recovery from the lows of early April, when tariff anxiety was at its peak. The durability of that move is not reducible to a single thesis. Nvidia delivered $81.6 billion in quarterly revenue against a $78.9 billion consensus estimate, validating the AI infrastructure spending cycle and lifting semiconductor names across the board. Dell Technologies surged 15% on its own server-side earnings beat. Progress — or at minimum, the credible appearance of progress — in US-Iran diplomatic talks removed a tail risk on oil prices that had been weighing on sentiment through much of May.

Warsh's role in Friday's move was more structural than directional. For months, market participants had operated without certainty about who would run monetary policy for the next four years. That uncertainty is now resolved. Even a hawkish Fed with a known face and a coherent policy framework is preferable, for valuation purposes, to an uncertain one — and the removal of that uncertainty has a measurable risk premium attached to it.

The macro backdrop against which the rally is occurring remains genuinely uncomfortable. April CPI came in at 3.8%. The University of Michigan's consumer sentiment index fell to 44.8 in May, the lowest reading in the survey's nearly 80-year history. Long-run inflation expectations rose to 3.9%. The S&P 500's all-time high is, on any conventional reading, an aggressive judgment about how those numbers resolve.

The Bond Market's Verdict

The rates market arrived at a different judgment. US 30-year Treasury yields breached 5.02% this week, with the 10-year sitting at 4.44%. The more telling moves were in London and Frankfurt: UK 10-year gilt yields climbed to 4.56%, up more than eight basis points on Friday's session alone, at levels not seen since 2008. German Bund yields moved in sympathy, with the 10-year at 3.127%.

The mechanism is straightforward. If the Federal Reserve under Warsh is not easing — and by Friday's close, the CME FedWatch tool placed an 82% probability on at least one rate hike by year-end — then the Bank of England and ECB have less political and market cover to ease themselves. BoE rate-cut timelines are being pushed further out across the swaps curve; ECB futures have repriced similarly. The April FOMC minutes, released earlier this month, showed officials prepared to hike if inflation stays elevated. Warsh's emphasis on "escaping static frameworks" gives no comfort to traders who had been positioned for a resumption of the 2024 easing cycle.

The FTSE 100, which had spent four consecutive weeks in retreat partly on these dynamics, staged a recovery. The index closed at 10,460, up 0.16%, snapping its losing streak on the back of gains in energy and financials: BP added 2.93%, Shell 2.32%, and Centrica 2.53%, as geopolitical de-escalation lifted crude and sector risk appetite more broadly. It was a rotation story — traditional cyclicals, not growth — consistent with a rates-stay-higher environment.

What Comes Next

Warsh's June FOMC meeting is the pivotal near-term date. It is his first as chair, and the decision — hold with hawkish language, hold with dovish language, or move — will do more to define the Warsh era than any ceremony remarks. The May CPI print, due in the second week of June, lands before that meeting and now carries asymmetric importance: a second consecutive reading above 3.5% would make a pause significantly harder to defend, and market-implied hike probabilities would ratchet higher still.

The political dimension will not recede. Trump's appetite for lower borrowing costs is a matter of public record, and Warsh's insistence on independence — stated explicitly at the swearing-in — will be tested by the first decision that diverges from the White House's preferences. That test may come sooner than anyone in the East Room on Friday was inclined to acknowledge.

For now, the S&P 500 sits at a record. The question the next eight weeks must answer is whether the economy that underlies it is growing into that valuation or growing away from it.
