SEC delays tokenised-equity trading on crypto platforms over investor protection concerns

The SEC's headquarters at 100 F Street, NE, Washington, D.C. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
The SEC shelved its innovation exemption for tokenised US equities on crypto platforms indefinitely, citing unresolved gaps in dividend payments, voting rights, and sanctions compliance.

The Securities and Exchange Commission has indefinitely shelved a draft "innovation exemption" that would have cleared the way for crypto-native platforms to offer tokenised versions of publicly listed American equities — a significant setback for the tokenisation-as-bridge narrative that has drawn considerable capital and regulatory attention this year.

SEC staff had been preparing to release the framework as soon as the week of 18 May, but the timeline slipped following a series of meetings with stock-exchange officials and other market participants who raised objections that the agency ultimately could not set aside. No new target date has been announced.

What the proposal would have done

The exemption, championed by SEC Chairman Paul Atkins as a centrepiece of his "Project Crypto" initiative, would have allowed crypto platforms to offer trading in blockchain-based representations of shares in companies such as Apple or Tesla. Crucially — and controversially — the draft envisioned permitting tokens issued by third parties, meaning firms unaffiliated with the underlying company could create and trade token representations without the issuer's knowledge or consent.

Commissioner Hester Peirce, typically among the most crypto-friendly voices at the agency, moved ahead of the delay to dampen market expectations, saying the exemption would be "limited in scope" and cover only "digital representations of the same underlying equity security that an investor could purchase in the secondary market today, not synthetics." She also expressed frustration at what she called the "hyperbole" surrounding the proposal.

Where the pushback came from

Traditional exchange officials were among the first to object, warning of diluted investor protections and potential competitive distortions. Former regulators added operational specifics: if tokenised shares change hands across pseudonymous blockchain wallets, how does a company pay a dividend or register a vote?

Austin Campbell, a professor at NYU Stern, was direct. "You can't pay a dividend when you don't know who owns the token," he said. Amanda Fischer of Better Markets, a former SEC official, put it in corporate terms: "If I was a corporate executive, I'd be very concerned about the implications." Joe Saluzzi of Themis Trading offered perhaps the bluntest assessment of the demand question: "Nobody is asking for this."

Compliance risks compounded the structural objections. Crypto platforms with weaker know-your-customer standards could, critics argued, give sanctioned entities equity exposure that existing market infrastructure would block — a problem that regulators focused on market integrity found difficult to wave away.

Markets registered the shift quickly. Coinbase shares fell roughly 4.4% on 22 May when the delay became public; bitcoin also declined.

What to watch

The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act, advancing in the Senate, provides some legislative scaffolding for the broader crypto-regulation agenda, but the tokenised-equity piece now sits separately. The question is whether the SEC re-engages with a narrower draft — one that excises the third-party token provision that drew the most fire — or lets the proposal expire quietly as the agency works through its wider rulemaking docket.

For trading strategies that assumed tokenisation as a near-term bridge between crypto and conventional market infrastructure, this delay is a recalibration. The appetite to build that bridge has not disappeared; the regulatory permit has simply been withdrawn.
