BP's Clair field stake surfaces as a candidate in its $20bn divestment push

The Clair Ridge platform during fabrication, 75 kilometres west of Shetland — BP holds a 45.1% operating stake in the wider Clair development. — Grahame C Anderson / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
BP's $20bn divestment push has reached the Clair field — its 45% stake in the UKCS's largest producer — as the 78% North Sea tax rate sharpens the capital calculus.

BP's determination to raise $20 billion through asset sales by the end of 2027 has reached its most sensitive question yet: whether the company will sell its stake in Clair, the largest producing field on the UK Continental Shelf.

Analysts and industry sources have flagged the 45.1% operating stake as a candidate for BP's emerging North Sea divestment phase, which follows a string of disposals elsewhere — most notably the $6 billion majority sale of its Castrol lubricants business last year. A full exit from the company's UK upstream portfolio, which includes the Eastern Trough Area Project hub alongside Clair, could fetch around £2 billion, according to figures cited by Bloomberg. Clair alone is the centrepiece: a field discovered in 1977, sitting 75 kilometres west of Shetland, with an estimated eight billion barrels of oil-in-place and plateau production capacity of around 120,000 barrels per day across its two operational phases.

The 78% problem

The fiscal arithmetic is stark. The UK's Energy Profits Levy, extended through March 2030 under the Labour government's November 2025 North Sea Future Plan, combines with ring-fence corporation tax and a supplementary charge to produce an effective rate of 78% on North Sea profits — among the highest imposed by any OECD country on its domestic hydrocarbon sector. North Sea production has already fallen from 1.1 million barrels per day in 2020 to roughly 474,000 bpd by late 2025. Official forecasts suggest North Sea tax receipts could collapse 93% to just £0.3 billion annually by 2030 if the current trajectory holds.

For BP's new chief executive Meg O'Neill — who took over in April as the first external appointment to the role — the North Sea review fits a consistent framework. "What are the assets that are with us for the long term," O'Neill said shortly after taking the job, "and what are things that might be of greater value in someone else's hands?" The question, applied to Clair, implies a threshold: BP must decide whether a 45% stake in one of the UK's most consequential energy assets is a strategic anchor or a capital allocation problem dressed up as geography.

BP has not confirmed any formal sale process, and Bloomberg noted the review "may not materialise" into a transaction. That caveat has not cooled interest in the M&A implications. The buyer universe is complicated: Shell and Equinor are consolidating their own UK North Sea positions into a joint venture, and Chevron, which holds a 19.4% stake in Clair itself, has been trimming rather than adding UK exposure. A private equity buyer, a national oil company, or a specialist North Sea independent would each carry different regulatory and strategic implications for a field the UK government regards as critical infrastructure.

The next material signal will be whether BP formally mandates an adviser on North Sea assets — a step that would shift the review from contingency planning into live deal territory. BP's Q2 results, due in late July, will also be watched for any language tightening around the divestment timeline.
