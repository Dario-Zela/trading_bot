Treasury issues 30-day licence for vulnerable nations to access stranded Russian oil

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent made the announcement via X on May 18, 2026 — U.S. Treasury Department / Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Treasury has issued its third 30-day sanctions waiver allowing vulnerable nations to buy stranded Russian seaborne oil, as the Hormuz closure drains global stockpiles.

The U.S. Treasury Department has issued a third consecutive 30-day general licence allowing vulnerable nations to purchase Russian crude oil currently stranded at sea — a quiet but telling concession to the supply emergency created by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Secretary Scott Bessent announced the measure on X on May 18, hours after the previous waiver lapsed on Saturday. The licence permits qualifying countries to buy Russian seaborne oil that would otherwise expose them to U.S. sanctions on Russia's energy sector. Bessent framed the purpose plainly: to "stabilise the physical crude market and ensure oil reaches the most energy-vulnerable countries."

Why stranded oil, and why now

The backstory is important. When the Iran war forced effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil previously flowed — tankers carrying Russian crude suddenly became a more valuable resource than their original buyers could handle. Nations that had relied on Gulf shipments found themselves scrambling for alternatives. At the same time, the existing U.S. sanctions architecture left those same nations exposed to penalties for transacting with Russia's major oil producers.

The first waiver came in March. A second followed in April. This one — the third in as many months — suggests the underlying emergency is not resolving.

IEA head Fatih Birol has called the current moment the "biggest energy security threat in history." The agency's own data shows commercial inventories are "depleting very fast"; global stockpiles fell 117 million barrels in April, following a 129-million-barrel drawdown in March. Brent has been trading above $111 a barrel. U.S. pump prices are averaging over $4.50 a gallon.

A secondary calculation

There is also a strategic undercurrent. Bessent was explicit that the licence is designed to limit China's capacity to stockpile discounted Russian oil. By enabling energy-vulnerable nations to access that stranded supply, Washington hopes to redirect crude away from Chinese reserves and toward countries with more acute need. Saudi Aramco CEO Amin Nasser has separately warned of a potential "multi-year" disruption if Hormuz shipping remains blocked.

India — one of the largest buyers of Russian oil — offered a measured response, with its Joint Secretary for Petroleum saying purchase decisions are made on "commercial sense" and guided by the interests of its people. The comment signals that some of the largest buyers will continue acting in their own interest regardless of Washington's framing.

The critics

Not everyone is persuaded by the humanitarian rationale. Democratic Senators Jeanne Shaheen and Elizabeth Warren argued the waivers were "lining Putin's coffers." Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy has been more direct: "Every dollar paid for Russian oil is money for the war."

The political tension runs deeper given Bessent's own position. He was in Paris for a G7 finance ministers' meeting when the announcement dropped — and while there, called on allies to enforce Iran sanctions more strictly to cut off "illicit finance that is fueling the Iranian war machine." Making that argument while simultaneously extending Russian oil access to a third monthly cycle is not a contradiction Treasury has yet resolved publicly.

What to watch

The current licence expires in mid-June. If Hormuz shipping remains blocked and inventories keep falling at the pace recorded in March and April, a fourth extension becomes the path of least resistance. The harder question — whether rolling monthly waivers quietly normalise a commercial relationship the original sanctions were designed to prevent — is one neither Treasury nor its critics have fully engaged.
