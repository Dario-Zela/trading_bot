SEC drafting framework for blockchain-based stock derivatives

SEC headquarters at 100 F Street NE, Washington, D.C. — AgnosticPreachersKid / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
The SEC is finalizing an 'innovation exemption' letting platforms trade tokenized stocks under lighter rules — a framework signal, not yet binding policy.

The Securities and Exchange Commission is preparing what it calls an "innovation exemption" — a conditional regulatory pathway that would let qualified platforms issue and trade tokenized versions of publicly listed stocks on blockchain networks, according to a Bloomberg report published Sunday and confirmed by subsequent reporting.

The framework, expected to be released imminently under SEC Chair Paul Atkins, would allow firms to offer tokenized equities under lighter registration requirements for an initial period of 12 to 36 months. After that window, issuers would either need to demonstrate "sufficient decentralization" of their platform or transition into standard securities regulation. Eligible assets under the proposal would include equities, bonds, and other real-world assets.

The catch — and it matters — is that stock tokens issued under this framework would not carry traditional shareholder rights. No voting. No direct dividend entitlement. The digital instrument represents a economic claim, not a share certificate. The SEC's January 2026 guidance had already underscored that tokenizing a security does not alter its classification under federal law; the innovation exemption carves out operating room without changing that principle.

More than a press release, less than a rule

Atkins, speaking at the Washington Economic Club on the first anniversary of his chairmanship, outlined what he called an "A-C-T" strategy: advance, clarify, transform. His stated aim is to help market participants place crypto assets into clear regulatory categories "instead of leaving them to guess" after issuance. The SEC has also signed a memorandum of understanding with the CFTC committing both agencies to harmonized rulemakings for digital assets — a notable structural step, even if it produces no binding rule.

What is not yet on the table: formal rulemaking, an acceptance of applications from platforms, or a defined compliance deadline for existing tokenized products operating outside U.S. regulatory perimeters.

Infrastructure is ahead of the rules

Wall Street's plumbing has been moving faster than regulators. The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation — the backbone of U.S. equity settlement — plans to begin limited production trades of tokenized securities in July, with a broader platform launch set for October. That timeline predates any formal SEC rulemaking and is built on the SEC's March approval of Nasdaq's tokenized securities pilot, in which tokenized shares trade alongside conventional equity with the same tickers and prices but settle on a blockchain ledger.

Intercontinental Exchange has formed a strategic alliance with crypto exchange OKX for crypto-asset derivatives, another indication that exchange infrastructure is positioning ahead of the regulatory green light rather than waiting for it.

What the exemption resolves — and what it doesn't

Critics, primarily traditional financial institutions, have flagged two concerns: competitive disadvantage relative to crypto-native platforms operating under lighter rules, and the risk that weaker anti-money laundering and custody standards could migrate into equity markets. The framework's guardrails — exposure limits, disclosure requirements, a finite grace period — are designed to answer those objections, though the specifics remain to be published.

For traders and investors, the directional signal is clear enough: the current administration has decided that tokenized securities belong inside the regulatory perimeter rather than outside it, and is designing the fence accordingly. Whether the exemption, once released, has the legal durability to survive the next administration or a court challenge is a separate question the exemption itself cannot answer.

The next concrete milestone is the publication of the exemption text itself. After that, DTCC's July pilot will be the first real-world stress test of whether blockchain settlement infrastructure performs as advertised at production scale.
