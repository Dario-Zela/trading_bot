U.S. denies Iranian report of oil sanctions deal during talks

Kharg Island, Iran's primary oil export terminal in the Persian Gulf, handles roughly 90% of Iranian crude exports — the flows at the center of the sanctions dispute. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Iran's Tasnim agency claimed the U.S. agreed to waive oil sanctions during talks; a U.S. official told CNBC's Megan Cassella the report was false, reversing a crude rally.

Iran's semi-official Tasnim News Agency touched off a brief but sharp reversal in crude markets on Monday after claiming that Washington had agreed to suspend oil sanctions against Tehran during ongoing peace negotiations — a claim a U.S. official promptly called false.

Tasnim cited an unnamed source close to Iran's negotiating team, reporting that "the United States has accepted in its new proposal to waive Iran's oil sanctions during the negotiation period." The agency was careful to frame it as a temporary concession rather than a full rollback, explaining that a waiver would remain in place "until a final understanding is reached" — as distinct from the complete sanctions removal Iran has publicly demanded.

The report did not hold up. A U.S. official, speaking to CNBC correspondent Megan Cassella, flatly denied it: the report was false, and no sanctions relief of any kind had been agreed to by either side.

What the markets did

The episode illustrated, in miniature, how fragile oil market sentiment has become under the weight of the Iran conflict. Brent crude had already climbed 2.6% to $112.10 a barrel — a two-week high — on supply-disruption fears before the Tasnim report circulated. After the U.S. denial, Brent reversed sharply, dropping 1.4% to $107.78. West Texas Intermediate followed the same arc: up $3.24 to $108.66 before falling 1.8% to $103.52.

The swing reflected something beyond a single false headline. Since the Iran war began, benchmark prices have ranged from a high of $144 a barrel to below $100, with every ceasefire rumor, every sanctions headline, and every Strait of Hormuz update registering within minutes on the front-month contract.

Why sanctions relief matters so much to traders

Iran holds the world's second-largest proven oil reserves and, before the current conflict, exported roughly 1.5 million barrels per day. U.S. sanctions — reinstituted under the Trump administration's "maximum pressure" policy in February 2025 and tightened repeatedly since — have cut legal export channels to near zero. Any credible pathway to resumed Iranian supply would represent a meaningful addition to a market the International Energy Agency says is depleting inventories at a record pace, with only weeks of commercial buffer remaining even as strategic reserves contribute roughly 2.5 million barrels per day.

That arithmetic is what makes a single Tasnim item — sourced to an unnamed figure and never confirmed by Washington — capable of moving prices by more than $4 a barrel in the span of a trading session.

The gap between Iranian and U.S. positions

The Tasnim framing also exposed how wide the two sides remain on sanctions. Tehran has consistently demanded complete removal of oil sanctions as part of any final agreement, not a temporary waiver tied to the duration of talks. The Tasnim source appeared to be floating the waiver concept as a U.S. concession — a step toward Iran's core demand. Washington denies even that partial step has been offered.

Pakistan has been acting as intermediary, physically ferrying proposals between delegations in separate rooms. The framework is close-hold enough that unverified leaks through state-adjacent media — Tasnim is semi-official — carry outsized weight simply because confirmed information is scarce.

Trump, meanwhile, warned the same day that Iran's time to reach a deal was running out, keeping military pressure as visible backdrop to the diplomatic track.

What to watch

The next substantive test will be whether Pakistan can bring a revised U.S. counterproposal to Tehran that moves meaningfully on the sanctions question — or whether Monday's denial signals Washington is holding its sanctions leverage for a final agreement rather than using it as a negotiating lubricant midway through talks. Any credible movement on that front will hit crude markets faster than almost any other headline from this conflict.
