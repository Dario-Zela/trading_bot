Citigroup nears approval for wholly-owned China securities unit

Citigroup CEO Jane Fraser, who joined President Trump's China delegation last week and met with CSRC chairman Wu Qing ahead of the approval — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
CSRC removes Citi's application from its pending list, signalling final approval for a wholly-owned mainland securities firm after a 4-year process.

China's securities regulator has removed Citigroup's application for a wholly-owned mainland brokerage from its list of pending approvals — a procedural step that effectively marks the end of a regulatory review process that stretched across more than four years.

The entity in question, Citigroup Securities (China) Co. Ltd., filed its initial application with the China Securities Regulatory Commission in December 2021. The CSRC didn't formally accept it for review until December 2023, and issued a feedback round in February 2024. With the application now off the pending list, Citi is expected to receive a formal operating licence authorising brokerage, underwriting, consulting, and sponsorship services on the mainland.

Seventh in a short queue

Citigroup will be the seventh foreign institution to hold a wholly-owned Chinese securities operation, joining a list that includes JPMorgan Chase — which received its approval in August 2020 — Goldman Sachs, UBS, Standard Chartered, BNP Paribas, and Mizuho. The pace of approvals has been slow by design: Beijing has opened this door selectively, using each licence as a signal rather than a flood.

For Citi specifically, the approval marks a structural shift. The bank previously held a 33.3% minority stake in a joint venture with Orient Securities, formed in 2012 and exited in 2019 after Citi failed to secure a controlling interest. A wholly-owned platform means it can now underwrite domestic equity and bond offerings, clear trades, and pitch Chinese issuers directly — without sharing fees or strategic control with a local partner.

The Fraser factor

The timing is hard to ignore. Citigroup CEO Jane Fraser travelled to China as part of President Trump's business delegation last week, one of 17 chief executives accompanying the president ahead of his summit with Xi Jinping. Fraser was among the handful of executives who met directly with CSRC chairman Wu Qing — the same regulator whose office issued this approval. Those discussions reportedly covered wealth management and cross-border financing cooperation.

Whether the delegation visit accelerated a decision already in motion or merely coincided with it is unclear. The CSRC's review process had been progressing through standard stages since late 2023, and the regulator had been scrutinising Citi's financials — including its 2022 profit decline and 2023 recovery — as well as questions about a proposed compliance officer's prior ties to the defunct CEFC Shanghai Securities. Those procedural steps suggest the approval was on its own track.

What the trip does underscore is Beijing's current posture toward Western financial institutions. Approvals for wholly-owned foreign brokerages have come in bursts, typically when China is managing external perceptions of its market openness.

What it opens up

The practical significance for Citi is meaningful. The bank divested its China consumer banking and wealth management business to HSBC in 2024, narrowing its mainland footprint to institutional and corporate clients. A fully-owned securities subsidiary extends that institutional presence into capital markets origination — a business where being onshore, with direct regulatory standing, matters for deal access and client relationships.

Citi has separately indicated plans to grow its Asia-Pacific prime brokerage headcount by roughly 10%, and a mainland securities licence would bolster that effort by enabling broader collateral and financing arrangements for institutional clients operating in Chinese markets.

What to watch

The formal licence issuance and entity registration still need to complete. Once operational, the unit's initial deal flow — particularly any lead roles on A-share listings or onshore bond underwriting — will be the first concrete measure of whether the licence translates to market share or remains largely a strategic placeholder. JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs, both further along in building out their wholly-owned mainland operations, will be the competitive benchmark.
