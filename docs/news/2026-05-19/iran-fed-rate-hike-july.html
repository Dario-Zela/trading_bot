Economists warn Iran conflict could force a July rate hike

The Marriner S. Eccles Federal Reserve Board Building in Washington, D.C., where the FOMC meets to set interest rate policy. — AgnosticPreachersKid / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Moody's Zandi warns 'unmoored' inflation could force rate hikes regardless of economic damage; Yardeni predicts a 25bp hike at the July 29 FOMC meeting.

The Federal Reserve's cautious, wait-and-see posture on interest rates is facing a challenge that two well-regarded economists say it cannot ignore: the inflationary arithmetic of a prolonged conflict in Iran.

Mark Zandi, chief economist at Moody's Analytics, and Ed Yardeni, president of Yardeni Research, have each warned this week that the Iran war — now in its twelfth week — is generating supply-side price pressures severe enough to force the Fed's hand. Their forecasts sit well outside current market consensus. CME FedWatch data put the probability of a July rate hike at roughly 4 to 5 percent. Yardeni, for one, puts it near certainty.

The inflation chain

The arithmetic starts with oil. Crude futures have risen approximately 60 percent since the conflict erupted in late February, with the Strait of Hormuz remaining closed to tanker traffic. Gasoline followed — up 5.4 percent in April alone, 28.4 percent over the past year, averaging $4.50 per gallon nationally against $2.98 before the fighting began. Energy costs accounted for more than 40 percent of April's overall CPI increase.

The headline result: the consumer price index climbed 3.8 percent year-over-year in April, its highest reading since May 2023. The monthly pace was 0.6 percent — a rate that, annualized, clears 7 percent.

That is the number alarming Zandi.

"Nothing spooks the Fed more than unmoored inflation expectations,"

he said, warning the central bank would raise rates "regardless of the hit to the broader economy" once price expectations start to drift. On the oil damage, he is direct: "I think the damage has already been done, in part because there's no going back on oil prices, at least not any time in the near future." The pass-through, he has noted, extends well beyond gasoline: "Anything that's put on a truck is going to cost more. That goes from groceries to Amazon packages."

Zandi's base case is that inflation keeps accelerating through the summer before retreating to roughly 3.3 percent by year-end — even if the conflict ends in the next few weeks. On rates, he has moved away from the cuts consensus: he no longer expects the Fed to cut at all in 2026, and has warned that if expectations keep rising, "not only cutting rates will be off the table, but even holding rates where they are is going to be pretty tough."

Yardeni goes further

Yardeni's call is more specific, and accordingly more jarring. He expects the Federal Open Market Committee to drop its easing bias at the June meeting and signal a tightening stance, followed by a 25-basis-point hike at the July 29 meeting. In a note this week, he argued the language change was necessary before conditions deteriorated:

"If the Fed fails to remove [the easing bias], investors will conclude that the central bank is falling behind the inflation curve and will demand a higher inflation risk premium."

The inflation risk premium he is citing is not abstract. Yardeni has invoked the bond vigilantes — market actors who sell Treasuries to force a policy response — as a real and proximate force. The 10-year Treasury yield was last quoted around 4.6 percent; Yardeni projects a peak between 4.75 and 5 percent "in the coming weeks," calling that range a buying opportunity — but only if the Fed acts to contain the pressure first.

Paradoxically, he argues that a hawkish pivot could serve the Trump administration's stated interest in lower real-world borrowing costs:

"By acting hawkishly, Warsh might have a chance of delivering what the White House wants: lower real-world borrowing costs."

The logic is that a credible tightening move, by anchoring inflation expectations, could reduce the term premium embedded in longer-dated yields — achieving through credibility what rate cuts could not.

A new chair inherits the problem

The timing matters. Jerome Powell's term ended May 15. Kevin Warsh — confirmed by the Senate 54-45, the most partisan Fed confirmation in the institution's history — took the chairmanship last week. He inherits a committee reportedly at odds over whether the next move is a hold or a hike.

Warsh has publicly positioned the current inflation surge as transitory: tied to the Hormuz disruption, expected to fade once the conflict ends and structural disinflationary forces reassert themselves. That framing holds if oil prices stabilize. But if the Strait remains closed into summer or second-round inflation effects broaden into services and shelter, the committee's patience will face a concrete test — not a theoretical one.

Consensus is still unmoved

The market does not yet believe either Zandi or Yardeni. FedWatch assigns a 98.8 percent probability of unchanged rates at the June meeting, 94.6 percent for July, and roughly 80 percent odds of no hike as late as September. The spread between those numbers and what two credible economists are forecasting is either a sign of market complacency about a genuine inflation breakout, or an indicator that respected voices are over-weighting a geopolitical shock that markets judge as temporary.

For rate-sensitive assets — rate futures, duration positions, anything priced on a terminal Fed funds path — that gap represents explicit repricing risk. It closes one of two ways: data confirms the inflation pressure is transient and Yardeni's call looks premature, or May CPI comes in hot again and the June FOMC statement signals the shift Yardeni is predicting.

The next major data points are the May CPI reading (due mid-June), the June 17 FOMC meeting and its post-statement language, and — above everything else — whether the Strait of Hormuz remains closed into the summer driving season. If oil holds above $100 per barrel through June, the July 29 meeting will arrive with the policy debate already decided in the bond market, whatever the FOMC's own preferences.
