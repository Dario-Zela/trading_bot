AstraZeneca's FDA wins raise questions on valuation, not just pipeline

AstraZeneca's global headquarters at the Cambridge Biomedical Campus, which houses over 2,200 scientists across 16 laboratories. — FDV / Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 4.0
In one sentence
AstraZeneca's three FDA approvals in a week confirm pipeline execution, but early-stage HER2+ breast cancer is a narrow market — Baxfendy carries the bigger commercial upside.

On May 15, AstraZeneca and its partner Daiichi Sankyo secured two FDA approvals for Enhertu (fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan) in early-stage HER2-positive breast cancer — one for the neoadjuvant setting, one for adjuvant use — followed three days later by the FDA's green light for Baxfendy (baxdrostat), a first-in-class treatment for uncontrolled hypertension. Three approvals in the span of a week is an unusual run. Whether it justifies the company's current valuation is a separate, harder question.

What the approvals actually are

The Enhertu neoadjuvant approval covers patients with Stage II or III HER2-positive breast cancer being treated before surgery. The recommended regimen is Enhertu for four cycles followed by the THP combination — taxane, trastuzumab (Herceptin), and pertuzumab (Perjeta). The data come from the DESTINY-Breast11 Phase III trial, which showed a pathologic complete response rate of 67.3% with the Enhertu-containing regimen versus 56.3% with the standard dose-dense anthracycline/taxane approach — an 11.2 percentage point improvement that reached statistical significance.

The adjuvant approval is for patients who have already gone through neoadjuvant therapy with a trastuzumab-based regimen and taxane and still have residual invasive disease in the breast or lymph nodes at surgery — the group that, until recently, would have received Roche's Kadcyla (trastuzumab emtansine, or T-DM1). The DESTINY-Breast05 trial, which enrolled 1,635 patients across North America, Europe, Asia, and Oceania, showed Enhertu cut the risk of invasive disease recurrence or death by 53% compared with Kadcyla. The three-year invasive disease-free survival rate was 92.4% for Enhertu versus 83.7% for T-DM1. The adjuvant approval arrived nearly two months ahead of schedule, with the original PDUFA date set for July 7.

There is a clinically important limitation in the label: patients who receive Enhertu as neoadjuvant treatment cannot continue it in the adjuvant setting under the approved prescribing information. This restriction narrows the addressable population and complicates sequencing decisions for oncologists — a wrinkle that contrasts with some of the earlier, broader descriptions of the drug's potential post-neoadjuvant role.

Baxfendy is a different kind of win. The drug is the first aldosterone synthase inhibitor to receive FDA approval anywhere, and the mechanism is genuinely novel: rather than blocking aldosterone at the receptor level, as spironolactone and eplerenone do, Baxfendy inhibits the enzyme (aldosterone synthase, also called CYP11B2) that produces aldosterone in the first place. In the Phase III BaxHTN trial — 796 patients already on multiple antihypertensives, including a diuretic — Baxfendy 2mg once daily produced a 15.7 mmHg reduction in seated systolic blood pressure from baseline, a placebo-adjusted reduction of 9.8 mmHg (p<0.001). AstraZeneca acquired the drug's developer, CinCor Pharma, in 2023 for $1.3 billion upfront — a 121% premium to CinCor's unaffected share price at the time.

The valuation question, carefully framed

For investors watching AstraZeneca's pipeline execution, a week like this one is reassuring. The company's revenue in Q1 2026 came in at $15.29 billion, up 8% year-on-year and ahead of the $14.93 billion analyst consensus. Enhertu itself generated $1.4 billion in combined quarterly sales in Q1 2026, a 31% increase, with Daiichi Sankyo projecting global Enhertu sales of approximately $5.4 billion for its fiscal year ending March 2027. Jefferies has estimated Enhertu's peak sales potential at $10 to $12 billion across all indications.

But there are grounds for caution on the margin, particularly around whether the early-stage oncology approvals will move the needle as much as their headlines suggest.

HER2-positive breast cancer accounts for roughly 15 to 20 percent of all breast cancer diagnoses. The early-stage setting is more constrained than the metastatic one: a meaningful fraction of early-stage patients achieve pathologic complete response with existing regimens and are effectively cured, limiting the pool for post-neoadjuvant adjuvant therapy. For the adjuvant use, only patients with residual disease after neoadjuvant treatment qualify under the current label. That is a subset of a subset. The DESTINY-Breast05 data are compelling in relative risk-reduction terms — 53% is a large hazard ratio improvement — but the absolute numbers still trace a relatively narrow treatment population compared to, say, a first-line metastatic approval in an unselected or HER2-low population.

The competitive terrain is not empty. Roche's Kadcyla held the adjuvant setting for years, and while Enhertu's superiority data are hard to argue with clinically, Kadcyla's U.S. patent expires in September 2026. The arrival of biosimilar T-DM1 will complicate the pricing environment in the adjuvant space even as Enhertu moves in. Guideline incorporation, payer negotiations, and physician habit all carry weight in practice — and early-stage breast cancer treatment is typically delivered by community oncologists who are slower to adopt newer agents than academic centers.

The stock has slipped roughly 9% over the past month, trading at around £137 per share against an analyst consensus price target of approximately £165 — implying, on consensus figures, about 17% upside. Simply Wall St's fair value model suggests a larger discount, estimating the stock trades some 42% below intrinsic value. The trailing price-to-earnings ratio sits near 27.8, the forward near 18, broadly in line with large-cap pharma with demonstrated oncology and cardiovascular pipeline depth. The market, in other words, is not obviously expensive — but it is also not obviously cheap given the uncertainty about how quickly the new approvals convert to revenue.

Baxfendy is arguably the more interesting near-term catalyst. AstraZeneca CEO Pascal Soriot has publicly described it as "definitely a big product," with internal projections of $5 billion or more in annual peak sales in hypertension alone and up to $10 billion if the drug succeeds in additional studies — including a chronic kidney disease programme. Jefferies analyst Dennis Ding has noted that commercial execution, not clinical data, will be the differentiating factor now that Baxfendy is approved, where AstraZeneca's large cardiovascular sales force provides a structural advantage. Mineralys Therapeutics is advancing a competing aldosterone synthase inhibitor, but Baxfendy holds first-mover status.

The broader picture

AstraZeneca has several simultaneous growth vectors in oncology that provide some insulation against the execution risks in any single segment. The company is building a broader breast cancer franchise that includes Datroway (a HER2-targeting ADC in development), camizestrant (an oral SERD targeting HR-positive, HER2-negative disease, with Phase III data showing a 56% progression-free survival improvement), and continued label expansion for Enhertu in HER2-low and HER2-ultralow disease, where the drug has already captured approximately 55 to 60% market share since its 2022 approval. Milestone payments of $155 million were triggered to Daiichi Sankyo on the back of the two Enhertu approvals this week, consistent with the commercial-sharing arrangements baked into the 2019 collaboration.

What to watch now: adoption data will matter more than the approval headlines. The neoadjuvant indication requires convincing oncologists to swap out an established anthracycline-containing regimen for an antibody-drug conjugate approach in Stage II and III patients — a significant change in practice with attendant safety monitoring requirements, including the boxed warning for interstitial lung disease and pneumonitis that appears on all Enhertu prescribing information. For the adjuvant indication, the transition away from Kadcyla toward Enhertu is more data-driven, but will face price competition as T-DM1 biosimilars enter the market.

The next meaningful data readouts to watch involve Enhertu's ongoing studies in HER2-low early breast cancer and the broader cardiorenal programme for Baxfendy. Quarterly sales figures from mid-2026 onward will offer the first real signal of how quickly physicians are incorporating the new indications into practice. At the current valuation, investors are essentially being asked to believe that the pipeline continues to execute — and that peak sales projections across Enhertu and Baxfendy are achievable, not just plausible. The approvals this week are evidence in favour of that view. They are not, in themselves, proof of it.
