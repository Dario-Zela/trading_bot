Shell launches $3bn buyback alongside 3.5% yield

Shell Centre, Shell's global headquarters on the South Bank of the Thames in London. — Marek Ślusarczyk / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 3.0)
In one sentence
Shell's $3bn buyback and 3.5% yield headline strong Q1 earnings, but a $3.5bn→$3bn cut and rising net debt tell a more complicated story.

Shell kicked off the second quarter by announcing a $3 billion share buyback programme on May 7, to be completed before its Q2 results land in late July. Add a 5% dividend increase — bringing the quarterly payout to $0.3906 per share and the yield to roughly 3.5% — and the headline looks straightforward: a cash-rich major returning money at pace.

The underlying numbers justify the confidence, at least in part. Shell's Q1 2026 adjusted earnings came in at $6.92 billion, roughly 23% above the year-ago figure of $5.58 billion and well ahead of analyst estimates around $6.1 billion. Cash from operations, excluding working capital, hit $17.2 billion — a 44% jump from the prior-year quarter. CEO Wael Sawan called it "strong results enabled by our relentless focus on operational performance in a quarter marked by unprecedented disruption in global energy markets." The disruption in question is the Iran crisis, which pushed Brent oil above $106 a barrel and inflated upstream revenue across the sector.

The reduction nobody is headlining

Bury the lede at your peril: this buyback is smaller than the previous one. Shell ran $3.5 billion in repurchases last quarter; the new programme represents a 14% cut. The reason sits on the balance sheet. Net debt climbed to $52.6 billion from $45.7 billion — a $7 billion rise in a single quarter — driven largely by the acquisition of ARC Resources, a Canadian liquids-and-gas producer that will add roughly 370,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day. Full-year capex guidance is now $24–26 billion, absorbing around $4 billion for ARC alone.

So the capital allocation story is really two stories in parallel. The buyback and dividend signal shareholder returns as a non-negotiable priority — this marks the 17th consecutive quarter Shell has repurchased at least $3 billion of its own stock. But the ARC deal, a pure fossil-fuel play with no renewables angle, tells you where the company sees durable value: in hydrocarbons, for decades to come. Energy transition investment remains a footnote in the quarterly numbers, not the headline.

What traders are watching

At a P/E of around 14.6 and a PEG ratio of 0.76, the stock screens cheap relative to near-term earnings growth — analysts covering SHEL have a consensus "Moderate Buy" with a mean price target of $99.15, roughly 12% above current levels. The 3.5% yield is competitive for an oil major, and with oil sustained above $100 by geopolitical premium, cash generation looks secure for now.

The test comes when that premium deflates. Any resolution to the Iran standoff — or even meaningful de-escalation — would pull oil back toward the $80s, which compresses the cash flow that funds both the buyback and the ARC integration. Q2 results, due in late July, will be the first clean read on whether the debt load is stabilising and whether the buyback pace can be restored to $3.5 billion or higher. Until then, Shell is paying you 3.5% a year to wait.
