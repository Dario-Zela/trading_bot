GE holds the quiet edge in Trump's China aircraft deal

A GE Aerospace GEnx turbofan engine on display at the Paris Air Show, 2013. The GEnx powers roughly 90% of Boeing 787s operated by Chinese carriers. — Tangopaso / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
Trump's China aircraft deal gives Boeing headlines but gives GE Aerospace 400–450 engine contracts plus decades of maintenance revenue regardless of delivery delays.

President Trump returned from Beijing last week with a headline number: China has agreed to buy 200 Boeing aircraft, with an option that could extend the order to 750. It is Boeing's first meaningful Chinese sale in nearly a decade, and the market treated it accordingly — Boeing shares moved on the news, even as analysts noted the initial figure came in below the roughly 500-jet figure traders had been pricing in.

But the more durable trade sits one level down the supply chain.

GE's place in every Boeing widebody

Trump, speaking to reporters aboard Air Force One, said China had also agreed to purchase 400 to 450 GE Aerospace engines as part of the broader arrangement. That figure was not accidental. GE's GEnx-1B powers approximately 90 percent of Boeing 787 Dreamliners operated by Chinese carriers — six of the seven Chinese airlines flying 787 fleets have selected the GEnx. China Southern was the Asia launch customer for GE's earlier GE90 engine on the 777 back in the 1990s. When Trump separately confirmed the order included 777 and 737 models, the engine math was already settled.

GE Aerospace Chairman and CEO H. Lawrence Culp travelled to Beijing alongside Boeing's Kelly Ortberg as part of the U.S. business delegation accompanying Trump — a visible signal of how central GE's role is to the deal's structure.

The contract logic

What makes the GE position structurally different from Boeing's is how revenue attaches. An engine sale is not a point-in-time transaction. Long-term service agreements — GE's TrueChoice contracts — lock in inspection, parts, and overhaul revenue for the life of the engine, often spanning two to three decades. That back-end maintenance stream is where margins are typically higher than on the initial equipment sale. Every aircraft that eventually rolls out of a Boeing factory and into a Chinese operator's fleet triggers both the equipment revenue and years of follow-on services, largely regardless of which airline ultimately takes the plane or when exactly delivery occurs.

Boeing's execution risk, in other words, is GE's scheduling risk — not its revenue risk.

The numbers in contrast

The gap between the two companies' current financials sharpens the point. GE Aerospace beat earnings-per-share estimates for the fourth consecutive quarter in Q1 2026, posting $1.86 against a $1.59 consensus. Free cash flow for the quarter hit $1.658 billion, up 27 percent year-over-year. The company's commercial services backlog stands at $170 billion, with services revenue up 39 percent and engine deliveries up 43 percent. Full-year free cash flow guidance sits at $8.0–$8.4 billion.

Boeing's balance sheet tells a different story: a core loss per share in its most recent quarter, $1.454 billion of free cash burned, $47.2 billion in consolidated debt, and a commercial airplanes operating margin of negative 6.1 percent. The company is rebuilding production rates and working through quality-control remediation — progress is real, but the timeline is long and the variables are many.

IBA, an aviation intelligence firm, values the 200-aircraft order at roughly $17–19 billion on an 80 percent narrowbody mix, rising toward $25 billion if widebodies account for a larger share. The engine and services contract attached to that order would layer additional predictable revenue over the aircraft delivery curve.

What to watch

The key question is whether the initial 200-plane order converts to firm contracts with defined delivery schedules, and whether the option on up to 750 planes advances. Boeing's ability to ramp production will determine how fast that revenue flows. For GE, the engine agreements are largely negotiated in parallel with airframe orders — the contractual clock starts ticking once aircraft are built and delivered, not when announcements are made.

Shares in both companies dipped after the announcement, a sign the market had expected a larger headline number. That reaction compresses the entry price for traders who want exposure to the China aviation theme — and the divergence in underlying financials suggests the two companies are not equivalent ways to play it. Boeing's stock will continue to move on deal sentiment, delivery updates, and production milestones. GE's engine backlog will grow more quietly, and more durably.
