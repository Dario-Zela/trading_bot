Third Point exits Microsoft, slashes Nvidia in sweeping Q1 overhaul

Nvidia Turing architecture GPU dies — TU104, TU106, and TU116 — the class of chips Third Point has largely exited in favour of upstream equipment makers. — Fritzchens Fritz / Wikimedia Commons (CC0)
In one sentence
Third Point cut Nvidia 94% and exited Microsoft in Q1, rotating into Meta, Alphabet, and semiconductor equipment plays KLA, Lam Research, and ASML.

Daniel Loeb's Third Point LLC filed its Q1 2026 Form 13F on May 15, disclosing one of the most sweeping portfolio reshuffles the fund has made in recent quarters: a near-complete exit from Nvidia, a full sale of Microsoft, and the addition of Meta, Alphabet, and a cluster of semiconductor equipment makers.

The Nvidia position — which stood at roughly 2.95 million shares at the end of 2025 — was cut by 94%, leaving just 190,000 shares on the books at March 31. Microsoft was sold outright; the position had been valued at approximately $448 million. The reductions extended well beyond those two names. Rail holdings were gutted: Union Pacific went from 1.81 million shares to 100,000, and Norfolk Southern from 975,000 to 100,000. Third Point also exited stakes in PG&E, Chipotle, Constellation Energy, Alibaba, Spotify, and Thermo Fisher Scientific.

Where the capital went

On the buy side, Loeb initiated new positions in Meta Platforms (90,000 shares), Alphabet (175,000 shares), and Broadcom (50,000 shares). More distinctive are three new semiconductor equipment holdings: KLA Corporation (11,000 shares), Lam Research (75,000 shares), and ASML (12,000 shares). The fund also added 869,563 shares of Hut 8, the crypto mining company, and took a position in aerospace supplier TransDigm.

The semiconductor equipment trio signals a specific thesis: the primary AI infrastructure trade has shifted from chip designers — companies like Nvidia that design the GPUs — toward the equipment makers that supply the tools used to manufacture them. KLA, Lam Research, and ASML sit upstream of the entire chip production chain. That framing — often called the picks-and-shovels argument — has circulated among institutional investors for at least a year, but the scale of the Nvidia reduction makes Third Point's adoption of it unusually explicit.

Loeb has not issued a public statement on the specific trades, and the investor letter for Q1 2026 remains behind closed doors. The 13F filing itself contains no explanatory text — it is a required disclosure of equity positions, nothing more.

Following the herd, or setting the pace?

The broader Q1 2026 13F season, with filings due May 15, has shown a discernible pattern: Microsoft exits and Alphabet accumulation appeared across multiple institutional managers, suggesting Third Point's moves are not contrarian but broadly in line with where large-scale capital was moving during the first quarter. Berkshire Hathaway, in a separate filing, showed a portfolio that had contracted from approximately $274 billion to $263 billion across fewer positions — a different fund with a different strategy, but another data point in a season of notable repositioning.

Third Point's Q1 performance was slightly negative: the flagship Offshore Fund returned -0.6%, and the Ultra Fund -0.3%. The fund ended the quarter with approximately $2.08 billion in disclosed 13F equity holdings against total assets under management of roughly $12 billion — the rest held in instruments not captured by the quarterly filing.

What the filing cannot tell you is timing. Positions are reported as of the final day of the quarter; a fund could have built a Meta stake in January or March, or sold down Nvidia across the whole period or in a single week. Loeb's Q2 letter, expected in late July, may offer colour on the rationale — if it does, the semiconductor equipment thesis will either look prescient or premature by then.
