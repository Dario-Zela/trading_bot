Enhertu approved in two new HER2-positive early breast cancer settings

Molecular structure of trastuzumab deruxtecan (Enhertu), an antibody-drug conjugate linking the HER2-targeting antibody trastuzumab to a topoisomerase I inhibitor payload — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
FDA approved Enhertu for two HER2-positive early breast cancer settings on May 15, with DESTINY-Breast05 showing a 53% cut in recurrence vs. Kadcyla.

The FDA on May 15 approved Enhertu in two new settings for HER2-positive early breast cancer—a regulatory milestone that extends the antibody-drug conjugate into a far larger patient population than the advanced disease it was first approved to treat.

The two new indications cover opposite ends of the surgical pathway. In the neoadjuvant setting—treatment administered before an operation—Enhertu can now be given followed by a standard regimen of taxane, trastuzumab, and pertuzumab (THP) for adults with Stage II or III HER2-positive disease. In the adjuvant setting—treatment after surgery—it is approved for patients left with residual invasive disease after having received standard neoadjuvant HER2-targeted therapy.

The efficacy data underpinning both labels are, on their own terms, substantial. In DESTINY-Breast11, the Phase III trial supporting the neoadjuvant label, Enhertu produced a pathologic complete response rate of 67.3%—no residual cancer detectable at surgery—against 56.3% for standard doxorubicin-cyclophosphamide–based chemotherapy followed by THP. The adjuvant label rested on DESTINY-Breast05, which showed a 53% reduction in invasive disease recurrence or death compared with Roche's trastuzumab emtansine (Kadcyla), the previous standard of care. Three-year invasive disease-free survival was 92.4% versus 83.7% for Kadcyla—a hazard ratio of 0.47, which is a large effect in a curative-intent setting.

The adjuvant decision arrived nearly two months ahead of the FDA's original July 7 deadline. Early approvals of that kind are uncommon and typically signal confidence in the submitted data package.

Where the commercial story gets complicated

Regulatory approval and clinical uptake are different things. In the neoadjuvant space, Enhertu enters a pathway managed by well-entrenched combinations, and oncologists treating patients with curative intent weigh long-term toxicity profiles more carefully than in metastatic disease. Interstitial lung disease and pneumonitis—a known class risk for antibody-drug conjugates—occurred in 4.4% of DESTINY-Breast11 patients (including one fatal case) and in 10% of DESTINY-Breast05 patients. Those figures will inform prescribing behaviour.

The adjuvant label also carries a sequencing restriction: patients who received Enhertu in the neoadjuvant setting cannot then receive it again as adjuvant treatment. That limits the drug's footprint in sequential-care models and may complicate coverage decisions at payer level, though AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo executives have indicated they expect physicians to re-treat patients where prior neoadjuvant exposure was limited.

Against Kadcyla in the adjuvant setting, the efficacy case is harder to argue away. Kadcyla had relied on this niche since losing the metastatic line to Enhertu years earlier; a hazard ratio of 0.47 puts that franchise directly at risk.

"HER2-positive breast cancer is an aggressive disease, and our goal is to reduce the risk of recurrence as early as possible." — Dr. Shanu Modi, Memorial Sloan Kettering, principal investigator, DESTINY-Breast11

What to watch

Enhertu is already tracking toward roughly $5 billion in annual sales. Q1 2026 combined revenues reached $1.4 billion, up 31% year-on-year. The two approvals triggered a $155 million milestone payment from AstraZeneca to Daiichi Sankyo—contractual recognition of the commercial stakes. Daiichi projects approximately $5.4 billion in Enhertu revenues for fiscal 2026.

Whether these new labels translate into material revenue acceleration depends on three things: formulary wins and payer coverage decisions at major cancer centres, guidance revisions when AstraZeneca next reports earnings, and the maturation of overall survival data—both trials' OS curves remain immature, and the long-term risk-benefit profile in a curative setting carries more weight than in late-stage disease. For traders tracking AZN: watch for guidance on early-breast penetration rates and listen for any commentary on Kadcyla erosion pace in the next Roche reporting cycle.
