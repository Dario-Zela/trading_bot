HSBC raises silver forecasts, then warns the upside may already be spent

Five one-ounce silver bars from APMEX. Silver touched a nominal record of $121 per troy ounce in late January 2026. — Gage Skidmore / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 2.0)
In one sentence
HSBC raised its 2026 silver average forecast to $75/oz from $68.25, but its own year-end target of $70 signals the bank expects prices to drift lower from here.

HSBC has raised its average silver price forecast for 2026 to $75 per troy ounce, up from a prior estimate of $68.25. It also set year-end targets of $70 for 2026 and $65 for 2027. Both of those numbers sit below where silver is trading today — and that gap is, more or less, the whole story.

The bank's upgrade is real. So is its warning. The reason both can be true simultaneously is arithmetic: silver touched a nominal record of $121 per ounce in late January, a run that pulled the full-year average well above HSBC's earlier models. Revising the 2026 forecast upward simply acknowledges what has already happened. The year-end targets, however, telegraph the bank's actual conviction about where things go from here — and they point down.

A deficit that is shrinking fast

The structural argument for silver has rested on persistent supply-demand deficits. HSBC still sees a deficit in 2026, but the number is contracting sharply. The global shortfall is expected to narrow from 143 million ounces in 2025 to 73 million ounces this year, and further to just 25 million ounces in 2027. That trajectory matters. A deficit that halves annually is a different proposition than a stable one.

James Steel, HSBC's chief precious metals analyst, put it plainly: "Moderating deficits, in our view, will not be sufficient to propel silver sharply higher for prolonged periods."

The demand side compounds the picture. Industrial consumption — which accounts for more than half of all silver use — fell in 2025 to 657 million ounces from 679 million the year before, and is forecast to slip again to 642 million ounces in 2026. Manufacturers have been trimming silver content in components where substitution is feasible. Jewelry demand is projected to fall harder, from 189 million ounces to 157 million ounces.

The gold-to-silver ratio warning

There is a second, subtler caveat in HSBC's note. Steel flagged that "the gold-to-silver ratio is likely to widen, allowing silver to ease even if gold rallies" — a pointed observation for anyone running a silver position as a leveraged gold proxy. The implication: gold's safe-haven premium, fuelled by tariff uncertainty and geopolitical stress, may not transmit to silver with the same force it has in recent months. Silver has both a monetary and an industrial identity; when the industrial side softens, the monetary premium has to do more work.

Why the upgrade, then?

HSBC's rationale for the higher average forecast is not hard to follow. Record gold prices, constrained near-term supply, and safe-haven flows tied to ongoing trade and geopolitical tensions have all provided genuine support. The January spike to $121 was not noise. But the bank appears to be drawing a line between support and momentum — prices finding a floor is not the same as prices continuing to climb.

The 2027 average forecast, raised to $68 from a prior $57, reinforces this reading. HSBC sees silver holding above historical norms but drifting lower through the forecast window, not breaking higher.

What to watch

The next meaningful data point will be physical demand figures out of China, which accounts for a large share of industrial silver consumption. Any acceleration in manufacturing activity — particularly in solar panels and electronics, both heavy silver users — could stress HSBC's deficit assumptions in the other direction. On the supply side, mine output responses to elevated prices typically take 18 to 24 months to materialize; that lag may yet tighten balances more than the bank currently projects.

For now, HSBC's message to silver investors is essentially: the rally was legitimate, the floor is higher than it was, but the easy money has likely been made.
