RPC beats Q1 estimates but stock falls 5.7% on margin pressure

Pressure pumping equipment at a hydraulic fracturing operation in the Bakken formation — the type of completions services that drive RPC's Technical Services segment. — Joshua Doubek / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
RPC stock fell 5.7% after Q1 results: revenue beat estimates handily on Pintail growth, but net income fell 92% YoY as costs and weak oil prices crushed margins.

RPC, Inc. turned in a headline-beating first quarter — revenue of $454.8 million cleared the consensus estimate by roughly 10%, and adjusted earnings per share of $0.03 doubled the $0.01 Wall Street had pencilled in. Investors sold the stock anyway. Shares of the Atlanta-based oilfield services company fell 5.7% in the session following the May 7 release.

The explanation lies beneath the top line. Net income collapsed to $0.9 million in Q1 2026, down from $12.0 million in the same period a year earlier — a 92% decline even as revenue climbed 36.7% year on year. Adjusted EBITDA of $53.5 million slipped from $55.1 million the previous quarter, and the EBITDA margin contracted 110 basis points to 11.8%, against 14.7% a year ago.

Costs outrunning revenue

Costs are the problem. RPC's cost of revenues hit $355.6 million in Q1, up from $243.9 million in Q1 2025 — a 46% increase against a 37% revenue gain. The company attributed the squeeze to higher fuel expenses, elevated materials and supplies costs, and a shift in job mix toward more inputs-intensive work. SG&A added another $48.2 million, up from $42.5 million a year prior.

Much of the top-line growth came from the Pintail Completions acquisition, closed in Q2 2025, which bolstered RPC's wireline position in the Permian Basin. Pintail's Q1 revenue was "relatively flat sequentially," management said, and the segment is expected to track Permian operator activity closely — a market that has faced headwinds as oil prices have softened.

Commodity backdrop

West Texas Intermediate averaged roughly $70.54 per barrel during the quarter, down about 2% year on year, while the domestic rig count fell 6.8% over the same period. The natural gas price of $4.81/Mcf offered some offset — up 16.2% YoY — but gas revenue is a smaller piece of RPC's mix.

CEO Ben Palmer was measured in his commentary. "During the first quarter we experienced modest revenue increases despite weather impacts to start the year," he said, adding that the company is "cautiously optimistic about the rest of the year as commodity prices are more supportive." At WTI in the low $70s, "more supportive" is a guarded formulation. Palmer also noted that operators are not yet re-evaluating 2026 spending plans given uncertainty about how long current prices last.

Balance sheet holds, free cash flow does not

The balance sheet remains clean: $200.7 million in cash, nothing drawn on the $100 million revolver. The board maintained its $0.04 quarterly dividend. But free cash flow for the quarter came in at -$0.9 million, as capital expenditures of $32.1 million slightly exceeded operating cash flow of $31.2 million — a thin margin that leaves little room for further cost creep.

RPC's Q2 revenue guidance of $417.7 million implies a sequential step-down from Q1's $454.8 million, suggesting some of the first-quarter strength reflected timing rather than a sustained acceleration. The next read on whether operators are loosening the purse strings will come when the major Permian E&Ps report mid-quarter activity updates. Until then, the market's verdict is that an earnings beat on a shrinking margin base is not a reason to buy.
