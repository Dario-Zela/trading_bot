Nasdaq falls 1%, oil tops $106 as Iran impasse rattles markets

A 12-inch silicon wafer — the basic unit of the semiconductor supply chain whose growth assumptions are now under investor scrutiny. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
Nasdaq drops 1.1% as AMAT, MU, ORCL and Vertiv crater on AI capex doubts, while WTI hits $107 and the 10-year yield holds at 4.6% on stalled Iran talks.

The technology trade that has powered markets for most of 2026 hit a wall on Monday, as investors pulled back from semiconductor and data-center names most exposed to a potential deceleration in AI infrastructure spending. The selloff was compounded by a surge in crude oil, as US-Iran nuclear negotiations produced no progress and geopolitical risk premium re-entered energy prices.

The Nasdaq 100 fell 1.1%, its worst two-session loss since late March. The S&P 500 shed 0.4%, closing at 7,381. The damage was concentrated in the names that have most directly proxied the AI capex trade.

Applied Materials dropped 5.3%. Micron Technology lost 4.2%. Oracle gave back 3.8%. Vertiv Holdings — the data-center power-management specialist that hit an all-time high just five days prior — tumbled 8.9%. The Technology Select Sector SPDR Fund fell 1.5% as a group, the worst-performing sector in the S&P 500.

The question the market is asking

No single catalyst drove the move. What changed on Monday was the temperature of a debate that has simmered beneath the chip rally for months: whether the AI capital expenditure cycle is sustainable at its current scale.

Hyperscaler spending projections for 2026 sit near $830 billion in aggregate, with roughly three-quarters earmarked for AI-specific assets. That figure has anchored the bullish case for chip-equipment companies, memory producers, and data-center infrastructure suppliers. Monday's selloff reflected mounting skepticism that the number will hold — or that, even if it does, the multiples already embedded in these stocks leave little margin for disappointment.

Applied Materials is the clearest illustration of the dynamic. The company sells deposition and etch tools to chipmakers; its revenue is a direct function of how aggressively TSMC, Samsung, and Intel are building out manufacturing capacity. AMAT had raised its 2026 semiconductor equipment revenue growth guidance to better than 30% — a number that justified a significant stock premium. The problem is not that guidance changed on Monday. The problem is that if hyperscaler capex peaks earlier or at a lower level than assumed, that guidance becomes the ceiling, not the floor.

Vertiv's situation is starker still. The company reported Q1 2026 net sales of $2.65 billion, up 30% year-over-year, driven by 44% organic growth in the Americas, and raised its full-year sales outlook to $13.5–$14.0 billion. None of that mattered. Vertiv's stock trades on what data-center power infrastructure demand will look like in 2028 and 2029, and those expectations shifted — not because of Vertiv's numbers, but because the macro assumptions underpinning those numbers came under pressure.

Oracle, which is not a chipmaker but has been spending aggressively to position its cloud division as an AI infrastructure landlord, was caught in the same current. The company has budgeted roughly $50 billion in capital expenditure for its data-center buildout, carrying total debt near $124 billion to fund it. With its remaining performance obligations — effectively its forward contract backlog — at a record $553 billion, the strategic bet is enormous. A material softening in hyperscaler demand would not just hit chip equipment stocks; it would reprice the economics of Oracle's entire infrastructure thesis.

Oil and the Iran impasse

The sell-off did not occur in isolation. WTI crude rose 1.5% to $106.96 per barrel, and Brent climbed 1.6% to $110.97, as US-Iran negotiations stalled conspicuously. The Trump administration has demanded what officials characterized as "documents of surrender" — full dismantlement of Iran's enrichment program — while Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian declared that talks proceed "with dignity, not concession" and that his government will "never bow." The Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of global oil supply transits, remains a pressure point in any escalation scenario.

Higher crude fed directly into inflation expectations. The 10-year Treasury yield held at 4.60%, a 16-month high. The 30-year reached 5.13%. Fed funds futures assigned roughly 60% probability to at least one additional 25-basis-point rate hike before year-end — a bet that would have seemed improbable in April. Kevin Warsh, who assumed the Federal Reserve chairmanship on May 14 with a reputation for hawkish discipline, has given no signal the Fed is inclined to loosen conditions while CPI is running at 3.8% year-over-year and the producer price index at 6.0%.

For growth stocks priced on discounted future earnings, the combination of higher rates and compressed risk appetite is doubly punishing. Valuation math that worked at 4.2% on the 10-year looks less comfortable at 4.6%.

Where money went

The rotation was predictable in direction if not in magnitude. The energy sector gained 1.8%, with Exxon Mobil up 1.5% and Chevron up 2.1%. Insurance names advanced 2.3% as a group, benefiting from the yield move. Dominion Energy surged 9.3% on a NextEra acquisition announcement, an idiosyncratic gain that masked the broader defensive bid.

Small-cap indices fared slightly better than large-cap tech, though the Russell 2000 still fell 0.7%. The pattern — money moving out of long-duration growth assets and into rate-sensitive or commodity-driven sectors — has threatened to become a sustained rotation several times in recent months without quite doing so.

What to watch

The durability of Monday's repricing depends on a few variables that will resolve in the coming weeks. US-Iran talks remain the most immediate: any agreement that removes the geopolitical premium from crude would relieve bond markets and reduce one source of upward pressure on yields. A breakdown, or any military action, runs the opposite risk.

On the AI capex question, the next major data points are hyperscaler earnings. Alphabet, Microsoft, and Amazon report in the coming months, and their capital expenditure guidance will be parsed with unusual intensity. If those companies hold or raise their data-center spending plans, Monday's move looks like a correction in a structurally intact trade. If any of them signals a pause or a slowdown in the pace of GPU orders and power infrastructure contracts, the repricing that started Monday has room to run.

The chip-equipment sector has been here before — brief episodes of doubt followed by re-acceleration. Whether this one resolves the same way depends on whether the AI revenue that is supposed to justify the spending eventually shows up in hyperscaler income statements. So far, cloud revenue growth has been strong enough to keep the thesis alive. The question now is whether it is strong enough to hold the line on capex commitments when oil is at $107, the 10-year is at 4.6%, and the Fed is no longer cutting.
