Iran's president: talks proceed with dignity, not concession

Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian, photographed in 2024 — Khamenei.ir / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 4.0)
In one sentence
Pezeshkian reaffirmed Iran's negotiating red lines on X — "dialogue does not mean surrender" — the same day Trump postponed a threatened military strike.

Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian took to X on May 18 to defend his government's decision to keep talking to Washington — while making clear that talking is not the same as capitulating.

Dialogue does not mean surrender. The Islamic Republic enters into dialogue with dignity, authority, and the preservation of the nation's rights. Under no circumstances will it retreat from the legal rights of the people and the country.

The timing was pointed. The post came on the same day President Trump announced he was calling off a scheduled military strike against Iran — reportedly at the urging of Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar — after weeks of intensifying pressure and a hardened US negotiating position.

The backdrop matters. A US-Israeli military campaign against Iran began in late February. A ceasefire came into effect on April 8, and multiple rounds of talks have since been held in Muscat, Geneva, and Islamabad. By early May, those talks had hit a wall. Trump called Iran's latest counteroffer "totally unacceptable" and declared the ceasefire "on life support." Tehran, for its part, rejected Washington's 14-point peace framework as "a demand for surrender" — language that echoes Pezeshkian's post almost word for word.

The core disagreements are structural and unresolved. Washington wants Iran to transfer roughly 400 kilograms of enriched uranium to the US, shut down all but one nuclear facility, reopen the Strait of Hormuz — which Tehran has kept closed since March, strangling global energy flows — and abandon demands for war compensation. Iran says its right to enrichment is non-negotiable, that the Strait question is sovereign territory, and that sanctions relief must be immediate and substantial.

Pezeshkian's statement threads a domestic needle as much as a diplomatic one. Within Iran, a segment of hardline opinion holds that any engagement with Washington represents ideological betrayal. By framing dialogue as an act of strength — conducted with "dignity and authority" — the president is shoring up the political space to keep negotiating, rather than announcing any shift in position.

Nothing in the post constitutes a new offer, concession, or timeline. It is a restatement of existing red lines, delivered at a moment when the military clock is ticking and the diplomatic track looks precarious.

What to watch: whether the Gulf states' intervention that paused Monday's threatened strike opens a window for a resumption of technical-level talks, or whether it simply delays a harder confrontation. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed, oil markets remain volatile, and the gap between the two sides' opening positions has not visibly narrowed.
