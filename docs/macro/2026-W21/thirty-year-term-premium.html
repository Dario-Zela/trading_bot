RATES · LONG END · By G. Mehlman · 2026-05-24 · 6 min read

Term premium is back — and the 30Y clearing 5% is its calling card

The US Treasury Building, Washington D.C. — headquarters of the department managing the world's largest sovereign debt market — Rchuon24 / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 3.0)
In one sentence
With the 3M rate anchored at 3.585%, the 30-year yield at 5.064% is term premium, not inflation panic — and term premium requires a fiscal fix, not a Fed cut.

The US 30-year Treasury yield has spent most of the past two decades below 5%. On Friday, May 24, it sat at 5.064% — not a spike, not a flash, but a settled level that the bond market has had a full week to reconsider and chose not to walk back. That persistence is worth examining closely, because the shape of the yield curve tells you what is actually happening.

What the geometry says

Start at the short end. The three-month T-bill rate is 3.585% — anchored firmly where it has been for months. A market pricing an imminent inflation shock would be selling the front end too, because a shock of that kind eventually forces the Fed's hand. It isn't. The 5-year sits at 4.256%, the 10-year at 4.558%, and the 30-year at 5.064%: a monotonically rising curve with every increment of duration costing the buyer meaningfully more. The 3-month-to-10-year spread has re-widened to 97.3 basis points — fully positive after an extended period of inversion that ran from 2022 into 2024.

That curve geometry is diagnostic. When inflation expectations are the driver of a bond sell-off, the front end moves with the long end — markets price Fed tightening alongside higher long-run rates. That is not this. The Fed funds path baked into short-term instruments has barely shifted. What has shifted is the premium investors require to hold duration — the extra yield that compensates for the uncertainty of lending to the federal government for thirty years rather than three months. That is term premium, and it is back.

The New York Fed's ACM model — the standard academic decomposition of nominal yields into expected future rates and term premium — showed the 10-year term premium at roughly 68 basis points as of late April, positive territory it had not reliably occupied for most of the post-GFC decade. QE suppressed it mechanically: the Fed bought duration regardless of price, crowding out private investors and removing the need for compensation. QT and the end of large-scale asset purchase programmes reversed that. The market is now price-sensitive again, and it is pricing duration risk accordingly.

The fiscal catalyst

Term premium does not rise in a vacuum. Something has to make duration-sensitive investors revise their assessment of the compensation they need. In the current cycle, two events have done structural damage to the long end's equilibrium.

The first was the passage of what the administration branded its flagship fiscal legislation in mid-2025 — a package that independent budget analysts at Yale's Budget Lab estimated would add $4.1 trillion to the deficit over a decade, or $5.5 trillion if the temporary provisions are extended. Annual deficits running at roughly 8% of GDP, combined with debt held by the public already exceeding 100% of GDP for the first time since the Second World War, have made the issuance calendar a structural feature of the market rather than a temporary emergency. Annual interest costs on the federal debt now surpass $1 trillion, consuming roughly twenty cents of every dollar of federal revenue collected.

The second was Moody's decision on May 16 to strip the United States of its last remaining top-tier sovereign credit rating, downgrading from Aaa to Aa1. S&P had moved in 2011, Fitch in 2023; Moody's had held its position as the last major holdout. The downgrade did not cause the sell-off in any mechanical sense — Treasuries are not automatically sold by institutions when a rating falls — but it crystallised a narrative that the long end had been pricing for weeks. The day after the announcement, the 30-year yield hit 5.198%, its highest reading since July 2007, before settling back to current levels. A $25 billion 30-year auction on May 13 had already cleared at 5.046% with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.30 — moderate, not buoyant.

The structural supply picture compounds the demand challenge. The Treasury's quarterly refunding confirmed heavy issuance at long maturities. Private investors must now absorb duration that central banks once bought price-insensitively. For every dollar of new supply, an institution that cares about yield needs to see an adequate return — and that return is expressed as term premium.

Why this is harder to resolve than the alternatives

Bear steepeners have different characters depending on what drives them. A growth-driven steepener, where the long end rises on expectations of a stronger economy, tends to be self-correcting — growth eventually pushes the Fed to tighten, the front end catches up, and the slope normalises. An inflation-driven steepener is similarly amenable to policy responses: the Fed can signal or deliver rate hikes, which pulls forward short-end pricing and constrains the inflation premium in long yields.

A term-premium-driven bear steepener is stickier. The front end is not wrong; it is simply pricing what the Fed is actually likely to do, which is not much. The long end is not wrong either — it is pricing the structural cost of absorbing decades of fiscal imbalance without a central-bank backstop. Resolving the gap requires convincing duration-sensitive investors that the fiscal trajectory is changing. That is not a monetary policy tool. It requires legislative action on the deficit path, and the current political arithmetic makes that unlikely in the near term.

Citigroup's macro rates strategist Jim McCormick flagged this arithmetic directly last week, saying that after the long end climbed to 5.16%, the focus in rates markets was likely shifting to 5.5% as the next round-number target. Bank of America's latest global fund manager survey showed 62% of respondents expect 30-year yields to reach 6% — a level last seen in the late 1990s.

The implication for other asset classes is consequential but not linear. A higher risk-free rate at the long end mechanically compresses equity multiples, particularly for growth stocks priced on distant cash flows. It also tightens financial conditions even if the overnight rate stays flat, because mortgages, corporate borrowing, and infrastructure financing all price off the long end. Credit spreads have so far held their ground — but as a companion piece in this edition notes, that divergence between spread stability and duration sell-off has a limited half-life.

The number to watch is not 5% — it has been crossed. The number to watch is whether the 30-year sustains above 5.10% through the next quarterly refunding announcement, due in early August, when Treasury will confirm whether issuance at long maturities is being increased. A credible fiscal consolidation plan — or even concrete signals of one — would be the single most powerful suppressor of term premium. Until that arrives, the long end has every reason to stay elevated, and duration buyers have every reason to ask for more.
