IG leads the credit selloff: this is a duration trade, not a credit stress event — for now

The NYSE trading floor — where the gap between investment-grade and high-yield returns this week had more to do with duration than with default risk. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
LQD's -1.17% week versus HYG's -0.55% is duration math, not default fear — but sustained 30Y yields above 5% are how rate stress eventually becomes credit stress.

Investment-grade corporate bonds fell harder than high-yield this week. That sentence sounds alarming — it shouldn't be, not yet — but it deserves more than a mechanical explanation.

LQD, the iShares IG corporate bond ETF, dropped 1.17% over five days. HYG, its high-yield counterpart, fell just 0.55%. The 61.6 basis-point gap in performance is not a sign that markets are suddenly more worried about Ford's balance sheet than about Carvana's. It is, at its core, a duration story. LQD carries an effective duration of roughly eight years. HYG runs closer to four. When the 30-year Treasury yield pushes through 5.1% — its highest level in a year, closing Friday at 5.147% — longer-duration paper gets hit harder by simple mathematics. For every 100 basis points of rate movement, LQD loses roughly twice what HYG loses. The differential this week is almost entirely explained by that gap.

Credit spreads confirm the read. IG option-adjusted spreads remain around 80 basis points — historically tight, not historically stressed. High-yield credit risk premia are not widening in any meaningful way. The HYG/LQD ratio sits at a z-score of roughly +1.0 over the past year, a reading that credit-spread trackers categorize as neutral, not risk-off. If this were a default-fear event, you would see HY spreads blowing out relative to Treasuries. You are not seeing that.

So the market's verdict is clear: this is a rates trade, not a credit trade.

The duration gap is structural

The reason IG bonds carry so much more duration than HY is embedded in how each market works. Investment-grade issuers — large, established companies with strong balance sheets — routinely issue 10-, 20-, and 30-year debt. They can, because investors trust their long-term credit quality enough to lend for those periods. High-yield issuers, rated BB and below, rarely get that luxury. Their debt typically matures in five to seven years, and their higher coupon payments further compress duration. The Bloomberg U.S. Corporate Investment Grade Index runs at roughly 6.8 years of duration; the high-yield equivalent sits near 3.3 years.

This structural difference means that when long rates move — as they have this week, driven by a combination of inflation persistence, rising term premium, and fiscal concerns following the U.S. fiscal deterioration trajectory — IG bonds take the brunt. It is not a sign of credit weakness. It is a sign of rate sensitivity.

The harder question

But the mechanical explanation, satisfying as it is, does not close the analysis. The question worth asking — the one that 2020 forces anyone who lived through it to ask — is at what point a sustained rate-driven selloff in IG creates secondary effects on credit quality itself.

The feedback loop is not complicated in outline. Rising long rates lift the all-in cost of new debt issuance. Companies facing a refinancing wall — and there is a large one approaching in 2026 and 2027, as pandemic-era cheap debt matures — have to roll at materially higher rates. That compresses interest coverage ratios. Declining asset values (bonds, real estate, equity collateral) tighten lending conditions as banks mark their collateral down. Credit availability tightens. The weakest IG borrowers, clustered in the BBB tier, find themselves closer to the high-yield threshold.

This is not hypothetical. Approximately $50 billion in corporate debt was downgraded to junk in 2025 alone. The BBB segment — which constitutes a substantial share of the investment-grade universe — is structurally the most exposed. These issuers operate near the cusp of high-yield status. Historical data shows BBB bonds average a 13% loss in the three months prior to a downgrade, with losses spiking sharply during broader downturns. Recent fallen angels include names like Centene, Paramount, and FS KKR.

The key variable is how long the 30-year yield stays elevated. A brief spike through 5% is a price event. A sustained period above 5% — call it six months or more — is a refinancing cost event. History suggests the credit feedback loop takes 50 to 100 basis points of further yield movement from an initial stress threshold before it materializes in spread widening. The 30-year is at 5.15% today. That math implies the loop starts in earnest somewhere around 5.5% to 5.65% on the long end, or if current levels simply persist long enough to bite the refinancing calendar.

What is driving the long end

The rate move itself is not mysterious. The global bond selloff this week was driven by a convergence of factors: rising oil prices stoking inflation fears, concern about the U.S. fiscal trajectory — the Congressional Budget Office projects federal deficits near 7% of GDP annually for the foreseeable future — and a term premium that has been rebuilding since Moody's stripped the U.S. of its last Aaa rating a year ago. Long rates are no longer anchored by the assumption of Fed intervention at every stress point. The market is pricing the cost of fiscal expansion into the long end, and that premium is unlikely to dissipate quickly.

That context matters for the IG-vs-HY debate. If the 30-year is elevated primarily because of term premium and fiscal risk rather than because of near-term growth expectations, the credit feedback loop develops more slowly — but it also does not self-correct quickly. Bond vigilante moves tend to be sticky.

The falsifiable call

The specific claim to watch is this: if the 30-year Treasury holds above 5% through the summer refinancing season, watch the BBB end of the IG universe for spread widening that goes beyond what duration alone explains. That would signal the beginning of the transition from a rates story to an early-stage credit story. It has not happened yet. IG spreads are tight. HY is calm. But the conditions for the next move — elevated long rates, a refinancing wall, a BBB tier under structural pressure — are in place.

The data point that would falsify the concern: a 30-year that retreats back below 4.75% on improved fiscal news or a meaningful demand shift. That would keep the feedback loop dormant.

For now, the credit market is correct that this week's underperformance in IG is mechanical. The risk is not in today's numbers. It is in whether today's rate environment becomes next quarter's refinancing cost.
