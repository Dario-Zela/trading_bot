Emerging markets face a two-front squeeze: dollar revival and an oil shock

A 200 Turkish lira banknote — the lira has weakened roughly 17% over the past twelve months as oil costs and carry-trade outflows compound currency pressure. — Wikimedia Commons / Central Bank of Turkey
In one sentence
DXY near 99 and oil +23% in twenty days is a pincer on oil-importing EM: costlier dollar debt service meets swelling import bills.

The dollar at 99 and oil at a multi-year high — this pairing is not random bad luck for emerging markets; it is among the most damaging configurations the asset class can face. For oil importers, which represent the majority of EM countries by number if not by benchmark weight, this week's numbers are close to a worst-case reading.

The DXY settled at 98.95, up 1.04% on the week. The USO fund, a crude oil proxy, gained 7.67% this week and 23.05% over the past 20 trading days — a supply-shock move, not a demand boom. The two forces compound each other in a specific and painful way: a stronger dollar raises the real burden of USD-denominated sovereign and corporate debt, while higher oil swells import bills and widens current account deficits. For EM governments trying to hold currencies steady, both are pulling in the same direction.

The bloc is split

Not every EM credit is in trouble. Oil exporters — Gulf sovereigns, Nigeria, Brazil, Mexico — benefit mechanically from the oil leg. Gulf central banks carry minimal FX risk given dollar pegs. Brazil's real and the Mexican peso have shown relative resilience. Base metals, meanwhile, fell 1.74% this week, partially eroding the terms-of-trade gain for commodity-exporting EM economies like South Africa and Chile — a reminder that even the 'winners' in this configuration are not cleanly positioned.

It is the oil-importing majority — Turkey, India, the Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia — that faces the squeeze.

The most exposed

Turkey sits at the acute end. The lira has weakened roughly 17% over the past 12 months. Turkey's current account deficit reached $14.54 billion in just the first two months of 2026, against $9.25 billion in the same period a year earlier. The Central Bank of Turkey spent an estimated $12 billion — approximately 15% of usable FX reserves — defending the currency during peak geopolitical turbulence. The lira carry trade, estimated at $40 billion at its peak, has seen roughly $15 billion unwound since the crisis began. Bank of America and Barclays have both exited long-lira positions. The central bank has held its benchmark rate at 37%, a level that sustains the currency at significant cost to domestic borrowers.

India is the next most visible case. The rupee has become the worst-performing Asian currency in 2026, down roughly 6% year-to-date and trading around ₹95.65 to the dollar. India imports 88% of its crude oil; every $10 per barrel increase adds approximately $13–14 billion to the annual import bill. The Reserve Bank of India has sold over $100 billion in spot and forward markets this fiscal year to slow the depreciation — reserves have fallen from a February peak of $728.49 billion to approximately $690.69 billion. The current account deficit is on track to widen to 1.7–2.0% of GDP. In April, the RBI moved beyond FX sales: banks were barred from offering non-deliverable forward contracts, and net open rupee positions were capped at $100 million.

The Philippines is smaller but proportionally exposed. Over 90% of its oil arrives from the Persian Gulf; the current account deficit is projected to widen from -3.3% to -4.4% of GDP in 2026; oil import costs now consume roughly 4% of GDP.

CDS and fiscal pressure

EM sovereign CDS spreads rose approximately 12% in aggregate through Q1 2026, with widening concentrated among net oil importers, where the terms-of-trade deterioration is largest. Fiscal pressure compounds this: in Egypt, fossil-fuel subsidies represent 28% of government spending; in Uzbekistan, 28.3%. Governments reluctant to remove those buffers quickly face a structural deficit expansion even before debt service costs are counted. Twelve EM countries are currently flagged for simultaneous spread widening and above-median debt maturities due in 2026.

Room to maneuver

The policy options are constrained. Rate hikes support currencies but carry recession risk — a trade-off Turkey's CBT has already made by holding at 37%. FX intervention burns reserves without addressing underlying deficit dynamics. Capital flow restrictions, as India has found, risk discouraging the inflows governments need most. Countries with thinner reserve cushions — Egypt, Kenya, Ghana — have less capacity to absorb the shock without returning to the IMF.

The near-term circuit breakers are identifiable. A Federal Reserve signal of earlier rate cuts would take the dollar leg off the table. Any supply-side resolution in the Middle East — ceasefire, shipping lane reopening — would move oil fast. The Philippines' Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas meets in June; the RBI's June review will be watched for any shift in intervention posture. Until one of those valves opens, the two pressures run simultaneously.
