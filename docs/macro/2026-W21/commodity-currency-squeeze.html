Commodity bloc FX faces a double headwind as energy reverses

A crude oil tanker at the BP Oil Refinery Jetty in Kwinana, Western Australia — the kind of cargo whose price now drives CAD and NOK in opposite directions to where they were a fortnight ago. — Visitor7 / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA 4.0)
In one sentence
USO's -4.93% weekly reversal erases a 20-day energy tailwind for petrocurrencies just as the dollar gropes toward the 100 threshold.

Oil has given back nearly all of last fortnight's gains in the space of a single week. USO, the US crude oil ETF, fell 4.93% for the five days ending May 23 — reversing course after a +6.44% run over the prior 20 trading days. Natural gas followed in the same direction: UNG dropped 3.44% on the week, unwinding a similar 20-day gain of 6.11%. For currencies that ride the energy cycle — the Canadian dollar, the Norwegian krone, and to a lesser extent the Australian dollar — the timing is awkward. A recovering US dollar is arriving at precisely the moment the commodity tide goes out.

Two forces drove the oil reversal. Progress in US-Iran diplomatic talks drained the geopolitical risk premium that had kept crude elevated since the Strait of Hormuz disruptions in late April; Secretary of State Marco Rubio cited "encouraging signs" mid-week, and markets trimmed the supply-disruption bid accordingly. On the production side, OPEC+'s May 3 decision to raise June output quotas by 188,000 barrels per day — 62,000 bpd each from Saudi Arabia and Russia, with smaller contributions from Iraq, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman — added incremental supply pressure, even if analysts characterised the move as largely symbolic given the scale of current inventory draws.

The dollar adds another layer

Compounding the energy reversal is a dollar that has quietly recovered ground. The DXY is trading around 99.3, up roughly 0.82% over 20 days, and within reach of the 100 level that has become a focal point for FX traders. A sustained break above 100 would represent a meaningful regime shift after the dollar's sharp decline from its early-2026 highs. For commodity exporters, a firmer dollar is a second-order pain: weaker commodity prices in USD terms translate into a lower volume of hard currency revenues even before the exchange rate effect is counted.

CAD and NOK are the most directly exposed. Canada earns roughly a third of its export revenues from energy; Norway's petroleum sector accounts for close to 20% of GDP and nearly half of total exports. Both currencies rely on elevated oil to support current-account balances and compensate for relatively high production costs. USD/CAD moved toward 1.37–1.38 through mid-May as softer domestic data reinforced expectations that the Bank of Canada has limited room to push back. NOK, a textbook petrocurrency with a tight historical correlation to Brent, has less insulation — Norway's sovereign wealth fund buffers the domestic budget but does not absorb krone depreciation in the FX market.

A partial offset in base metals

AUD occupies a somewhat different position. Copper hit an all-time high of $6.63 per pound on May 13, and the Invesco DB Base Metals ETF (DBB) posted gains of +1.85% on the week and +2.82% over 20 days, underpinned by selectively constructive Chinese industrial demand. Bank of America flagged AUD upside driven by the copper-to-S&P 500 ratio and the RBA's rate posture. That support is real — but it is not sufficient, on its own, to offset a broad commodity-bloc selloff if energy weakness extends and the dollar continues its slow grind toward 100.

The near-term variable is the Iran negotiation timeline. A credible ceasefire agreement would cement the exit of the supply-risk premium from oil markets; a breakdown would push crude back toward its recent highs and restore at least some of the petrocurrency tailwind. Watch Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic data in early June — that is where the negotiating progress, or the absence of it, will show up first.
