Dollar back at 99: the recovery nobody expected is creating winners and losers fast

Historical chart of the U.S. Dollar Index (DXY), which measures the dollar against a basket of six major currencies including the euro, yen, and sterling. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
DXY at 98.95 — up 1% on the week — is the single variable connecting gold's fall, silver's collapse, and mounting stress on dollar-indebted emerging markets.

In April, the dollar looked like a one-way trade going down. US tariffs were upending the post-Bretton Woods assumption that dollar assets are the safe harbor for global capital, and investors were quietly reducing exposure. By early May, the DXY — the index that measures the dollar against a basket of six major currencies — had slid below 98, and the consensus was writing its obituary.

The obituary was premature.

The DXY ended the week at 98.95, up 1.04% from Monday's open. Over the past twenty trading days it has added a further 0.92%. That is not a dead-cat bounce. Two weeks of steady accumulation, bought on dips, is a trend signal — and this one has already rippled through gold, silver, and developing-world balance sheets.

Why the dollar was supposed to stay weak

The original bear case for the dollar was coherent. When the US imposes sweeping import tariffs, the textbook response is a softer currency: the trade deficit narrows mechanically, surplus countries accumulate fewer dollars, and rate differentials compress if the Fed is forced to ease into the growth slowdown. In April, markets ran with that thesis hard.

But the growth shock hasn't materialized on schedule. US producer prices rose 1.4% in April — the biggest single-month gain since March 2022 — and 6.0% year-on-year, almost three times the 0.5% consensus forecast. Consumer prices came in at 3.8% annually. Hot inflation doesn't give the Fed cover to cut. Futures markets now price Fed funds on hold through year-end, with a roughly 39% probability of an outright hike materialising before December. The dollar, which lives and dies by rate differentials, has noticed.

The oil paradox

The most striking feature of this week's price action is not the dollar's recovery in isolation — it's who it's recovering alongside. Oil rose 7.67% over the same period, with Brent crude touching $106 per barrel. Dollar and oil moving in tandem is the exception, not the rule. Oil is priced in dollars, which means a stronger dollar raises the real cost for non-US buyers and should mechanically compress demand. They have historically moved in opposite directions.

That both are rising at once points to a supply story overwhelming the FX math. Unresolved tensions around the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly a fifth of global oil trade passes — have sustained a geopolitical risk premium that dollar headwinds alone cannot offset. Supply-driven inflation doesn't require demand strength. And demand-insensitive oil price spikes feed US producer prices, which then feed the rate hike bets propping the dollar. The loop is internally consistent, if uncomfortable.

Two explanations compete for why the dollar is strong in this environment. The first is US exceptionalism reasserting itself: rates are high, nominal growth is holding even as real activity slows, and capital is flowing back into US assets because the alternatives — euro-area growth is soft, China's recovery uneven, and the Bank of Japan is still navigating its exit from ultra-loose policy — look worse. The second is a simpler risk-off story: when geopolitical tensions run hot, dollar assets remain the world's de facto safe harbour, tariff narrative or not. The truth is probably both, and their relative weight will become clearer once Thursday's preliminary PMI data offers a cleaner read on US activity.

The 100 level and G10 crosses

Technically, DXY at 98.95 puts the round-number 100 handle back in focus. That threshold carries real consequences. The euro comprises 57.6% of the DXY basket. When the index crosses 100, EUR/USD tests parity support, imposing deflationary impulses on an already growth-constrained euro area while tightening financial conditions globally for all dollar borrowers. Sterling, which underperformed most G10 peers this week, extends its slide. The yen — already strained by the Bank of Japan's cautious yield-curve normalization — faces renewed USD/JPY pressure, a problem for Tokyo because a weak yen exports inflation it does not want.

Most major FX desks don't have a sustained break above 100 as their base case. The consensus view — Cambridge Currencies, ING, StoneX — has DXY ending 2026 in the low-to-mid 90s, reflecting slower US growth, widening fiscal deficits, and fading rate advantage. But that consensus underestimated the move from 97.50 on May 6 to Friday's intraday high of 99.40. A clean close above 99.40 removes a layer of resistance and opens the path to 100. Whether that level holds or breaks is the most significant near-term FX question in the market.

Emerging markets: a two-front problem

For developing-country borrowers, the question isn't really about technical chart levels — it's about survival arithmetic. Dollar-denominated debt is the structural vulnerability. A stronger dollar raises the local-currency cost of servicing external obligations, drains foreign exchange reserves, and forces central banks to choose between defending their currencies and easing monetary policy to support slowing growth. In Q1 2026, EM local currency debt lost 2.25% in USD terms, driven primarily by adverse FX moves. Thirteen of nineteen currencies in the key EM index depreciated against the dollar in the most recent tracking period.

For net oil importers among that group — Turkey, India, Egypt, several Southeast Asian economies — the simultaneous dollar and oil shock compounds the pain. Higher import bills land in weaker currencies. The resulting pressure on current account deficits forces either reserve drawdown or rate hikes that choke domestic growth. The two-front squeeze is not hypothetical; it is already visible in reserve data and spread widening across frontier-market sovereign issuers.

Gold and silver as collateral damage

Gold's -3.73% weekly drop and -5.35% over twenty days fit neatly within the dollar-strength frame. Gold pays no interest; when dollar assets yield more and the dollar itself appreciates, the opportunity cost of holding gold rises on both dimensions simultaneously. The metal had already run from roughly $2,700 at the start of the year to an all-time high near $5,589 in January — a parabolic move that left it sensitive to any reversal catalyst. Dollar strength was that catalyst.

Silver's -10.33% collapse in a single week is a different magnitude entirely. Silver runs on two demand engines: monetary (like gold) and industrial (electronics, solar, manufacturing). A dollar strengthening on inflation rather than real growth is bad for the monetary component. Signs of slowing EM demand — particularly from Chinese manufacturing, which has been uneven — are bad for the industrial component. Silver caught both barrels, which explains why its weekly loss is nearly three times gold's.

What's next

Three variables will determine whether the 98.95 level is a consolidation zone or a launching pad. Thursday's preliminary PMI data for May is the most immediate trigger — a reading above 50 on both manufacturing and services removes the last macro argument for near-term easing and pushes DXY closer to 100. The Iran situation is the wild card: FXStreet noted Monday that DXY eased to 99.10 on renewed hopes of a Pakistani-brokered peace deal, and any genuine de-escalation along the Strait of Hormuz would unwind the oil risk premium and potentially reverse the dollar safe-haven bid simultaneously. Finally, whether thirty-year Treasury yields extend their move through 5% — driven by the fiscal premium that is increasingly dominating the long end — will shape the capital-flow calculus underpinning the whole trade.

The dollar was not supposed to be at 99 in mid-May. The consensus was built on a growth slowdown that has yet to arrive, a Fed that has yet to pivot, and a tariff shock that has so far transmitted more into inflation than into demand destruction. Until those conditions change, the direction of least resistance is higher.
