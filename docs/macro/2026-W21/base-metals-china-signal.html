Base metals diverge from energy — China's demand signal is selectively constructive

Copper cathode — the refined form in which copper is traded and consumed by industrial buyers globally. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
DBB +1.85% on the week while oil and gold retreat points to real Chinese industrial demand driving base metals — not macro-hedge flows.

The commodity markets are sending contradictory signals this week, and the contradiction is the story. The Invesco DB Base Metals Fund (DBB) has gained 1.85% over five days and 2.82% over twenty, even as the United States Oil Fund (USO) dropped 4.93% on the week and the SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) shed 4.48% over the past month. In a broad commodity selloff, base metals should fall alongside energy. They haven't — and that asymmetry points somewhere specific.

In one sentence: DBB gaining while oil and gold both fall is an unusual commodity configuration that implies real Chinese industrial demand, not macro-hedge positioning.

DBB holds futures on copper, aluminum, and zinc — the three metals most tightly bound to physical industrial activity. Copper alone accounts for roughly 52% of the fund's weighting. Unlike oil, which is sensitive to OPEC+ supply decisions and geopolitical choke points, or gold, which moves with real interest rates and safe-haven appetite, base metals are consumption commodities. Factories absorb them. Construction sites require them. Power grids are wired with them. When base metals hold up while financial and energy commodities retreat, it is usually because someone, somewhere, is actually buying the physical.

A Specific Buyer

That buyer is, almost certainly, Chinese industry. China accounts for roughly 57% of global refined copper consumption and holds comparably dominant positions in aluminum and zinc demand. The evidence that the buying is real, not speculative, lies in the inventory data: LME copper stockpiles have been declining steadily, a signal that downstream users are drawing on available supply rather than letting it accumulate. Copper briefly crossed $14,000 per tonne on the LME in mid-May — its first visit to that level since January's all-time high near $14,500 — and the move came alongside, not before, the inventory drawdown.

China's April manufacturing PMI lends support. The NBS reading came in at 50.3, while the Caixin survey — which skews toward export-oriented and smaller manufacturers — reached 52.2, its strongest expansion reading since December 2020. Neither figure is euphoric, but both are consistent with genuine industrial throughput rather than a rebound from an unusually depressed base.

The energy and gold retreats tell a different story. OPEC+ supply discipline is fraying, pushing crude markets toward surplus; USO's near-5% weekly drop reflects that arithmetic. Gold's pullback from January highs is consistent with reduced macro-hedge demand as institutions reassess positioning after a historically strong two-year run for precious metals. Taken together — energy down on supply, gold down on receding safe-haven flows — the macro backdrop is neither particularly bullish nor acutely alarmed. What base metals add to the picture is a functioning industrial demand signal, operating independently of the financial positioning that has driven the rest of the commodity complex.

For emerging-market investors, the divergence falls unevenly by country. The DXY at 99.32 represents a general headwind: a dollar near par raises the local-currency cost of dollar-denominated debt and compresses capital inflows. But Chile, where copper represents roughly half of export revenues, and Peru, the world's second-largest zinc producer, sit on the right side of this particular divergence. The dollar's weight is softened when the commodity holding firm is a metal, not a barrel.

The configuration's durability will be tested soon. The NBS releases its May manufacturing PMI on May 30. A reading above 50.5 would confirm that Chinese industrial activity is sustaining base metals at current levels; a dip toward 49 would reopen the question of whether the move reflects genuine demand or a positioning overhang that has yet to unwind.
