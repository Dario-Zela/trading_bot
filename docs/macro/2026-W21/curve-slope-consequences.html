RATES · CURVE MECHANICS · By G. Mehlman · 2026-05-24 · 5 min read

Ninety-seven basis points of slope has consequences beyond the curve itself

The Marriner S. Eccles Federal Reserve Board Building in Washington, D.C. — the institution now caught between long-end inflation pressure and short-end over-tightening risk. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
At 97 bps of 3M-10Y slope, bank carry has returned — but the 30Y Treasury at 5% prices mortgages that squeeze buyers and leaves the Fed with no clean exit.

The yield curve spent most of 2023 and 2024 inverted — short rates above long rates, announcing recession risk and squeezing bank margins in equal measure. That period has ended. The 3-month to 10-year Treasury spread now stands at +97 basis points, the steepest reading of the current normalization cycle, and what was once a chart footnote has become an operational fact across three distinct parts of the economy: bank profitability, housing affordability, and the Federal Reserve's remaining policy room.

Carry is back — but the market already knew

A positive 3M-10Y spread means the fundamental business of banking is viable again. Banks fund themselves at rates near the short end — deposits, overnight facilities — and deploy capital at longer maturities. When the curve inverted, that trade bled. At 97 basis points of slope, with the 3-month Treasury yield anchored near 3.9% and the 10-year in the high 4s, the net interest margin math has improved materially.

The Financial Select Sector ETF (XLF) recorded a 1.64% gain over the past five trading sessions. The move is real but muted — and deliberately so. Much of the benefit from curve steepening was priced into bank stocks over the preceding weeks as the inversion unwound. The weekly tick reflects confirmation rather than discovery. Regional lenders and diversified banks have already guided to expanded net interest margins in their Q1 commentary; XLF is marking that consensus as correct.

The carry revival does not resolve every risk in the sector. Investment-grade and high-yield spreads have held broadly stable even as duration has sold off — a divergence that implies either credit optimism is misplaced or the rate selloff is overdone. One of those will have to move.

The long end's affordability tax

While banks benefit from the slope, the long end of the curve is imposing costs on homebuyers. The 30-year Treasury yield cleared a recent auction at 5.064% — a level last seen before the financial crisis — and traded higher still through last week, briefly crossing 5.19%. That move matters because 30-year fixed mortgage rates are priced at roughly 130-150 basis points above the 10-year Treasury yield. With the 10-year in the upper 4s, conforming mortgage rates have moved above 6.5%.

At 6.5%, the monthly payment on a median-priced new home has pushed affordability beyond the reach of most buyers. Industry data indicates roughly 65% of US households cannot afford a median-priced new home — a figure that tightens with every additional basis point at the long end.

That context makes the week's real estate performance difficult to read as a buy signal. XLRE gained 3.08% over five trading sessions. Its 20-day return is 1.67%. The divergence between the weekly spike and the trend tells its own story: a 1.67% drift over three weeks does not become a fundamental re-rating when it accelerates to 3% in the final five days. Short-covering and sector rotation are the more plausible explanations.

The 30-year Treasury above 5% is a durable headwind for real estate investment trust valuations. It raises discount rates directly and caps the appeal of rental yield relative to risk-free alternatives. Term premium — the extra return investors demand to hold long-duration debt rather than rolling short-term bills — has repriced upward after years of suppression. That shift is not a one-week phenomenon.

The Fed's narrowing exit

The Federal Open Market Committee held its target range at 3.5-3.75% at its April 29 meeting. Officials project just one quarter-point reduction for the remainder of 2026 — a cautious posture that reflects a genuine bind.

Cutting rates now risks inflating long-end inflation expectations further, steepening the curve and pushing mortgage rates higher still. The market has already demonstrated it will move before the Fed does when inflation looks sticky: the 30-year yield's climb from the mid-4s to above 5% since late spring has been driven by inflation uncertainty and fiscal concerns, not by any Fed action. Not cutting, on the other hand, risks compounding what is already a mild over-tightening at the short end as the labor market softens. Core PCE inflation is projected to end 2026 at 2.5% — above target, but not at a level that forecloses easing.

The 3M-10Y spread captures this tension in a single number. It is 97 basis points wide not because the economy is posting exceptional growth, but because short rates are held by an on-hold central bank while long rates have been pushed higher by fiscal concern, term premium, and inflation risk. It is a different variety of steep curve from the growth-driven steepening of prior expansions — and it is transmitting differently as a result: carry returns to banks, affordability deteriorates for buyers, and the policy exit narrows for the Fed simultaneously.

The next data points that matter are the May CPI release and the subsequent FOMC meeting in mid-June. If core inflation prints above 2.5%, the single expected rate cut for the year becomes harder to justify — and the exit from the current configuration narrows further. The 30-year Treasury above 5% is the market's running verdict on how long that holds. Ninety-seven basis points of slope is not the anomaly here. It is the consequence.
