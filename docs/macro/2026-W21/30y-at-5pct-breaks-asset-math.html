RISK · LONG RATES · By S. Vance · 2026-05-18 · 5 min read

The 5% 30-year: the level at which asset-class mathematics starts to break

The U.S. Treasury Building in Washington, D.C. — whose debt issuance at the long end is now setting the rate that reprices private asset underwriting. — Rchuon24 / Wikimedia Commons / CC BY-SA 3.0
In one sentence
At 5.15%, the 30-year doesn't need to go higher — staying here for four quarters is enough to reprice pensions, LBOs, and CRE equity.

The 30-year Treasury yield settled at 5.147% this week — its highest print since May 2025 — and the phrase "five percent" is already being treated as psychologically significant. That framing understates the case. Five percent on the long bond is not a round-number fetish. It is the level at which the actuarial and underwriting mathematics that underpin three distinct asset classes begin to bend against the holders.

Pension funds, private equity sponsors, and commercial real estate equity investors all built their plans around long rates below 4%. Some of those assumptions have already been revised. What has not been fully priced — because it is inherently slow-moving — is the cumulative damage that accumulates when rates stay at 5% or above for four consecutive quarters.

The pension arithmetic

For corporate defined-benefit plans, the immediate picture looks healthy. Milliman's monthly index of the 100 largest U.S. corporate DB plans shows a funded ratio of 108.9% as of March 31, 2026, with a benchmark discount rate of 5.65%. Higher discount rates reduce the present value of future liabilities — which mechanically improves funded ratios — and that is exactly what happened through most of the past twelve months.

The trap is on the asset side. March's Milliman data captured the dynamic in miniature: despite the higher discount rate, total funded status fell by $9 billion in the month, because plan assets returned -3.33%. Liabilities fell by $27 billion; assets fell by $20 billion. The discount rate helped — but equities gave most of it back. In a sustained 5%+ regime, if equities reprice lower (as elevated long rates tend to produce with a 6-18 month lag through valuation compression), that offset shrinks. A representative plan with $1 billion in assets, split 50/50 between fixed income and equities, currently at 108% funded, approaches 100% funded on a 12-point equity drawdown if rates simultaneously edge 20 basis points higher. Surplus does not translate to cushion as symmetrically as the headline ratio suggests.

Public pension plans carry a different problem. Most still use assumed rates of return of 6.5-7.5% as their liability discount rate — a convention that insulates reported funded status from market rates. With a risk-free 30-year at 5.15%, the equity risk premium embedded in those assumptions is implausibly thin. The adjustment, when it comes, runs in the wrong direction: acknowledging a lower sustainable return forces a higher reported liability and a lower funded ratio. That reckoning has historically arrived with a lag, and it is seldom linear.

The LBO underwriting problem

Private equity's 2024 vintage is particularly exposed. Deals closed that year carried average debt multiples of 5.2x EBITDA and average interest coverage of 2.34x — a record low, per PitchBook data. Sponsors were paying financing yields averaging 9.5% and contributing equity checks of 48-51% of enterprise value, the highest on record, precisely because debt was expensive and lenders were demanding more cushion.

The assumption embedded in those underwriting models was that rates would normalize over the hold period. A 2024 LBO financed at 9.5% carrying cost, with an entry multiple of 11-12x EBITDA, needs to exit at a comparable or higher multiple to generate a target IRR of 20-25%. If the 30-year stays above 5%, exit multiples compress: acquirers in 2027-2028 face the same high-cost debt environment, and the multiple they can justify underwriting shrinks accordingly. The deals that were already marginal at entry — thin interest coverage, cyclical revenue, limited pricing power — are the ones where a sustained rate environment converts a below-target return into a realized loss. The rate does not need to go higher to do damage. Staying here for four more quarters is sufficient.

Commercial real estate: the equity wipeout math

The mechanism in commercial real estate is more direct. Asset values are a function of net operating income divided by the capitalization rate — and cap rates are, in practice, benchmarked against risk-free rates. When the 10-year was at 1.5%, a 4.5% cap on a multifamily or industrial asset offered roughly 300 basis points of spread. With the 30-year at 5.15%, that same 4.5% cap rate offers negative spread. The market has already begun adjusting: office cap rates averaged 7.4% in Q4 2025, multifamily 5.9%, retail 6.7%, industrial 6.4%.

Consider the arithmetic for a stabilized asset purchased at a 4.5% cap in 2022 with $10 million in net operating income. Purchase price: $222 million. Debt at 65% LTV: $144 million. If cap rates normalize to a 200 basis-point spread over the 30-year — roughly 7.1% — that same $10 million in NOI implies a property value of $141 million. Debt remains at $144 million. Equity is underwater. CMBS distress is already running at 7.29%, and first-loss tranches in affected deals have experienced price declines of multiple percentage points in recent months.

None of this is an immediate-crisis dynamic. It is a slow-moving feedback loop. The equity market, however, is not oblivious: XLRE has given back 1.84% over five trading days and 1.99% over twenty. LQD — the liquid proxy for long-duration investment-grade paper — is down 1.17% on the week, a duration trade rather than a credit stress event. Utilities, the other large yield-proxy sector, have dropped 3.96% over twenty sessions. These moves are the liquid market's read on a rate level that the illiquid markets will price more slowly.

What to watch

The practical damage horizon is four to six quarters. The deterioration from a sustained 5%+ regime does not appear in Q2 2026 earnings calls; it shows up in 2027 pension actuarial reports, in LBO sponsor mark-to-market disclosures, and in commercial real estate appraisals that lag transaction prices by six to twelve months.

The signal worth tracking is not the yield level itself — it's the equity market's response to long-rate persistence. If equities hold here while rates stay elevated, pension funded ratios drift lower and LBO holds get more expensive, but orderly. If equities correct in response to multiple compression, the drawdown accelerates the feedback through every channel simultaneously.

The May 2026 Milliman Pension Funding Index, due later this month, will capture April's market moves against the rate backdrop. The Fed's June meeting — where markets are pricing approximately 25 basis points of cuts — provides the other pillar: whether short-rate relief materializes fast enough to interrupt the feedback loop, or whether the fiscal premium now embedded in the long end overrides whatever happens at the short end.
