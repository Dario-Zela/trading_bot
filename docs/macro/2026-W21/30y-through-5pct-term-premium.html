The 30-year crosses 5%: term premium is back and it has demands

The US Treasury Building in Washington, DC — the institution at the center of a long-bond market demanding higher compensation for duration risk. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
The 30-year Treasury at 5.147% — its highest since 2007 — is a term premium and fiscal risk trade, not a growth upgrade, and the auction data confirms the market is pricing duration scarcity.

For the first time since the eve of the 2008 financial crisis, the United States government is paying more than 5% to borrow money for thirty years. That threshold — 5.147% on the 30-year Treasury as of Monday — is not arbitrary. It is the level at which the math of nearly every major asset class begins to shift: mortgage affordability, pension liability discounting, equity risk premiums, and the implicit assumption that duration is eventually rewarded. The bear steepener that has been building since April has now delivered on its worst-case scenario for the long end.

The curve tells the story in a single line of data. The 3-month bill yields 3.568%. The 5-year sits at 4.280%. The 10-year is at 4.623%. And the 30-year is at 5.147% — an 86.7 basis point spread between the 5-year and the long bond. That gap is not a rounding error. It means the market is demanding disproportionately higher compensation for duration at the very long end, and the compensation is accelerating.

What 5% actually touches

The 30-year Treasury is not just a benchmark; it is an input into pricing systems that govern trillions of dollars of real-economy obligations.

Mortgage rates, which track the 10-year more directly, currently average 6.36% for a 30-year fixed loan. The 10-year's move to 4.623% has already done most of the damage — but a 30-year above 5% signals that the rate environment is not stabilizing. Fannie Mae's May housing forecast puts the average 30-year mortgage rate at 6.3% through the first quarter of 2027. That projection was built before the long end pushed through this level.

For pension funds, the arithmetic runs in the opposite direction. Discount rates for defined-benefit liabilities are tied to corporate bond yields and, through them, to the Treasury curve. Current effective discount rates for calendar-year plan sponsors are running in the 5.3%–5.7% range. A sustained rise in the 30-year actually improves funded ratios on paper — liabilities shrink when you discount them at a higher rate. But plan sponsors with substantial long-duration fixed-income allocations are absorbing mark-to-market losses on the asset side simultaneously. The net effect depends on the specific hedge ratio, and most plans are not fully duration-matched.

For equities, the mechanism is more direct and more uncomfortable. With the implied equity risk premium running around 4.2%–4.5% over Treasuries, a 30-year at 5.147% means the required return to justify holding equities — the risk-free rate plus the premium — is now above 9%. The S&P 500's earnings yield is nowhere close to that. The multiple compression that a sustained 5%-plus regime would imply is not a tail scenario; it is the arithmetic.

Term premium or growth re-rating?

The critical diagnostic question is whether the 30-year is rising because markets are upgrading their long-run growth expectations — which would be at least partially self-correcting through stronger revenues and corporate earnings — or because investors are demanding more compensation simply to hold long-duration US government paper. The distinction matters for almost every subsequent policy decision.

The evidence tilts heavily toward the second interpretation. A genuine, clean growth upgrade typically attracts capital into US assets, strengthening the dollar alongside equities and keeping credit spreads contained. What the market has produced instead is a rising dollar — the DXY has recovered to 99 — accompanied by a selloff in long-duration credit. LQD, the iShares investment-grade bond ETF with roughly eight years of duration, is down 1.17% on the week. That pattern — dollar up, long bonds down, IG credit down — is a term premium and fiscal risk trade, not a growth upgrade.

The inflationary backdrop reinforces the term premium interpretation. CPI has risen to 3.8% year-over-year, with PPI jumping to 6.0%, the fastest pace since 2022. Oil near $100 per barrel — driven by the Iran conflict and ongoing supply constraints — is flowing through to producer prices with a lag that has not yet fully appeared in consumer data. When the bond market is pricing for an inflation environment that may get worse before it gets better, and the fiscal picture shows a cumulative deficit of $1.2 trillion through March 2026, investors are not being irrational in demanding extra compensation for thirty-year duration.

The NY Fed's Adrian-Crump-Moench term premium model had the 10-year term premium deeply negative through much of the 2021–2023 QE era. It has now turned positive. That regime shift — from negative to positive term premium — is structural, not a one-week phenomenon.

The auction speaks plainly

The Treasury Department's May 13 auction of $25 billion in 30-year bonds cleared at 5.046%, the first time a long bond auction has priced above 5% since August 2007. The result did not inspire confidence about the depth of demand at these levels. The bid-to-cover ratio came in at 2.303, below the six-auction trailing average of 2.43 and the weakest reading since November 2025. The auction also exhibited a tail — the final awarded yield came in 0.5 basis points above the when-issued yield of 5.041%, meaning investors demanded more than the market expected to take the debt down. It was the second consecutive long-bond auction to tail.

The government sold $691 billion in Treasury securities across all maturities during the week of May 11–15. That weekly volume is not unusual in the current fiscal environment. What is unusual is the combination of that volume with weak marginal demand at the long end. When the bid-to-cover falls and the tail widens simultaneously, the market is signaling that it can absorb this supply — but only at prices the issuer did not plan for.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has signaled no plans to increase long-duration auction sizes further, preferring to fund at the short end via bills. That preference may be prudent, but it is also finite: a strategy of perpetual rollover in bills creates its own duration mismatch and leaves the long-duration financing problem unresolved.

What the Fed can and cannot do

The arrival of Kevin Warsh as Federal Reserve Chair — confirmed 54–45 on May 13, the most divisive Fed confirmation in modern history — adds a layer of uncertainty that the bond market is actively pricing. The gap between the effective federal funds rate at 3.63% and the 30-year yield at 5.147% is now 149 basis points. That spread reflects the market's view that the front end is too low for the inflation environment, but that cutting short rates — the tool that would most directly relieve pressure at the front end — would likely accelerate the long-end selloff rather than arrest it.

Chris Rupkey, chief economist at FWDBONDS, put the policy bind plainly: the inflation data is "another nail in the coffin of the idea Fed officials have to welcome the new Fed Chair with an interest rate cut this year." J.P. Morgan's base case is for the Fed to hold rates steady through the end of 2026.

If that base case holds, and the 30-year remains at or above 5% for a full quarter, the consequences compound. Mortgage application volumes, already suppressed by the housing affordability crisis, face another leg down. Corporate issuers with long-duration refinancing needs will either delay or pay substantially higher coupons. And the implicit assumption that the Treasury market can absorb any volume of supply at a spread that doesn't destabilize equities will face a real test.

The Japan parallel is not comforting. The 30-year JGB yield hit a record 4.17% on Monday — the highest since the tenor was first issued in 1999. The global bond market is not experiencing an America-specific fiscal scare; it is repricing the long end of sovereign curves simultaneously, with inflation and supply the common thread.

What comes next

The next significant data point is the May CPI print, due in mid-June. If the oil-driven PPI surge of 6.0% begins to flow through to consumer prices — which have a typical lag of one to three months — another CPI beat would make a full-quarter stay above 5% on the 30-year far more likely than a reversal. The next 30-year auction, scheduled for mid-June, will show whether the May tails were a one-off or a new clearing mechanism. And the first Warsh FOMC meeting will provide the clearest read yet on whether the new Fed leadership is prepared to lean against the long-end selloff, or whether it regards the term premium rise as something the market must work through on its own.

The 30-year at 5.147% is not a crisis. It is a constraint — on housing, on corporate financing, on equity valuations, and on the government's own cost of capital. Constraints of this kind have a way of clarifying what the next set of decisions must address.
