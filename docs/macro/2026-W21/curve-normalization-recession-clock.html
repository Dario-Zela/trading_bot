Curve normalization at +105bps: the recession clock has been reset — but read the fine print

The 10-year minus 2-year Treasury yield spread during the 2022 inversion — the 3M-10Y spread followed a similar trajectory before this year's bear-steepened re-normalization. — Wikimedia Commons
In one sentence
The 3M-10Y spread's flip to +105bps looks like an all-clear, but a bear steepener driven by fiscal risk historically precedes recession — not reprieve.

The 3-month/10-year Treasury spread closed at +105.5 basis points on Monday — a positive number after nearly three years in negative territory. By the textbook definition that most traders and economists still use, an uninverted curve means recession risk has dissipated. The recession clock, the conventional wisdom goes, has been reset.

Read the fine print.

The steepening that produced this number is driven entirely from the long end. The 30-year yield has crossed 5.147%; the 10-year sits near 4.623%. Meanwhile the 3-month bill is parked at 3.568%, barely moved from where it was six months ago. The Federal Reserve has not materially eased. Short rates haven't fallen. What has happened is that the long end has risen — sharply and persistently — on a combination of inflation anxiety, term premium expansion, and a bond market with diminishing appetite for US fiscal risk.

That is a bear steepener. It is not the same thing as a bull steepener, and the distinction is not academic.

The Two Paths Out of Inversion

A yield curve un-inverts in one of two ways. In the first — a bull steepener — short-term rates fall because the Fed is cutting in response to economic weakness. Recession is either underway or imminent; the Fed responds; the front end drops. This is broadly what markets expected when the 2022–2024 inversion began to unwind.

In the second path — a bear steepener — long-term rates rise while the short end stays anchored. The curve normalizes arithmetically, but through a different mechanism: not monetary easing, but term premium expansion. Long-duration investors demand more compensation to hold Treasuries. That can reflect inflation expectations, fiscal risk, or plain supply pressure. Financial conditions don't ease under a bear steepener; in important respects they tighten, because the long rates that set mortgage costs and corporate borrowing costs are moving up, not down.

The historical record on bear-steepened re-normalizations is not comforting. In September 2000, the yield curve bear-steepened out of its inversion — recession followed three months later. In May–June 2007, the same pattern emerged; the recession began in December. In 1969, the lead time was two months. The spread flips positive, the recession clock officially resets — and then the downturn arrives anyway.

The Fingerprint of Fiscal Risk

The drivers here are specific. Core CPI printed at 2.8% year-over-year in April, above the 2.7% consensus — the highest reading in nearly three years. The US government sold $691 billion in Treasury securities in a single week in mid-May. The federal deficit for fiscal year 2026 is tracking toward $1.9 trillion; the first five months alone produced a $1 trillion shortfall. Debt held by the public sits at 101% of GDP, on a trajectory toward 120% by 2036 per CBO projections.

In that context, the 30-year at 5.147% is not a mystery. It is the bond market pricing a risk premium that wasn't required in the post-financial-crisis era of suppressed term premia. Term premium is back — and it is being built into the long end faster than the short end can respond to any growth slowdown.

The falsifiable call: this is a fiscal and inflation-driven bear steepener, not a growth-driven one. A growth-driven bear steepener would arrive alongside rising breakevens, hot activity data, and an economy running above capacity — the curve repricing upward because things are going better than expected. That is a different animal, and in that scenario the uninversion would be closer to a genuine all-clear. What the data shows instead is sticky inflation and supply-constrained growth, with Treasury issuance flooding a market where demand has limits.

The curve has normalized on paper. Financial conditions have not eased.

What to Watch

The June 16–17 FOMC meeting will test whether the Fed reads the long-end move as an implicit tightening that reduces the urgency of rate cuts, or dismisses it as a fiscal phenomenon outside its control. The next CPI print carries disproportionate weight: another upside surprise would cement the bear steepener narrative and likely push the 30-year higher still. And upcoming 30-year auctions — which have recently shown demand soft spots — are the operational test of whether term premium has found a floor or still has room to expand.

If the 30-year stabilizes here and economic data genuinely weakens, the Fed could begin cutting and convert this into a bull steepener — which would be a cleaner signal. But if long rates keep rising while the front end holds, the +105bps spread will look, in retrospect, not like the end of the recession risk window but the middle of it.
